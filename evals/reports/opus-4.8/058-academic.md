# Neoanalysts vs. Oralists on the *Iliad* and the Epic Cycle

## Bottom line

The divide is real but, in current scholarship, narrowing. **Neoanalysts** (Burgess, *The Tradition of the Trojan War in Homer and the Epic Cycle*, 2001; Currie, *Homer's Allusive Art*, 2016; Tsagalis, *The Oral Palimpsest*, 2008) hold that the *Iliad* presupposes and transforms identifiable pre-existing Trojan-War narrative traditions, reflecting their motifs through "motif transference" with a discernible *direction* of influence. **Oralists** (Nagy, Foley, Bakker) hold that shared themes arise from a common oral-formulaic system whose elements resonate against the whole tradition ("traditional referentiality," "mythological intertextuality") with no text holding priority over another — the *Iliad* and the Cyclic poems being related *collaterally* (as siblings), not by descent [1][2].

The methodological hinge is **directionality and priority**: neoanalysts posit dependence on source-narratives (even oral ones) and a one-way transfer; oralists posit a synchronic web of resonance. The most consequential development since 2000 has been *internal convergence*: Burgess and Currie both detach neoanalysis from its old assumption of fixed written sources, producing hybrid "oral allusion" models that overlap substantially with Foley's "traditional referentiality" — leaving open the question whether the two camps now describe the same phenomenon at different levels of resolution [2][3].

One premise of the question requires **correction**: **P.Oxy. 3829** (*Oxyrhynchus Papyri* vol. LVI) is not a fragment of the Cypria or any Cyclic poem. Its content is a *catechism and hypotheses (summaries) to* Iliad *Book 1* — a philological/school text on the *Iliad* itself (Mertens–Pack³ 01157.010; Trismegistos/LDAB 1676). It has not materially reshaped the *Cypria*/Epic-Cycle debate; the post-2000 impact on that debate comes from other tools (below) rather than from this papyrus [4].

| Dimension | Neoanalysts (Burgess, Currie, Tsagalis) | Oralists (Nagy, Foley, Bakker) |
|---|---|---|
| Relation of *Iliad* to Cycle | Directional dependence; *Iliad* reflects/transforms prior narrative material ("motif transference") | Collateral/sibling variants of a shared tradition; no priority |
| What is "alluded to" | A specific (even if oral) source-narrative or "fabula" | The tradition as a whole; resonance, not citation |
| *Dios boulē* (Il. 1.5 / Cypria F1) | Iliadic echo of the Cypria's Plan of Zeus to relieve Earth | Same theological "Will of Zeus" realized as different plots; systemic, not derivative |
| Achilles' foreknowledge of death | Drawn from the Aethiopis/Memnon death-pattern (Schadewaldt–Kakridis) | A traditional theme of the heroic-death type |
| Zeus–Thetis scene (1.493–533) | Evokes Thetis's cosmic power / Themis prophecy of a son mightier than his father | Supplication type-scene; characterization, not allusion |
| Indo-European poetics | Skeptical of probative value for textual relationships | Deployed (Nagy/Watkins on *kleos aphthiton*) as evidence of deep system |
| Relative dating | Pre-Iliadic source-traditions presumed | Cycle textualized *later*, in the *Iliad*'s wake (Nagy's evolutionary model) |

## The methodological divide

Neoanalysis descends from *Quellenforschung* (Schadewaldt, Kakridis): the *Iliad* reflects motifs that "belong" to identifiable prior narratives, and the transfer is detectable through discrepancies and "insufficient motivation." Burgess defines the core mechanism — "**motif transference**, the secondary Homeric reflection of a *primary* specific motif that exists in oral traditions" — and stresses that neoanalysts "**assume priority**" in describing it: a secondary motif evokes a primary one [2]. The oralist position, by contrast, treats the *Iliad*, *Odyssey* and Cyclic poems as "related **collaterally, rather than by descent**," preserving "story patterns, motifs, and type-scenes that are as archaic as the material in the Homeric poems" [1].

The deeper fault line is the **status of "Homer."** Finkelberg frames the older neoanalyst (largely German-speaking) view as holding that the Homeric poems enjoyed a *special status* owing to composition with the aid of writing, against the oralist axiom that a traditional text's special status is "indefensible," since "all traditional texts are by definition variants of the common tradition" [1]. Crucially, Kullmann (1984, *GRBS* 25:307–23) conceded that Homer's *sources* were oral, not written — the Cyclic poems being written down after Homer but their contents circulating orally long before — which removed literacy as the necessary ground of neoanalysis [1][2].

## *Dios boulē*: the Plan/Will of Zeus (Cypria F1 ↔ *Iliad* 1.5)

The linchpin case is the verse **Διὸς δ' ἐτελείετο βουλή**, which stands identically at *Iliad* 1.5 and at **Cypria fr. 1.7 Bernabé**. The Cypria fragment makes the "plan" explicit: countless human groupings weighed down broad-chested Earth; Zeus pitied her and resolved "to lighten all-nourishing Earth of her burden of humans," fanning "the great strife of the Trojan War... to empty the burden by death" [5].

- **Neoanalyst / source-critical reading:** Kullmann (1960) adduced **eleven** Iliadic passages (Il. 2.3–4, 37–40; 11.52–55; 12.13–16, 20–27; 13.222–227; 14.83–89; 17.647; 19.86–89, 270–274; 20.21) as allusions to the Cypria's Plan of Zeus, implying the *Iliad* presupposes that overpopulation-relief design. Davies, reviewing the case, finds **none convincing** — the first three refer rather to Zeus's pledge to Thetis to help Achilles (Il. 1.503–510), and 20.21 (Zeus's sympathy, *mélousí moi ollúmenoí per*) actually *undercuts* a master-plan to wipe out mankind [6].
- **Oralist reading:** Notopoulos (1964) argued that "the theme of a quarrel involving the will of a god is a common theme in old oral epics," so "it is unreasonable to insist on the dependence of the Cypria on the Iliad... the key phrase... is a **formula**" [6]. Nagy's treatment is the mature oralist position: the *Will of Zeus* is a **theological** idea, not merely a poetic plot-device; the *Iliad* and Cypria realize "two different Plans of Zeus" (different plots) that nonetheless **converge** as one "grand Master Plan" — the willed destruction of countless Achaean and Trojan lives. The shared verse marks the *telos* of both poems; the relationship is systemic convergence within a common theology, not one text quoting another. He corroborates the theme at *Odyssey* 8.82 (*Diòs... dià boulás*, plural) [5].

The directionality is genuinely contested *both ways* in the literature Davies surveys — some hold the Cypria depends on the *Iliad* (and is thus later), others that the *Iliad* draws on the Cypria's design — which is precisely why the oralist "shared formula/theme" reading is attractive as a way out of an undecidable priority dispute [6][5].

## *Iliad* 1 and 24 as "Cypria motifs"

The *Iliad* opens (Book 1) with Apollo's plague, the seer **Kalchas**, and the quarrel, and closes (Book 24) with Priam's journey and the ransom of Hector. Neoanalysts read such elements as the displacement into the *Iliad* of material proper to the wider war narrative; oralists read them as **type-scenes** (supplication, ransom, prophecy) actualized from a shared repertoire. Kalchas is a useful test: introduced as "the best of the augurs" who "knows past, present and future" (Il. 1.69–72), his prophetic role is integral to the war saga known to the Cypria (the Aulis portent, the ten-year prediction, recalled at Il. 2.299–330), yet the *Iliad* deploys him only where its own plot needs him — illustrating Finkelberg's point that Homer uses inherited material "in a highly idiosyncratic way," reshaping rather than merely reproducing it [1]. The Cypria's own treatment of prophecy (e.g. doubled warnings to Paris; Cassandra) is, by contrast, "typisch" of a poem fonder of marvels than the *Iliad* — the kind of contrast neoanalysts use to argue for distinct, locatable source-traditions [6].

## Achilles' foreknowledge of his own death

Achilles' awareness of his fate — the dual *kēres*/two fates (Il. 9.410–416) and Thetis's prophecy — is for neoanalysts a reflex of the **Aethiopis/Memnon** death-pattern (the Schadewaldt–Kakridis case): the *Iliad* models Patroclus's death and Achilles' impending death on the Antilochus–Memnon–Achilles sequence. Burgess systematizes this as the "**Achilles fabula**" — a chronological sequence of motifs (death before the walls of Troy with Apollo's involvement, an elaborate funeral, funeral games) of which **Patroclus is the principal "doublet"**: his duel with a foreign defender of Troy, his death "with Apollo's assistance," and his elaborate funeral-with-games "correspond to motifs we know were featured in the later life of Achilles." Even the *Iliad* 18 mourning (Thetis and the Nereids wailing, Thetis cradling her son's head) is, on this view, "a traditional event... chronologically displaced," anticipating Achilles' own funeral [2]. "Foreknowledge of dual fates" is listed among the very features that mark minor figures as Achilles-doublets [2].

Burgess's decisive qualification — and the bridge to oralism — is that the source is a **fabula**, "a narrative abstraction that is **not identical** to a specific poem's version," not a fixed *Aethiopis* text. He notes that even Lord (1960) recognized Patroclus as "a double of Achilles," so "this type of argument... is essentially a **neoanalyst** argument" that is nonetheless **compatible with oral theory** [2]. The oralist reading proper treats Achilles' death-foreknowledge as a *traditional theme* of the heroic-death pattern, available across the tradition rather than borrowed from one poem. Currie, by contrast, presses the harder line, arguing in *Homer's Allusive Art* that the *Iliad* alludes to a hypothetical ***Memnonis** and attempting to fix "the likely direction of influence" (p. 60) — exactly the move oral-traditionalists warn against [3].

## The Zeus–Thetis supplication (*Iliad* 1.493–533)

In the scene, Thetis supplicates Zeus on Achilles' behalf, reminding him (Il. 1.396ff.) of her **prior service** — having freed Zeus when Hera, Poseidon and Athena tried to bind him.

- **Neoanalyst / allusive reading:** the scene's emotional weight and Thetis's leverage over Zeus point beyond the immediate plot to Thetis's suppressed **cosmic power** — specifically the **Themis prophecy** that the son of Thetis "would be **mightier than his father**" (κρείττονα τοῦ πατρός, Apollodorus *Bibl.* III.13.5; a variant has Prometheus warn Zeus the son would "rule heaven"). By the succession logic, Zeus escapes the fate of Cronus and Uranus by marrying Thetis to the mortal Peleus, making Achilles mortal; Pindar *Isthmian* 8.27–37 attests the tradition. The *Iliad* never states the prophecy outright — which neoanalysts read as deliberate, motivated suppression of known background [7][6].
- **Oralist / traditional-referentiality reading:** Slatkin's *The Power of Thetis* (1991) — itself a fusion of neoanalytic *Quellenforschung* with oral theory — argues that Homeric epic systematically **demotes** powerful non-Olympian deities (Nymphs, Nereids, Thetis) to serve pan-Hellenic goals, so the *Iliad*'s "reticence and indirectness" toward Thetis's stature is a *systemic* feature of the tradition's self-presentation, not a footnote-style allusion to one source-poem; the cosmic Thetis "reverberates" against the whole tradition [1][7]. The strict supplication-type-scene reading goes further still: Kelly (2012) argued that Thetis's mourning is fully "in keeping with her characterisation throughout the poem," requiring no recourse to allusion to the future — to which Currie replies with a detailed counter-argument (Appendix E of *Allusive Art*) [3]. The same Thetis-power logic recurs in **Book 24**, where Zeus must appeal to Thetis to obtain the release of Hector's body, "admitting that the gods are powerless to rescue the corpse without her intervention" [7].

## The textual evidence each side cites

- **Bernabé fragments.** Both schools cite Albert Bernabé's *Poetarum Epicorum Graecorum* (1987/1996) as the standard edition of the Cyclic fragments; the Cypria's F1 (Plan of Zeus) and F2 (Hera rewards Thetis; Zeus in anger swears she will wed a mortal — also Hesiod fr. 210 M–W) are the workhorse texts [6][5]. M.L. West's Loeb *Greek Epic Fragments* (2003) and M. Davies's editions provide the competing reconstructions and numbering that scholars cross-cite (Davies's CHS commentary *The Cypria* is the running scholarly treatment used here) [6].
- **Aethiopis (Proclus).** The neoanalyst Memnon-transference case rests on Proclus's *Chrestomathia* summary of the Aethiopis (with the fragments in Bernabé); Currie devotes an appendix (A) precisely to the difficulties and benefits of **Proclus** as a witness to the Cycle [3].
- **Scholia.** The *Venetus A* (Marcianus Graecus 454) and the **bT scholia** preserve ancient awareness of variants, Aristarchan atheteses and critical signs that bear on these cruxes — e.g. Zenodotus athetized *Iliad* 1.399–406 (Thetis's rescue of Zeus), the scholia worrying over the "improbable alliance of rebellious gods," and schol. bT ad 399ff. recording a range of allegorical (including meteorological) readings of the attempted overthrow of Zeus, which also allegorizes Thetis herself as the force ordering the universe (τὴν θέσιν καὶ φύσιν τοῦ παντός); Zenodotus likewise obelized *Iliad* 1.4–5, the *Dios boulē* lines themselves [6]. Eustathius (1.33 Van der Valk) glossed the Iliadic *Dios boulē* as the divine plan exceeding Achilles' mere mortal anger [7][6]. These show that ancient editors already registered the tensions both modern schools now theorize.

## Comparative Indo-European poetics

This is asymmetrically deployed. The **oralists** invoke it as evidence that the system reaches back before any of these poems: Nagy's argument (building on Adalbert Kuhn's 1853 identification and Calvert Watkins's reconstruction) treats *kleos aphthiton* / reconstructed *\*ḱlewos n̥dʰgʷʰitom* — the Vedic *śrávas... ákṣitam* parallel — as a inherited Indo-European poetic formula underwriting the heroic-glory theme. The probative force, for oralists, is that deep formulaic-thematic systems, not borrowing between fixed Greek poems, explain shared content. **Neoanalysts** are skeptical that such deep-system parallels can establish *textual* relationships between the *Iliad* and a specific Cyclic poem: showing a theme is Indo-European does not settle which Greek poem transferred which motif. (The formulaic status of *kleos aphthiton* itself has been contested in the philological literature, which sharpens the neoanalyst caution.)

## Dating and textualization

The chronology dispute tracks the methodological one. Neoanalysts typically presume **pre-Iliadic source-traditions**: the matter the *Iliad* transforms existed (orally) before it, even if the Cyclic poems were written down only in the 7th–6th c. BCE [1][2]. **Nagy's evolutionary/multitextual model** inverts the intuitive order: the *Iliad* and *Odyssey* crystallized first and shaped a pan-Hellenic standard, with the Cyclic poems being **textualized later, in the Iliad's wake**, to fill the narrative gaps the Homeric poems left — so the Cycle's *texts* are post-Homeric even where its *themes* are archaic [1][5]. This is why the surface "the Cypria precedes the Iliad" inference is unsafe: the date of a *text* and the antiquity of its *material* must be separated.

## Post-2000 papyri and digital tools

- **P.Oxy. 3829** does *not* feed the Cycle debate: it is a **catechism plus hypotheses to** Iliad **1** (Mertens–Pack³ 01157.010; LDAB 1676), a philological/school text on the *Iliad*, not new Cyclic material [4]. The question's premise on this point should be set aside.
- The real post-2000 impact is from **digitization and multitextual editing**. The **Homer Multitext Project** (Center for Hellenic Studies) and the high-resolution **digital facsimile of the Venetus A** have made the scholia and Aristarchan critical signs broadly accessible, which strengthens the *evidentiary base* both schools draw on for ancient awareness of variants — and operationally favors Nagy's multitextual framing, since it foregrounds the manuscript tradition's plurality rather than a single fixed *Urtext* [7][5]. New and re-edited Cycle papyri and fragment editions (Bernabé; West's 2003 Loeb) have refined the fragment texts that anchor the *Dios boulē* and Thetis cruxes without overturning the basic interpretive standoff [6][5].

## Synthesis attempts and their limits

The notable development of the last two decades is **internal hybridization** rather than a clean victory:

- **Burgess** rejects the old neoanalyst reliance on fixed written *Quellen*. Because motifs derive from "multitudinous" traditional myth rather than a single text, fine detail does not stay uniform, so "the old neoanalyst strategy of seeking as many detailed correspondences as possible becomes unconvincing." He re-describes motif transference as "**mythological intertextuality**" recognizable by "a mythologically informed audience" — "significant allusion, at least in an oral, intertextually neoanalyst manner" — explicitly mixing neoanalysis with oralist method (citing Danek 2002, "Traditional Referentiality and Homeric Intertextuality") [2]. His later *megatext*/"metacyclic" framing (in *The Death and Afterlife of Achilles*, 2009) treats the *Iliad* as drawing on a whole notional body of Trojan-War song; he concedes such transference "may well be regarded as traditional and therefore not alien to oral poetry," while maintaining it is *more characteristic of Homer* than of other traditional poetry [1][2].
- **Currie's** *Homer's Allusive Art* (2016) is the most assertive synthesis-by-pressure: an "**expanded neoanalysis**" focused on *allusive poetics* (not genesis or mythology) that, unlike most of his contemporaries, deliberately retains the loaded term "**source**," posits **fixed texts** as the precondition of intertextuality (so allusion runs one way, later→earlier), and urges that Homer be read like later Greek and Latin poetry rather than as "a special case," reading "both up and down" the tradition [3]. His epilogue is the sharpest counter to the oralist consensus: "in the decades since Foley's arguments about '**traditional referentiality**' became widespread," scholarship has gone "too far in privileging the tradition over individual poetic usage," and individual usages "are not merely jetsam discarded to keep the ship of Tradition afloat" (p. 228) [3].
- **Tsagalis's** "**oral intertextuality**" (*The Oral Palimpsest*, 2008) and Finkelberg's proposal of Homer as a non-reciprocal "metaepic" both aim at common ground, the latter arguing the *Iliad*'s unique, reshaping relationship to the tradition holds **whether or not** writing was involved — a deliberately ecumenical formulation meant to let oralists and neoanalysts meet [1].

**Are they really opposed?** The convergence suggests that Foley's "traditional referentiality" and the Currie/Burgess "oral allusion / motif transference" may be the **same phenomenon described at different levels**: both accept that a Homeric passage gains meaning from material outside it that the audience supplies. The residual, genuine disagreement is over **resolution and directionality** — whether the evoked material is a diffuse traditional field (oralist) or a specific, locatable source-narrative with a determinable direction of borrowing (neoanalyst). Currie's insistence on fixed texts and one-way "sources," and Nagy's insistence on systemic convergence and later Cyclic textualization, mark the limits of the emerging consensus through the mid-2020s: the camps now share a vocabulary and much method, but still split on whether one can name the *prior text* and the *direction* of the debt [1][2][5][3].
