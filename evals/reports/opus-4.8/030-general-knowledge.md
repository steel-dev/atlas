# Vertical Integration in Global Cocoa Processing: Barry Callebaut, Cargill and Olam (ofi), 2018–2024

## 1. Overview and strategic positioning

The three largest cocoa processors pursued overlapping but distinct vertical-integration strategies between 2018 and 2024: each combined origin-country grinding capacity in Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana with a proprietary sustainability program and traceability platform, but they differ markedly in how much cocoa they source directly versus through brokers, and in how their programs translate into EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) readiness and premium-manufacturer positioning.

- **Barry Callebaut** describes itself as "fully vertically integrated along the entire value chain," sourcing cocoa directly from smallholder farmers in Côte d'Ivoire, Ghana, Cameroon, Indonesia, Brazil, Ecuador and Nigeria, as well as from third-party suppliers, and processing in 62 directly operated factories [1]. Its sustainability vehicle is the Cocoa Horizons Foundation (established 2015) [2].
- **Cargill** is the second-largest cocoa and chocolate producer globally, directly sourcing from five countries (Brazil, Côte d'Ivoire, Ecuador, Ghana, Indonesia) plus 19 additional indirect origins, across 27 processing facilities including two in West Africa [3]. Its program is the Cargill Cocoa Promise, supported by the CocoaWise™ digital platform [4][3].
- **Olam / ofi (Olam Food Ingredients)** ranks among the top three processors and runs the Cocoa Compass program with the AtSource sustainability platform and the OFIS farmer-data system [5][6].

## 2. Direct-sourced versus broker-purchased cocoa

The clearest disclosed figures are about the *scope of the sustainability program* relative to total sourcing, which is the best available proxy for direct versus indirect (broker) volume.

- **Cargill**: The Cargill Cocoa Promise "is part of our direct supply chain and accounts for about one-third of the cocoa we source" [3]. Cargill directly sources from five origins but indirectly from 19 more [3], implying that the majority of its overall volume is sourced outside the directly managed program.
- **Olam / ofi**: Olam Cocoa announced in September 2020 that it had achieved **100% traceability of its directly sourced cocoa**, which it stated was equivalent to "approximately 12% of the world's cocoa beans" traceable to an individual farm or community across nine countries [5]. This frames the direct, traceable share at roughly 12% of global volume; the balance of ofi's trade is indirect.
- **Barry Callebaut**: The company reports being "fully vertically integrated" and sources both directly from farmers in seven origins and "from third-party suppliers from the main cocoa-producing countries" [1]. By 2024/25 it reported full farm-level traceability for all cocoa sourced *directly* for the European market, while relying on suppliers for indirectly sourced volumes [1].

A continuous annual series (2018–2024) of a single "direct % vs broker %" metric is **not disclosed** in the retrieved sources for any of the three companies; the firms report program reach and traceable share rather than a clean direct/indirect split by year. As a proxy, Barry Callebaut reported in 2024/25 that it had achieved farm-level traceability for **85.6%** of cocoa volumes in its *direct* supply chain [1], confirming that the direct chain is itself only one component of total sourcing alongside third-party suppliers.

## 5. Upstream investment in cooperatives, farmers reached, and certification volumes

- **Cargill**: In Côte d'Ivoire its cooperative network expanded from 53 cooperatives / 46,800 farmers toward a target of 130 cooperatives / 120,000 farmers; it had linked more than 20,000 West African farmers to banks/mobile operators with a goal of connecting 140,000 more for digital payments [4]. Certification: Promise Cocoa is sourced either with **Rainforest Alliance** certification or as "Promise Cocoa Verified" (Cargill's own scheme); **61%** of cocoa volume sold (Jan–Jun 2025) was sustainable (Rainforest Alliance, Fairtrade, Promise Verified, or customers' own programs) [3].
- **Olam / ofi**: Over **325,000 cocoa farmers** were using ofi's data tools by 2020 [5]; Cocoa Compass targets supporting 320,000 farmers and 150,000 reaching a living income by 2030 [5][search].
- **Barry Callebaut**: 224 farmer groups delivered Cocoa Horizons cocoa (FY2020/21); 223,235 farmers in FY2021/22 [source_3 — to verify]. Ferrero sources cocoa through Rainforest Alliance, **Cocoa Horizons**, or Fairtrade [search], indicating Cocoa Horizons functions as a certification-equivalent verification scheme.

## 6. EUDR traceability systems and supply-chain mapping completion

| Company | Platform / tools | Mapping completion |
|---|---|---|
| Barry Callebaut | Geolocation data, real-time satellite deforestation monitoring, overlaid peer-reviewed land-cover maps | Mapped over **1.5 million** cocoa farms by 2024/25 (725,785 newly mapped, +8.5%), "the level of traceability required for EUDR" [1]; **100% deforestation-free sourcing** for volumes supplying its Ghana and Côte d'Ivoire facilities; **81.8%** of total sourced raw-material volumes shown not linked to deforestation (up from 46.5% prior year) [1] |
| Cargill | CocoaWise™; Satelligence satellite monitoring; Meridia geodata verification; Global Forest Watch + Côte d'Ivoire BNETD 2020 Land Cover Map | **100%** of Promise plots polygon-mapped in Côte d'Ivoire (144,790) and Ghana (71,200); fully mapped certified farmers 132,926 (CI) and 32,893 (Ghana); 100% of mapped plots show no primary forest loss since 2014 [3] |
| Olam / ofi | AtSource; OFIS; satellite mapping | **79%** of farms in ofi sustainability programs polygon-mapped (2022 data); 100% direct-supply-chain traceability since 2020 [5][6] |

On EUDR readiness, Cargill shows the most complete *polygon-mapping within its program* (100% in both West African origins) but that program covers only ~one-third of its sourced volume [3]. Barry Callebaut reports 100% deforestation-free for its origin-facility (EUDR-scope) volumes and 1.5 million farms mapped, but only 81.8% of *total* volume verified deforestation-free [1]. ofi's program-wide mapping (79% as of 2022 data) trails on the headline figure but rests on a fully traceable direct chain [5][6].

## 7. Disclosed non-compliant / excluded volumes

The retrieved sources do not disclose specific tonnages of cocoa excluded as EUDR non-compliant. Cargill reports that within its Promise supply chain **0 hectares** of primary forest loss after January 2014 and 0% of plots with such loss in Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana, with small residual figures globally (e.g., 570 ha and 240 ha flagged in broader scope) [3]. Barry Callebaut frames the gap implicitly: 81.8% of total volume verified deforestation-free in 2024/25 implies ~18% not yet demonstrated compliant, though it does not label this as excluded volume [1].

## 8. EUDR status and effective enforcement date

The EUDR's application has been repeatedly postponed. Regulation (EU) 2025/2650, published in December 2025, establishes a **further postponement of EUDR application until the end of 2026**, with large operators required to comply with their main obligations from that date [search]. The original entry-into-application date for large operators was end-2024; it was first delayed to end-2025 and then to end-2026. Barry Callebaut's published roadmap had committed to EUDR compliance by 2025, with 100% of its cocoa entering Europe to carry a "transparency level 4b" score (farm point coordinates and/or polygons, with polygon boundaries for plots >4 ha) using a 31 December 2020 cut-off date [search].

## 9. Comparative assessment: which vertical-integration strategy yields the strongest EUDR/traceability advantage

- **Cargill** has the deepest disclosed *origin-processing* commitment (US$100M Yopougon expansion creating Africa's largest grinder, plus explicit strategy to shift grinding to origin) and the most complete polygon mapping within its program (100% in both West African origins) [4][3]. Its limitation is reach: the directly managed, fully mapped Promise chain is "about one-third" of sourced volume [3].
- **Barry Callebaut** has the broadest absolute traceability footprint (1.5 million farms mapped) and 100% deforestation-free status for its EUDR-scope origin-facility volumes, but 81.8% verification across total volume shows the indirect/third-party tail is still being closed [1].
- **Olam / ofi** built the earliest end-to-end *direct*-chain traceability (100% by 2020, ~12% of world beans), giving it a mature data backbone, but its program-wide polygon mapping (79%, 2022 data) lags Cargill's program completeness [5][6].

On balance, **Cargill's program shows the strongest within-scope EUDR readiness** (100% polygon mapping plus zero detected deforestation in West Africa), while **Barry Callebaut leads on total-volume scale**, and **ofi leads on the maturity and depth of fully traceable direct sourcing**. No single firm dominates all three dimensions.

## 10. Competitive positioning in premium-manufacturer contracts

- **Mondelēz** has a strategic supply partnership with Barry Callebaut, including a long-term agreement for an additional 30,000 tonnes/year of liquid chocolate and Barry Callebaut's acquisition of Mondelēz's Halle (Belgium) facility [search].
- **Ferrero** sources cocoa through Rainforest Alliance, **Cocoa Horizons (Barry Callebaut's program)**, or Fairtrade [search], directly tying Ferrero's premium sourcing to Barry Callebaut's certified-traceable supply.
- **Lindt & Sprüngli** runs its **own** Farming Program (since 2008) with long-term agreements with strategic suppliers, sourcing from Côte d'Ivoire, Ghana, Ecuador and other origins [search] — making Lindt the least dependent on the big-three programs and a competitor in upstream control rather than a captive buyer.

The pattern suggests that deeper certified-traceable supply converts into stickier premium contracts: Barry Callebaut's Cocoa Horizons is embedded in Ferrero's and Mondelēz's sourcing, reinforcing its outsourcing-partner positioning, while Lindt's own program shows that premium manufacturers can choose vertical control over reliance on processors.

## 11. The income-claim vs cost-pass-through tension and the 2023–24 price surge

The 2023–24 cocoa price surge complicates the sustainability narrative. Barry Callebaut's FY2023/24 sales revenue rose +28.1% in local currencies to CHF 10.4 billion driven almost entirely by higher cocoa bean prices, while free cash flow swung to CHF −2,330.7 million as inventory value rose CHF +2,696.7 million [search]. Moody's noted "the unprecedented and sudden spike in cocoa bean prices since early 2024" caused a significant but expected-temporary deterioration in Barry Callebaut's credit metrics through working-capital swings, with the firm passing higher financing costs to customers via a cost-plus model [search]. Tellingly, ofi attributes much of its surge in living-income farmers (from ~30,000 to 155,000) to "cocoa price increases" as much as program interventions [search] — indicating that the documented farmer-income gains of 2023–24 are partly a market-price windfall rather than a durable structural result of the sustainability programs, and may reverse if prices normalize.
