# WISC-V versus NEPSY-II for Diagnosing ADHD and Detecting Working Memory Deficits in Children Aged 8–12: A Critical Literature Review

## Scope and bottom line

This review compares the Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children, Fifth Edition (WISC-V) and the NEPSY-II as instruments used in the assessment of ADHD and working-memory deficits in children aged 8–12, with attention to diagnostic accuracy, ecological validity, the theoretical frameworks underpinning each test, conflicting evidence, and research gaps.

A key framing caveat must be stated at the outset: **the available evidence base does not contain a direct, head-to-head diagnostic-accuracy trial of WISC-V versus NEPSY-II for ADHD in 8–12 year-olds reporting paired sensitivity/specificity for working memory.** The conclusions below are therefore synthesized across (a) single-instrument validity studies, (b) one mixed-battery clinical study using NEPSY-II together with the WISC-IV, and (c) conceptual/methodological reviews. Where this review "ranks" the instruments, it does so against the weight and consistency of indirect evidence, not against a definitive comparative trial — a gap that is itself one of the most important findings.

## 1. The two instruments: structure, working-memory/attention measures, norms, intended uses

**WISC-V.** The WISC-V is an individually administered intelligence test for ages 6:0–16:11, built around five primary index scores intended to reflect five Cattell-Horn-Carroll (CHC) broad abilities: crystallized ability (Gc, Verbal Comprehension), fluid reasoning (Gf), visual-spatial ability (Gv), working memory (Gwm), and processing speed (Gs) [1]. The **Working Memory Index (WMI)** is built on Digit Span (the primary subtest) plus Picture Span and Letter-Number Sequencing as additional subtests (with Spatial Capacity and Sentence Recall as supplementary working-memory measures) [2]; an ancillary Auditory Working Memory Index (AWMI) is also available [3]. WISC-V is an IQ/cognitive-ability instrument; in the ADHD/SLD context it is used to characterize a cognitive profile and to rule out intellectual disability, not as a stand-alone diagnostic test [3][2].

**NEPSY-II.** The NEPSY-II (Korkman, Kirk & Kemp) is a developmental neuropsychological battery for ages 3–16, organized into six theoretically (Lurian), not statistically, derived content domains: Attention and Executive Functioning, Language, Memory and Learning, Social Perception, Sensorimotor, and Visuospatial Processing [4]. It comprises roughly 32 subtests plus delayed tasks; attention/executive measures include Auditory Attention and Response Set, Inhibition, Animal Sorting, Clocks, and Design Fluency, while verbal working memory is tapped within Memory and Learning (e.g., Word List Interference) [4]. The normative sample is a national stratified sample of about 1,200 children ages 3–16 (≈100 per age band), collected 2005–2006, with clinical special-group studies including an ADHD sample [4]. **Crucially, the NEPSY-II produces no domain composite/index scores** — interpretation is at the subtest (and process/contrast-score) level, so it cannot generate a single "Working Memory Index" comparable to the WISC-V WMI [4]. This structural difference is decisive for the comparison: the WISC-V offers a psychometrically aggregated WM composite; the NEPSY-II offers granular, process-level executive measures (notably inhibition and set-shifting) but no aggregated WM score.

## 2. Diagnostic accuracy of the WISC-V for ADHD and working-memory deficits (ages 8–12)

The evidence is mixed and method-dependent:

- **Group-difference profile studies** consistently show children with ADHD (often with comorbid SLD) scoring lower on WMI and Processing Speed (PSI). Becker et al. (2021), comparing ADHD+SLD, SLD, and controls on the WISC-V, found the ADHD+SLD group worse than controls on WMI, PSI, FSIQ, AWMI, and CPI with **large effect sizes**, but with **no** differences on Visual-Spatial, Fluid Reasoning, Quantitative Reasoning, or GAI [3]. The authors explicitly cautioned that these profiles "can…not be used to confirm a diagnosis" and that the impairments were **non-specific**, overlapping with SLD [3]. Prior WISC-II/III/IV literature similarly shows ADHD children weaker on WM/PSI [3].
- **Individual-level diagnostic-validity studies** are less favorable to the WMI. A study of WISC-V diagnostic validity in students with ADHD found that **only the Processing Speed subscale** showed diagnostic validity for distinguishing ADHD from non-ADHD; the Working Memory subtests (Digit Span forward/backward/sequencing, Picture Span, Letter-Number Sequencing) did **not** demonstrate adequate diagnostic validity [2]. Cited within that literature, Canivez et al. reported sensitivity/specificity above 0.70 in exceptional children including ADHD, while Gilbert et al. advised against relying on the WISC-V as a stand-alone diagnostic tool [2].
- **Predictive value in the general population** is weak. In a large general-population sample (N=865) of 7–8 year-olds, WM deficits on the WISC WMI were present in 22%, but the majority of those children had **no** behavioral-regulation or hyperactivity/inattention problems; only 4% had WM deficits combined with such problems, and of those only about one third met inattentive-ADHD criteria — leading the authors to conclude that a low WISC WMI does "not always signal" behavioral-regulation or hyperactivity/inattention problems [5].

**Methodological characteristics.** The group-profile evidence is largely cross-sectional, often confounded by comorbid SLD and small ADHD-only subsamples [3]; the large general-population study is a methodological strength for estimating predictive value but used the WISC-IV, not WISC-V [5]. Net assessment: the WISC-V WMI reliably detects *group-level* working-memory weakness in ADHD with large effect sizes, but has **poor individual-level sensitivity, specificity, and positive predictive value** for ADHD, and processing speed often discriminates better than working memory [2][5][3].

## 3. Diagnostic accuracy of the NEPSY-II for ADHD and attention/executive deficits (ages 8–12)

Direct NEPSY-II diagnostic-accuracy evidence in this age band is thin. The most relevant study (Panvino et al., 2025; n=48 children aged 8–15) assessed ADHD, anxiety disorder (AD), and comorbid ADHD+AD using the WISC-IV and NEPSY-II [6]. Findings:

- **No** significant group differences in overall cognitive profile (WISC-IV VCI/PRI/WMI/PSI), in attention, or in working memory [6].
- The **only** discriminating measure was the NEPSY-II **Inhibition** subtest (IN-B, Inhibitory Control and Cognitive Flexibility, combined score): the ADHD group performed significantly worse/slower than the AD group (F(2,45)=4.63, p=0.015, ηp²=0.171), with response *time* identified as the key variable [6].
- Working memory and attention measures did **not** differentiate the groups [6].

The authors framed inhibition as the discriminating executive feature of ADHD and supported routine NEPSY-II use **in combination** with other tools [6]. **Rigor limitations are substantial:** very small sample (n=48), low statistical power explicitly acknowledged as a reason significant differences may have been missed, EF assessed only via NEPSY-II (no convergent measure), use of WISC-IV rather than WISC-V, and an age range extending beyond 8–12 [6]. No study in this review reports formal sensitivity/specificity values for NEPSY-II subtests in ADHD.

## 4. Head-to-head comparison: which is more sensitive to working-memory deficits?

Synthesizing the indirect evidence:

| Dimension | WISC-V (WMI/AWMI) | NEPSY-II |
|---|---|---|
| Aggregated WM composite | Yes (WMI, AWMI) [2][3] | No domain/composite scores [4] |
| Detects group-level WM weakness in ADHD | Yes, large effect sizes [3] | Not demonstrated; WM n.s. in available study [6] |
| Discriminates ADHD at individual level | Weak; PSI > WMI; poor PPV [2][5] | Inhibition (not WM) discriminates [6] |
| Best discriminating construct for ADHD | Processing speed [2] | Inhibition/response time [6] |

On the **specific question of sensitivity to working-memory deficits**, the WISC-V is the better-supported instrument, largely by default: it provides a standardized, normed WM composite that reliably shows large group-level differences in ADHD samples [3], whereas the NEPSY-II lacks an aggregated WM score and, in the one available clinical comparison, did not differentiate ADHD groups on working memory at all [4][6]. However, this advantage is **narrow and weakly evidenced**: it reflects measurement structure and group-level effect sizes rather than demonstrated diagnostic accuracy, and both instruments converge on the finding that working memory is *not* the construct that best identifies ADHD — processing speed (WISC-V) and inhibition/response time (NEPSY-II) discriminate more strongly [2][6]. The strength and consistency of the comparative evidence is **low**, owing to the absence of a direct trial, small/heterogeneous samples, and reliance on WISC-IV in the one mixed-battery study.

## 5. Ecological validity: NEPSY-II versus WISC/WISC-V

The premise that the NEPSY-II is "more ecologically valid" than the WISC requires careful scrutiny, and the sources do not establish it.

- The construct "ecological validity" (EV) is itself **highly inconsistently defined** across the executive-function literature — "shrouded in conceptual and methodological confusion" [7]. A systematic review of 90 articles distinguished two components: **veridicality** (a test predicts real-world functional outcomes; ~86% of explicit definitions included this) and **verisimilitude** (a test resembles real-world demands) [7]. The review concluded that EV is so inconsistently used that the term should arguably be **retired in favor of "criterion validity,"** and that clinical utility depends on *incremental* prediction of outcomes beyond other instruments, with veridicality lying on a continuum [7].
- Critically, **both the WISC-V and NEPSY-II are performance-based tests** administered under standardized, "sterile" conditions; the EV critique in the literature is directed at performance tests *as a class* relative to informant **rating scales** (e.g., BRIEF) theorized to capture executive function "in the wild" [8]. Performance tests and EF rating scales correlate only weakly (r≈.20–.25, sharing ~9% variance) — divergent rather than convergent validity — and head-to-head studies generally find that **rating scales predict functional outcomes better than performance tests** [8][9]. Whether this reflects superior ecological validity of ratings or instead mono-method/mono-informant bias (ratings may index goal-pursuit/externalizing behavior rather than cognition) remains contested [8].

Implication: the available sources provide **no direct evidence that the NEPSY-II has better ecological/veridical validity than the WISC-V**. Both share the performance-test limitation; any "ecological" advantage attributed to NEPSY-II tasks would, on this evidence, be a claim about *verisimilitude* (task appearance) rather than demonstrated *veridicality* (prediction of real-world function), and that distinction is exactly what the EV literature warns against conflating [7][8].

## 6. Seminal/foundational and influential work

- **Barkley (1997)**, "Behavioral Inhibition, Sustained Attention, and Executive Functions: Constructing a Unifying Theory of ADHD" (*Psychological Bulletin* 121(1):65–94) — the foundational theoretical model casting ADHD as a primary deficit in behavioral inhibition, with working memory and other executive functions as *secondary* downstream impairments [10].
- **Baddeley's multicomponent working-memory model** — the dominant framework for working memory (phonological loop, visuospatial sketchpad, central executive, and the later episodic buffer), reviewed at length in its "fifty years on" retrospective [11]; the episodic-buffer paper (Baddeley, 2000) is heavily cited (cited >7,000 times per index data) [11].
- **Barkley & Murphy (2010)** on EF ratings versus tests predicting occupational impairment (cited >460 times) is the most-cited empirical anchor for the "ratings outpredict tests" finding [9].
- For the instruments themselves, the **WISC-V CHC structural literature** (Dombrowski et al., 2022; Canivez and colleagues) is the influential body questioning the five-factor CHC interpretation [1], and the **NEPSY-II design-and-purpose documentation** anchors its Lurian framework [4].

Formal bibliometric citation analysis was not available among the retrieved sources; citation counts cited here are those reported by the indexing providers for individual papers [9][11].

## 7. Theoretical frameworks and how they shape interpretation

- **Cattell-Horn-Carroll (CHC)** underpins the WISC-V's five-index design (Gc, Gf, Gv, Gwm, Gs) [1]. However, independent factor-analytic work (N=5359) finds the WISC-V more likely measures **four factors, not five**, and that the publisher's five-factor CHC alignment is not replicated; interpretation should emphasize the general (FSIQ) factor, with group factors such as Gwm interpreted only secondarily and cautiously [1]. This weakens confidence in index-level WMI interpretation for diagnostic decisions.
- **Baddeley's model** frames working memory as multicomponent; the WISC-V WMI primarily taps verbal/auditory storage-and-manipulation (phonological loop + central executive), not the full construct [11].
- **Barkley's executive-function model of ADHD** reframes the disorder around inhibition, predicting that an **inhibition-sensitive measure** should detect ADHD's core deficit better than a working-memory composite [10]. This theoretical prediction aligns strikingly with the empirical pattern observed: the NEPSY-II *Inhibition* subtest discriminated ADHD while WM measures did not [6], and WISC-V *processing speed* outperformed *working memory* for diagnostic validity [2].
- The **NEPSY-II's Lurian** framework is process-oriented and domain-theoretical rather than psychometric/composite-based [4], which is why it yields subtest-level rather than index-level inferences.

Framework choice thus materially affects interpretation: a CHC/IQ-profile lens privileges the WISC-V WMI as the ADHD marker, whereas a Barkley/EF lens predicts (and the data support) that inhibition and speed — not working memory per se — are the more diagnostically informative signals [10][2][6].

## 8. Conflicting evidence, synthesis, and research gaps

**Conflicts.** (a) WISC-V WMI shows *large group-level* ADHD effects [3] yet *weak individual-level* diagnostic validity and PPV [2][5]; these are reconciled by recognizing that group mean differences do not translate into individual classification accuracy, and that WM impairments are non-specific (shared with SLD) [3]. (b) Some literature treats WMI as an ADHD marker, but the construct's psychometric standing is contested by the four-versus-five-factor debate [1]. (c) On ecological validity, the apparent superiority of rating scales over performance tests for predicting outcomes is disputed as possibly a mono-method artifact rather than true EV [8].

**Synthesis.** Across both instruments and frameworks, working memory is a **real but non-specific and individually unreliable** correlate of ADHD in 8–12 year-olds; inhibition (NEPSY-II) and processing speed (WISC-V) discriminate more sharply, consistent with Barkley's theory [10][2][6]. Neither instrument should be used as a stand-alone diagnostic test; both authors and reviewers recommend multi-method assessment [2][6][3].

**Gaps and limitations.**
1. **No direct comparative diagnostic-accuracy trial** of WISC-V versus NEPSY-II for ADHD in 8–12 year-olds with paired sensitivity/specificity — the central gap for the present question.
2. The one mixed-battery clinical comparison is small (n=48), uses WISC-IV, and reports no formal accuracy statistics [6].
3. The NEPSY-II's lack of composite WM scores precludes a clean WMI-to-WMI comparison [4].
4. Using IQ-based tests for ADHD diagnosis is questioned: low WMI frequently occurs without ADHD symptoms [5], and inattentive/impulsive test behavior may depress scores artifactually [3].
5. "Ecological validity" is poorly operationalized; future work should report **veridicality with incremental validity** rather than invoking EV loosely [7][8].
6. Performance-based EF tests' weak link to everyday functioning argues for complementary informant ratings and, prospectively, naturalistic/virtual-reality measures [8].

**Practical conclusion for the assessment update.** For *measuring* a working-memory deficit with a normed composite, the WISC-V (WMI/AWMI) is the more appropriate and better-supported instrument [3][2]; for *discriminating ADHD*, the strongest single signals in the reviewed evidence are NEPSY-II inhibition/response-time and WISC-V processing speed rather than working memory itself [6][2]. Neither test is diagnostically sufficient alone, and claims of NEPSY-II ecological superiority over the WISC are not substantiated by the retrieved evidence [7][8].
