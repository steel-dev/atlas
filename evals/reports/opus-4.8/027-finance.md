## Bottom line

Array Technologies reports two segments — **Array Legacy Operations** (U.S. solar trackers) and **STI Operations** (international trackers, built on the January 2022 STI Norland acquisition) — and measures each at the gross-profit level [1]. The relationship between their gross margins inverted over the analysis window. In early 2023 STI ran *ahead* of the legacy business; by 2024 the legacy U.S. business had pulled decisively ahead, driven largely by Section 45X manufacturing tax credits flowing through legacy cost of revenue, while STI's margin collapsed and Array recorded a large impairment tied to the international operations.

|---|---|---|---|
| Q1 2023 (Mar 31, 2023) | 16.0% | 18.3% | **−2.2** (STI ahead) |
| FY 2023 | 27.1% | 24.3% | **+2.8** |

Segment-margin figures for the quarterly/TTM points are from stockanalysis.com's segment series [2]; the FY figures are computed directly from Array's 10-K segment tables [3][1]. The gap swung from roughly −2 pp (STI superior) to well over +20 pp (legacy superior) — a wide, persistent and *accelerating* divergence in favor of the legacy U.S. business.

## Segment structure and the basis of measurement

Array discloses **two reportable operating segments** under ASC 280: **Array Legacy Operations** ("amounts earned from the design and delivery of solar arrays in the United States") and **STI Operations** ("amounts earned from the design and delivery of solar arrays outside of the United States," primarily Spain and Brazil with expansion into other international markets) [1]. The chief operating decision maker — the CEO — **assesses each segment's performance using gross profit**, which is also the measure used in the annual operating plan and budgeting/forecasting process [1]. Segment gross profit is segment revenue less product cost, amortization of developed technology, depreciation, and other costs (primarily outbound freight and certain overhead); for STI in 2022–2023 depreciation and "other" were immaterial and folded into product cost [3].

**STI Operations** is the reporting embodiment of **STI Norland** — Soluciones Técnicas Integrales Norland, S.L. — one of Europe's leading solar-tracker manufacturers, which Array acquired and **completed on January 11, 2022** [4]. Founded in 1996 and headquartered in Pamplona, Spain, with manufacturing in both Spain and Brazil, STI Norland was a top-three manufacturer in Spain and the **number-one tracker position in Brazil**, with more than 12 GW shipped or awarded and ~$416M of executed/awarded order value as of Sept 30, 2021 [4]. The acquisition was explicitly framed as creating "the largest solar tracker company in the world" with positions across North America, Latin America and Europe and a "complementary product and a diversified supply chain" — i.e., a **geographic-diversification and supply-chain rationale** [4].

## Magnitude and persistence of the gap

The differential did not merely persist — it **reversed and then widened sharply and monotonically**:

- **Early 2023 (Q1–Q3):** STI led the legacy business by 1–2 pp each quarter; the combined company's international arm was the *higher*-margin unit [2].
- **Q4 2023 onward:** the lines crossed; legacy moved ahead by ~2.8 pp at year-end 2023 [2][3].
- **Through 2024:** the gap blew out to +6 pp (Q1'24), +13 pp (Q2'24), +22 pp (Q3'24), and ~+30 pp by Q4'24 / FY2024 [2][3].

This is a structural divergence, not noise: legacy margin roughly **tripled** (12% in FY2022 → 41% in FY2024) while STI margin **fell by more than half** (16% → 11%) over the same span [3].

## What drives the trend

The dominant driver of the legacy uplift is the **Inflation Reduction Act**, specifically the **Section 45X Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit** and domestic-content provisions. Array records 45X manufacturing-credit benefits as a **reduction of cost of revenue in the legacy U.S. segment**, mechanically lifting legacy gross margin; STI's international (Spain/Brazil) production does not qualify for these U.S. credits, so no comparable benefit accrues to STI. Combined with falling U.S. revenue (legacy revenue fell from 1.17B in 2023 to 662M in 2024) that nonetheless carried richer margins, this points to a **price/mix and policy-credit effect** rather than pure volume [3][1].

On the STI side, the deterioration reflects **weaker international pricing and mix and lower volumes** in EMEA/LATAM markets — STI revenue fell from 404M (2023) to 254M (2024) with gross profit collapsing from 98M to 28M [3][1]. Brazil revenue alone roughly halved (258M → 135M), and Brazil PP&E was written down to near zero (3,305 → 13), consistent with a sharp pullback and impairment in the international footprint [1]. Array recorded a large consolidated loss before taxes in 2024 (−250.6M) inclusive of substantial operating charges (total operating expenses of −524.7M), reflecting impairment activity weighing on the period [1].

## Strategic-value and efficiency reading

- **Legacy U.S. (Array Legacy Operations):** now the clear margin and strategic anchor. A ~41% FY2024 gross margin on 662M of revenue [3] reflects both Array's incumbent U.S. tracker franchise and a policy moat — 45X/domestic-content credits that competitors without U.S. manufacturing cannot easily replicate. This is the higher-efficiency, higher-strategic-value unit on current economics.
- **STI Operations (international):** at ~11% FY2024 gross margin [3], STI is now **margin-dilutive** to the consolidated 32.5% [3]. Its value is **diversification** — geographic reach into Europe and the #1 position in Brazil [4] — rather than near-term profitability. The decline plus impairment signals integration and competitive challenges (international pricing pressure, terrain-specialized dual-row product, no IRA tailwind).

## Competitive positioning: U.S. vs international

The contrasting trends map directly onto two different competitive environments. In the **U.S.**, Array benefits from IRA-era reshoring economics; 45X credits booked through cost of revenue convert domestic manufacturing into a durable margin advantage, and the legacy business's climb from 16% (Q1'23) to 36–41% (late 2024) tracks the ramp of those credits [2][3]. In **EMEA/LATAM**, STI competes without that subsidy in markets with tougher pricing; its margin slid from ~18% (Q1'23) to the low-teens (late 2024) even as it retained its Brazilian leadership [2][4]. The result is that Array's competitive strength is increasingly **policy- and geography-specific**, concentrated in the protected U.S. market.

## Implications for acquisition strategy and integration priorities

The 2022 STI Norland deal was sold on **geographic diversification and a lower-cost, diversified supply chain** [4], and in 2022–early 2023 STI delivered on that promise, even out-earning the legacy segment on margin [3][2]. But the sustained, widening margin gap through 2024 — STI dropping to ~11% and dragging the consolidated margin below legacy's, alongside Brazil-centered asset write-downs and a large 2024 loss before taxes [3][1] — indicates the international unit is now **diluting blended profitability** rather than enhancing it. That argues for integration priorities centered on **restoring STI's cost structure and pricing discipline, rationalizing the Brazil/Spain footprint, and capturing the diversification benefit (supply-chain optionality, non-U.S. demand) without letting it erode group margins**, while the legacy U.S. business — turbocharged by 45X — remains the portfolio's profit engine. The diversification thesis remains intact strategically (Array is global #1 in trackers via the deal [4]), but the financials show the value of STI through 2024 is optionality and reach, not current margin.
