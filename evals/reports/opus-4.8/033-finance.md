## Bottom line

Rubber Leaf Inc.'s gross margin swung from **+2.86% in Q1 2023 to −6.65% in Q1 2024**, a year-over-year deterioration of **9.52 percentage points** [1]. The one-time **$190,703 loss on factory relocation** embedded in Q1 2024's total cost of sales explains **6.45 pp** of that swing; removing it lifts the quarter's gross margin only to **−0.20%** — still **3.06 pp below** the Q1 2023 baseline of +2.86% [1]. The verdict: the relocation charge is the largest single driver of the headline loss, but it is not the whole story. Even on a clean, relocation-adjusted basis the business barely broke even at the gross line and underperformed the prior year, which points to **structural margin pressure tied to the pivot toward the indirect (Model B) supply channel**, not merely a temporary expense. On the evidence, the strategic shift is **weakening core gross profitability during this transition**, with the relocation cost amplifying — not creating — the problem.

The figures are from Rubber Leaf Inc. ## Step 1 — Year-over-year gross margin change

- Q1 2023 reported gross margin = $67,121 / $2,345,541 = **+2.86%**
- Q1 2024 reported gross margin = −$196,625 / $2,954,607 = **−6.65%**
- **YoY change = −6.65% − 2.86% = −9.52 percentage points** [1]

This is the baseline impact of the operational transition: a near-10-point collapse in gross margin into negative territory.

## Step 2 — Adjusting Q1 2024 for the one-time relocation loss

The Q1 2024 total cost of sales of $3,151,232 includes a $190,703 loss on factory relocation that is wholly absent from the Q1 2023 comparative [1]. Stripping it out isolates ongoing operating performance:

- Adjusted cost of sales = $3,151,232 − $190,703 = **$2,960,529**
- Adjusted gross profit = $2,954,607 − $2,960,529 = **−$5,922**
- Adjusted gross margin = −$5,922 / $2,954,607 = **−0.20%**

## Step 4 — Structural vs temporary

Removing the temporary relocation charge does **not** restore the business to its prior-year footing:

- Adjusted Q1 2024 margin of −0.20% is still **3.06 pp below** the Q1 2023 margin of +2.86% [1].
- Even excluding the one-time cost, the company essentially broke even at the gross line and could not cover its cost of goods sold.

So the margin pressure is **part temporary, part structural**. The factory relocation is a one-time event that, by itself, pushed the quarter from roughly breakeven to a clear gross loss. But the residual ~3-point YoY erosion that remains after the adjustment is structural — consistent with the changing channel mix toward the lower-margin indirect supply model.

## Mechanism: how the direct-to-indirect shift compresses gross margin

Rubber Leaf reports two operating/reporting segments by sales channel — direct supply and indirect supply [1].

- **Direct Supply Model (Model A):** After OEM on-site inspection, RLSP is listed as a **first-tier supplier** and contracts directly with the auto OEM (e.g., eGT). RLSP independently procures raw materials, **manufactures the finished product in-house**, and delivers directly to the OEM's warehouse; revenue is recognized on acceptance, with the OEM paying RLSP directly [2][1]. RLSP acts as principal across the full value chain, recognizing the full sale price as revenue and its own manufacturing cost as COGS — the configuration that supports the higher/full gross margin.

- **Indirect Supply Model (Model B):** RLSP receives purchase orders from related parties — **Shanghai Xinsen and Hangzhou Xinsen ("Xinsen Group"), a certified second-tier supplier** to branded auto manufacturers; CEO/Chairperson Xingxiu Hua formerly held 90% of Shanghai Xinsen, reduced to 15% effective October 1, 2022 (indirect Hangzhou stake cut from 63% to 10.5%) [2][1]. Here RLSP sits one step further from the OEM (it supplies the OEM's first-tier suppliers via Xinsen) and **outsources production to third-party manufacturers**, in two forms: (1) buying raw materials and subcontracting work-in-process, then performing manual finishing (welding/constructing) and QC; or (2) buying raw materials and subcontracting finished products, tracing production with a focus on final QC [2]. Revenue is recognized at the invoice date when Xinsen Group's customers accept delivery [2].

In both models RLSP procures the raw materials and bears inventory and replacement risk, so it continues to recognize revenue on a **gross (principal) basis** rather than net (agent) [2]. The margin compression therefore does not come from a gross-to-net revenue reclassification; it comes from **economics**: in Model B, RLSP pays third-party manufacturers to produce goods it previously made itself, and it operates a tier further from the OEM through a related-party intermediary. Outsourced production cost plus the intermediary's position in the chain leave a thinner spread between the sale price and cost of sales than the in-house, direct-to-OEM Model A. As volume mix tilts toward the indirect channel — revenue grew 26% YoY while gross margin turned negative — the blended gross margin is dragged down structurally [1].

## Verdict

The strategic pivot from direct to indirect supply is, on this evidence, **weakening Rubber Leaf's core gross profitability** during the transition. Two effects are at work and should be read together:

1. **Temporary:** the $190,703 factory relocation loss accounts for 6.45 pp of the 9.52 pp YoY margin decline and is the proximate cause of the reported gross loss; it should not recur once the relocation is complete [1].
2. **Structural:** even after removing that one-time cost, the adjusted Q1 2024 gross margin of −0.20% remains 3.06 pp below the +2.86% earned a year earlier, when the mix was less weighted to the outsourced, lower-spread indirect model [1].

The pivot is expanding top-line revenue but, after normalizing for the relocation charge, has not preserved the prior year's already-thin gross margin — the core profitability engine is contracting, not strengthening, as the indirect model scales. The relocation expense is best read as an amplifier of an underlying structural margin headwind rather than the sole cause of it.
