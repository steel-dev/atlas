# Lithium Extraction Methods and Environmental Footprints, 2015–2024: Atacama Brine, Australian Hard Rock, and China's Salt Lakes

## Bottom line

Across 2015–2024 the three dominant lithium routes traded one environmental burden for another, and no jurisdiction's regulation has yet forced a clean exit from those trade-offs:

- **Chile's Atacama brine** is the cheapest route (~$6,000–7,000/t LCE pre-royalty per the SQM–Codelco agreement [1]) but the most water- and land-intensive, consuming on the order of **1,500–2,500 L of brine per kg LCE** (~1.5–2.5 million L/t; COCHILCO cites ~1,900 L/kg) [2], with documented aquifer drawdown, up to **2 cm/year of land subsidence** over the extraction zone [3], and the most acute Indigenous-rights conflict.
- **Australian hard rock (spodumene)** uses far less water (~**500–1,000 L/kg LCE** mine-to-product) but is energy-intensive and the marginal-cost producer (~$12–15/kg LCE integrated) [2][1], governed by a comparatively codified rehabilitation regime under WA's Mining Act 1978 [4].
- **China's Qinghai salt lakes** carry a high-magnesium cost penalty historically, but state-driven adoption of **direct lithium extraction (DLE)** — including the world's first raw-brine DLE plant — has cut that penalty and is the only place DLE is genuinely at commercial scale [5].
- **DLE** (10–100 L/kg LCE, compact footprint, brine reinjection) is the lowest-impact route on water and land but carries higher capex and reached only limited commercial scale by end-2024 [2][5][6]. It eases, but does not by itself resolve, the tension between energy-transition lithium demand and local water/Indigenous costs.

## 1. Water consumption per ton of LCE

Compiled COCHILCO/industry data place **Atacama solar-evaporation operations at ~1,900 L of brine per kg LCE**, with a working range of **1,500–2,500 L/kg LCE** (≈1.5–2.5 million L/t) — among the highest of any route, made more significant because the Atacama receives **less than 15 mm of rainfall per year** [2]. A 2025 localized water-footprint study of the salt flat (Link et al., using SQM's 2020 hydrogeological model outputs) found that **~97% of the water in extracted brine evaporates in the ponds (~0.68 kg water per kg brine abstracted)** and estimated **net evaporation at ~18.34 million m³/year** [7].

**Australian hard rock plus Chinese downstream conversion** runs **~500–1,000 L/kg LCE** for the full mine-to-battery-grade chain — spodumene mining ~300–600 L/kg and Chinese chemical processing adding ~200–400 L/kg — generally in less water-stressed regions than the Atacama [2]. (The sources frame Australian water use in the ~300–600 L/kg mining range rather than the ~170–200 L/kg figure posited in the question; the lower figure is not borne out by the retrieved data.)

**DLE** is dramatically lower: **adsorption DLE ~10–100 L/kg LCE, electrochemical DLE ~10–50 L/kg**, and geothermal/produced-water co-production **<10 L/kg incremental**, because depleted brine is reinjected and net withdrawal approaches zero [2]. For **China's salt lakes**, the retrieved sources document the technology shift (raw-brine and "old-brine" DLE) rather than a specific Qinghai/Tibet L/kg figure. No Qinghai- or Tibet-specific water-consumption figure (L/kg or L/t LCE) is published in the retrieved sources; as an indicative proxy only, the **DLE adsorption range of ~10–100 L/kg LCE** [2] is the closest applicable benchmark for the salt-lake DLE routes now being deployed in Qinghai, but it should be read as indicative — not a measured Qinghai/Tibet value [5].

## 2. Pond acreage versus DLE footprint

The two licensed Chilean miners, **SQM and Albemarle**, have drilled **308 brine-extraction wells** across a salt flat spanning **1,700 km²**, with extraction (and associated subsidence) concentrated in an **8 × 6 km** zone [3]. The retrieved sources describe Atacama operations as depending on large solar-evaporation ponds — water-consumptive "by design," requiring "enormous land areas in sensitive ecosystems" — whereas **DLE facilities are compact** [2]; precise hectare figures for SQM's and Albemarle's pond footprints are not given in the retrieved sources. DLE's land advantage is qualitative but consistent across sources: compact plant footprint plus brine reinjection versus sprawling evaporation cascades [2][5].

## 3. DLE adoption, 2015–2024

By 2024 DLE was widely piloted but only narrowly commercial:

- **China** is the locus of commercial DLE. Several Chinese firms run DLE after first removing sodium and potassium [5]. State-backed **China Salt Lake (中国盐湖, a China Minmetals/Wukuang entity)** brought online Salt Lake Co.'s 40,000 t/yr integrated lithium-salt project, Qinghai Huixin's 20,000 t/yr high-quality lithium carbonate project, and **Wukuang Salt Lake's 15,000 t/yr — described as the world's first raw-brine direct lithium extraction (原卤提锂) project — reaching 115,000 t total capacity nine months ahead of schedule**, with optimized recovery and energy/environmental indicators [report on 14th Five-Year Plan achievements].
- **Outside China**, the only long-running commercial DLE operator was **Arcadium Lithium**, which for decades has used an evaporation pond to pre-concentrate brine before DLE in Argentina [5] (Arcadium is being acquired by Rio Tinto [1]).
- **Eramet** commissioned the **Centenario plant (Salta, Argentina)** as the first industrial-scale deployment of its DLE technology, delivering first lithium carbonate in **December 2024**, designed for **24,000 t/yr battery-grade lithium carbonate** and targeting first-quartile cost [6].
- Eramet, International Battery Metals, and startups are pursuing next-generation DLE aiming at lower cost and freshwater use; analysts caution that commissioning problems commonly emerge before full capacity [5].

## 4. Aquifer decline and ecological impact in the Atacama

SQM and Albemarle together extract **more than 63 billion liters of brine per year (~2,000 L/second)** [3]. A 2024 University of Chile radar study (Castillo et al., IEEE) found the extraction zone **subsiding by up to 2 cm/year**, attributed to groundwater stocks "diminishing and not being replenished" and subsoil compaction that reduces porosity and the aquifer's water-storage capacity [3]. Much of the groundwater is **fossil water**, and underground springs feed the **Chaxa, Cejar and Tebenquiche lagoons**, flamingo habitat in protected areas; scientists warn subsidence could spread along the underlying tectonic fault [3]. The 2025 footprint study independently modeled water-table drawdown and reduced phreatic evaporation from SQM's own hydrogeological model [7]. (Specific DGA monitoring readings are not quoted in the retrieved sources.)

## 5. Indigenous water-rights disputes

In Chile, the **Council of Atacameño Peoples (Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños, CPA)**, representing **18 Indigenous communities**, filed an environmental-damage complaint with the Superintendency of the Environment (SMA) in **October 2024**, accusing SQM and Albemarle of causing the salt-flat subsidence; legal adviser Sergio Chamorro stated the findings could trigger a review of the companies' environmental permits [3]. The dispute is rooted in the **Lickanantay** people's water-centered cosmovision ("Puri" in the Kunza language) and predates the complaint: SQM's earlier sanctioning included documented failures to involve communities and complaints of non-compliance with **ILO Convention 169** [8][3]. Indigenous consultation is also a stated condition precedent in the CORFO/Codelco–SQM contract amendments [9]. For **Australia**, the retrieved EPA assessment addresses native vegetation, fauna and heritage-adjacent conservation factors at the Mt Holland/Earl Grey lithium project but does not document a specific Native Title or Aboriginal-heritage case for Pilbara/Greenbushes [4]; the retrieved sources do not establish a named Australian Indigenous litigation outcome.

## 6. Regulatory frameworks and their effects

**Chile — environmental assessment and enforcement.** SQM's operations are governed by environmental permits (RCA) under Chile's assessment system. Following a sanctioning process that began in **2016**, the SMA in **August 2022 approved SQM's fourth compliance program (Programa de Cumplimiento), valued at more than 46 billion pesos (~US$51.7 million)**, to remedy **six infractions** centered on **over-extraction of brine** and lack of community involvement, plus damage to native carob (algarrobo) trees, incomplete information, a missing contingency plan for the Peine system, and failure to perform meteorological and hydrogeological monitoring [8]. Among **52 committed actions**, SQM agreed to **reduce brine extraction to 822 L/s from 2027 and industrial-water flow to 120 L/s** [8] — a direct regulatory driver toward higher extraction efficiency per unit of water. Chile's **2023 National Lithium Strategy** (Boric government) restructured the resource around state participation [3]; under the **Partnership Agreement signed 31 May 2024**, Codelco and SQM will jointly exploit the Atacama, with a **First Period (2025–2030)** that increases SQM's production quota by **300,000 t LCE** (CORFO–SQM contract amendments) and a **Second Period (2031–2060)** governed by new CORFO–Tarar contracts, with Codelco holding majority/Series A control and SQM retaining ~20% of margin over cost thereafter [9][1]. The deal extends operations to 2060 but cedes operational control to the state and conditions effectiveness on Indigenous consultation [9].

**Australia — codified mining and rehabilitation regime.** Lithium projects in WA (e.g., Covalent Lithium's Earl Grey/Mt Holland) are assessed by the **EPA under s.44 of the Environmental Protection Act 1986**, with mining development, decommissioning and **progressive rehabilitation regulated through a Mine Closure Plan approved under the Mining Act 1978** [4]. Multiple statutory decision-makers apply — DMPE under the Mining Act 1978 (development/closure proposals, closure plans) and the Mines Safety and Inspection Act 1994; DWER under Part V of the EP Act (effluent, dewatering, tailings) and the Rights in Water and Irrigation Act (bore/dewatering licences); DBCA under the Biodiversity Conservation Act 2016 [4]. Historic failures at the site previously saw **unconditional performance bonds called in by the State Government** after tenement surrender — the policy backdrop to WA's shift toward pooled rehabilitation assurance [4]. The framework's emphasis is on land/vegetation rehabilitation and water-quality protection rather than the water-quantity drawdown that dominates the Atacama [4]. (The retrieved EPA report does not itself name the Mining Rehabilitation Fund Act 2012; the question's reference to it is not confirmed in the retrieved sources, though the Mine Closure Plan / bond mechanism it describes is the operative rehabilitation instrument.)

**China — state-directed salt-lake development.** Salt-lake resources are treated as **national strategic resources**; Qinghai holds China's first-ranked reserves of potassium, magnesium and lithium salts [Qinghai action plan]. Following Xi Jinping's March 2021 directive, Qinghai and the **Ministry of Industry and Information Technology issued the "Action Plan for Building a World-Class Salt Lake Industrial Base (2021–2035)" in January 2022** under the 14th Five-Year Plan, targeting optimized resource allocation and breakthroughs in comprehensive utilization, with anhydrous-magnesium-chloride dehydration flagged as a technical bottleneck [Qinghai action plan]. The result was rapid, coordinated capacity build-out and DLE commercialization in Qinghai (Golmud, "China Salt Lake City") [5][14th-FYP report]. The retrieved sources establish Qinghai's framework but do not detail Tibet-plateau-specific environmental controls or Ministry of Natural Resources mining-rights provisions beyond the strategic-resource designation.

## 7. Purity standards, impurity profiles, and the brine-vs-hard-rock route

Battery-grade lithium carbonate in China is governed by national/industry standards: **GB/T 23853-2022 "Lithium carbonate made of brine" (卤水碳酸锂)**, issued **11 July 2022** by SAMR/SAC; the general **GB/T 11075-2013 "Lithium carbonate"**; and industry standard **YS/T 582-2023 for battery-grade lithium carbonate (电池级碳酸锂)** [10]. The functional battery-grade threshold is ≈**≥99.5% Li₂CO₃**, the level the question references; the retrieved standard pages confirm the standards' existence and scope but do not reproduce the full numeric impurity table. The salient technical divide is the **Mg/Li ratio**: high-magnesium brines (characteristic of many Chinese salt lakes) historically required costly magnesium separation to reach battery grade, whereas hard-rock spodumene yields a cleaner conversion route to high-purity hydroxide/carbonate — the reason hard rock has been favored for high-nickel battery hydroxide despite its higher energy and cost [2][1]. China's salt-lake DLE/refining advances are explicitly aimed at overcoming this Mg penalty to produce "high-quality" carbonate [14th-FYP report; source_3].

## 8. Processing costs and producer positioning

**Cost curve (per t LCE):** Brine-carbonate producers in the Lithium Triangle (Argentina/Chile) sit at the low end — profitable at or below ~$10,000/t LCE, with the **SQM–Chile agreement citing ~$6,000–7,000/t LCE pre-royalty/tax for the Atacama** [1]. Integrated **spodumene-to-hydroxide** producers are the marginal suppliers at **~$12,000–15,000/t LCE** to break even; Citi pegs **Greenbushes** (the lowest-cost hard-rock asset) at ~$600/t concentrate, ~$7,500/t hydroxide [1]. (The ~$4,000/t Atacama figure in the question is below the ~$6,000–7,000 pre-royalty cost the primary agreement discloses; the higher, sourced figure is used here.) China's high-Mg/Li salt-lake costs carried a premium that DLE is narrowing [1][5].

**Producers (2024):**

- **SQM** — largest Atacama operation and the largest currently profitable lithium operation in the world; concessions/quotas run to 2030, after which it cedes operational control to a Codelco-led JV through 2060, retaining ~20% of margin over ~$6–7k/t cost; minor Australia mine JVs and China hydroxide processing [1][9].
- **Albemarle** — Atacama brine concession to **2044 (~2.5 Mt LCE)** plus 50% of **Greenbushes** (with Tianqi/IGO), the world's largest, highest-grade, cheapest spodumene mine; weighed down by over-investment in hydroxide processing in a weak hydroxide market [1].
- **Pilbara Minerals (Pilgangoora, WA)** — **FY24 (to 30 June 2024) record spodumene concentrate production of 725,329 dmt (725.3 kt, +17%)** and sales of 707.1 kt, but the **realised price collapsed to US$1,176/t (−74%)**, cutting revenue to A$1,254M (−69%), EBITDA to A$538M (−84%) and statutory profit to A$257M (−89%) [1]; Pilgangoora holds ~6 Mt LCE reserves [1].
- **Ganfeng Lithium** (A-share 002460, H-share 01772) — first A+H-listed Chinese lithium company, vertically integrated across upstream resources, midstream salts/metal, downstream batteries and recycling, with resources in Australia (Mount Marion, its main current source), Argentina, Ireland, Mali and China (Qinghai, Jiangxi, Inner Mongolia); heavily exposed to (loss-making) hydroxide processing in the 2024 downturn [Ganfeng disclosures; source_8].

## 9. Synthesis: does DLE resolve the central tension?

The three routes embody a clean trade-off: **brine evaporation is cheap and low-energy but water- and land-intensive in exactly the arid, Indigenous-inhabited basins least able to absorb it; hard rock inverts that — low water, high energy and cost; DLE promises low water and land at higher capex.** Regulation has so far reinforced rather than dissolved these trade-offs. Chile's enforcement (the 2022 SMA compliance plan, mandated brine cuts to 822 L/s, the Indigenous-consultation conditions in the Codelco JV) pushes Atacama operators toward water efficiency and state oversight without yet mandating a switch to DLE [8][9]. Australia's Mining Act 1978/EPA regime is mature on rehabilitation and water quality but addresses a fundamentally lower water-quantity risk [4]. China's state-directed strategy is the one framework that has actively financed and standardized lower-impact extraction, delivering the only at-scale raw-brine DLE [5][14th-FYP report].

DLE's water and land profile (10–100 L/kg, reinjection, compact plant [2]) directly attacks the Atacama's core harms — drawdown, subsidence, lagoon impacts — and would in principle ease the Lickanantay water conflict. But by end-2024 it was commercial essentially only in China and at Eramet's single new Argentine plant [5][6], carries higher capex, and still faces commissioning risk [5]; reinjection mitigates but does not eliminate hydrological disturbance, and it does not address the Indigenous communities' deeper objection to extractivism on sacred water systems [3]. DLE therefore narrows, but does not yet resolve, the conflict between surging energy-transition lithium demand and local water and Indigenous-rights costs.
