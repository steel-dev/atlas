## Bottom line

DiamondRock Hospitality Company's (NYSE: DRH) financing strategy has, on the evidence of its 2023–2025 SEC filings, balanced interest-rate-risk management against capital-allocation flexibility reasonably effectively. As it shifts from secured mortgage debt to unsecured term loans, the company has held total interest expense almost flat — within roughly $0.6 million per quarter — even as base rates rose and debt composition changed, because a layered book of SOFR-to-fixed interest-rate swaps actively reduced interest expense (by $2.4 million in Q2 2024 and $1.2 million in Q3 2024). The swap portfolio's fair value swung from a net asset to roughly break-even as front-end rates fell, but that mark-to-market volatility largely sat in other comprehensive income, not the income statement. Free cash flow for the nine months ended September 30, 2025 was approximately **$114.8 million** (operating cash flow of $175.7 million less $60.9 million of capital improvements), which fully funded the $295.8 million mortgage repayment program when paired with a new $300 million unsecured term loan — confirming that operational cash generation, asset sales, and unsecured-debt capacity together support the transition while leaving room for dividends and buybacks.

## Entity and reporting structure

DiamondRock Hospitality Company (NYSE: DRH), based in Bethesda, Maryland, is a lodging-focused real estate investment trust (REIT) that owned a portfolio of 36 premium hotels and resorts in the United States as of mid-2024 [4]. It files Form 10-Q and 10-K reports with the SEC under CIK 1298946 (file number 001-32514) [5], reporting on a consolidated basis with interest expense disaggregated into mortgage debt, unsecured term loan, and credit-facility components in a debt footnote [2][1].

The composition shifted materially within that small net move: the two legacy 1.63% swaps (each $87.5M notional, maturing July 25, 2024) decayed from $1,660/$1,658 to $216/$216 as they approached maturity, while the newer 3.36% ($75M, Jan 2028) and 3.50% ($75M, Jan 2027) swaps appreciated from $554/$449 to $1,958/$1,537 [1]. ## Debt composition and the mortgage-to-unsecured transition

At June 30, 2024, total debt of $1,173.3 million carried a 5.22% weighted-average interest rate and broke down as [1]:

- **Mortgage (secured) debt (~$373.3M):** Courtyard New York Manhattan/Midtown East, 4.40%, $73.4M (Aug 2024); Worthington Renaissance Fort Worth, 3.66%, $72.8M (May 2025); Hotel Clio, 4.33%, $55.4M (Jul 2025); Westin Boston Seaport District, 4.36%, $171.7M (Nov 2025) [1].
- **Unsecured term loans ($800M):** a $500M term loan at SOFR + 1.35% (Jan 2028) and a $300M term loan at SOFR + 1.35% (Jan 2025, extendable) — the floating rate was 6.80% before swaps at June 30, 2024 [1].
- **Senior unsecured credit facility:** SOFR + 1.40% (Sept 2026), undrawn ($0) [1].

Mortgage stated rates (3.66%–4.40%) were lower than the unsecured term loans' pre-swap all-in rate (6.80%), but the swaps fixed the SOFR component of the term loans at 3.07%–3.50% [2][3], bringing the term loans' effective rate down close to or below the mortgages once the 1.35% spread is added (roughly 4.4%–4.9% effective). This is the crux of the trade-off: the company is replacing modestly cheaper but inflexible secured debt with unsecured debt whose rate risk is neutralized by swaps, gaining prepayment flexibility and unencumbered collateral.

During the nine months ended September 30, 2025 the company executed the transition decisively: it made **$295.8 million of mortgage debt principal payments** and drew a new **$300.0 million senior unsecured term loan**, recording a $5.85 million loss on debt extinguishment [3]. By September 30, 2025 it had no borrowings on its senior unsecured credit facility and $145.3 million of unrestricted cash [3]. The swap book at September 30, 2025 carried fixed rates of 3.07%–3.50% on most legs (one new 3.23% leg, $75M, maturing Jan 2029, and a $50M swap added October 15, 2025 effective January 2027), all hedging the unsecured term loans [3].

## Hedging effectiveness as the debt mix shifted

The swaps materially dampened interest-expense volatility across both windows. Amounts reclassified from OCI as a **decrease** to interest expense [1][2]:

| Period | Swap benefit to interest expense ($000s) |
|---|---|
| Q2 2023 | (2,395) |
| Q2 2024 | (2,351) |
| Q3 2023 | (2,694) |
| Q3 2024 | (1,155) |
| Six months 2023 | (2,542) |
| Six months 2024 | (4,708) |
| 9M 2023 | (5,235) |
| 9M 2024 | (5,863) |

Despite the unsecured term-loan base rate sitting at 6.80% before swaps in mid-2024 [1], term-loan interest rose only $760K (Q2) and the all-in interest bill rose just $0.6M year-over-year, because the swaps offset roughly $2.3–2.4 million of cost per quarter [1]. The near-flat $125K change from Q3 2024 to Q3 2025 [3] is the strongest evidence of effectiveness: across that year DiamondRock retired nearly $300M of fixed-rate mortgages and added a $300M floating term loan, yet hedging held the consolidated interest bill essentially constant. Net change in financing cost from the mortgage-to-unsecured transition is therefore close to neutral — the higher gross spread on unsecured debt is recaptured by swap fixings in the 3.07%–3.50% range — while the company gains the structural benefits of unsecured borrowing. Cash paid for interest actually **fell** to $43.97 million for 9M 2025 from $48.65 million for 9M 2024 [3], confirming the transition has not raised the cash cost of debt.

The chief residual risks are (i) swap fair-value swings (the $4,321 → $3,927 → $(310) → $4,674 → $168 path shows the mark moves with the curve [1][2][3]), borne mostly in OCI rather than earnings, and (ii) roll/reset risk as individual swap legs mature (legs run off between 2026 and 2029), requiring continued re-hedging — which the company is doing, as shown by the new January-2027 and January-2029 legs and the October 2025 $50M addition [3].

## Free cash flow and self-funding of the transition

For the nine months ended September 30, 2025 [3]:

- Net cash provided by operating activities: **$175.7 million** (up from $148.6 million in 9M 2024)
- Capital improvements / capital expenditures: **$60.9 million** ($60,941K)
- **Free cash flow ≈ $175.7M − $60.9M = $114.8 million**

This $114.8 million of free cash flow, together with $89.0 million of net proceeds from the sale of the Westin Washington, D.C. City Center and the $300.0 million new term loan, funded the $295.8 million of mortgage repayments without straining liquidity [3]. Financing activities for 9M 2025 used $137.2 million net, comprising $295.8M of mortgage repayments, $89.2M of common/preferred distributions, $35.5M of share repurchases, and $11.6M of financing costs, partially offset by the $300.0M term-loan draw [3]. The company ended the period with $145.3 million of unrestricted cash, $50.1 million of restricted cash, $50.0 million set aside in property-improvement reserves, and a fully undrawn unsecured revolver [3].

## Assessment: balancing rate risk against capital allocation flexibility

On the dimensions the question poses, the strategy holds up:

- **Rate-risk management:** Effective. Swaps fixed the term loans at 3.07%–3.50% and offset ~$2.3–2.4M of quarterly interest cost, holding total interest expense within $0.6M (Q2) and $0.125M (Q3) year-over-year despite a rising-rate base and a shift toward floating-rate debt [1][2][3]. Cash interest paid fell year-over-year [3].
- **Capital-allocation flexibility:** Improved. Moving from asset-specific mortgages to prepayable unsecured term loans (and an undrawn revolver) unencumbers hotels, simplifies the capital structure, and preserves covenant capacity under the senior unsecured credit agreement's leverage-based pricing grid and fixed-charge-coverage tests [1]. The cost is a modestly higher gross credit spread, largely neutralized by swaps, plus a one-time $5.85M extinguishment loss [3].
- **Self-funding:** Confirmed. ~$114.8M of nine-month free cash flow plus asset-sale proceeds covered the refinancing while still funding $89.2M of dividends/distributions and $35.5M of buybacks [3].

The principal caveat is that the strategy depends on continued access to unsecured capital and ongoing re-hedging as swap legs mature through 2029; the swaps reduce earnings-level rate volatility but leave fair-value (OCI) and roll risk, which DiamondRock has so far managed by laddering new swap maturities [3].
