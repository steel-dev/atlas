# New Zealand Construction Metalworks (Structural Steel Fabrication) Market — 2024 Analysis

## Scope and definitions

This report covers structural steel fabrication for construction and infrastructure applications in New Zealand — the cutting, drilling, welding, painting/galvanising and shipping of steel framework for buildings and bridges, as defined by Steel Construction New Zealand (SCNZ) and ANZSIC industry code C2221NZ [1][2]. It excludes general engineering, manufacturing-plant fabrication and stainless/specialty applications except where they bear on the construction market.

A note on data quality: there is no single official Stats NZ or MBIE time series isolating "structural steel fabrication" revenue and tonnage for 2018–2024. The most authoritative quantitative anchors available are (i) SCNZ's industry tonnage and capacity figures, (ii) IBISWorld's C2221NZ market sizing, and (iii) the audited results of NZX-listed steel distributor Steel & Tube Holdings as a market proxy. Some company-level revenue and ownership detail sits behind Companies Office filings and paid IBISWorld reports that could not be fully retrieved; those gaps are flagged below.

The broader steel industry employed 10,670 FTEs in 2019 (BERL analysis cited by SCNZ), with a further 28,290 linked FTEs across the economy [1]. For wider context, the construction sector overall contributed 6.3% of real GDP (over NZ$17.6 billion) and employed nearly 308,500 in the year to March 2023, per MBIE's Building and Construction Sector Trends Annual Report 2023.

Two facts shape the 2018–2024 trajectory. First, structural steel's share of the multi-level construction market exceeded 50% nationally and over 80% in Christchurch (driven by its seismic performance post-Canterbury earthquakes) [1]. Second, the period ended in a sharp construction recession: IBISWorld notes import penetration "plummeted in recent years" alongside "weaker local construction trends" [2], and Steel & Tube's FY24 (year to 30 June 2024) volumes fell 21.1% to 115.5 ktonnes and revenue 18.7% to $479.1m [3]. The directional picture is therefore an industry that expanded capacity through the late 2010s and the post-COVID building surge, then contracted sharply into 2024.

## (c) Import tariffs on raw and structural steel (HS 7207–7216)

NZ Customs' Tariff (Section XV, Chapter 72, schedule effective 1/2025) shows that raw and structural steel inputs are overwhelmingly duty-free, especially from NZ's main trading partners [5]:

| HS heading | Product | Normal (MFN) tariff | Preferential tariff |
|---|---|---|---|
| 7206 / 7207 | Ingots, semi-finished iron/non-alloy steel | Free | Free |
| 7208–7212 | Flat-rolled products (hot/cold-rolled, coated) | 5% | Free (AU, CN, EU, KR, RCEP, UK, etc.) |
| 7213–7215 | Bars and rods (incl. reinforcing) | mostly 5% | Free under FTAs |
| 7216.32 / 7216.33 | **I and H sections** (primary structural beams) | **Free** | **Free** |
| 7216.10/.31/.40/.61/.91/.99 | U/L/T and worked/cold-formed sections | 5% | Free under FTAs |
| 7301.20 | Welded angles, shapes and sections | 5% | Free under FTAs |

Two points are decisive for fabricators. First, the headline structural mill products — I-beams and H-columns (7216.32/.33) — carry **no tariff at all**, regardless of origin [5]. Second, where a 5% MFN ("Normal") tariff does apply to other sections and flat products, it is waived to **Free** under preferential rates for Australia (AU), China (CN), Korea (KR), the EU, UK and the RCEP bloc, which together account for essentially all of NZ's steel imports [5]. The Customs schedule's standing note confirms that "AAN, AE, AU, CN, CPT, EU, HK, KR, LLDC, MY, Pac, PPP, RCEP, SG, TH, TPA, TW, and UK rates in the Preferential Tariff are Free" unless otherwise indicated [5].

**Effective margin impact:** Because raw steel feedstock enters duty-free from China and Australia, the tariff schedule imposes essentially **no cost penalty on local fabricators sourcing raw steel** — but, by the same token, it offers them **no tariff protection** against imported *pre-fabricated* steel from the same origins. The only meaningful trade barrier is anti-dumping/countervailing duty applied case-by-case (see below), not the standing tariff. A precise, sourced figure for tariff-driven gross-margin compression could not be established, because the binding tariff on the relevant structural inputs is zero; the margin pressure local fabricators face is competitive (import substitution) and cyclical (the downturn), not tariff-driven.

## (e) Strategic risks and opportunities

### Top 3 risks

1. **Project-pipeline volatility / cyclical demand.** The single largest risk is demand cyclicality, evidenced by Steel & Tube's FY24 volume drop of 21% and NPAT fall of 85% [3], and by IBISWorld's note of contracting import penetration on "weaker local construction trends" [2]. With ~120,000 t capacity and ~37% spare even in 2021 [1], capacity utilisation is highly sensitive to project timing.
2. **Import competition without tariff protection.** Because raw and fabricated steel enter duty-free from China and Australia [5], local fabricators compete directly with imported pre-fabricated steel on price. Chinese imports have been blamed for NZ fabricator job losses [search], and the trend toward importing fabricated sections "to minimise cost" is documented [search].
3. **Supply-chain / import concentration.** NZ relies heavily on imported steel, with China the dominant single supplier (e.g. HS 732690 articles: China US$45.6m vs Australia US$25.7m in 2023) [search]. Concentration in one origin exposes fabricators to freight-cost shocks (which IBISWorld notes already eroded import competitiveness) [2] and geopolitical/trade-policy risk.

### Top 3 opportunities

1. **Infrastructure pipeline.** The $68b five-year infrastructure programme, CRL, Dunedin Hospital and stadium upgrades provide a multi-year demand base [3][note][search]; with significant spare capacity, the sector can absorb a demand spike without going offshore [1].
2. **Anti-dumping protection and quality/compliance differentiation.** MBIE's willingness to investigate and apply anti-dumping duties (e.g. galvanised wire from China, 2020) [search], combined with SCNZ's Steel Fabrication Certification scheme covering 90% of output [1], lets local fabricators compete on assured compliance where imported product carries quality risk [search].
3. **Prefabrication and value-add.** Steel's 10–15% programme-time advantage from off-site fabrication and "just-in-time" delivery [1], plus the higher-value product/services strategy that lifted Steel & Tube's gross margin to $901/t even in a downturn [3], point to value-add (detailing, automation, ECI engagement) as the durable competitive moat against commoditised imports.

### Weighing risks against opportunities

The opportunities are largely contingent on the risks resolving favourably: the infrastructure pipeline only helps if projects proceed on schedule (mitigating risk 1), and value-add/compliance differentiation only protects margins where buyers reject the cheapest imports (mitigating risks 2 and 3). The tariff structure is neutral-to-negative for fabricators — it neither taxes their inputs nor shields their output — so competitiveness rests on cost-efficiency, certification and the pace of the public works pipeline rather than on trade policy.
