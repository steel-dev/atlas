# Feminist Legal Theory Across Four Legal Traditions: Property, the Body, and the State

Feminist legal theory is not a single project but a family of arguments developed inside very different legal orders — the liberal common-law state, the religious-legal order governed by Islamic jurisprudence, the post-colonial plural-legal state of sub-Saharan Africa, and the socialist-turned-market state of China. Each tradition starts from a different account of *why* law subordinates women, and therefore reaches a different conclusion about *how* law might be changed. This report traces that evolution, compares how each system structures women's property rights, bodily autonomy, and political participation, and works out three cross-cutting analytical questions: the universalism/cultural-relativism tension, the theory of the state and women's subordination, and the methodological split between reform-from-within and structural critique.

## 1. Anglo-American critical legal feminism: from dominance to vulnerability

**Catharine MacKinnon — dominance theory.** MacKinnon's *Toward a Feminist Theory of the State* (1989) makes the foundational structural claim of radical feminist jurisprudence: law is *male*, in that it "adopts the male point of view" and presents that standpoint as neutral objectivity [1]. Gender inequality is not a matter of irrational difference-treatment but a *social hierarchy of men over women*, and its linchpin is sexuality — "a social construct of male power: defined by men, forced on women, and constitutive of the meaning of gender" [1]. Law encodes this. Rape doctrine, which requires both force and the absence of consent, takes the male viewpoint of when sex is or is not a violation, so that the legal test tracks what men think is normal sex rather than what women experience [1]. The privacy/abortion right is, for MacKinnon, a trap: by "translat[ing] traditional liberal values into individual rights," privacy law assumes women are already equal "in there" — in the bedroom and the home — and so has "shielded the place of battery, marital rape, and women's exploited domestic labor" [1].

This generates her distinctive theory of the **liberal state**. MacKinnon rejects both the liberal account (state as neutral arbiter, women as one interest group within pluralism) and the orthodox-left account (state as mere instrument of class) [1]. Her own claim is that the liberal state "legitimates itself through noninterference with the status quo": because society is constituted unequally *prior* to law, a state that restrains itself — staying out of "body and home, especially bedroom" — simply ratifies male dominance and calls that ratification freedom [1]. Law is "a form of state power in a social context in which power is gendered" [1].

**Martha Fineman — vulnerability theory.** Fineman's *The Vulnerable Subject* (2008), building on *The Autonomy Myth* (2004), shares MacKinnon's distrust of formal equality but relocates the critique. Where MacKinnon centers the gendered hierarchy of men over women, Fineman argues that equal-protection law's "sameness of treatment" model is "post-identity"-blind: it is formal, fixated on discrimination against defined groups, and "inattentive" to the disparities of privilege and disadvantage that institutions actually produce [2]. Her central move is to argue that the "vulnerable subject" *must replace* the autonomous, independent liberal subject, because vulnerability — the embodied, ever-present possibility of dependency through illness, injury, or disaster — is "universal and constant, inherent in the human condition," and far more representative of real lived experience than the "myth" of self-sufficiency [2]. The autonomy myth underwrites a "restrained state" that does too little; Fineman calls instead for a **responsive state** — active but non-authoritarian — that monitors privilege and is responsible to the vulnerable subject [2].

**The contrast matters for method.** MacKinnon's is a *gender-specific structural-domination* account: the problem is men's power over women, embedded in sexuality and ratified by a falsely neutral state. Fineman's is a *post-identity universalist* account: she deliberately moves away from the sex-discrimination frame toward a shared human condition, critiquing the *autonomy myth* rather than male dominance as such, and demanding a positive welfare-type state rather than the dismantling of male standpoint [2]. Both reject liberal formal equality, but MacKinnon would politicize and ungender the "private," while Fineman would re-found equality on common vulnerability.

## 2. Islamic feminism: reinterpreting Sharia from within

Islamic feminist legal theory accepts the authority of the religious-legal tradition and contests its content from inside.

**Ziba Mir-Hosseini — Sharia vs. fiqh.** Mir-Hosseini's framework ("Muslim Women's Quest for Equality," *Critical Inquiry* 2006) rests on a single, decisive distinction. *Sharia* is revealed law — "the way," the totality of God's will, and so sacred, universal, and eternal. *Fiqh* ("understanding") is the human science of jurisprudence: the fallible effort of jurists to extract rules from the Qur'an and Sunna, and therefore — like any jurisprudence — "subject to change" [3]. The error she targets is the conflation of the two: patriarchal fiqh, "patriarchal in both spirit and form," is invoked *as God's law* to silence women [3]. Once fiqh is recognized as human, patriarchal interpretations "can and must be challenged at the level of fiqh" — and this can be done "as a Muslim," in religious language, rather than against the faith [3]. Reform, on this view, must operate *within* the closed legal system of fiqh; her study *Marriage on Trial* examines Islamic family law in practice in Iran and Morocco [3]. (Mir-Hosseini's co-founding of the transnational Musawah movement for equality in the Muslim family is part of this same reform-from-within program, though the present sources document the Sharia/fiqh framework most fully.)

**Amina Wadud — the tawhidic paradigm.** Wadud supplies the hermeneutic method. Treating the Qur'an and Sunnah as the primary sources from which "Muslim scholars, who were human agents, developed fiqh," she grounds gender equality in *tawhid*, the oneness of God [4]. Her argument is structural: because God is always the highest focal point — "when two persons [are] together, Allah makes the third" — the only legitimate relation between any two human beings is *horizontal reciprocity* on a single plane under God [4]. Patriarchy, which places man *above* woman on a vertical plane, therefore commits "a kind of *shirk*" — associating a partner with God — by elevating one human over another [4]. The method demands women's agency in *both* interpretation (tafsir) and jurisprudence (fiqh), pursued at two levels: individual *taqwa* (God-consciousness) and *tawhid* worked out in social practice and legal codes [4]. Wadud's later public leading of a mixed-gender congregational prayer (2005) dramatized this claim to interpretive authority, though it is her textual hermeneutic that constitutes the legal method.

## 3. African feminism: living customary law against colonial codification and imported rights

Sylvia Tamale's *Decolonization and Afro-Feminism* (2020) reframes the African feminist problem as one of *coloniality*, attacking on two fronts.

**Against "customary law."** Tamale's pivotal distinction is between *colonial/state "customary law"* and *living customary law*. Pre-colonial African norms were flexible and responsive; colonialism "ossified," "fossilized and frozen" them, reducing fluid custom to fixed written codes with fixed punishments — codification attempted in Tanganyika, Senegal, and South Africa — through judicial precedent and the work of Western legal anthropologists, producing "an inflexible colonial residue" [5]. Crucially, colonial authorities selectively entrenched the customs that served imperial control, distorting practices such as *lobola* (bride-price) and fusing "patriarchy, capitalism, and the colonial state" [5]. Her feminist payoff: scholars wrongly pit "culture" against "rights" as irreconcilable, but that opposition rests on the *colonial* version of customary law, which is "inimical to reform" [5]. *Living* customary law, by contrast, is flexible and amenable to change, and "African women have a say in how [it] develops"; the decolonial-feminist task is to protect the space where those shifts happen [5]. She points to the South African Constitutional Court's recognition of the distinction, where a hybrid of living customary law and constitutionalism has produced "emancipatory outcomes for women" [5].

**Against universal human rights.** Tamale queries the universality of the human-rights frame itself: the formal legal system and treaty-based rights were "directly imported from the colonial metropoles and imposed," and "evolved to support the capitalist economic system," prompting the question "Which humans are protected by which rights?" [5]. The very concept of "gender equality," rooted in a capitalist class structure, "rings hollow" to many African women's lived realities and shows "the inefficacy of treaty rights within the African cultural context" [5]. Yet she rejects *both* extremes — pure universalism (rights "constant for all time and the same for all") and pure cultural relativism (culture "static and cast in stone") — endorsing instead, via Celestine Nyamu-Musembi, a "contextual cultural approach" in which people in plural legal orders "mix and match" regimes and local norms can be "pathways" rather than "fences" [5]. Her constructive proposal is *Ubuntu* — humanness, communitarianism, solidarity, interdependence — as an African basis for reconceptualizing justice [5].

**The Maputo Protocol (2003).** The regional instrument that institutionalizes this debate is the Protocol to the African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights on the Rights of Women in Africa (adopted 11 July 2003, Maputo). Its provisions on marriage and family directly target the customary practices Tamale analyzes: requiring free and full consent of both parties, a minimum marriage age of 18 for women, encouragement of monogamy as the preferred form, written registration of marriages, and a married woman's right to retain her maiden name and her nationality [6]. On property specifically, Article 6(j) guarantees a woman the right "to acquire her own property and to administer and manage it freely" during marriage, while Article 7(d) governs the division of marital property on divorce, separation, or annulment [6]. Tellingly, the commentary on Article 6 is authored by Celestine Nyamu-Musembi — the same scholar Tamale invokes — and it stresses the central limit of these reforms: in countries such as Botswana and Lesotho the statutes abolishing "marital power" "do not apply to customary and religious marriages" because of personal-law exemption clauses in the constitutions, so the gains "are out of reach for women married under customary law" [6]. The Protocol thus represents an *African* regional codification of women's rights — neither simply the imported universalism Tamale critiques nor pure relativism, but a continental instrument operating within, and partly blocked by, the same plural-legal field.

## 4. Chinese feminist legal scholarship: from socialist emancipation to market retrenchment

China's trajectory is one of state-led emancipation followed by market-era reversal. The **1950 Marriage Law**, a cornerstone of the Communist Revolution, granted women property rights; later revisions, including the **1980 Marriage Law**, strengthened the principle of *common marital property*, treating marital houses as joint property [7][8]. The decisive reversal came with the **2011 Supreme People's Court interpretation** of the Marriage Law, which held that family homes purchased before marriage automatically belong to the registered buyer — historically the husband — upon divorce, so that "unless legally contested, marital property essentially belongs to the person whose name is on the property deed" [7][8].

Although the rule is gender-neutral on its face, its effect is sharply gendered, because property is overwhelmingly registered to men: a 2012 survey of Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, and Shenzhen found over 80% of marital homes owned or co-owned by men and only about 30% of deeds bearing the woman's name, reinforced by a "bride-price" custom under which husbands provide the house [7][8]. Feminist NGO leader Li Ying argued the interpretation "violates the Marriage Law's basic principle of common marital property" [7]. Yale sociologist Emma Zang's study quantified the harm: the ruling potentially affected up to 890 million people, caused a short-term one-point drop on a five-point life-satisfaction scale for about one in fifteen married women, and produced no significant effect on men; over five years roughly 42% of couples changed ownership status (about 9% adding the wife's name, 3% transferring to the wife, 29.5% to children), so that "most women did not fully regain their property rights" [8].

On **bodily autonomy and violence**, China stalled for years on anti-domestic-violence legislation despite feminist NGO lobbying; an official statistic put DV at one-quarter of Chinese women, yet courts routinely reclassified it as "family conflict" because the pre-existing law was too vague — the gap the **Anti-Domestic Violence Law (effective 1 March 2016)** was meant to close [7]. China's reproductive regime — the one-child policy, son preference, and sex-selective abortion — produced a male-surplus sex-ratio imbalance [7]. Distinctively, the official state-feminist body, the **All-China Women's Federation**, established by the Party to "protect women's rights and interests," stayed silent on the 2011 property change while running the "leftover women" (*sheng nü*) campaign, which from 2007 stigmatized unmarried women over 27 and pressured educated urban women to marry — state feminism here described as "contributing to bolstering male supremacy" [7].

## 5. Comparative structuring of property, the body, and political participation

| Domain | Anglo-American (liberal) | Islamic (fiqh) | African (plural) | Chinese (socialist→market) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Property** | Marital/community-property regimes; MacKinnon argues "privacy" hides exploited domestic labor [1] | Inheritance/marital shares set by human *fiqh*, contestable once distinguished from divine Sharia [3][4] | Land/property via customary tenure; colonial "customary law" froze patriarchal forms, living custom is reformable [5] | 1950/1980 common marital property reversed by 2011 SPC rule favoring registered (male) owner [7][8] |
| **Bodily autonomy** | Abortion as "privacy" (critiqued by MacKinnon); rape/consent law tracks male viewpoint [1] | Body and consent governed by fiqh, reinterpretable through tawhidic equality [4] | Sexual/marriage rights; Maputo Protocol mandates consent and minimum age 18 [6]; culture-vs-rights debates [5] | One-child policy, sex-selective abortion, sex-ratio imbalance; DV addressed only by 2016 law [7] |
| **Political participation** | Suffrage/representation; women as "interest group within pluralism" (MacKinnon's critique) [1] | Interpretive authority contested — Wadud's claim to lead/interpret [4] | Maputo Protocol's regional rights framework [6]; living-law participation [5] | Representation channeled through party-state body (All-China Women's Federation), itself a constraint [7] |

On **property**, the regimes diverge most sharply in their default. Anglo-American community/marital-property law presumes shared ownership but, on MacKinnon's reading, leaves the gendered private sphere intact [1]; Islamic fiqh fixes shares through human jurisprudence that Mir-Hosseini and Wadud argue is reformable [3][4]; African customary tenure is genuinely plural, with the colonial codified version far more patriarchal than the living version [5]; and China traveled from socialist common property (1950/1980) to a 2011 rule that, while formally neutral, restores de facto male ownership [7][8].

On **bodily autonomy**, the contrast is between rights framed as individual privacy (and critiqued as such by MacKinnon [1]), bodily integrity grounded in religious reinterpretation [4], the African culture/rights contest crystallized in the Maputo Protocol's consent and minimum-age guarantees [6][5], and China's demographically driven reproductive regime with belated violence protection [7].

On **political participation**, the most telling comparison is institutional. The named regional mechanism in Africa is the Maputo Protocol's rights architecture [6]; in China, women's representation runs through a party-state apparatus (the Women's Federation) that the sources show can suppress as much as advance women's interests [7]; in the Anglo-American frame, MacKinnon's critique is precisely that treating women as one pluralist "interest group" understates structural subordination [1]; and in Islamic contexts the participation struggle is over *interpretive* authority itself, as in Wadud's claim to the right to interpret and lead [4]. (Specific constitutional gender quotas — e.g., Rwanda's — and detailed suffrage chronologies are widely documented but were not established in the sources gathered here; this remains the most volatile, least-pinned element of the comparison.)

## 6. Three analytical syntheses

**Universalism vs. cultural relativism.** The four traditions occupy distinct positions. Anglo-American critical feminism is internally universalist but anti-formalist: MacKinnon treats male dominance as a near-universal structure yet attacks the *false* universality of the "neutral" male standpoint [1], while Fineman pushes a genuinely universalist alternative grounded in shared human vulnerability rather than group identity [2]. Islamic feminism reframes the debate entirely: rather than choosing between Western universal rights and cultural particularism, Mir-Hosseini and Wadud locate a *universal* egalitarian claim (tawhidic equality, divine justice) inside the tradition, treating only the human *fiqh* as particular and revisable [3][4]. African feminism, in Tamale, most explicitly thematizes the tension and refuses both poles — rejecting essentialized universal rights as colonial-capitalist impositions yet also rejecting frozen relativism, settling on a contextual "mix and match" pluralism and an indigenous justice frame (Ubuntu) [5]; the Maputo Protocol is the institutional embodiment of that middle path [6]. Chinese scholarship in these sources engages the tension obliquely: the critique is that *formally neutral, universal-sounding* law (the "gender-neutral" 2011 interpretation) produces gendered harm in context [7][8] — an empirical echo of Tamale's and Fineman's distrust of decontextualized formal equality.

**The state and women's subordination.** Each tradition theorizes a different state. For MacKinnon the *liberal* state subordinates women through self-restraint: by declining to interfere with an already-unequal private order, it ratifies male dominance [1]; Fineman's *responsive* state is the proposed correction — an active state responsible to vulnerable subjects rather than a restrained one [2]. For Islamic feminism the relevant authority is the *religious-legal* order, where the state and clerical establishment derive power from conflating human fiqh with divine Sharia; the feminist task is to break that conflation [3][4]. For African feminism the state is *post-colonial and plural-legal*: it inherited an imposed metropolitan legal system layered over distorted "customary law," so subordination is produced by the colonial legal architecture itself, and reform means decolonizing law and protecting living custom [5]. For Chinese feminism the state is *socialist-then-market*: once the engine of emancipation (1950 Law), it became, through the market-era 2011 interpretation and a co-opted official women's movement, an agent of retrenchment [7][8].

**Method: reform-from-within vs. structural critique.** The sharpest methodological divide runs between the Islamic and African projects on one side and Anglo-American critical feminism on the other. Islamic feminism is explicitly *reform-from-within*: Mir-Hosseini insists feminist argument operate inside the "closed legal system" of fiqh and in religious language [3], and Wadud's tawhidic hermeneutic re-reads the sacred sources rather than displacing them [4]. African feminism is similarly reconstructive — Tamale would revive and protect *living* customary law and Ubuntu rather than scrap indigenous law for imported rights, treating local norms as reformable "pathways" [5]. Anglo-American critical feminism, by contrast, is *structural critique*: MacKinnon does not seek to reinterpret existing doctrine sympathetically but to expose the entire liberal-legal order as the institutionalized male point of view [1], and Fineman calls to *replace* the foundational liberal subject and rebuild the state around vulnerability [2]. The implication for legal change is significant: the reinterpretive strategies offer legitimacy and traction within their own communities (reform looks like fidelity, not betrayal), but are bounded by the authoritative texts and traditions they work inside; the structural strategies promise more thoroughgoing transformation but must overcome the deep institutional and ideological commitments — neutrality, autonomy, privacy — they identify as the very source of subordination. The Chinese case sits between these poles as a cautionary empirical lesson: there, neither structural critique nor textual reinterpretation but *administrative reinterpretation by the state itself* set the terms, and feminist actors — NGOs and even the official Women's Federation — operated within tightly constrained space [7][8].
