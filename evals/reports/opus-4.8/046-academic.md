# Legal Status of Enslaved People in the 1730s British Empire: Statutes, Codes, and the Molasses Act Debate

## Bottom line

For your 1730s setting, the single most important legal fact is a **division of labor between metropolitan and colonial law**. Parliament at Westminster wrote *commercial* law for the empire — trade, navigation, customs — and never enacted a metropolitan slave code or a statutory definition of an enslaved person as property. The explicit, brutal classification of human beings as **"chattels"** and **"real estate,"** together with the criminal and police machinery of slavery (passes, whipping, branding, maiming, execution, owner compensation, trials without juries), lived entirely in the **colonial plantation codes** of Barbados, Jamaica, and South Carolina [1][2][3].

The **Molasses Act of 1733 (6 Geo. II c. 13)** is a trade-regulation statute — a protectionist duty on foreign sugar, rum, and molasses passed for the British West India sugar lobby. It contains **no slave-control or chattel clause whatsoever** [4]. The "slave control" framing of the Molasses Act is not in the statute and not the scholarly consensus; the recognized debate is instead about *whose trade* it protected (West Indian planters vs. northern mainland merchants) and why it failed in practice.

One correction to your working assumptions: the *metropolitan* statute that did treat colonial enslaved people as seizable property was **not** the Molasses Act but the **Debt Recovery Act 1732 (5 Geo. II c. 7)**, which made colonial "negroes" liable to creditors as assets — the closest Parliament came in the decade to a chattel rule [5].

## 1. The Molasses Act 1733 — what it actually says

| Element | Detail | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Citation | 6 Geo. II c. 13 | [4] |
| Long title | "An act for the better securing and encouraging the trade of his Majesty's sugar colonies in America" | [4] |
| Royal assent | 17 May 1733 (collection to begin 25 Dec 1733; Act computed in force from 24 June 1733) | [4] |
| Duration | Declared a "Publick Act," in force five years from 24 June 1733 | [4] |
| Duty — foreign rum/spirits | **9d** (money of Great Britain) per gallon | [4] |
| Duty — foreign molasses/syrups | **6d** per gallon | [4] |
| Duty — foreign sugar/paneles | **5s** per hundredweight avoirdupois | [4] |
| Slavery/chattel clause | **None** | [4] |

The preamble states the purpose plainly: the "Welfare and Prosperity of Your Majesty's Sugar Colonies in America are of the greatest Consequence and Importance to the Trade, Navigation, and Strength of this Kingdom," and the planters "have of late Years fallen under such great Discouragements, that they are unable to … carry on the Sugar Trade upon an equal Footing with the Foreign Sugar Colonies" [4]. The duties fall only on produce of colonies "not in the Possession or under the Dominion of His Majesty" — i.e., the French, Dutch, and Spanish islands — imported into British American colonies. The operative text concerns rum, molasses, sugar, and paneles, customs officers, forfeitures, drawbacks on re-exported refined sugar, and the Irish trade; nowhere does it touch the status, control, or punishment of enslaved people [4].

**For citation/verisimilitude:** the standard printed edition is **Danby Pickering, *The Statutes at Large*** (Cambridge, 1762–), where 6 Geo. II c. 13 and 5 Geo. II c. 7 both appear (vol. 16 for the 1732 Act) [5][4]. Period spelling capitalizes nouns ("Molasses or Syrups of such Foreign Produce," "Sugars and Paneles," "Hundred Weight, Avoirdupois") [4].

## 2. The metropolitan correction: the Debt Recovery Act 1732 (5 Geo. II c. 7)

The decade's one Parliamentary statute that classified colonial enslaved people as property was the **Debt Recovery Act 1732**, long title "An act for the more easy recovery of debts in his Majesty's plantations and colonies in America," effective **29 September 1732** [5]. Section IV provides:

> "the houses, lands, **negroes**, and other hereditaments and **real estates**, situate or being within any of the said plantations belonging to any person indebted, shall be **liable to and chargeable with all just debts** … and shall and may be **assets for the satisfaction thereof**, in like manner as real estates are by the law of England liable to the satisfaction of debts due by bond … for **seizing, extending, selling or disposing** of any such houses, lands, **negroes** … towards the satisfaction of such debts … in like manner as personal estates … are seized, extended, sold or disposed of" [5].

This is the precise answer to your CONFIRM/CORRECT item: **yes**, a 1730s metropolitan statute — 5 Geo. II c. 7, not the Molasses Act — treated colonial "negroes" as seizable property/assets for creditors, assimilating them to both real estate and personal estate for debt enforcement. (Notably, even here Parliament did not write a free-standing definition of enslaved status; it folded "negroes" into an existing commercial remedy.) The clauses relating to slaves were repealed only in 1797, by 37 Geo. III c. 119 [5]. The printed source is **Pickering, *Statutes at Large*, vol. 16** [5].

## 3. Colonial plantation codes: the explicit chattel/property classifications

### Barbados — *An Act for the Better Ordering and Governing of Negroes* (1661)

This was the **first comprehensive separate English colonial slave code** and the template virtually every other English colony copied [1][3]. The 1661 text does not survive; the earliest surviving copy is the **1667 version (23 clauses)**, which reflects 1661–67 amendments [1]. The two extant manuscript copies are held at **The National Archives (TNA), Kew, and the Huntington Library** [1].

The property logic and the dehumanizing rationale are in the preamble, which calls the enslaved "an **heathenish, brutish, and an uncertaine dangerous kind of people**," notes that the laws of England give "**noe tract to guide us where to walke, nor any rule sett us, how to governe such Slaves**," and resolves to "**protect them as we doe many other goods and Chattels**, and alsoe somewhat further as being created Men" [1]. The police machinery follows immediately: Clause 1 forbids enslaved people leaving the plantation on Sabbath or holy days without a written **ticket** from the master, on penalty of **500 lbs of Muscovado sugar**; an enslaved person found without a ticket is to be punished by "**moderate whipping**," with penalties recoverable before a **Justice of the Peace** [1]. The Act also empowers searches for runaways and **compensation to owners for lost property** [1]. A separate Barbados act, *An Act declaring the Negro Slaves of this Island to be Real Estate* (No. 94 in Rawlin's *The Laws of Barbados*), supplied the **real-estate-for-descent** classification alongside the chattel framing.

### Jamaica — *Act for the Better Order and Government of Slaves* (consolidated 1696, in force through the 1730s)

Jamaica's consolidated slave act treated the enslaved as a **legal hybrid — real estate for inheritance, but chattels for debts and legacies**. The governing clause (Cap. 21 of the consolidated act, as digested in the *Abstract of the Laws of Jamaica Relating to Slaves*) provides:

> "That **no slave shall be free by becoming a Christian**; and **for payment of debts and legacies all slaves shall be deemed and taken as all other goods and chattels are** in the hands of executors or administrators; and where other goods and chattels are not sufficient to satisfy the said debts and legacies, then so many slaves as are necessary … sold, and the remaining slaves, after the payment of the debts and legacies, shall be **judged, deemed, and taken**" [2].

The surrounding provisions confirm the property mechanics: an "interest for life may be taken for debt," children "stand in the parents' situation," and slaves pass by will and to a widow's dower [2]. The enforcement and punishment apparatus is elaborate. Slaves were tried in **slave courts** of three justices (capital matters drawing **juries of twelve** from the quarter-sessions panel), and could be sentenced to **death, transportation, or hard labour for life** [2]. Critically, the same court that condemned a slave also fixed the owner's compensation: it "shall also enquire of the jury, upon their oaths, what sum or sums of money the owner, proprietor, or possessor of the said slave or slaves ought to receive … so that such sum … do not exceed the sum of **one hundred pounds** for each slave so sentenced … and, if the conviction be for running away, the value … shall not exceed **fifty pounds**" [2]. Where a slave was "sentenced to death or confinement to hard labour for life, and be valued," that valuation was "paid for by the **receiver-general** of this island" out of public funds [2]; in militia/maroon-pursuit service, a slave killed was paid for at full valuation and one "disabled by loss of limb" at the difference in value, capped at **two hundred pounds** [2]. The printed primary sources are the **Acts of Assembly of Jamaica** and the *Abstract of the Laws of Jamaica Relating to Slaves* [2].

### South Carolina — the 1730s precursors to the Negro Act of 1740

South Carolina's slave law descends directly from Barbados: the **1690 "Act for the Better Ordering of Slaves" borrowed heavily from the Barbadian code, codified chattel slavery, and authorized corporal punishment by branding, nose-slitting, and emasculation** before being disallowed by the Lords Proprietors [3]. A replacement followed in 1696 "again based on Barbadian principles," then a series of acts in **1712, 1722, and 1735** [3]. In the **Cooper & McCord *Statutes at Large of South Carolina*** these appear as No. 57 (1690, p. 343), No. 314 (1712, p. 352), No. 476 (1722, p. 371), and No. 586 (1735) [3]. The comprehensive consolidated **Negro Act of 1740 — which most fully declared enslaved people "chattels personal" — post-dates your window**; it was a reaction to the **1739 Stono Rebellion**, and the bill that became it had its second reading on 27 January 1738 [3]. So for a story set strictly in 1730–1739, the operative South Carolina statute is the **1735 act** in the line running 1712→1722→1735, with the famous 1740 "chattels personal" language just over the horizon [3].

## 4. Analysis: metropolitan commercial law vs. colonial chattel-and-police codes

The contrast is structural, not incidental:

- **Westminster wrote commerce.** The Molasses Act (6 Geo. II c. 13) is a customs-and-navigation measure; even the Debt Recovery Act (5 Geo. II c. 7) reaches enslaved people only as *assets in a creditor's remedy*, never as a defined status [5][4]. Parliament built no metropolitan slave code, no statutory definition of the enslaved person, and no criminal-control regime for slavery — it relied on the broader framework of trade regulation (the Navigation Acts) and chartered-company structure for the African trade.
- **The colonies wrote the person.** The explicit reduction of human beings to **"goods and Chattels"** (Barbados, Jamaica) and **"real estate"** for descent (Jamaica, Barbados), plus the entire apparatus of passes, whipping, branding, maiming, mutilation, execution, special non-jury or jury-of-the-master-class courts, and owner compensation, lives in the **colonial assemblies' statutes** [1][2][3].

**Mechanism of imperial delegation.** Barbados's own preamble names the gap that drove this: English law offered "noe tract to guide us … how to governe such Slaves," so the colonists invoked their "legislative power … of provisionary Lawes … not being contradictory to the Lawes of England" [1]. Because the metropole declined to define or police slavery, that work **devolved onto local legislatures**, which converted the institution's economic logic into statutory dehumanization. Westminster's role was largely supervisory (the Crown's disallowance of South Carolina's 1690 act shows the metropole reviewing but not authoring such codes) [3]. The result: the harshest, most explicitly dehumanizing law in the empire was *colonial* law, while metropolitan law touched the enslaved chiefly through the commercial side door of debt and customs [5][1][2].

## 5. Analysis: Jamaica vs. Barbados — comparing the control and property regimes

| Dimension | Barbados (1661/1667 code) | Jamaica (1696 consolidated act) |
|---|---|---|
| Property classification | "goods and Chattels"; separate act declaring slaves **real estate** for descent | **Hybrid**: real estate for descent/inheritance, **chattels** for debts and legacies [2] |
| Inheritance | Real-estate descent under the dedicated "Real Estate" act | Slaves descend to heirs as realty; pass by will and widow's dower [2] |
| Debt enforcement | Slaves treated as goods/chattels seizable for default | Provost-marshal seizure and sale; "interest for life may be taken for debt" [2] |
| Movement control | Ticket/pass required off-plantation; penalty 500 lbs sugar | Comparable pass-and-patrol regime |
| Punishment of slaves | "moderate whipping"; broader code authorized severe corporal penalties; recovery before JP | **Death, transportation, hard labour**; slave courts of 3 justices + juries of 12 in capital cases [1][2] |
| Owner compensation | Compensation to owners for lost/harbored slaves [1] | Public **indemnity** paid by the receiver-general: up to **£100** per executed/transported slave, **£50** for a runaway, up to **£200** for one killed/disabled in armed service [2] |
| Religion | — | "no slave shall be free by becoming a Christian" [2] |

**Divergence to use in fiction:** Barbados originated the model and leaned on a *separate* "Real Estate" act layered over a chattel base; Jamaica's 1696 act is the more elaborate single instrument, with a fully articulated **hybrid** status — realty for keeping estates intact across generations, chattel for letting creditors and legatees reach the enslaved — and a more developed court-and-compensation system [1][2]. Both treat compensation to owners as integral, signaling that the law's first concern is the *property interest*, not the life destroyed.

## 6. Analysis: the Molasses Act — trade regulation or slave control?

The evidence is one-sided: the Molasses Act was **protectionist sugar-and-trade policy**, not a slave-control statute. The statutory text carries duties on foreign sugar/rum/molasses and nothing on slavery [4]. The scholarly debate is entirely internal to *trade* — about which British interest the Act served and why it failed:

- **Richard B. Sheridan**, "The Molasses Act and the Market Strategy of the British Sugar Planters" (*Journal of Economic History* 17, no. 1 (1957): 62–83), frames the controversy as (1) Anglo-French commercial rivalry, (2) a conflict between two British colonial regions — the **West Indian sugar islands vs. the northern mainland colonies**, (3) a measure aiding one set of British colonies at another's expense, and (4) a precedent for later parliamentary taxation disputes. Sheridan stresses the **West India planter lobby's market strategy** as the driver.
- **John C. Miller** argued the duty "threatened New England with ruin, struck a blow at the economic foundations of the Middle colonies, and … opened the way for the British West Indians … to wax rich at the expense of their fellow subjects on the mainland" — emphasizing the **regional redistribution** toward the islands.
- The Act passed **at the insistence of the British West Indies sugar planters**, to make British sugar products cheaper than the French islands' by taxing the latter; it was **largely opposed by mainland colonists, rarely paid, and undermined by widespread smuggling** — an enforcement failure rather than a working revenue measure.

**Where does the "slave control" reading come from?** It is an *inference*, not a statutory fact: because the West India planter interest the Act protected was a slaveholding interest, and because cheaper protected sugar underwrote the plantation-slavery economy, a structural argument can be made that the Act *served* a slave economy. But that is an argument about the Act's economic beneficiaries, not about its legal content — the Molasses Act regulated **commodities, not people**, and no clause governs the status, discipline, or punishment of the enslaved [4]. For your novel, the legally accurate statement is: the Molasses Act protected the planters who owned slaves; it did not legislate about slaves.

## 7. Recent scholarship (peripheral note)

The foundational scholarly anchor for the Molasses Act debate remains Sheridan (1957) and Miller (1943) cited above. On the colonial codes, the **Slavery, Law & Power project (slaverylawpower.org)** provides side-by-side transcriptions of the Barbados code from the TNA and Huntington manuscripts [1], and the **South Carolina Encyclopedia** entry on slave codes traces the 1690→1696→1712→1722→1735→1740 lineage [3]. Recent peer-reviewed scholarship anchors the legal-classification point directly:

- **Lee B. Wilson**, "Chattel," Chapter 1 of *Bonds of Empire: The English Origins of Slave Law in South Carolina and British Plantation America, 1660–1783* (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, online 2 July 2021; DOI 10.1017/9781108861762.002) [6]. Wilson shows that English property law "bifurcated property into real estate or chattels personal," that **most plantation colonies settled upon some mixture of chattel and real property**, and that **South Carolina colonists ultimately adopted pure chattel slavery** in order to facilitate commercial transactions and expand their credit with British merchants — analogizing enslaved people to livestock and moveable objects "not because they believed them to be the same as those objects, but because they believed them to be the same at law" [6].
- **Holly Brewer**, "Creating a Common Law of Slavery for England and its New World Empire," *Law and History Review* 39, no. 4 (2021): 765–834 (DOI 10.1017/S0738248021000407) [7]. Brewer addresses the **metropolitan/colonial slave-law divide**, challenging the consensus that English common law was merely "somewhat confused" while coerced servitude was legal in England before 1772 and "certainly in its empire" [7].
