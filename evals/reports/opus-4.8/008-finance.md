# Columbus McKinnon: Capital Allocation and Financial Risk Profile

This report evaluates Columbus McKinnon Corporation's (Nasdaq: CMCO) capital allocation and financial risk profile through four interrelated lenses — pension de-risking, deleveraging during active M&A, employee-retention exposure embedded in equity compensation, and the durability of its acquisition-driven intangible base. Figures are drawn from the company's fiscal 2025 Form 10-K (year ended March 31, 2025) [1] and its fiscal 2023 Annual Report (year ended March 31, 2023) [2]. Dollar amounts are in thousands unless noted.

## 1. Pension funded status: March 2024 vs. March 2025

The company measures its defined-benefit plans at March 31. Funded status is the fair value of plan assets minus the projected benefit obligation (PBO) [1]:

| Item | March 31, 2025 | March 31, 2024 |
|---|---|---|
| Projected benefit obligation (PBO) | $158,629 | $268,025 |
| Fair value of plan assets | $94,240 | $199,592 |
| **Funded status (assets − PBO)** | **$(64,389)** | **$(68,433)** |

**Net change:** the deficit narrowed by **$4,044** (from −$68,433 to −$64,389), a modest improvement in funded status [1].

The headline, however, is the scale of the *gross* shrinkage on both sides of the balance sheet. The PBO fell $109,396 (from $268,025 to $158,629) and plan assets fell $105,352 (from $199,592 to $94,240). Both moves were driven overwhelmingly by a **$95,080 settlement** during fiscal 2025 (versus $20,510 in fiscal 2024), plus $18,733 of benefits paid and a $6,974 transfer of assets out related to a plan termination [1]. In other words, the small improvement in net funded status masks a deliberate, large-scale removal of pension liabilities from the balance sheet.

## 2. Pension settlement activity and de-risking strategy

Columbus McKinnon has executed a multi-year pension wind-down [1]:

- **Fiscal 2024:** terminated both of its Canadian pension plans; made lump-sum payments to electing participants in Q3 and Q4; recorded a settlement charge of **$4,984** in Other (income) expense, net.
- **Fiscal 2025:** terminated one of its U.S. pension plans; on **September 30, 2024 purchased annuity contracts** to settle the remaining liabilities of the terminated U.S. plan, generating a **non-cash settlement charge of $23,634** in Other (income) expense, net.
- At termination, the U.S. plan held an asset surplus of $6,974, which is being used to fund obligations under the company's U.S. defined-contribution plans; the remaining surplus was $6,342 at March 31, 2025 [1].

This pattern — lump-sum offers followed by annuity buyouts and plan terminations — is a textbook pension de-risking program that transfers longevity, interest-rate, and asset-return risk to insurers. The fiscal 2023 disclosures show the strategy was already underway in spirit: the company stated its investment policy was a "glide-path method to de-risk the portfolio by increasing liability-hedging investments as the pension liability funded status increases" [2]. The fiscal 2025 annuity buyout is the culmination of that glide path. The cost is a large non-cash earnings charge ($23,634), which materially affected reported results, but the benefit is the near-elimination of defined-benefit volatility and a clear shift toward defined-contribution arrangements. Notably, the $23,634 settlement charge is a primary driver of the gap between fiscal 2025's negative GAAP net income and its underlying operating performance.

## 3. Net debt reduction, March 2022 to March 2023

From the fiscal 2023 five-year balance-sheet data and debt footnote [2]:

| Item | March 31, 2023 | March 31, 2022 | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Total debt | $471,592 | $511,226 | −$39,634 |
| Cash and cash equivalents | $133,176 | $115,390 | +$17,786 |
| **Total debt, net of cash** | **$338,416** | **$395,836** | **−$57,420** |
| Total debt / capitalization | 36.1% | 39.8% | −3.7 pts |
| Net debt / net total capitalization | 28.9% | 33.9% | −5.0 pts |

Net debt fell by **$57,420** over the year — a deleveraging that came from both sides: roughly $39,634 of gross debt repayment (the term loan dropped from $502,560 to $462,560) plus a $17,786 build in cash [2]. Operating cash flow rose to $83,636 (from $48,881), and the company directed $49,987 to net financing outflows (including debt repayment and $8,008 of dividends) [2]. The leverage ratio improvement (net debt/net capitalization from 33.9% to 28.9%) confirms an explicit deleveraging priority [2].

## 4. Deleveraging vs. M&A during fiscal 2022–2023

The fiscal 2022–2023 window shows a sequenced strategy: borrow to acquire, then rapidly pay down. The large debt build occurred in fiscal 2022, when the company completed two acquisitions in precision conveyance — **Dorner** and **Garvey Corporation** [2]. To fund Dorner, it incurred $450,000 of Term Loan B debt (following a $207,000 equity offering in May 2021), and for Garvey it drew another $75,000 of Term Loan B via an accordion feature [2]. As a result, total debt jumped from $248,954 (FY2021) to $511,226 (FY2022), and financing activities provided $420,700 of cash that year [2]. Once the acquisitions were funded, fiscal 2023 became a deleveraging year: $39,634 of net debt repayment (term loan from $502,560 to $462,560) and a return to net financing *outflows* of $49,987 [2]. The pattern — heavy debt-financed M&A in fiscal 2022, then disciplined paydown in fiscal 2023 — indicates that deleveraging is prioritized in the year after a major transaction; indeed, by April 2023 the company had announced its next deal, the montratec GmbH automation acquisition (expected to close May 31, 2023, in fiscal 2024), having first repaired the balance sheet [2].

## 5. Stock options outstanding and underwater concentration (March 31, 2025)

The fiscal 2025 option roll-forward [1]:

| Date | Options outstanding | Wtd-avg exercise price | Wtd-avg remaining life | Aggregate intrinsic value |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| March 31, 2023 | 975,171 | $34.54 | 7.13 yrs | $5,497 |
| March 31, 2024 | 1,193,840 | $35.37 | 6.93 yrs | $12,392 |
| March 31, 2025 | 1,253,626 | $36.38 | 5.99 yrs | $80 |
| *Exercisable 3/31/25* | 802,964 | $37.51 | 4.91 yrs | $80 |

The closing stock price on March 31, 2025 was **$16.70** [1]. The company disclosed that only **51,957 options were in-the-money** at that date [1]. Against 1,253,626 outstanding, that implies:

- **In-the-money: 51,957 options ≈ 4.1%**
- **Underwater (out-of-the-money): ≈ 1,201,669 options ≈ 95.9%**

Aggregate intrinsic value collapsed from $12,392 (FY2024) to just **$80** (FY2025) [1]. With a weighted-average exercise price of $36.38 — and recent grants struck at $36.19 (FY2024) and $45.34 (FY2025) — exercise prices sit more than double the $16.70 market price, leaving virtually the entire option pool worthless [1].

## 6. Stock performance and retention risk

The option data is itself a record of stock-price deterioration. The grant prices climbed ($34.91 in FY2023, $36.19 in FY2024, $45.34 in FY2025) even as the share price fell to $16.70 by March 31, 2025 [1]. Intrinsic value of options actually exercised shrank to $120 in FY2025 (from $870 in FY2024) [1]. The implications for retention:

- **Equity incentives have lost their pull.** With ~96% of options underwater, the option component of compensation offers almost no upside to employees at current prices, eroding its golden-handcuffs function [1].
- **The repricing/refresh dilemma.** Reissuing at $16.70 would dilute shareholders and reset the bar, while leaving options as-is leaves key employees holding paper with $80 of total intrinsic value [1].
- **Compounding stress events.** The retention challenge coincides with a year of negative GAAP net income (driven in part by the $23,634 pension settlement charge and elevated acquisition/integration costs), a falling share price, and the strategic disruption of a major pending transaction — conditions under which the loss of equity-incentive value is most likely to drive attrition [1].

In short, the 95.9% underwater concentration is a quantified retention risk: the principal long-term retention tool is, at fiscal-year-end, economically inert.

Total amortization expense was **$29,946** in fiscal 2025 (versus $29,396 in FY2024 and $26,001 in FY2023; the FY2024 step-up reflected the montratec acquisition) [1].

**Weighted-average amortization rate:**
- Against total gross carrying ($541,284): 29,946 ÷ 541,284 = **5.53%**, an implied average life of **~18.1 years**.
- Against only finite-lived gross ($494,990, excluding the $46,294 indefinite-lived trademark): 29,946 ÷ 494,990 = **6.05%**, implied average life of **~16.5 years** — consistent with the disclosed 16-year blended period [1].

Customer relationships ($355,845 gross, 17-year life) dominate the base, followed by acquired technology ($112,507 gross, 15-year life) [1].

## 8. What the intangibles reveal about acquisition durability and strategic value

The low ~5.5–6.0% weighted-average amortization rate (16-year blended life) signals that management assigns *long economic lives* to the assets it acquires — most importantly customer relationships (17 years) and acquired technology (15 years) [1]. Several conclusions follow:

- **Acquisition strategy is relationship- and technology-led.** Customer relationships ($226,379 net) and acquired technology ($69,927 net) together make up the large majority of the $356,562 net intangible base, indicating M&A aimed at sticky installed-base/customer access and differentiated product technology rather than commodity capacity [1].
- **Durability is signaled by long lives but front-loaded by goodwill.** Goodwill stood at $710,807 at March 31, 2025 (net of $113,174 of cumulative impairment) [1] — larger than the entire identifiable intangible base — meaning much of the acquisition value sits in goodwill that is tested for impairment rather than amortized. The $113,174 of accumulated goodwill impairment is a reminder that not all of the acquired value has proven durable.
- **A slow, steady earnings drag.** At ~$29.9 million per year and a 16-year life, amortization is a predictable, non-cash charge that depresses GAAP earnings for well over a decade — which is why the company highlights amortization (and the pension settlement) in its non-GAAP adjusted figures [1].
- **The 17-year customer-relationship life is the key durability bet.** Assigning the longest life to customer relationships implies management expects acquired customer bases to generate cash flows for nearly two decades; this is the central assumption underwriting the strategic value of recent deals (montratec and earlier transactions), and it is the assumption most exposed to revision if customer retention weakens [1].

## Synthesis: strategic priorities and risk profile

Across these four threads, a coherent capital-allocation philosophy emerges. Columbus McKinnon (i) pursues **debt-funded, technology-and-customer-relationship-led acquisitions**, then **rapidly deleverages** in the following year (net debt down $57,420 in FY2023, leverage from 33.9% to 28.9%) [2]; (ii) has **systematically de-risked its pension** via lump sums and a 2024 annuity buyout, accepting a $23,634 non-cash charge to extinguish defined-benefit volatility and shift to defined-contribution plans, leaving only a $64,389 residual deficit [1]; and (iii) carries a long-dated intangible base (16-year blended life, ~5.5% amortization rate) that bets on the durability of acquired customer relationships and technology [1].

The principal *unmanaged* risk by contrast is **employee retention**: with the share price at $16.70 and ~95.9% of 1.25 million options underwater (aggregate intrinsic value of $80), the company's main long-term equity-retention mechanism is effectively impaired precisely as it absorbs a loss-making year and pursues further strategic change [1]. The balance sheet and pension risks are being actively and deliberately managed; the human-capital risk embedded in worthless equity awards is the one this evidence shows as least addressed.
