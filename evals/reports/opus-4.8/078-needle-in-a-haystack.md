## Who designed Longwood Gardens' 2008 treehouses, and what contemporaneous source documents them?

**Bottom line:** Longwood Gardens' 2008 installation **"Nature's Castles: The Treehouse Reimagined"** is real and well-documented. It featured **three treehouses designed by two specialist treehouse firms, not a single architect**: the **Canopy Cathedral** and the **Birdhouse** were created by **Peter Nelson (with Jake Jacob) of TreeHouse Workshop, Inc. of Seattle, Washington**, and the **Lookout Loft** was created by **Forever Young Treehouses of Burlington, Vermont** [1][2][3]. The best contemporaneous (2008) sources documenting the design concept and construction are two newspaper articles dated April 24, 2008 — Jane Holahan's piece in *Lancaster Online* [2] and the *Daily Local News* report [1] — supplemented by the *Delco Times* [4], *The Reporter* [5], and a May 16, 2008 *Philadelphia Inquirer* feature [6]. The installation opened the weekend of April 25, 2008 (Arbor Day weekend) and was billed to run through November 23, 2008, but the treehouses were built as permanent pin-founded structures and **remain standing at Longwood today** [2][3][7].

### The exhibit and its timeline

"Nature's Castles: The Treehouse Reimagined" debuted at Longwood Gardens, 1001 Longwood Road, Kennett Square, PA, in conjunction with Arbor Day weekend, opening to the public Friday, April 25, 2008 and continuing through November 23, 2008, open daily 9 a.m.–5 p.m. [2][4]. The project was "two years in the making" and cost about **$1 million** [5][6]. Three large-scale treehouses were sited in different parts of the gardens, with a cell-phone audio tour and "Meet the Treehouse Designers" sessions during opening weekend [5][4].

Although marketed as a seasonal exhibit, the treehouses were **built as permanent structures**. *Lancaster Online* reported in 2008 that "the three treehouses were built as permanent structures," held up by pin foundations rather than resting on the trees, and "far too wonderful to take down" [2]; the *Daily Local* quoted Director Paul Redman that the duration was undetermined but the houses "definitely would not be torn down in November" [1]. Longwood's own current (2026) website resolves the question: the treehouses "were meant to live through Labor Day of the year they were constructed, but they proved so popular they remain here today" — the Canopy Cathedral now sits near the Large Lake, and the Lookout Loft and Birdhouse stand in Forest Walk [3][7]. **They are permanent features, not a one-season exhibit.**

### The three treehouses and their designers

| Treehouse | Location / theme | Designer (per 2008 + Longwood) | Size & notable features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Canopy Cathedral** | Near the Italian Water Garden / Large Lake; inspired by a **Norwegian stave church** | **Peter Nelson & Jake Jacob, TreeHouse Workshop Inc., Seattle** [1][3] | Two-story, ~400 sq ft (Longwood) / 589 sq ft main floor + 118 sq ft balcony (*Lancaster Online*); 10+ ft up; intricate glass façade, hand-carved fantastical creatures; "prow"/large window by Scheel & Sharp of Norristown [5][4][2] |
| **Birdhouse** | Terrace Woodland near the Peirce-du Pont House; "squirrel's-eye view" | **TreeHouse Workshop (Peter Nelson)** [5][2] | Smallest at 255 sq ft but tallest at 20+ ft up; copper cable acting as a lightning rod; barn door with a handle made from a rhododendron branch [5][2] |
| **Lookout Loft** | Forest Walk, overlooking the 35-acre Meadow; rustic **Adirondack** style | **Forever Young Treehouses, Burlington, VT** [4][2] | ~720–900 sq ft, two viewing platforms; 75+ ft of limb-shaped ramps to platforms up to 14 ft up; fully ADA/wheelchair accessible; handrail inspired by Longwood's Gothic Folly [6][4][2] |

The three were **not the work of one designer or of Longwood's internal staff**: contemporaneous coverage and Longwood agree that **two outside specialist firms** produced them — TreeHouse Workshop (the Canopy Cathedral and the Birdhouse) and the nonprofit Forever Young Treehouses (the Lookout Loft, whose mission is fully accessible treehouses "in every state") [1][2][4].

### Design concept, construction, and materials

The articulated concept was environmental sensitivity and integration with the living landscape. Sharon Loving, head of Longwood's horticulture department, stressed at the press preview that the houses were designed and built with sensitivity to the environment: **recycled/reclaimed lumber** was used, **no root systems were disturbed**, and the houses **do not rely on the trees for structural support** [4]. Instead, all three are nestled around groups of trees and held up by **pin foundations** so the trees are unharmed; standing trees soar up through the structures [2]. The Canopy Cathedral alone uses **37 steel columns, 34 steel beams, and 8 steel braces** [2]. Construction of the Canopy Cathedral began in **February 2007**, when Peter Nelson came to Longwood and scouted the site during a snowstorm [2].

Reclaimed and salvaged materials dominated the build [5][4][2]:
- Floorboards from a dismantled toothpaste factory in Toronto; 2×8 rafters from a dismantled Vancouver, BC warehouse
- Douglas-fir timbers milled from salvaged logs recovered from a 2002 Oregon wildfire; storm/landslide-damaged Douglas firs from Washington (also used for tight-grained Douglas-fir windows)
- Lumber from a dairy barn in Chimacum, WA, and wood recovered from an old dairy barn and an old chocolate factory (Birdhouse)
- Locally harvested Vermont cedar, "ironwood," and eastern hemlock; sustainably harvested Brazilian hardwood (ipe); the Lookout Loft ramps used Vermont hop-hornbeam and Brazilian ipe shaped like tree limbs with wrought iron and metal accents

Accessibility was central to the Lookout Loft, designed to be enjoyed by people with disabilities — Forever Young Treehouses "is known for creating and promoting construction of treehouses that can be enjoyed by individuals with disabilities and special needs," and a large ramp "makes it accessible to all" [4].

### Reconciling the attribution: TreeHouse Workshop / Forever Young vs. Millan Architects

The firm attribution holds up across both contemporaneous and retrospective sources. The **2008 newspapers** — the most credible and contemporaneous documentation here, written and bylined at the time of the press preview (*Daily Local* and *Lancaster Online*, both April 24, 2008; *Delco Times* and *The Reporter*, April 24, 2008) — credit **TreeHouse Workshop / Peter Nelson** and **Forever Young Treehouses** [1][2][4][5]. Longwood Gardens' **own official website (accessed 2026)** independently confirms the Canopy Cathedral was "Designed by Peter Nelson of TreeHouse Workshop, Inc. in Seattle" [3]. Because a primary, official source agrees with the period reporting, the design credit is firmly established.

A Pennsylvania firm, **Matthew Millan Architects** (Philadelphia), also lists all three treehouses in its institutional portfolio, and its own page text (retrieved via a 2023 web-archive capture) does not claim sole authorship: it states the firm "worked with Longwood Gardens and the two premier treehouse companies in the U.S. to create these unique arboreal structures" and "designed each treehouse to respond to their individual settings" [8]. Read against the 2008 reporting and Longwood's official pages, this reconciles cleanly: the named treehouse builders — **TreeHouse Workshop** (Canopy Cathedral, Birdhouse) and **Forever Young Treehouses** (Lookout Loft) — were the originating treehouse designers, while Millan Architects was the collaborating local architecture firm (design collaborator / architect of record), and the project was not the work of Longwood's internal staff [1][2][3][8].

On the specific cross-check requested: the **Canopy Cathedral** is a single named, built treehouse — not a generic category — and it is attributed to a notable specialist, **Peter Nelson of TreeHouse Workshop** (Nelson later became widely known as the host of *Treehouse Masters*), with the prominent prow window fabricated by **Scheel & Sharp of Norristown** [3][5].
