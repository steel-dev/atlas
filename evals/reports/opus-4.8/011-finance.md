# Open NCD Public Issues in India as of December 8, 2025: Credit, Yield and Suitability Analysis for a Fixed-Income Retiree Portfolio

**Prepared for institutional/IC review. Data current as of December 8, 2025.**

## 1. Executive summary and investment thesis

As of December 8, 2025, two public NCD (Non-Convertible Debenture) issues were open for subscription in India: **Edelweiss Financial Services Ltd. (EFSL)** and **Muthoot Mercantile Ltd.** [1][2][3]. A third issue, **Prachay Capital Ltd.**, opens only on December 11, 2025 and was therefore *not* open on December 8 [search]. Reports of a Muthoot Fincorp tranche show an open date of December 23, 2025 — also outside the December 8 window [search].

The two open issues sit at sharply different points on the credit/risk spectrum:

| Issuer | Rating | Agency | Max effective yield | Tenor range | Post-issue D/E |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Edelweiss Financial Services | A+ / Stable | CRISIL | ~10.10% p.a. (120m) | 24–120 months | (large diversified group; see §6) |
| Muthoot Mercantile | BBB / Stable | India Ratings | ~11.73% p.a. | 400 days–75 months | **5.19x** |

**Thesis for a retiree/senior-citizen fixed-income mandate:** Neither issue is a high-grade (AAA/AA) instrument, which is the conventional suitability threshold for a capital-preservation-focused retiree. EFSL (CRISIL A+) is the more defensible of the two on credit quality but is only "adequate safety" and carries group-level complexity; Muthoot Mercantile (India Ratings BBB, the lowest investment grade, with post-issue debt/equity of 5.19x and thin Tier-II capital) is **not appropriate** as a core holding for a risk-averse retiree. In a falling-rate environment (RBI repo cut to 5.25% on December 5, 2025), the longer-tenor EFSL series let an investor lock a ~10% coupon, but the incremental ~165 bps offered by Muthoot Mercantile is inadequate compensation for the multi-notch drop in credit quality. The DHFL, IL&FS and Reliance Capital precedents (§8) make the case for caution concrete: retail NCD holders in those NBFCs faced frozen capital and large haircuts.

## 3. Credit-rating comparison

The two issues differ by roughly four notches on the standard scale:

- **EFSL: CRISIL A+ / Stable** — investment grade, "adequate safety / low credit risk" [1].
- **Muthoot Mercantile: India Ratings BBB / Stable** — the lowest rung of investment grade, "moderate safety / moderate credit risk" [2].

For an IC reviewing retiree suitability, neither reaches the AAA/AA tier typically required for "capital-preservation" fixed income. BBB sits one downgrade away from sub-investment-grade ("speculative") territory, which materially raises the conditional default and liquidity risk.

## 4. Post-tax yield: generic investor vs. senior citizen

**Tax treatment (FY2025-26).** NCD *interest* (coupon) is taxed as "Income from Other Sources" at the investor's applicable slab rate — there is no concessional rate [search]. Critically, **Section 80TTB (the up-to-₹50,000 senior-citizen interest deduction) applies only to interest from bank/co-operative/post-office savings and fixed/recurring deposits — it does NOT cover NCD/debenture interest** [search]. Consequently, a senior citizen receives **no special exemption on NCD coupon income**; the only differences versus a generic investor are (a) the slab the income falls into and (b) the higher basic exemption threshold for seniors. Listed NCDs held >12 months attract LTCG at 12.5% on capital gains (post-Budget 2024), but the coupon stream is always slab-taxed [search].

**Illustrative post-tax yields** (coupon × (1 − marginal rate); rates incl. cess approximated):

| Series (illustrative) | Pre-tax yield | Generic investor (30% slab, ~31.2% w/ cess) | Senior in 20% slab (~20.8%) | Senior in 5% slab (~5.2%) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| EFSL 120m | 10.10% | ~6.95% | ~8.00% | ~9.57% |
| Muthoot 75m | 11.73% | ~8.07% | ~9.29% | ~11.12% |

The senior-citizen advantage is **purely a function of the lower slab** a modest-income retiree may sit in — not any NCD-specific relief. A high-net-worth senior in the 30% bracket is taxed identically to a generic investor. (Figures are indicative; exact post-tax yield depends on the client's total taxable income.)

## 5. Relative value and competitive positioning

- **Yield per unit of rating risk:** Muthoot Mercantile offers roughly +165 bps of effective yield at its top tenor (11.73% vs 10.10%) but at four notches lower rating (BBB vs A+) and from a far smaller, single-product (gold-loan) NBFC. On a risk-adjusted basis the spread is thin for the credit gap.
- **Tenor:** EFSL provides a 120-month option to lock a ~10% coupon — attractive given the rate outlook (§7). Muthoot caps at 75 months.
- **Sector:** Both are NBFC/financial-sector issuers, so neither diversifies a retiree away from NBFC credit risk.
- **Scale/liquidity:** EFSL's ₹250 Cr issue from a 27-subsidiary group with FY25 assets of ₹41,622.50 Cr [1] is materially larger and more liquid than Muthoot Mercantile's ₹150 Cr issue.

## 6. Issuer financials and leverage (high-D/E flag)

**Muthoot Mercantile — HIGH-LEVERAGE FLAG.** Standalone, as on September 30, 2025 [3]:
- Total debt = **₹94,336.43 lakh** (debt securities ₹44,012.76L + other borrowings ₹31,320.23L + subordinated liabilities ₹19,003.44L)
- Net worth = ₹21,059.09 lakh
- **Debt/Equity = 4.48x pre-issue, 5.19x post-issue** [3]
- Tier-II Capital Adequacy Ratio only **8.95%** [3]
- PAT: FY25 ₹2,808.93L, FY24 ₹2,427.84L, FY23 ₹1,819.02L; total assets ₹1,21,127.05L [3]
- A 5.19x post-issue D/E is high in absolute terms (though leverage of 4–6x is not unusual for NBFCs); combined with the BBB rating and thin Tier-II buffer, this is the **highest-risk** of the open issues and warrants a flag for conservative mandates.

**EFSL.** Restated consolidated (₹ Cr) [1]: FY25 assets 41,622.50, total income 9,518.71, PAT 535.82 (FY24 528.05, FY23 405.56); reserves & surplus 58,260.31. EFSL is a diversified Mumbai-based financial group (credit, asset/wealth management, ARC, insurance) with 27 subsidiaries [1]. The chittorgarh page did not disclose an explicit consolidated D/E for the December issue; leverage assessment should be drawn from the prospectus.

## 7. Forward indicators and rate outlook

- The **RBI MPC cut the policy repo rate by 25 bps to 5.25% on December 5, 2025**, maintaining a neutral stance; SDF 5.00%, MSF/Bank Rate 5.50% [search]. (Note: the working assumption of "5.5%" in the brief is incorrect — the actual repo rate is 5.25%.)
- Backdrop: Q2 FY26 GDP growth of 8.2% and inflation at a multi-decade low [search].
- **Implication:** a falling-rate regime raises **reinvestment risk** for retirees rolling maturing deposits. This argues for locking longer-tenor coupons now — favouring EFSL's 120-month ~10% series — while being mindful that lower-grade issuers' refinancing costs and rollover risk are precisely what stress in a downcycle.

## 8. Risk assessment

- **Credit/default risk:** Highest for Muthoot Mercantile (BBB, 5.19x leverage, thin Tier-II). EFSL (A+) is "adequate" but not high grade.
- **Liquidity risk:** Both list on BSE, but secondary-market liquidity in retail NCDs is typically thin; exit before maturity may require a price concession. Muthoot's smaller float compounds this.
- **Interest-rate/reinvestment risk:** In a rate-cutting cycle, shorter tenors expose the retiree to reinvestment at lower future rates; longer tenors mitigate this but extend credit exposure.
- **Tail risk specific to NBFC issuers:** sector-wide funding shocks, asset-quality deterioration, or a single large default can trigger rapid downgrades and freeze refinancing — the IL&FS/DHFL pattern.

## 9. Historical precedent and pattern recognition

NBFC NCDs marketed to retail/retiree investors have a documented record of severe loss:
- **DHFL:** ~₹15,000 Cr of retail money was stuck in DHFL NCDs and FDs; ~₹6,188 Cr was owed to ~one lakh FD holders, with recovery uncertain through bankruptcy proceedings [search].
- **Reliance Capital:** defaulted on ₹534.64 Cr of interest on NCDs in which the EPFO had invested ₹2,500 Cr [search].
- **IL&FS** triggered the 2018 NBFC liquidity crisis that cascaded across the sector.

The pattern: attractive headline coupons from NBFCs can precede downgrade-and-default episodes, and retail/retiree holders rank behind secured creditors and endure long, lossy resolutions. This is the central caution for a capital-preservation retiree.

## 10. Regulatory landscape

Public NCD issues are governed by the **SEBI (Issue and Listing of Non-Convertible Securities) Regulations, 2021** and SEBI's **Operational Circular SEBI/HO/DDHS/P/CIR/2021/613 dated August 10, 2021** [4], with debenture trustees additionally regulated under the SEBI (Debenture Trustees) Regulations, 1993 and SEBI's master circular for debenture trustees [search]. The framework mandates a credit rating, an appointed debenture trustee, asset cover for secured NCDs, and listing on a recognised exchange — all present in both open issues (Beacon Trusteeship for EFSL; Mitcon Credentia for Muthoot Mercantile) [1][2]. The NCS Regulations were most recently amended as of January 21, 2026 [search].

## 11. Recommendation

For an income-seeking but capital-preservation-focused retiree:
1. **EFSL (CRISIL A+)** is the relatively preferred of the two if the client accepts single-A NBFC credit; the 120-month ~10% series suits the rate outlook. Size as a *satellite*, not core, allocation.
2. **Muthoot Mercantile (BBB, 5.19x D/E)** should be **flagged and generally avoided** for conservative retiree capital; its ~165 bps yield pickup does not compensate for the four-notch rating gap, thin capital, and small scale.
3. Neither reaches the AAA/AA grade that the DHFL/IL&FS/Reliance Capital history shows is prudent for retiree fixed income; consider AAA PSU/bank instruments or SCSS/government schemes for the core, using these NCDs only for a small, diversified yield-enhancement sleeve.

*Caveats: post-tax figures are illustrative and depend on the client's total income; EFSL consolidated D/E was not disclosed on the aggregator page and should be confirmed from the prospectus; some figures (TDS mechanics, leverage norms) are drawn from secondary tax/industry sources rather than primary filings.*
