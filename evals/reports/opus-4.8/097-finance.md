## Bottom line

CME Group's capital structure comfortably supports sustainable growth while preserving strategic flexibility. In Q1 2025 the business converted earnings to cash more efficiently than a year earlier — operating cash flow grew **+25.1% to $1,116.6M** on **+11.8% net income growth ($956.2M)**, lifting cash conversion from **1.04× to 1.17×** — while carrying only a modest, entirely fixed-rate, long-dated debt load of **~$3.45B par ($3,419.4M carrying)** with a **par-weighted average maturity of ~11.8 years** and effectively no near-term refinancing exposure. Against **$2.3B of untapped corporate revolver and $1.4B of cash**, leverage is low (debt is **0.92×** of revolver-plus-cash and **1.49×** of the unused revolver alone). The real constraint on flexibility is not the balance sheet but CME's large variable-dividend payout policy, which routes most free cash to shareholders. All figures from CME's Form 10-Q for the quarter ended March 31, 2025 [1].

## Cash generation efficiency: Q1 2024 → Q1 2025

| Metric (Q1, $M) | Q1 2024 | Q1 2025 | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Net income | 855.2 | 956.2 | +11.8% |
| Net cash from operating activities | 892.7 | 1,116.6 | +25.1% (+$223.9M) |
| OCF / net income (conversion) | 1.04× | 1.17× | +0.13× |
| Δ Accounts receivable | (93.4) | (196.8) | $103.4M larger use of cash |
| Δ Income taxes payable | +151.4 | +280.9 | $129.5M larger source of cash |

Operating cash flow outgrew net income by more than two-to-one, pushing the conversion rate from roughly breakeven-on-earnings to **1.17×** — cash generated exceeded reported profit by ~17% [1].

**What drove the improvement (working-capital mechanics).** The swing is almost entirely a working-capital story layered on higher trading volume (the filing attributes the OCF increase "largely … to an increase in trading volume") [1]:

- **Income taxes payable** was the dominant tailwind: a **+$280.9M** inflow in Q1 2025 versus **+$151.4M** in Q1 2024 — **$129.5M more cash** released through tax-accrual timing, consistent with higher pre-tax earnings.
- **Accounts receivable** was a partial offset: a **$(196.8)M** use of cash versus **$(93.4)M** — a **$103.4M larger drag** as higher-volume billings built receivables (a sign of business momentum, not deterioration).
- Net of these two items, the tax-payable inflow (+$129.5M) outweighed the incremental receivables build (–$103.4M), so working capital was a net contributor and conversion rose. Smaller non-cash and accrual items (stock-based comp $21.2M, intangible amortization $55.2M, D&A $27.3M) were broadly stable year over year [1].

## Liquidity and debt-to-liquidity ratio

- **Unused corporate revolver:** ~**$2.3 billion** of excess borrowing capacity for general corporate purposes **at March 31, 2025**, under the multi-currency revolving senior credit facility — equal to the full facility size, as no borrowings were outstanding (the 10-Q states "At March 31, 2025, we have excess borrowing capacity for general corporate purposes of approximately $2.3 billion") [1]. The facility matures April 2030 and can be upsized to $3.3B with lender consent [1].
- **Cash and equivalents:** **$1.4 billion** at March 31, 2025 (down from $2.9B at year-end 2024) [1].
- A separate **$7.0 billion 364-day secured facility** exists but is ring-fenced for clearing-house use, so it is excluded from corporate liquidity here [1].

**Debt-to-available-liquidity:**

| Basis | Calculation | Ratio |
|---|---|---|
| Debt ÷ unused corporate revolver | 3,419.4 / 2,300 | **1.49×** |
| Debt ÷ (revolver + cash) | 3,419.4 / 3,700 | **0.92×** |

On the broader measure, total debt is slightly less than available corporate liquidity — a conservative position for an investment-grade exchange operator (rated Aa3 / AA– / P1, "stable," per the filing's agency table) [1].

## Refinancing risk: maturity profile and weighted-average maturity

The maturity ladder is heavily back-loaded, with **nothing due in 2026 or 2027** and only **$500M (≈15% of par) due within ~3 years** (June 2028) [1]:

| Year | Years from 3/31/25 | Par due ($M) |
|---|---|---|
| 2026 | — | 0 |
| 2027 | — | 0 |
| 2028 | 3.2 | 500 |
| 2030 | 5.0 | 750 |
| 2032 | 7.0 | 750 |
| 2043 | 18.5 | 750 |
| 2048 | 23.2 | 700 |

**Par-weighted average maturity ≈ 11.8 years** from March 31, 2025, computed as the par-weighted mean of each note's year-fraction to maturity: (500×3.2 + 750×5.0 + 750×7.0 + 750×18.5 + 700×23.2) ÷ 3,450 = 40,715 ÷ 3,450 = **11.80 years**. The two longest tranches (2043 and 2048) contribute ~8.7 of those years, reflecting the back-loaded ladder. With all coupons fixed (and the 2043 notes swapped to a fixed 4.73%), there is no floating-rate exposure and refinancing is staggered across two decades — near-term refinancing risk is minimal, and the March-2025 maturity was already addressed via the new 2030 issuance [1].

CME returns cash primarily through dividends rather than buybacks. Although the board authorized a **$3 billion Class A share repurchase program on December 5, 2024**, the Q1 2025 financing-activities section of the cash flow statement contains **no share-repurchase line item** — confirming **no buyback cash outflow occurred in Q1 2025** (the only financing uses were debt repayment, dividends, and minor restricted-stock tax payments) [1]:

The **$2,567.4M dividend** reflects CME's regular plus large annual variable dividend concentrated in Q1; debt activity was a near-wash refinancing (+$742.3M issued / –$750.0M repaid); and capex is light (~$14M) for a technology-and-clearing platform [1].

## Verdict: does the structure support sustainable growth with flexibility?

**Yes.** Quarterly operating cash flow of $1.12B (growing 25% year over year at a 1.17× conversion rate) dwarfs a modest $3.45B fixed-rate debt load that carries no floating-rate risk, a ~11.8-year weighted-average maturity, no maturities in 2026–2027, and only $500M due within three years. With the full $2.3B corporate revolver undrawn, $1.4B of cash, debt at just 0.92× of revolver-plus-cash, and an investment-grade rating, CME retains ample balance-sheet headroom to fund acquisitions or organic investment opportunistically — as the clean March-2025 refinancing demonstrates [1].

The binding constraint on strategic flexibility is therefore not leverage or refinancing risk but **policy**: the large variable-dividend payout ($2.57B in Q1 2025) deliberately distributes the bulk of free cash to shareholders, meaning sizeable M&A would more likely be funded with incremental debt (well within capacity) or a temporary moderation of the variable dividend rather than from a large retained-cash war chest [1].
