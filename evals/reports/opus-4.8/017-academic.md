# Process Tracing versus Bayesian/Causal-Inference Approaches: Methodological Tensions in Comparative Causal Analysis

## 1. Two ways to define a "causal mechanism"

The deepest tension between the process-tracing tradition associated with Andrew Bennett and Jeffrey Checkel and the formal causal-inference programs of Judea Pearl and James Mahoney lies in what counts as a *mechanism* and where the inferential leverage comes from.

For Bennett and Checkel, process tracing is the disciplined examination of the **within-case observable implications of a hypothesized causal mechanism** — the diagnostic "clues" a mechanism would leave in the historical record of a single case. Its power is presented as fundamentally Bayesian rather than frequentist: what matters is the *probative value* of individual pieces of evidence, not their number, so the seemingly intuitive practice carries real inferential risk and requires explicit methodological safeguards [1]. Bennett and Checkel adopt Van Evera's four evidentiary tests, each defined by the **uniqueness × certainty** of the evidence a hypothesis predicts:

| Test | Certain? | Unique? | Logic | Effect of passing / failing |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Straw-in-the-wind | No | No | Neither necessary nor sufficient | Individually weak; a *series* can shift confidence [1] |
| Hoop | Yes | No | Necessary, not sufficient | Failing **disqualifies** a hypothesis; passing mainly *excludes alternatives* (e.g., "was the accused in the state on the day of the murder?") [1] |
| Smoking-gun | No | Yes | Sufficient, not necessary | Passing **strongly affirms**; failing does not exonerate [1] |
| Doubly-decisive | Yes | Yes | Necessary *and* sufficient | Confirms one hypothesis and eliminates rivals (e.g., a bank camera) [1] |

Mahoney sharpens this into an explicit ontology: the core of process tracing is the identification of **mechanisms understood as intervening *events*, not intervening *variables*** [2]. Events differ from variables because they are treated **set-theoretically** — as sets in which a case either has or lacks membership. On this account, Bayesian updating over set memberships explains why qualitative work does *not* suffer from a "small-N problem" or from standard causal-identification problems: leverage comes from the prior odds and the likelihood ratio of a clue within a single case, not from sample size [2].

Pearl's structural causal models locate the mechanism in a different place entirely. A mechanism is a **structural equation / intervening variable in a directed acyclic graph (DAG)**, and a causal claim is a statement about an intervention, formalized with the *do*-operator: `P(y|do(x))` is defined via the counterfactual `Yx` in the submodel `Mx` obtained by deleting all arrows pointing into X [3]. The *do*-calculus — three inference rules keyed to *d*-separation in the graph — is proven **complete** (Shpitser & Pearl 2006): if a query cannot be reduced to *do*-free observational probabilities, it is *not* identifiable from observational data without stronger assumptions [3].

The contrast is therefore foundational. Pearl's mechanism is a *type-level variable in a population graph*; Mahoney's and Bennett–Checkel's mechanism is a *token-level event inside one case*, certified by the diagnostic traces it leaves. Pearl asks whether an average interventional effect is identified; process tracing asks whether *this* case ran through *this* sequence of events.

## 2. Equifinality: three incompatible operationalizations

**Charles Ragin's QCA** is built around equifinality. Boolean minimization of a truth table produces multiple **causal "recipes"** — e.g., `A•B + C•D → Y` — each a conjunction of conditions *jointly sufficient* for the outcome; a case with membership in *either* recipe is likely to display Y [4]. Minimization applies Boolean absorption (`A•B•C` is absorbed by `A•B`; `A•D•E + A•D•~E` collapse to `A•D`) to yield a *logically minimal* solution: as few recipes as possible, each with as few ingredients as possible [4]. Causation is **set-theoretic and asymmetric**: a recipe is assessed as a *subset* of the outcome via **consistency** (degree to which the subset relation holds, conventional threshold ≈0.80) and **coverage** (empirical relevance), with necessary conditions appearing as *superset* relations [4]. Recipes routinely overlap — "overlapping coverage is the rule, not the exception" [4]. This is multiple conjunctural causation: distinct combinations, not a single net effect.

**Process tracing** handles equifinality at the level of the individual case: it can establish that an explanation was *likely true in this case* even if it represents only **one of several possible paths** to the outcome (George & Bennett 2005) [1]. It traces the path actually realized rather than estimating an average.

**The Pearlian / potential-outcomes program** sits at the opposite pole. Its target quantity — `P(y|do(x))`, an average interventional effect — is a *single-effect estimand* defined over a population [3]. Equifinality is not a primitive of the framework; multiple pathways are modeled (if at all) as mediation/heterogeneity *within one structural model*, not as a disjunction of distinct sufficient configurations. Where QCA expects several irreducible recipes, the potential-outcomes worldview presses toward one estimable effect — the core ontological friction running through the whole debate.

## 3. Confounder identification: three remedies

- **Pearl — graphical/back-door.** A set S is admissible for adjustment iff (1) no element of S is a descendant of X and (2) S blocks all back-door paths from X to Y (paths ending in an arrow into X) [3]. The adjustment formula `P(Y=y|do(X=x)) = Σ_s P(Y=y|X=x,S=s)P(S=s)` then identifies the effect from observational data; the back-door criterion implies conditional ignorability `X ⊥ Yx | S` — the Rosenbaum–Rubin condition — "providing the scientific basis for most inferences in the potential-outcome framework" [3]. Confounder selection becomes a matter of reading the diagram "by inspection" rather than a judgment about ignorability made in the analyst's head [3].
- **Process tracing — within-case mechanistic evidence.** Confounding (spuriousness, reverse causation) is addressed not by adjustment but by examining whether the hypothesized mechanism actually left its predicted traces, and by ruling rival hypotheses out through hoop and doubly-decisive tests [1]. Bennett and Checkel are alert to a distinct, source-level threat: the *instrumental* incentives of actors who control the evidence (newly empowered actors release only negative information about opponents), so each clue must be weighed for context, authorship, and audience [1].
- **QCA — configurational.** "Confounding" is reframed as the search for the correct *conjunction*: a condition's effect is assessed only inside the combinations in which it appears, and consistency/coverage diagnostics discipline which configurations count as sufficient or necessary [4].

These are not three routes to the same answer. Pearl's solution presumes a correctly specified graph and a population over which to adjust; process tracing's presumes rich within-case evidence and well-specified rival hypotheses; QCA's presumes a defensible calibration of set memberships.

## 4. Skocpol as the contested test case

Skocpol's *States and Social Revolutions* (1979) is the natural battleground because it sits *between* the traditions. She defines social revolutions as "rapid, basic transformations of a society's state and class structures... accompanied and in part carried through by class-based revolts from below," distinguished by two coincidences — societal-structural change with class upheaval, and political with social transformation [5]. Methodologically she uses **Mill's methods** (agreement and difference) across France, Russia, and China to find macro-structural **necessary and sufficient** causes [5]. Her causal claim is **conjunctural**: revolution follows from the coincidence of (1) incapacitation/breakdown of the central state under international military and economic pressure from more-developed competitors — for China, European imperialist pressure from the mid-nineteenth century that weakened and delegitimized the imperial state, plus the pre-1911 devolution of power to regional warlords — and (2) agrarian structures that left an oppressed peasantry able to revolt autonomously [5].

Crucially, Skocpol's design is *Millian and structural, not process-tracing*: it "does not attempt to disaggregate revolutionary processes into underlying causal social mechanisms," presupposing macro conditions that co-vary with the outcome and giving little attention to contingency or path dependency [5]. This makes her vulnerable on every axis above. From a Pearlian standpoint she selects on the outcome and offers no adjustment set; from a QCA standpoint her account is genuinely conjunctural but rests on a tiny, deterministically coded N; from a process-tracing standpoint she skips the within-case mechanistic evidence that would license causal inference. The critique (Little) that her antecedent structural conditions "did not suffice" — multiple outcomes were possible and CCP organizational agency mattered — is precisely the equifinality-and-contingency objection that later within-case methods were built to answer [5].

## 5. The quantitative critique and its rebuttal

King, Keohane, and Verba's *Designing Social Inquiry* (1994) pressed a statistical template onto qualitative work, warning especially against **selecting on the dependent variable**. Collier, Mahoney, and Seawright's "Insights and Pitfalls" (1996) reconstructs that logic faithfully: **truncation** — selecting cases on extreme DV values, e.g. studying only revolutions, regime breakdowns, or high-growth episodes — tends to *attenuate the regression slope toward zero*, whereas selecting on the explanatory variable does not bias it [6]. That is exactly the mechanism that makes Skocpol-style designs suspect.

But the rebuttal is equally precise: (1) qualitative scholars do not estimate numerical coefficients, so the mechanical attenuation result does not transfer directly; (2) KKV (and Geddes) **conflate selection bias with a distinct "no-variance" problem** — no-variance is *symmetric*, arising with selection on either the dependent *or* the explanatory variable, and is really about lacking a comparison rather than producing biased coefficients; and (3) once clarified, the practical recommendations converge on classic comparative-method and small-N wisdom [6]. Mahoney's later set-theoretic/Bayesian argument extends this defense: because inference rests on the likelihood ratio of within-case clues, the "small-N" framing misdescribes where the leverage lies [2].

Pearl's own apparatus actually *generalizes* the selection-bias worry beyond the qualitative target. He defines selection bias as distortion "induced by preferential selection of units for observation, usually governed by unknown factors," which "cannot be removed by randomized experiments and can rarely be detected," and addresses it graphically through **selection diagrams** and data-fusion/transportability results (Bareinboim, Tian & Pearl 2014; Bareinboim & Pearl 2016) that decide recoverability in polynomial time [3]. The same DAG logic that flags post-treatment conditioning (never adjust for a descendant of X — back-door condition 1) is what post-2010 critics turned on case selection: choosing cases on a *consequence* of the treatment is the qualitative analogue of conditioning on a collider or mediator [3].

## 6. How process-tracing protocols changed after 2010

The discipline's response was not to abandon process tracing but to *formalize* its Bayesian core and tighten its transparency.

- **Explicit Bayesian process tracing (Fairfield & Charman 2017).** Inference is written as the odds-ratio form of Bayes' rule: posterior odds (Hᵢ vs Hⱼ) = prior odds × likelihood ratio `P(E|Hᵢ,I)/P(E|Hⱼ,I)` [7]. Their protocol mandates: articulate **mutually exclusive and exhaustive** hypotheses and never compare H against ~H (because ~H smuggles in all rivals with an ill-defined likelihood); state all hypotheses *before* assigning priors; choose priors by background information, by the principle of indifference, or by running multiple prior distributions as a sensitivity check; treat distinct source-types as separate evidence at a middle granularity; and compute the **"weight of evidence"** (log-likelihood-ratio on a logarithmic scale), the step that lets the analyst ignore features common to all hypotheses [7]. The approach is demonstrated on Fairfield's Latin-American tax-policy case (one hypothesis against three rivals, six observations) with explicit sensitivity analysis [7].
- **Caveats that bound the formalization.** Fairfield and Charman are candid that assigning numbers to priors and likelihoods is hard and subjective, that the method is "intractable beyond relatively simple causal models," and — importantly against the transparency rationale — that "explicit Bayesian analysis and transparency are not synonymous"; scholars can make assumptions explicit without numbers, and much of the gain survives as *heuristic* Bayesian reasoning [7]. (They contrast their likelihood-based route with Humphreys & Jacobs's practice of placing priors directly on clue probative value [7].)

The four Van Evera tests are themselves now read as the qualitative shadow of this Bayesian machinery — hoop ≈ a high-`P(E|H)` necessary-evidence test, smoking-gun ≈ a low-`P(E|~H)` test — which is why Bennett and Checkel present probative value rather than evidence-count as decisive [1].

## 7. Integrating within-case and cross-case evidence: is there a consensus?

There is a shared *vocabulary* of integration but two distinct *philosophies*.

- **Nested analysis (Lieberman 2005, 2009)**, endorsed within the process-tracing program, runs process tracing on a few cases drawn from a statistical analysis to check (a) the direction of causal influence (IV→DV, not reverse) and (b) whether observed correlations are spurious — so that within-case work on hypothesized mechanisms "greatly increase[s] confidence in the causal significance of the correlations" found cross-case [1].
- **Integrative multi-method research (Seawright 2016).** Seawright pushes integration further onto quantitative ground: qualitative and quantitative work should share **"causation as a shared standard"** — the potential-outcomes/counterfactual definition — and case studies are chosen *after* regression specifically to *test the regression's assumptions* (measurement error, omitted variables/confounding, causal pathway, heterogeneity) [8]. For such discovery goals he argues, against intuition, that **deviant cases or extreme cases on the main independent variable** outperform random or typical cases [8]. This is the "shared-standard" pole, in tension with the Mahoney/Goertz "two-cultures" view that set-theoretic, event-based qualitative inference answers a *different* causal question than average-effect estimation [2].

A representative post-2010 application of the *qualitative-comparative* side is Cleary & Öztürk's "When Does Backsliding Lead to Breakdown?" (2021), which uses structured comparison plus within-case tracing across five countries (Bolivia, Ecuador, Thailand, Turkey, Venezuela), citing George & Bennett (2005), to argue that executive aggrandizement produces breakdown *conditional on opposition strategy* — irregular attempts to remove incumbents (especially after electoral defeats) hasten breakdown, moderate responses preserve democracy [9]. It exemplifies the genre but is comparative-and-process-traced rather than nested-statistical — illustrating that "integration" in practice still runs along the qualitative track as often as the Seawright track.

**Bottom line.** The evidence shows *partial convergence on procedure but not on foundations*. Both camps now (i) treat case selection and selection bias as live threats, (ii) endorse combining within-case and cross-case evidence, and (iii) accept Bayesian updating as the logic of within-case inference [1][7]. But they remain divided on the prior question of what a mechanism and a cause *are*: Pearl and Seawright work toward a single identifiable interventional effect under a potential-outcomes standard [3][8]; Mahoney, Ragin, and the process-tracing tradition treat causation as set-theoretic, configurational, and equifinal, where within-case event evidence — not adjustment over a population — carries the inference [2][4]. Genuine consensus exists on the *toolkit*; it does not yet exist on the *ontology*.
