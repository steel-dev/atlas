# Vicarious Liability for the June 14, 2024 Rear-End Collision: Applying Respondeat Superior on Summary Judgment

## Bottom line

On the present record, **summary judgment for the delivery company is unlikely to be granted.** Scope of employment is "ordinarily a jury question" and becomes a question of law only when the facts are undisputed *and* "no conflicting inferences are possible" [1][2]. Here, the same facts support two competing inferences — an authorized, employer-benefiting "team support errand" (within scope) versus a forbidden personal frolic (outside scope) — so the issue is for the jury. The supervisor's text, the metadata-confirmed deleted approval email (with its potential adverse inference), and the retroactive mileage reimbursement under the "team support errands" policy each independently create genuine disputes of material fact that preclude judgment as a matter of law for the employer. A plaintiff's cross-motion to establish scope as a matter of law would also likely fail for the same reason — the inferences cut both ways — though the substantive law tilts against the company on several discrete points.

## 1. The legal framework

### Respondeat superior and its three operative questions
Respondeat superior holds an employer "legally responsible for the wrongful acts of an employee or agent, if such acts occur within the scope of the employment" [3]. The doctrine applies to **employees, not independent contractors** [3]. The Restatement (Third) of Agency states the rule directly:

> **§ 7.07(1):** "An employer is subject to vicarious liability for a tort committed by its employee acting within the scope of employment." [4]

The three factors the question poses — (a) **employee status**, (b) **scope of employment**, and (c) **benefit to employer** — map onto this framework. Employee status and scope are formal elements of § 7.07. "Benefit to employer" is not a strict element under the modern Restatement (the "serve any purpose" test, below), but it is central under the "Benefits Test" some jurisdictions apply [3] and is the linchpin of the **ratification** theory [5].

### Employee status (§ 7.07(3); control test)
An agent is an **employee** "only when the principal controls or has the right to control the **manner and means** through which the agent performs work" [4]. Cornell's Wex catalogues the Restatement balancing factors used to separate employees from independent contractors: extent of control over work details, whether the agent has a distinct occupation, whether the work is customarily supervised, skill required, who supplies tools and the place of work, length of engagement, pay by job vs. by time, whether the work is part of the principal's regular business, the parties' belief, and control exercised in practice [3].

### Scope of employment
The modern Restatement formulation:

> **§ 7.07(2):** "An employee acts within the scope of employment when performing work assigned by the employer or engaging in a course of conduct subject to the employer's control. An employee's act is **not** within the scope of employment when it occurs within an **independent course of conduct not intended by the employee to serve any purpose of the employer**." [4]

State articulations are consistent. The Kansas pattern instruction (PIK 3d 107.06), quoted in *O'Shea v. Welch*: an employee acts within scope "when [he] is performing services for which [he] has been [employed], or when [he] is doing anything which is **reasonably incidental** to [his employment]. The test is **not necessarily whether this specific conduct was expressly authorized or forbidden**, but whether such conduct should have been **fairly foreseen** from the nature of the employment" [2]. The Mississippi instruction is "substantially similar" [2]. California's CACI 2926 standard: conduct is within scope if "(a) reasonably related to the kinds of tasks that the employee was hired to perform; or (b) reasonably foreseeable in light of the employer's business or the employee's job responsibilities" [6]. Texas Labor Code § 401.011 defines scope as activity that "has to do with and originates in the work, business, trade, or profession of the employer," and notes it is "not limited to the exact moment when the employee reports for work" nor "to the place where work is done" [6].

### Frolic and detour
A **detour** is a minor departure from duties that remains **within** scope; a **frolic** is a major departure for the employee's own benefit that falls **outside** scope and removes employer liability [7] (doctrine originating in *Joel v. Morison* (Eng. 1834)). *O'Shea v. Welch* adopts a six-factor "slight vs. substantial deviation" test (from *Felix v. Asai*, 237 Cal. Rptr. 718, 722): (1) the employee's intent; (2) the nature, time, and place of the deviation; (3) the time consumed; (4) the work for which the employee was hired; (5) incidental acts reasonably expected by the employer; and (6) the freedom allowed the employee in performing the job [2]. Critically, "[t]o release an employer from liability, the deviation must be **so material or substantial as to amount to an entire departure**," and "[d]ual purpose ventures may be considered within the scope of … employment" — where an employee pursues "a business errand and a personal objective simultaneously, he will still be acting within the scope of his employment" [2].

### Summary judgment standard
Under Fed. R. Civ. P. 56, the movant is entitled to judgment "as a matter of law" only where there is **no genuine dispute of material fact**, with facts viewed in the light most favorable to the non-movant [2]. Scope of employment is **"generally … a question of fact"** [1]; it "becomes a question of law **when the facts are undisputed and no conflicting inferences are possible**" [1]. *O'Shea* reversed summary judgment for the employer precisely because "whether the turn was within [the employee's] scope of employment is for the jury" [2].

## 2. Application to Daniel Rocha and the collision

### (a) Employee status — established
Rocha is a **delivery driver dispatched on a company-assigned route**, with the company controlling the route, the dispatch, and vehicle use, and reimbursing his mileage. That right to control the "manner and means" of his work makes him an **employee**, not an independent contractor, under § 7.07(3)(a) [4], and satisfies the Wex/Restatement balancing factors (work is part of the principal's regular business; control over route details; ongoing engagement; mileage reimbursement evidencing control) [3]. This element is not genuinely disputable on these facts, so respondeat superior is available in principle.

### (b) Scope of employment — genuinely disputed, hence for the jury
This is the contested element, and the evidence cuts both ways:

| Pulls the conduct **within** scope | Pulls the conduct toward a **frolic** (outside scope) |
|---|---|
| Supervisor's **text instructing** Rocha to pick up lunch for two warehouse coworkers [authorization] | Detour was **four blocks off** the assigned route |
| Deleted approval email (metadata-confirmed) — potential adverse inference it **authorized** the run [8][9] | Company policy **prohibits personal detours** |
| Errand for **coworkers** = arguably a "course of conduct subject to employer's control" and serving an employer purpose (§ 7.07(2)) [4] | Rocha **glanced at his phone** (personal distraction) two seconds before impact |
| Retroactive mileage reimbursement under "**team support errands**" policy [5] | The lunch run was, in part, a personal/coworker accommodation |
| Rocha was **on schedule** and intended to resume deliveries immediately | |

Under § 7.07(2), the decisive question is whether the detour was "an **independent course of conduct not intended … to serve any purpose of the employer**" [4]. Because the run was instructed by a supervisor, served coworkers, and was later treated and reimbursed as employer business, a jury could readily find it was **not** an independent, purely personal course of conduct — and the "serve any purpose" standard is broad: a mixed or dual purpose keeps conduct in scope [4][2]. Under the *O'Shea* six factors, a four-block, on-schedule detour for an instructed errand is far from "so material or substantial as to amount to an entire departure" [2]. *O'Shea* itself reversed summary judgment on materially similar facts — a manager on a company errand who deviated for a personal car-maintenance stop and crashed [2].

Because the facts support "conflicting inferences," scope is a **jury question** and summary judgment for the employer should be **denied** [1][2].

### (c) The policy-vs.-authorization tension does not rescue the employer
The company's policy prohibiting personal detours does **not** automatically defeat liability. Scope turns on whether the employee was engaged in the employer's business, "**not necessarily whether this specific conduct was expressly authorized or forbidden**" [2]. *Perez v. Van Groningen & Sons, Inc.*, 41 Cal.3d 962 (1986), is squarely on point: an employee who violated an **express company rule** (no passengers on a tractor) was **still within scope** because he was carrying out the employer's business, and the court quoted Prosser & Keeton: "**A master cannot escape liability merely by ordering his servant to act carefully. If he could, no doubt few employers would ever be held liable**" [1]. California's CACI 3722 likewise instructs that "[a]n employee's … conduct may be within the scope of employment **even if it breaks a company rule** or does not benefit the employer" [10]. Here the tension is sharper still: a **supervisor's text** affirmatively instructed the very detour the written policy forbade — a classic jury question about which authorization controlled.

### (d) Benefit to employer and ratification — established as triable, and adverse to the company
"Benefit to the employer" supports liability through two channels:

- **Benefits Test / scope:** Where the employee's pursuit is endorsed by express or implied permission and is "conceivably of some benefit to the employer," the employer is liable [3]. The supervisor's instruction supplies the permission; supporting warehouse coworkers supplies the benefit. (Note: under *Perez*, benefit is **not even required** for respondeat superior — an employer can be liable for unauthorized acts within scope despite lack of benefit [1].)
- **Ratification (CACI 3710):** An employer is responsible for conduct it "approved … after it occurred," and "[a]pproval … can be **inferred** if [the employer] **voluntarily keeps the benefits** of [the] unauthorized conduct after [it] learns of it" [5]. "A principal cannot split an agency transaction and accept the benefits thereof without the burdens" (*Reusche*) [5]. The company's **retroactive mileage reimbursement** for the detour after the collision, invoking the **"team support errands" policy effective May 1, 2024**, is textbook ratification evidence — a voluntary, after-the-fact adoption of the detour as employer business. Whether an employer ratified is "**generally a factual question**" [5], reinforcing that summary judgment is improper.

That the "team support errands" policy was effective May 1, 2024 — six weeks **before** the June 14 collision — also undercuts any claim the detour fell outside sanctioned conduct on the date in question.

### (e) Spoliation of the deleted approval email
The metadata-confirmed deleted supervisor email is **electronically stored information (ESI)** governed by **Fed. R. Civ. P. 37(e)**. The threshold (objective) test is whether the party "failed to take reasonable steps to preserve" ESI that is lost and cannot be restored [9]. The most potent sanctions under **Rule 37(e)(2)** — presuming the lost information was unfavorable, an **adverse-inference instruction**, dismissal, or default — require a finding the party "acted with the **intent to deprive** another party of the information's use in the litigation"; no separate showing of prejudice is needed once intent is shown, and the sanctions are discretionary ("may," not "must") [9]. The intent question can go to the jury under FRE 104(b) if the court finds a reasonable jury could find intent by a preponderance [9].

The representative pattern instruction (N.C.P.I.—Civil 101.39) frames the permissive inference: where the evidence was in a party's exclusive possession, was destroyed, and the party had notice of the potential claim, the jury "may infer, though you are **not compelled** to do so, that the [destroyed evidence] would be **damaging** to" that party [8]. **Effect on the record:** if the company deleted the supervisor's approval email after litigation was reasonably anticipated and with intent to deprive, the factfinder may infer the email **authorized** the lunch run — directly bolstering the scope/authorization showing. Even short of the severe sanction, a court may permit argument and evidence about the failure to preserve as a curative measure under Rule 37(e)(1) [9]. Either way, the spoliation issue enlarges, rather than narrows, the genuine factual disputes — further defeating the employer's summary-judgment motion.

## 3. Underlying negligence, causation, and damages (peripheral but necessary)
Vicarious liability presupposes an underlying tort by the employee. The negligence facts are strong against Rocha: dashcam shows he **glanced at his phone two seconds before impact**; he **rear-ended** a sedan that braked for **fallen lumber on Route 7**; and the sedan's **brake lights functioned properly**, eliminating a common comparative-fault defense to a rear-end collision. Note that negligence, like scope, is "generally … a question of fact" and is decided as a matter of law "only if reasonable jurors … could draw only one conclusion" [1] — but the distraction-plus-rear-end facts here make Rocha's negligence comparatively easy to establish. **Damages** rest on the sedan driver's **soft-tissue neck injury confirmed by medical imaging**, a cognizable injury supporting recovery if liability is imputed.

## 4. Jurisdictional note
There is **no national standard** for respondeat superior; states adopt their own tests, most using either a "**Benefits Test**" or a "**Characteristics Test**" [3]. The controlling articulation will be jurisdiction-specific. The most widely followed modern statement is **Restatement (Third) of Agency § 7.07** [4]; representative state law includes California (*Perez v. Van Groningen*, 41 Cal.3d 962; CACI 3710, 3722, 2926) [1][5][10][6] and Kansas as applied federally (*O'Shea v. Welch* (10th Cir. 2003)) [2]. Across all of them, the same conclusion holds: on this record the **scope-of-employment determination belongs to the jury**, and the company is **not entitled to summary judgment** on vicarious liability.

## Summary of the disposition
- **Employer's motion for summary judgment:** should be **denied** — employee status is clear, and scope/benefit/ratification present genuine, conflicting-inference disputes [1][2][5].
- **Plaintiff's cross-motion (scope as a matter of law):** also likely **denied** — the policy violation and personal phone glance permit a contrary inference, so the issue is for the jury (contrast *Perez*, where the facts were truly one-sided) [1].
- **Net:** The matter proceeds to trial on vicarious liability, with the supervisor's text, the spoliation inference, and the retroactive "team support errands" reimbursement as the plaintiff's strongest evidence that Rocha's detour stayed within the scope of his employment [4][5][8][9].
