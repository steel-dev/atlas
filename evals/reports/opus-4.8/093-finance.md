# Net Take-Home: US vs Germany for a Single US-Citizen High Earner (Tax Year 2024)

## Bottom line

For an identical gross compensation package, **the United States leaves materially more in the pocket than Germany — roughly $40,000+ per year more on a ~$220,000–$230,000 package.** Under Scenario A ($220,000 W-2 salary), estimated net take-home is **≈ $162,800 in the US vs ≈ $116,800 in Germany** (a ~$46,000 gap). Under Scenario B ($200,000 salary + $30,000 long-term capital gain), it is **≈ $173,500 US vs ≈ $127,800 Germany** (a ~$45,700 gap). The driver is Germany's combined ~42% top marginal income tax plus solidarity surcharge and uncapped-ish social contributions versus the US's lower federal rates and capped FICA. Capital gains are also taxed more heavily in Germany (26.375% Abgeltungsteuer vs 18.8% US LTCG+NIIT).

The decisive complication is **US citizenship-based taxation**: a US citizen resident in Germany owes tax to *both* countries and must use Foreign Tax Credits to avoid double taxation. Because German tax on the same income is *higher* than US tax, foreign tax credits will generally zero out the US income-tax liability on German-taxed wages — so the binding burden is the German one, and the US-vs-Germany net figures below approximate the real choice between *living in* each country, not a stacked double tax (with important exceptions on capital gains, noted in §4).

All EUR figures use the **2024 annual-average rate EUR/USD = 1.0822** ($ per €1) [1]. No US state income tax is assumed throughout.

## 2. Sensitivity analysis

### ±10% income variance

| Scenario | US net (USD) | Germany net (USD-equiv) | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| A −10% ($198,000) | ≈ $148,000 | ≈ $108,000 | ~$40,000 |
| A baseline ($220,000) | ≈ $162,800 | ≈ $116,800 | ~$46,000 |
| A +10% ($242,000) | ≈ $176,000 | ≈ $124,000 | ~$52,000 |

The gap *widens* with income because Germany's 42% marginal rate (above €66,761) bites every additional euro, while the US marginal rate sits at 24–32% and FICA is largely capped above $168,600. Germany's pension/unemployment contributions are flat above the €90,600 ceiling and health/care flat above €62,100, so additional German income is taxed at the marginal income-tax rate only — but that rate is higher.

### Alternative capital-gains scenarios (Scenario B variants)

| Gain treatment | US tax on gain | Germany tax on gain | Net difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| $30,000 **long-term** | $4,500 (15%) + $1,140 NIIT = **$5,640 (18.8%)** | €6,680 + €367 Soli = **€7,048** after €1,000 allowance (25.42% effective) | Germany ~$1,950 more |
| $30,000 **short-term** | taxed as ordinary income at 24–32% marginal (≈ $7,200–$9,600) + 3.8% NIIT | Same 26.375% Abgeltungsteuer (flat) | US worse if short-term |
| $100,000 long-term gain | 15% bracket holds to $518,900 TI → 15% + 3.8% NIIT = 18.8% ($18,800) [2][8] | 26.375% flat (€24,370) | Germany ~$6,000+ more |
| $250,000+ long-term gain | portion above $518,900 TI taxed at 20% + NIIT (23.8%) [2] | 26.375% flat | gap narrows |

Key asymmetry: the US **distinguishes short- vs long-term** (short-term = ordinary rates up to 37%; long-term = 0/15/20% + 3.8% NIIT), whereas Germany applies a **flat 25% Abgeltungsteuer + 5.5% Soli = 26.375%** regardless of holding period. Germany is harsher on long-term gains; the US is harsher on large short-term gains.

## 3. Line-item basis and rates used

**United States (2024, single filer):**
- Standard deduction $14,600; brackets 10%/12%/22%/24%/32%/35%/37%, with 24% spanning $100,525–$191,950 and 32% $191,950–$243,725 [2].
- OASDI 6.2% on wages up to the $168,600 wage base (max employee $10,453.20); Medicare 1.45% uncapped; Additional Medicare Tax 0.9% on wages over $200,000 (single) [6][8].
- Long-term capital gains: 0% / 15% (from $47,025) / 20% (from $518,900) [2]. NIIT 3.8% on the lesser of net investment income or MAGI over $200,000 (single) [8].

**Germany (2024, Steuerklasse I):**
- Einkommensteuer per §32a EStG (2024): tax-free Grundfreibetrag €11,604; progressive zones to €66,760; **42% flat-rate band €66,761–€277,825 (0.42·x − 10,602.13)**; 45% above €277,826 [3]. At the taxable base of €203,290 this yields exactly **€74,779.67**; at €184,809 (Scenario B wages), **€67,017.65** [3][4]. The figures above use the **originally enacted €11,604** Grundfreibetrag. Germany retroactively raised the 2024 Grundfreibetrag to **€11,784** (Gesetz zur steuerlichen Freistellung des Existenzminimums 2024, published 05.12.2024), changing the 42%-band formula to **0.42·x − 10,636.31** [4]; this lowers the tax by exactly **€34.18** at any income in the band (to €74,745.49 / €66,983.47 respectively) — immaterial to the comparison.
- Solidaritätszuschlag 5.5% of income tax (full rate applies well above the €18,130 exemption limit) [5].
- Social security employee shares with ceilings (West): pension 9.3% and unemployment 1.3% up to €90,600/yr (€7,550/mo); statutory health 7.3% + half of the ~1.7% Zusatzbeitrag, and long-term care 2.3% (+0.6% childless surcharge) up to €62,100/yr (€5,175/mo) [7].
- Abgeltungsteuer on private capital gains: flat 25% + 5.5% Soli on the tax = **26.375%** marginal. The first **€1,000 Sparer-Pauschbetrag** of investment income is tax-free; applied to the €27,721 gain, the taxable base is €26,721, giving Abgeltungsteuer €6,680.25 + Soli €367.41 = **€7,047.66 total (effective 25.42% on the full gain)**, versus €7,311.41 if the allowance were ignored.

**Health insurance treatment (Versicherungspflichtgrenze):** A high earner above the 2024 compulsory-insurance threshold (Versicherungspflichtgrenze, €69,300/yr) may opt out of statutory health insurance (GKV) into private cover (PKV). This analysis models **GKV at the €62,100 contribution ceiling** (employee health+care ≈ €6,862/yr), which is the conservative/standard case. PKV premiums are individually underwritten (age/health-based) rather than a statutory figure; for a healthy single high earner an indicative range is **roughly €400–€700/month (≈ €4,800–€8,400/yr)**, broadly comparable to or modestly below the GKV ceiling contribution — so PKV could modestly improve the German net but does not change the ranking.

## 4. Regulatory and compliance risks

**US citizenship-based taxation.** A US citizen remains subject to US tax on worldwide income regardless of residence. Two relief mechanisms:
- **Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE, Form 2555):** excludes ~$126,500 of *earned* income (2024) but not capital gains, and disqualifies that income from foreign tax credit use. At this income level the FEIE alone leaves a large unexcluded remainder.
- **Foreign Tax Credit (FTC, Form 1116):** generally the better choice here because German income tax (≈42%+Soli) exceeds US tax on the same wages, generating excess credits that fully offset the US income-tax liability on German-taxed employment income. The practical result: little or no *additional* US income tax on wages — the German burden governs.

**Double-tax risk on capital gains.** This is the sharp edge. Under the US–Germany income tax treaty, **Article 13 (Gains)** assigns the residence state primary taxing rights over gains from the alienation of securities (other than real property and certain business assets), so Germany taxes the gain as country of residence; the US nonetheless taxes its citizen under the **saving clause in Article 1**, which preserves US taxation of US citizens as if the treaty had not entered into force. The US allows an FTC for the German Abgeltungsteuer. Because German 26.375% exceeds US 18.8% (LTCG+NIIT), the German credit normally covers the US capital-gains tax — **but the 3.8% NIIT is widely treated as not creditable/not covered by the treaty's FTC, creating a residual US exposure** on investment income. Investors should expect the NIIT to potentially stick.

**Compliance obligations (assume these apply):**
- **FBAR (FinCEN Form 114):** required if aggregate non-US financial accounts exceed $10,000 at any point in the year — easily triggered by a German salary account.
- **FATCA (Form 8938):** required for specified foreign financial assets above the higher thresholds applicable to taxpayers living abroad (for a single filer abroad, generally >$200,000 at year-end / >$300,000 at any time).
- **German tax residency:** established by domicile (Wohnsitz) or habitual abode (gewöhnlicher Aufenthalt, broadly >183 days). Once resident, Germany taxes worldwide income; the treaty's **residence tie-breaker (Article 4: permanent home → centre of vital interests → habitual abode → nationality)** resolves dual residence, but the saving clause (Article 1) preserves US taxation of its citizens regardless.
- German PKV/GKV election, church tax (Kirchensteuer, 8–9% of income tax if registered with a church — omitted here), and US passive-foreign-investment-company (PFIC) rules on any non-US mutual funds/ETFs are further traps for the relocating investor.

## 5. Recommendation

**On pure net take-home, the US position is superior by roughly $45,000–$46,000/year in both scenarios**, and the advantage grows with income. If the decision is purely financial and the choice is between earning this package as a US resident vs a German resident, the US wins decisively. Germany's higher net cost buys broader social insurance (pension, unemployment, statutory health, long-term care) that the US figures only partially match.

**If the relocation is fixed and the real question is structuring within Germany:**
1. **Elect the Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116), not the FEIE**, given German rates exceed US rates — this neutralizes US income tax on wages.
2. **Realize long-term capital gains before establishing German residency** where possible: US-only LTCG is 18.8% vs Germany's 26.375%, a ~7.6-point saving on the gain, and avoids the German flat tax entirely.
3. **Budget for the non-creditable 3.8% NIIT** on investment income as a genuine residual double-tax cost.
4. **File FBAR and Form 8938; avoid non-US pooled funds (PFIC exposure).**

### Stated assumptions and data-quality flags
- EUR/USD fixed at the **2024 average 1.0822**; a weaker dollar would shrink Germany's USD-equivalent net and a stronger dollar widen the gap [1].
- German income tax is computed on **gross EUR salary as the taxable base**; in practice deductible Vorsorgeaufwendungen (pension/health contributions) reduce taxable income, so the German income-tax figures here are **conservative (slightly overstated)** and the true German net is somewhat higher — narrowing but not closing the gap.
- US health-insurance premium assumed at **~$5,000/yr employee share** (illustrative; employer plans vary). German GKV modeled at the contribution ceiling; **PKV premiums are individually underwritten and not a statutory figure** — a flagged estimate.
- Church tax, US state tax, and the €1,000 German savings allowance are excluded by assumption.
- Grundfreibetrag shown as the originally enacted €11,604; the retroactive €11,784 figure changes the result by only a few euros at this income.
