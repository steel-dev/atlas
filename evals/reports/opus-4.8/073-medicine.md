# Monitoring Strategy and Disposition for Recurrent Near‑Syncope with Documented Bradyarrhythmia

## Bottom line

This 68‑year‑old man meets guideline criteria for a **high‑risk syncope presentation** and should **not** be discharged to await an outpatient monitor. He has recurrent near‑syncope and two witnessed episodes of transient loss of consciousness ("went pale and couldn't talk" for ~30 seconds), an **already‑documented bradyarrhythmia and conduction abnormality** on his ED ECG, structural/conduction disease physiology, and an outpatient pathway that has demonstrably failed (Holter denied twice, cardiology 6 weeks out). Both the 2018 ESC and the 2017 ACC/AHA/HRS syncope guidelines support **admission for immediate in‑hospital continuous ECG (telemetry) monitoring** with expedited inpatient cardiology/electrophysiology consultation, rather than another attempt at outpatient evaluation [1][2]. A critical, reversible contributor — **metoprolol** — must be addressed before any conduction disease is attributed to intrinsic, pacemaker‑requiring disease [1][3].

## ECG interpretation: this is most consistent with Mobitz I (Wenckebach)

The described tracing — sinus bradycardia (HR 52), occasional dropped beats, and a PR interval that **varies between 180 and 240 ms** — fits **Mobitz type I (Wenckebach) second‑degree AV block**. In classic Mobitz I, the PR interval *progressively lengthens* with each beat until a P wave is not conducted and the QRS is dropped; the first conducted beat after the pause has the shortest PR, and the cycle repeats [3]. The defining contrast is the behavior of the PR interval:

| Feature | Mobitz I (Wenckebach) | Mobitz II |
|---|---|---|
| PR interval | Progressively **lengthens** before the dropped beat | **Constant** PR, then a beat is suddenly dropped [3] |
| Usual site of block | AV node (nearly always if QRS narrow); His‑Purkinje in 60–70% if QRS wide (>0.12 s) [3] | His bundle (25%) / bundle branches (75%) — infranodal, **always pathologic** [3] |
| Escape rhythm if it progresses | Reliable junctional escape, 35–55 bpm | Slow (20–40 bpm), **unreliable**, may give way to asystole [3] |
| Progression to complete block | Usually slow | **Typical and may be sudden** [3] |

A **varying** PR therefore argues against Mobitz II. This distinction is the central driver of disposition: Mobitz I is usually benign and AV‑nodal, whereas Mobitz II is infranodal with a high, often abrupt risk of progression to complete heart block, and a pacemaker is indicated absent a reversible cause [3].

**Two important caveats that keep this patient high‑risk despite a "benign" rhythm label:**
- If the QRS is **wide** (>0.12 s), even Mobitz I is more likely His‑Purkinje (infranodal) than AV‑nodal — so QRS width on the actual tracing must be examined [3].
- In **high‑grade** second‑degree block (two or more consecutive P waves blocked), Mobitz I and II cannot be reliably distinguished, and a pacemaker is usually indicated [3]. The "occasional dropped beats" plus syncope warrant continuous recording to characterize the block fully.

Critically, the *rhythm label alone does not exonerate this patient*: under ESC 2018, **Mobitz I second‑degree AV block and sinus bradycardia are explicitly listed as high‑risk ECG features** that suggest arrhythmic syncope and warrant further cardiac evaluation, even though, unlike Mobitz II/third‑degree block, they are not by themselves *diagnostic* of arrhythmic syncope [1].

## Metoprolol is a reversible cause that must be addressed first

The patient takes metoprolol 50 mg twice daily — a beta‑blocker that slows the sinus rate and AV nodal conduction and can itself produce both sinus bradycardia and Mobitz I block. Guidelines are explicit that **reversible causes must be excluded before attributing block to intrinsic conduction disease or proceeding to a pacemaker**: the Merck reference notes treatment is often unnecessary in Mobitz I "unless the block causes symptomatic bradycardia *and* transient or reversible causes have been excluded" [3], and ESC 2018 states flatly that **"cardiac pacing is not indicated in patients when there are reversible causes for bradycardia" (Class III)** [1]. The appropriate first step is therefore to **hold or down‑titrate the metoprolol** (with an alternative antihypertensive as needed) and observe on telemetry whether the bradycardia and AV block resolve. This is best done in a monitored setting given the symptomatic, recurrent presentation.

His other potentially reversible/contributing causes are **already largely excluded** by today's workup: electrolytes are normal (K 4.2, Mg 2.0), thyroid function is normal (TSH 2.4), and serial troponins have been normal across three visits with an unremarkable chest X‑ray, making acute ischemia an unlikely driver. The principal remaining reversible factor is the beta‑blocker.

## Why admission, not another outpatient attempt

Both guidelines anchor disposition on the presence of a serious/high‑risk condition, and this patient qualifies on multiple counts.

**2017 ACC/AHA/HRS** identifies "the presence of ≥1 serious medical condition … as the key determinant for further in‑hospital management" after syncope [2]. Its Table 7 of conditions warranting hospital‑based evaluation includes **"symptomatic conduction system disease," "symptomatic bradycardia or sinus pauses not related to neurally mediated syncope,"** and **"major traumatic injury due to syncope"** [2]. This patient has symptomatic conduction disease and symptomatic bradycardia with near‑syncope and witnessed transient loss of consciousness.

**2018 ESC** favors **admission** (over an ED observation unit/fast‑track) when high‑risk features coexist with a need for urgent evaluation/treatment that cannot be achieved otherwise — explicitly listing the need for **ECG monitoring, echocardiography, electrophysiological study, or device evaluation** as admission‑warranting [1]. It also notes structural/conduction disease is a major risk factor for sudden cardiac death and overall mortality in syncope [1].

**The failed outpatient pathway makes the case decisive.** ESC contemplates rapid, intensive outpatient workup in a syncope unit as an alternative to admission — *but only when that workup can actually be delivered* [1]. Here it cannot: the Holter was denied twice (appeal pending) and new‑patient cardiology is 6 weeks away. With a documented bradyarrhythmia, recurrent symptoms, and witnessed LOC, deferring for another 6+ weeks of unmonitored time is not a safe "expedited outpatient" plan. The inpatient route also delivers what the outpatient route is failing to: immediate continuous monitoring, a supervised metoprolol washout, echocardiography to assess for structural disease, and **same‑admission cardiology/EP consultation** to correlate rhythm with symptoms and decide on pacing.

## Choosing the monitoring modality

ESC 2018 frames the choice around **symptom frequency and the need for real‑time safety**, and emphasizes that ECG monitoring is justified mainly when there is a high pre‑test probability of capturing an arrhythmia — which is the case here because a bradyarrhythmia is already documented [1]. The graded recommendations are:

| Modality | Best when | Recommendation (ESC 2018) |
|---|---|---|
| **Immediate in‑hospital telemetry** | High‑risk patient, especially right after an event; "to avoid immediate risk to the patient" | **Class I (Level C)** — *applies to this patient* [1] |
| **Holter (24–72 h, up to ~2 wk)** | Frequent symptoms (≥1/week); detectable within days | Class IIa B [1]; selection: "symptoms frequent enough to be detected within a short period (24–72 h)" [2] |
| **External loop recorder** | Inter‑symptom interval ≤4 weeks; records over weeks to months | Class IIa B [1][2] |
| **Mobile cardiac outpatient telemetry (≤30 d)** | High‑risk patients whose rhythm "requires real‑time monitoring" | Patient‑selection criterion in ACC/AHA/HRS Table 8 [2] |
| **Implantable loop recorder (ILR, 2–3 y battery)** | Recurrent, **infrequent**, unexplained syncope, suspected arrhythmic, after nondiagnostic workup | Class I A (no high‑risk criteria) [1][2] |

The yield/duration logic matters: Holter captures only what occurs over days; an external loop recorder extends to weeks; an ILR covers months–years for rare events [1][2]. The transtelephonic/patient‑activated event monitor is explicitly of **limited use in frank syncope with sudden incapacitation**, because an incapacitated patient cannot trigger it [2].

For this man, the recommended sequence is:
1. **Now (inpatient):** continuous telemetry — Class I, and the only modality that provides real‑time safety while the metoprolol is withdrawn and high‑grade block is excluded [1][2].
2. **If admission captured a diagnostic, treatable rhythm** (e.g., persistent high‑grade block off the beta‑blocker), proceed to treatment (pacing) rather than further ambulatory monitoring.
3. **If telemetry plus metoprolol washout is non‑diagnostic** and symptoms recur unpredictably, an ambulatory device chosen by recurrence interval is appropriate — an external loop recorder/MCOT for events expected within weeks, or an **ILR** for infrequent recurrence after a nondiagnostic comprehensive workup; ESC specifically endorses the ILR in high‑risk patients in whom evaluation finds no cause and who lack a conventional pacing indication [1][2]. (Holter alone would only suffice if events are essentially weekly or more.)

## Clinical thresholds: admission versus expedited outpatient

**Findings that mandate admission / inpatient monitoring (this patient already meets several):**
- **Mobitz II second‑degree or third‑degree (complete) AV block** — high‑risk and, off reversible causes, a pacemaker indication [3][2]; ESC treats Mobitz II/third‑degree block as *diagnostic* of arrhythmic syncope [1].
- **Symptomatic bradycardia with hemodynamic compromise** or symptomatic conduction system disease [2].
- **Persistent sinus bradycardia <40 bpm or sinus pauses >3 s in the awake state** (ESC: diagnostic of arrhythmic syncope) [1]; symptomatic sinus pauses not from neurally mediated syncope warrant hospital evaluation [2].
- **Syncope causing injury**, or persistent vital‑sign abnormalities [1][2].
- **Need for urgent inpatient testing/treatment** (telemetry, echo, EPS, device) that cannot be delivered promptly as an outpatient — directly applicable given the insurance denial and cardiology backlog [1].

**Situations where expedited *outpatient* evaluation can be appropriate** (not this patient, because the pathway has failed and risk is high): low‑risk reflex syncope with a benign profile, where hospital evaluation "is unlikely to provide benefit" [2], or stable patients who can access a rapid syncope‑unit / observation‑unit workup [1]. If any outpatient interval were used, it must be paired with explicit safety counseling.

## Pacemaker indications and the role of EP consultation

The definitive question for cardiology/EP is whether intrinsic, pacemaker‑requiring conduction disease is present once metoprolol is removed. The relevant framework:

- **Pacing for second/third‑degree AV block:** for **Mobitz II second‑degree, high‑grade, or third‑degree AV block**, a permanent pacemaker **is indicated in the absence of a transient or reversible cause** — because progression to complete heart block is typical and may be sudden, with a slow, unreliable escape rhythm [3]. (This corresponds to the Class I pacing recommendation in the 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS bradycardia guideline for acquired Mobitz II/high‑grade/third‑degree block not due to reversible or physiologic causes.) For Mobitz I/AV‑nodal block, treatment is generally unnecessary unless there is **symptomatic bradycardia** and reversible causes have been excluded — i.e., symptoms that correlate with the bradyarrhythmia [3].
- **ESC concordance:** cardiac pacing is indicated when a **documented correlation between symptoms and the rhythm** is established, and in syncope with bundle‑branch block plus a positive EPS or ILR‑documented AV block (Class I B) — but **not** when a reversible cause is present (Class III) [1].

This is precisely why admission for telemetry plus a supervised beta‑blocker washout is the right move: it can establish symptom–rhythm correlation, unmask whether the block is intrinsic or drug‑induced, and let EP determine whether the patient has a true Class I pacing indication — none of which the stalled outpatient pathway can deliver.

## Patient and family safety counseling

Regardless of disposition, the patient and his daughter should be counseled on red‑flag symptoms that warrant immediate return to the ED or emergency services: **recurrent syncope or near‑syncope, prolonged or repeated loss of consciousness, chest pain, marked shortness of breath, or any injury from a fall**. These map to the same high‑risk features that drive admission — symptomatic bradycardia/conduction disease, syncope with injury, and persistent symptoms — under both guidelines [1][2]. The witnessed 30‑second episodes of pallor with inability to speak are themselves a red flag consistent with transient cerebral hypoperfusion from a bradyarrhythmia and should be treated as escalating, not reassuring, history.

## Summary judgment

- **The ECG is most consistent with Mobitz I (Wenckebach)**, but QRS width and whether any high‑grade block is present must be confirmed on the actual tracing; even Mobitz I qualifies as a high‑risk ECG feature here [3][1].
- **Metoprolol is the key reversible cause** and must be held/adjusted before any conduction disease is called intrinsic or a pacemaker is considered; electrolytes, thyroid, and ischemia are already largely excluded by today's labs [3][1].
- **Admit for immediate continuous telemetry (Class I)** with expedited inpatient cardiology/EP consultation; the documented bradyarrhythmia, recurrent symptomatic events with witnessed LOC, and the collapsed outpatient pathway (denied Holter, 6‑week cardiology wait) make inpatient evaluation the appropriate disposition rather than another outpatient attempt [1][2].
- **Reserve ambulatory monitors (loop recorder / MCOT / ILR) for after** a non‑diagnostic inpatient evaluation, matched to symptom recurrence interval [1][2].
