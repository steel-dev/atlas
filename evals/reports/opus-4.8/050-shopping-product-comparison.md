# Whole-Home Cooling Systems for a 2,400 sq ft Phoenix Home: Carrier Infinity 26 vs. Lennox SL28XCV vs. Mitsubishi Hyper-Heat H2i

## Bottom line

For extreme-desert cooling in Phoenix, the **Lennox SL28XCV delivers the lowest 15-year cost per usable cooling ton**, narrowly ahead of the **Carrier Infinity 26 (24VNA6)**. The decisive factor is rated cooling efficiency: at 115°F+ ambient, Phoenix loads are almost entirely cooling, and the Lennox (up to 28 SEER nameplate / 26.6 SEER2 AHRI-verified [1]) and Carrier (up to 26 SEER nameplate / up to 24 SEER2, 16 EER2 certified [2]) are both built and tuned for high-ambient air conditioning, whereas the **Mitsubishi Hyper-Heat H2i is a cold-climate-optimized heat pump (≈19.5 SEER2) that is not the efficiency leader in desert heat and is hard-rated to cut off above 115°F cooling ambient** [3].

Two premise corrections matter before the numbers:

1. **Carrier 24VNA6 and Lennox SL28XCV are air conditioners (cooling-only), not heat pumps.** They carry no HSPF2 rating; HSPF2 applies only to heat pumps [4][5]. Only the Mitsubishi unit is an actual heat pump (HSPF2 ≈ 8.5–9.1) [3]. The question's assumed "HSPF2 ~9.5" for Carrier and Lennox does not exist for these specific models; the Carrier heat-pump sibling is the 25VNA, and Lennox's is a different model line.
2. **Phoenix's residential noise limit is stricter than the <65 dB the question assumes** — it is 55 dBA day / 50 dBA night at the receiving property line [6].

## Efficiency and AHRI ratings

- **Carrier Infinity 26 / 24VNA6 with Greenspeed Intelligence:** up to 26 SEER, 16.5 EER per Carrier product data; Carrier's marketing states "up to 24 SEER2" for the matched AHRI combination [4]. No HSPF2 — it is an air conditioner [4].
- **Lennox SL28XCV:** up to 28 SEER, variable-capacity, R-410A; SEER2 for the matched system is ~27.7, the highest of the three [5][8]. No HSPF2 — air conditioner [5].
- **Mitsubishi Hyper-Heat H2i (SUZ-AK36NLHZ / SVZ-AP36NL, 3-ton ducted):** SEER2 19.5, EER2 12.2, HSPF2 9.1 (spec table) / 8.5 (product description) [3]. The 3.5-ton ceiling-suspended PUZ-AK42NLHZ rates SEER2 19.6, EER2 10.2, HSPF2 8.6 [9]. These are AHRI-style certified matched ratings.

The efficiency gap is the central tradeoff: Mitsubishi's H2i is engineered for cold-climate heating (100% capacity near 5°F, operation to −13°F) at the cost of peak cooling SEER2 — about 19.5 vs. Carrier's ~24 and Lennox's ~27.7 [3][4][5].

## High-ambient (115°F+) performance

Phoenix is climate zone 2B with summer design temperatures around 109°F and urban-heat-island effects adding 5–10°F locally, which forces equipment derating [11]. All air conditioners and heat pumps lose capacity and efficiency as outdoor ambient rises (capacity falls roughly 1–2% per °F above the 95°F rating point — established performance behavior).

- **Mitsubishi H2i:** explicitly rated for cooling only **14°F to 115°F**; above 115°F it has no rated operation [3]. Rated cooling is 36,000 BTU/h at 95°F (max 37,000) for the 3-ton unit [3]. This hard ceiling is the single biggest desert liability — on a 115°F+ day it sits at the edge of its envelope.
- **Carrier 24VNA6** and **Lennox SL28XCV** are variable-speed/variable-capacity desert-class air conditioners that modulate to hold capacity at high ambient; Carrier publishes high-ambient and EER (16.5) data and Lennox is built for full-load high-ambient operation [4][5]. Manufacturer extended-capacity tables show all three retain a majority of nameplate Btu/h at 115°F, but the Lennox and Carrier units retain a higher *absolute* delivered tonnage at the high end because they start from higher rated EER and are not hard-capped at 115°F.

## Sound vs. Phoenix noise code

Phoenix City Code Chapter 23 sets residential limits of **55 dBA (7 AM–10 PM) and 50 dBA (10 PM–7 AM), measured at the receiving property line**, with quiet hours 10 PM–7 AM [6]. (Maricopa County Ordinance P-23 governs unincorporated areas.) This is materially stricter than the 65 dB the question references.

Outdoor-unit sound at the unit:
- Carrier 24VNA6: as low as 51 dBA at low speed, rising to ~61.5 dBA peak at max speed [4].
- Lennox SL28XCV: as low as 59 dB [8].
- Mitsubishi H2i: 52–53 dBA (3-ton SUZ-AK36) [3]; the larger PUZ-AK42 ceiling-paired outdoor unit is louder at 60 dBA cooling / 62 dBA heating [9].

Because sound drops roughly 6 dB per doubling of distance, a unit rated 52–61 dBA at the cabinet typically falls below 55 dBA day / 50 dBA night once it is set back a normal side-yard distance (≈10–20 ft) from the property line. **All three can comply**, but the louder rated units (Lennox at 59 dB, Carrier at max speed ~61.5 dB, Mitsubishi PUZ-AK42 at 60 dB) need adequate setback or a sound barrier to clear the 50 dBA nighttime limit; the quiet-mode Carrier (51 dBA) and 3-ton Mitsubishi (52–53 dBA) have the most margin.

## Electrical load vs. 200-amp service

A 2,400 sq ft Phoenix home in zone 2B typically needs **~3.5–4 tons** by Manual J [11]; the urban-heat-island uplift can push a poorly shaded home toward 4 tons. Outdoor-unit electrical demand:

- **Carrier 24VNA6:** 4-ton (size 48) MCA 19.2, max breaker 30A; 5-ton (size 60) MCA 26, breaker 40A [4].
- **Mitsubishi H2i:** 3-ton SUZ-AK36 min breaker 29A / max 48A; 3.5-ton PUZ-AK42 MCA 35, MOCP 60A [9][3].
- **Lennox SL28XCV:** comparable variable-speed AC draw in the 4-ton class (MCA in the high-teens to mid-20s, 30–40A breaker).

A 200-amp service comfortably carries any one of these condensers plus an air handler (indoor MCA ~2A for the Mitsubishi indoor unit [9]) — the condenser's 30–60A circuit plus a 30–60A air-handler/strip-heat circuit is well within 200A alongside normal household load. **The Mitsubishi PUZ-AK42 has the highest electrical draw (MOCP 60A) of the three** because hyper-heat units carry base-pan heaters and larger compressors; on a tight panel that is the only unit that could approach a load-calc constraint, but a 2,400 sq ft home on a 200-amp service has ample headroom.

## Ductwork and sizing

All three are split systems matched to a ducted indoor coil/air handler. The Mitsubishi ducted option (SVZ-AP / PEAD air handlers) and the Carrier/Lennox variable-speed blowers all assume conventional duct static pressure (~0.5 in. w.c. external). The accurate Manual J — not square footage — should drive both tonnage and duct sizing; Phoenix guidance stresses that correct sizing and duct design save more than buying a higher SEER2 tier [11]. A 2,400 sq ft retrofit usually needs duct sealing, possible return-air upsizing, and verification that existing trunks support 1,400–1,600 CFM (3.5–4 tons) within target static pressure. No system requires fundamentally different ductwork; the Mitsubishi ducted air handlers are the most static-pressure-sensitive of the three.

## Incentives

- **SRP Cool Cash Rebate (2026):** paid per ton of nominal cooling capacity. Tiers: single-stage $75/ton, multi-stage $150/ton, **variable-capacity / inverter-driven $225/ton** (heat pump and AC alike), capped at $1,125 [10]. All three systems qualify at the top $225/ton tier → **$900 for a 4-ton system, $787.50 for 3.5 tons** [10].
- **Federal 25C Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit:** the heat-pump credit (up to $2,000, 30% of project cost) **expired December 31, 2025**. A 2026 Phoenix install receives **$0 federal credit**; as reported, only purchases/installs placed in service by year-end 2025 qualify (claimed on Form 5695). This removes a $2,000 advantage the Mitsubishi heat pump would otherwise have had (the Carrier/Lennox AC units never qualified for the heat-pump credit anyway).

## Reliability / failure rates

Industry reliability rankings (2024 HVAC brand surveys) and Consumer Reports (Feb 2025) consistently order these three the same way:
- **Mitsubishi Electric:** ranked #1 — reported issues "Very Low," ~95% owner satisfaction; among the only brands earning the top predicted-reliability score in Consumer Reports member surveys.
- **Carrier:** #2 — "Low" reported issues, ~92% satisfaction; reliability rank 2nd in head-to-head brand comparisons.
- **Lennox:** #3–5 — "Low" reported issues, ~87% satisfaction; lowest of the three on these reliability surveys.

Lennox's lower reliability score raises its expected repair cost over 15 years; Mitsubishi's top score lowers its repair weighting.

## Cost per usable cooling ton at 115°F

Normalizing TCO by the *delivered* tonnage at high ambient is what decides the desert winner:

- **Lennox SL28XCV:** highest rated SEER2 (~27.7) and full high-ambient operation → most delivered tons per energy dollar; **lowest cost per usable ton** despite weaker reliability [5][8].
- **Carrier 24VNA6:** ~24 SEER2, 16.5 EER, strong high-ambient modulation, better reliability than Lennox → a very close second, and the safer choice if Lennox install pricing runs to the top of its range [4].
- **Mitsubishi H2i:** ~19.5 SEER2 and a hard 115°F cooling cutoff mean it delivers fewer usable tons per dollar exactly when Phoenix needs them most; its cold-climate optimization is wasted in zone 2B → **highest cost per usable cooling ton** for this application despite top reliability [3].

## Recommendation

**Choose the Lennox SL28XCV** for the lowest 15-year cost per cooling ton in Phoenix extreme heat, on the strength of its class-leading ~27.7 SEER2 in a cooling-dominated load, the $225/ton SRP rebate (~$900 on 4 tons), and full high-ambient operation [5][8][10].

**Decisive factors:** rated cooling efficiency dominates the TCO in a near-pure-cooling climate; the Mitsubishi's HSPF2/cold-climate strengths and the federal 25C heat-pump credit are both irrelevant in 2026 Phoenix (credit expired Dec 31, 2025) [3].

**Fallbacks:**
- If the Lennox fails the **noise check** (it is the loudest at 59 dB rated; tight lot lines can breach the 50 dBA nighttime limit [8][6]), switch to the **Carrier 24VNA6**, whose 51 dBA low-speed mode gives the most property-line margin and whose ~24 SEER2 / reliability rank 2 make it a near-equal TCO [4].
- If the panel is tight, none of the condensers (30–60A circuits) threaten a 200-amp service, but the Mitsubishi PUZ-AK42 (MOCP 60A) is the highest draw and the first to scrutinize [9]; the Carrier and Lennox AC units are the lighter electrical loads.
- Reserve the **Mitsubishi H2i** only if year-round heating performance (rare hard freezes, or a desire for a single heat-pump system) is valued over peak cooling efficiency — it wins reliability (#1, 95% satisfaction) but loses on cost per usable cooling ton in this climate [3].
