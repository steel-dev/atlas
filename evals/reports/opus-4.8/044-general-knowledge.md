# Women's Labor Force Participation, 1970–2025: Saudi Arabia, South Korea, Iceland, and India

## Overview

Across four economies, the half-century from 1970 to 2025 produced four distinct trajectories of women's labor force participation: a **rapid late surge in Saudi Arabia**, a **sustained high plateau in Iceland**, a **gradual climb out of an M‑shaped age profile in South Korea**, and a **paradoxical decline-then-recovery in India**. These divergences track closely to the timing and design of policy interventions — guardianship and mobility reform, parental leave and father quotas, childcare subsidies, and inheritance law — interacting with structural and cultural conditions.

A measurement caveat frames everything below: the cross-country time series uses the **ILO modeled estimate of the female labor force participation rate (LFPR) as a share of the female population aged 15+**, which allows comparison but runs lower than national survey measures. Where national statistics offices report higher figures (Saudi GASTAT, India's PLFS, Statistics Iceland), both are given.

## Saudi Arabia: a compressed, policy-driven surge

For most of the period Saudi female participation was among the world's lowest and nearly flat: roughly 15% in 1990, 16.4% in 2000, 17.6% in 2010, and still only 21.6% by 2015 (ILO modeled) [1]. The inflection is sharp and recent — the rate jumped from **24.4% (2019) to 29.3% (2020) to 34.2% (2021)**, roughly doubling between 2017 and 2022, and stood at **33.6% in 2025** (ILO) [1]. Saudi Arabia's own register-based statistics (GASTAT) put the **female LFPR at 36.0% (Q4 2024)** with an employment rate of 31.8%; the rate is concentrated in prime-age women (50.2% for ages 25–54 versus 17.3% for 15–24 and 15.3% for 55+) [2].

This timing maps directly onto **Vision 2030** and the legal reforms surrounding it. Vision 2030 originally targeted 30% female participation — a goal reached around 2021, years ahead of schedule [2]. The enabling reforms were concentrated in 2018–2019: the **driving ban was lifted in June 2018**, and an August 2019 package — a Council of Ministers decision endorsed by **royal decree M.134** — let women over 21 obtain passports and travel abroad without male-guardian permission, register births/marriages/deaths/divorces, be recognized as "head of household," and benefit from a revised Labor Law that explicitly defines a "worker" as female as well as male, prohibits dismissal during pregnancy or maternity leave, and adds anti-discrimination protections [3]. Removing mobility and legal-personhood constraints — not childcare policy — was the binding intervention here.

**Composition.** Employed Saudi women are highly educated and concentrated in skilled occupations. By education, **47.8% hold a bachelor's degree** and roughly 52% hold tertiary qualifications overall (associate diploma 2.9%, intermediate diploma 5.1%, bachelor's 47.8%, master's 3.4%, PhD 0.8%), with 26.4% at secondary and 11.4% below secondary [2]. By sector, the **private sector is now the largest employer** of Saudi women (≈996,929) ahead of government (506,028) and other public bodies (157,603) [2]. Occupationally, women cluster in professional (35.5%), clerical (19.8%), technical (10.0%), and managerial (6.6%) roles; they make up 45.6% of Saudi health workers and 45.8% of the Saudi tourism workforce [2]. The mean gender pay gap is 27.6% and female unemployment fell from 19% (2022) to about 13% (2024) [2].

**Demographics.** With a total fertility rate of **2.28 in 2023** — near replacement and high relative to the other three — and a young, fast-growing population (35.3 million mid-2024), Saudi Arabia faces little aging pressure; future female participation depends mainly on sustaining demand and continuing reform rather than on demographic necessity [1][2].

## South Korea: climbing out of the M‑curve

Korea's female LFPR rose **gradually** rather than dramatically: 47.2% (1990), 49.4% (2000), a dip to 49.8% around 2010, then 52.4% (2015), 53.8% (2019), and **56.8% in 2025** (ILO modeled) [1]. National-estimate data extend the story back further: the series averaged 47.6% over 1960–2017, hit a **record low of 27.23% in 1977**, and an all-time high of 52.6% in 2017 — implying a U-shaped decline through early industrialization followed by a long recovery [4].

The defining feature is not the time path but the **M-shaped age profile**: participation peaks in the 20s, dips sharply during the prime childbearing/childrearing years (late 20s–30s), and recovers in the 40s. Korean policy has explicitly targeted this trough. Statutory **childcare leave** runs up to **one year per parent** for a child aged 8 or under (or grade 2 or below); the leave itself is unpaid but carries wage-based government subsidies subject to a floor and cap [5]. To pull fathers into caregiving, **enhanced "both-parents" subsidies were expanded effective 1 January 2024 — from 3 to 6 months of eligibility, with the qualifying window extended from the child's first 12 to first 18 months and a higher wage ceiling** [5]. A 2024 Supreme Court ruling also made employers' obligations to accommodate workers with young children (adjusted/reduced hours, restricted overtime) judicially enforceable [5]. The OECD reports women's participation rising alongside growth in full-time and service-sector jobs, with Korea's 15–64 employment rate at 69.3% in May 2023.

**Composition.** Korea is highly tertiary-educated — **53% of 25–64-year-olds hold tertiary qualifications**, exceeding the upper-secondary share (38%) — so the female workforce skews college-educated (OECD Education at a Glance 2023). Employment is overwhelmingly services-based, and recent gains have been concentrated in full-time service jobs (OECD Employment Outlook 2023).

**Demographics.** Korea's challenge is the most acute of the four: the **total fertility rate fell to 0.7 in 2023 — the world's lowest** — with a slight estimated rebound to **0.75 in 2024**, far below the 2.1 replacement level, and the population has already begun to decline . A shrinking working-age population makes raising female (and elderly) participation an economic necessity, and is the explicit rationale behind the childcare-leave and father-quota expansions; whether they reverse the M-curve depends on actually shifting the unequal division of care.

## Iceland: a sustained high plateau

Iceland's female participation was already very high in 1990 (66.2%) and rose to a peak of **73.5% in 2000**, settling into a sustained band around **70% through 2025** (ILO modeled, 15+) [1]. National measures run higher still: the overall activity rate for ages 16–74 was **79.4% in Q1 2023** . Iceland consistently ranks among the world's highest for female participation.

Rather than lifting participation from a low base, Iceland's policies aimed at **gender equality within an already-high baseline** — the division of caregiving and of corporate power. The **Act on Maternity/Paternity Leave and Parental Leave No. 95/2000** (*Lög um fæðingar- og foreldraorlof*) introduced a **3-month non-transferable father quota** — longer and faster than any prior Nordic country — alongside 3 months reserved for the mother and 3 months shared, for **9 months total**, originally paid at **80% of average wages** subject to a ceiling [6]. Take-up was high: **87.7% of fathers** took leave by 2006, using about a third of all parental-leave days, though benefit cuts during the 2008–2009 financial crisis (ceiling reduced ISK 400k→300k) dampened father uptake [6]. The leave was later expanded toward a 12-month, equally split (6+6) model. Iceland's **1975 Gender Equality Act** (current version 2008) and a **2010 law requiring 40% gender balance on the boards** of companies with more than 50 employees (effective 2013, without punitive sanctions) round out the framework [6].

**Composition.** Employment is overwhelmingly services: in 2023, **services 77.98%, industry 18.32%, agriculture 3.71%** [7]. Iceland's high headline participation coexists with a large share of women in part-time work and persistent gender gaps in pay and decision-making power, which the parental-leave and board-quota measures were designed to address [6].

**Demographics.** Iceland's population is small but growing — 389,000 in January 2025, projected to reach a most-likely 550,000 by 2075 (range 450,000–700,000 depending on migration) . With participation already near a practical ceiling and growth driven substantially by migration, future change is more about quality (closing remaining gaps) than quantity of participation.

## India: the paradox of decline amid growth

India's series is the outlier. The ILO modeled rate **peaked around 34.9% in 2005, then fell steeply to 28.4% (2010), 26.6% (2015), and a trough of 26.0% in 2020**, before recovering to **32.4% by 2024–2025** [1]. India's own **Periodic Labour Force Survey (PLFS)** reports much higher levels — because it captures unpaid family work and self-employment — with the female LFPR (15+, usual status, ps+ss) at **41.7% in 2023–24, up from 37.0% in 2022–23 and just 23.3% in 2017–18** [8]. The 2023–24 PLFS rate is markedly higher in rural areas (47.6%) than urban (28.0%), and the female worker-population ratio rose in parallel from 22.0% to 40.3% over the same period [8]. Both the ILO and PLFS series agree on the shape: a notable decline through the 2000s–2010s followed by a post‑2019 rebound.

The **central puzzle** is that participation fell precisely while female education rose sharply, fertility declined, GDP grew 5–9% a year, and household infrastructure improved — all forces that "should" have freed and pulled women into paid work [9]. Desai and Joshi (2019), using National Sample Survey and India Human Development Survey data for 2004–05 and 2011–12, test three families of explanation and weigh them explicitly [9]:

- **Measurement.** Much of the recorded rural decline "may be overstated"; fragmented, discontinuous work is poorly captured by NSS instruments (time-use surveys record far more female work). Measurement error cannot be ruled out [9].
- **Supply / income effect.** Rising household incomes and education do reduce labor supply, and women's education has a well-known **U-shaped relationship with participation** (withdrawal at moderate education levels, partly an income/status effect). But supply factors explain only a **relatively small proportion** of the decline [9].
- **Demand / structural.** The dominant driver is structural: women were **pushed out of agriculture** as land fragmentation cut farm size and labor demand, while alternative jobs failed to materialize ("jobless growth"). The decline is concentrated among the **least-educated, poorest, smallest-farm women** [9].

Crucially, where opportunity expanded — better transport, and public works through **MGNREGS** — women's participation **rose**, indicating latent willingness to work rather than voluntary withdrawal [9]. A counterintuitive finding: income's negative effect on participation was stronger in *less* conservative households, complicating a purely cultural-norms story [9]. Cultural constraints (purdah/ghunghat seclusion, stigma against manual labor for educated women) are real but, on this evidence, secondary to demand-side collapse and measurement.

**Legal reform.** On inheritance, the **Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005 (Act 39 of 2005), in force 9 September 2005**, amended **Section 6** of the Hindu Succession Act 1956 to make daughters **coparceners by birth** in joint Hindu family property with the same rights and liabilities as sons — a landmark equalization of ancestral-property rights . Its labor-market effects are indirect (asset ownership, bargaining power) and not isolated in the participation series.

**Demographics.** India's fertility has fallen to about replacement level, and it holds the world's largest working-age population — a potential "demographic dividend" whose realization hinges on creating the kind of non-farm jobs and mobility/childcare support that the evidence shows draw women in [9].

## Cross-country synthesis: how policy timing and type explain divergence

The four trajectories are best understood by **what was binding** in each context and **when policy addressed it**:

- **Saudi Arabia (rapid rise):** The binding constraint was legal and mobility-based — guardianship, driving, legal personhood. Concentrated 2018–2019 reforms plus Vision 2030 demand-side push produced the steepest, most compressed rise of the four, roughly doubling participation in five years [1][3][2].
- **Iceland (sustained high):** Participation was already high; policy (1975/2008 equality acts, the 2000 father quota, 2010 board quota) targeted the *internal* distribution of care and power rather than the headline level, holding participation near 70%+ while attacking gender gaps [1][6].
- **South Korea (M-curve):** The binding constraint is the career interruption around childbirth. Korea's gradual climb reflects the slow, only-partial success of childcare-leave and father-quota incentives (expanded 2024) against deeply unequal care norms — and now an existential demographic motive (TFR 0.7) [1][4][5].
- **India (paradoxical decline):** Here the constraint was demand-side — destruction of agricultural work without non-farm substitutes — compounded by measurement and an income/education U-curve. Inheritance reform (2005) addressed assets, not jobs; participation only recovered when work opportunities (transport, public works, and recent survey-period demand) expanded [1][9][8].

In short, **mobility/legal reform unlocked Saudi Arabia; care-and-power reform maintained Iceland; care-interruption policy is slowly reshaping Korea; and India's swing was driven less by any single policy than by the structure of labor demand and how work is measured.** Demographics point the four in different directions: Korea's collapse in fertility makes raising female participation imperative, Saudi Arabia's youthful population gives room to grow, India's dividend is contingent on job creation, and Iceland sits near a practical ceiling where the frontier is equality of pay and power rather than headcount.

*Note on sources and comparability:* cross-country figures rely on ILO modeled estimates (15+) for consistency [1]; national measures (GASTAT 36.0%, PLFS 41.7%, Statistics Iceland 79.4%) are higher because of differing age bands and treatment of informal/self-employment, and are cited where available [2][8]. Several breakdowns — e.g., explicit part-time/full-time splits for women in Saudi Arabia and India, and Korea's detailed education-by-participation cross-tabs — were not isolated in the retrieved sources and are characterized qualitatively rather than with exact shares.
