# The Grounded Theory Schism: Glaser vs. Strauss–Corbin vs. Charmaz

## Bottom line

Grounded Theory (GT) fractured along an **emergence-versus-forcing** axis and then along an **epistemological** axis. The original 1967 program of Glaser and Strauss prized the *generation* of theory from data over its verification; after their collaboration ended, **Glaser's *Theoretical Sensitivity* (1978)** held that theory must EMERGE through loosely-held "theoretical codes/coding families," while **Strauss and Corbin's *Basics of Qualitative Research* (1990)** imposed a fixed analytic skeleton — axial coding and the conditions/context/actions–interactions/consequences "coding paradigm" — plus explicit validation steps. In 1992 Glaser published a polemical rebuttal, *Basics of Grounded Theory Analysis: Emergence vs. Forcing*, accusing Strauss and Corbin of "forcing" data into preconceived categories [1]. **Kathy Charmaz's *Constructing Grounded Theory* (2006)** reframed the whole quarrel as a family dispute *within* objectivism/post-positivism, recasting GT on a relativist, constructivist footing — data are co-constructed by researcher and participant, not discovered [2]. This maps directly onto the **paradigm wars** typology of Guba and Lincoln (positivism / postpositivism / critical theory / constructivism) in the Denzin & Lincoln *Handbook* [3].

On current dominance: in 2020–2024 methodology literature, GT is described as "the most cited qualitative methodology," with its growth driven specifically by Charmaz's **constructivist school** [4], even though the **Strauss–Corbin "systematic design" remains the most frequently *applied* procedure** in applied-research training because of its step-by-step structure [5]. The contemporary field uses a standard tripartite taxonomy — Classic/Glaserian, Straussian, Constructivist [6][5].

## The original 1967 formulation

*The Discovery of Grounded Theory* (Glaser & Strauss, 1967) was an attack on the hypothetico-deductive mainstream. Its authors criticized "the overemphasis in current sociology on the verification of theory, and a resultant de-emphasis on the prior step of discovering what concepts and hypotheses are relevant for the area that one wishes to research" (1967, pp.1–2), and complained that departments had become "repositories of 'great-man' theories," producing an antagonism between "theoretical capitalists" and a "proletariat" of testers [1]. The book aimed "to strengthen the mandate for generating theory… a defense against doctrinaire approaches to verification" (p.7) [1]. The core procedural tenets were the **constant comparative method** (Glaser had set this out in 1965, *The constant comparative method of qualitative analysis*, *Social Problems* 12:436–445), **theoretical sampling**, the **emergence of theory from data**, and the **suspension of preconceived hypotheses** [1][4].

## Glaser, *Theoretical Sensitivity* (1978): emergence and coding families

Glaser elaborated "theoretical sensitivity" through the notion of **theoretical coding**, which he demarcated from **substantive coding** [1]. Substantive codes are generated *ad hoc* during open coding and refer to the empirical substance of the field; theoretical codes "conceptualize how the substantive codes may relate to each other as hypotheses to be integrated into a theory" (1978, p.72) [1]. To support theoretical coding he offered an extended, loosely-structured list of **coding families** — e.g. a "degree family" (limit, range, extent, amount), a "dimension family" (element, part, facet, slice, sector, aspect, segment), and families capturing causes, contexts, consequences, and conditions [1]. Crucially, these are concepts the analyst *has available* and applies selectively as the data warrant — a flexible repertoire, not a mandatory template — and the analyst is warned not to force theoretical concepts onto the data [1].

## Strauss & Corbin, *Basics of Qualitative Research* (1990): axial coding and the coding paradigm

Strauss and Corbin agreed that any inquiry needs an explicit or implicit theoretical framework to identify and relate categories, but where Glaser supplied a loose list, they drew on **one general model of action rooted in pragmatist/interactionist social theory** to build a "skeleton" or "axis" [1]. Their **coding paradigm / paradigm model** — **causal conditions, phenomenon, context, intervening conditions, action/interaction strategies, and consequences** — was used "to think systematically about data and to relate them in very complex ways" (1990, p.99), and was applied during **axial coding**, "intense analysis done around one category at a time in terms of the paradigm items" [1]. They explicitly recommended this paradigm as "especially helpful to beginning analysts" who otherwise merely précis the data [1]. They also took a more liberal stance on literature: "all kinds of literature can be used before a research study is begun" (1990, p.56) [1]. The third leg of their systematic apparatus is the **conditional (later conditional/consequential) matrix** — a set of concentric, interconnected circles, each representing a different source of conditions/consequences ranging from the most macro (international, national) inward through community and organizational levels to the most micro (group, individual, interaction) — an analytic tool for bridging micro and macro conditions and tracing how consequences ripple outward (Strauss & Corbin 1998, Fig. 12.1, pp.183–193) [7]. The matrix anchors their systematic verification framework: it forces the analyst to locate a grounded phenomenon within a traceable chain of conditions and consequences across levels, and their later writing pays sustained attention to how grounded categories and propositions can be **further validated** — the verification drift [1][7].

## Glaser's 1992 rebuttal: *Emergence vs. Forcing*

In 1992 Glaser turned against Strauss and Corbin in a monograph published through his own Sociology Press, *Basics of Grounded Theory Analysis: Emergence vs. Forcing*, written, as Kelle notes, "in an exceptionally polemic style," charging that they had "betrayed the common cause" [1]. The recurring thread of his critique: by using "axial coding" and "coding paradigms," researchers **force** categories onto the data instead of letting them **emerge** [1]. Glaser's defense rests on an inductivist, almost foundationalist confidence — Kelle calls it "a gleam of epistemological fundamentalism (or 'certism')" — encapsulated in Glaser's claim that "Grounded theory looks for what is, not what might be, and therefore needs no test" [1].

Kelle's analytical resolution is that the quarrel is narrower than it appears. Both camps confront the **"theory-ladenness of observation"**: GT tells researchers both to approach the field without preconceptions *and* to use prior theoretical knowledge to spot relevant phenomena [1]. Glaser's own 1978 theoretical codes are themselves concepts held "independently from data collection and data analysis," so the real difference "boils down to" whether one applies a single fixed coding paradigm (Strauss–Corbin) or selects from many coding families *ad hoc* (Glaser) [1]. Each has trade-offs: the Strauss–Corbin paradigm is user-friendly for novices and carries low forcing risk because its terms have "only limited empirical content," whereas Glaser's pluralistic families fit more theoretical traditions but are hard for novices and invite eclectic misuse [1].

## Charmaz, *Constructing Grounded Theory* (2006): the epistemological shift

Charmaz's decisive move was to classify **both** prior camps together. She labels Glaser (1978, 1992, 1998) **and** Strauss & Corbin (1990, 1998) as **objectivist grounded theory** with "roots in mid-20th-century positivism," noting that despite their feud the two versions "shared basic premises about an external reality, the discovery of provisional truths in this reality, the role of the observer, and an unproblematic representation of research participants" [2]. Objectivist GT, in her account, answers "why" questions and seeks explanation and prediction abstracted from the specific research site [2].

Against this she sets **constructivist grounded theory**, which attends to "what" and "how" questions, locates understanding in the specific circumstances of the research process, and "assumes relativity, acknowledges standpoints, and advocates reflexivity" [2]. Her ontology is a qualified realism — constructivism "assumes the existence of an obdurate, real world that may be interpreted" — paired with a relativist, interpretive epistemology in which GT strategies are "strategies for creating and interrogating our data, not routes to knowing an objective external reality" [2]. This is the epistemological shift: **relativist ontology + constructivist/interpretive epistemology** displacing the implicit post-positivism shared by Glaser and Strauss–Corbin [2].

**What Charmaz resolved, retained, and abandoned.** She dissolved the emergence-versus-forcing impasse by denying its shared premise — that categories are *out there* to be either discovered cleanly (Glaser) or imposed (Strauss–Corbin); on a constructivist view, categories are co-constructed, so neutral "emergence" and illegitimate "forcing" are both misdescriptions of the same interpretive act [2]. She **retained** the core GT toolkit — coding, constant comparison, memo-writing, and theoretical sampling — but **abandoned** the objectivist "discovery" framing and the positivist apparatus of both predecessors [2]. She also abandoned the search for a single "basic social process," explicitly aligning with Glaser's own 2003 disavowal and with Clarke (2005) [2]. Notably, she observes that Strauss's *procedures* "gave grounded theory an objectivist cast" even though his Chicago, symbolic-interactionist roots leaned toward social constructionism — locating the constructivist potential in the tradition itself [2].

## Situating the schism in the paradigm wars

Guba and Lincoln's "Competing Paradigms in Qualitative Research," the framing chapter of the Denzin & Lincoln *Handbook of Qualitative Research*, lays out the four contending paradigms of the era: **positivism, postpositivism, critical theory (and related ideological positions), and constructivism** [3]. They describe postpositivist methodology as "modified experimental/manipulative," emphasizing "critical multiplism" (a refurbished triangulation) to *falsify* rather than verify hypotheses, reintroducing "discovery," and they cite grounded theory (Glaser & Strauss 1967; Strauss & Corbin 1990) as an exemplar of grounding theory in natural settings [3]. The chapter itself names "the paradigm wars" (citing Gage's 1989 "The paradigm wars and their aftermath") [3].

Mapped onto this spectrum, the GT split is a migration from postpositivism toward constructivism: **Glaser** (inductivist, theory "needs no test") and **Strauss–Corbin** (systematic procedures, validation steps) both sit in the **postpositivist** band, which is precisely why Charmaz can collapse them into "objectivist GT"; **Charmaz's** reformulation moves GT into the **constructivist** band [2][3]. The further postmodern extension is **Adele Clarke's *Situational Analysis: Grounded Theory After the Postmodern Turn* (2005)**, which adds situational/relational/positional **maps**, redefines the "situation" to absorb what was treated as "context," and deliberately includes "missing or otherwise silenced data" — pushing constructivist GT toward the postmodern/critical end of the spectrum [8].

## Later editions that complicate the comparison

The clean three-way contrast is muddied by successive revisions. **Strauss & Corbin's *Basics* 2nd ed. (1998)** continued the systematic line; after Strauss's death (1996), **Corbin & Strauss issued the 3rd ed. (2008) and 4th ed. (2015)**, in which Corbin softened the earlier objectivism and the rigid paradigm/axial-coding apparatus, moving toward a more interpretive stance — so "Straussian" GT is itself a moving target [2][5]. Charmaz issued a **2nd edition of *Constructing Grounded Theory* (2014)** that substantively expanded the book — adding fuller treatment of coding (initial and focused coding) and intensive interviewing as "emergent interactions in which social bonds may develop" (2014, p.91), and folding in an explicit **social-justice inquiry** agenda: she "boldly asked (and answered)" whether knowledge should transform practice and social processes ("Yes"), whether grounded theory studies can contribute to a better world ("Yes"), and whether such questions should influence what and how we study ("Yes") (2014, pp.339–340) — a focus she increasingly aligned with her method across subsequent work [4]. Glaser, for his part, continued publishing through Sociology Press (*Doing Grounded Theory*, 1998; later works), maintaining the classic line [2][4].

## Dominance in contemporary training (2020–2024)

The contemporary field standardizes on a **tripartite taxonomy** — Classic/Glaserian, Straussian (Corbin & Strauss "systematic design"), and Constructivist (Charmaz) — across methodology guidance and reporting guidelines (e.g., GUREGT) [6][5]. Two distinct senses of "dominance" emerge from the recent literature:

- **Most cited / fastest growing school: Charmaz's constructivist GT.** A 2025 *New Trends in Qualitative Research* article states flatly that "Grounded theory is the most cited qualitative methodology, and its popularity is continually increasing through researchers' engagement with its constructivist school," and notes cross-school collaboration via the International Association of Grounded Theorists (conferences 2021, 2024) [4]. Charmaz (2006/2014) is the reference point for the interpretive turn in GT training [4].
- **Most frequently *applied* procedure: the Strauss–Corbin / Corbin–Strauss systematic design.** A 2023 systematic review concludes that "in Grounded Theory research, the systematic design is favoured above other designs," precisely because its explicit coding stages give novices a usable, step-by-step procedure [5].

The reconciliation is that the **constructivist (Charmaz) paradigm dominates the epistemological framing and citation profile of methodology journals and PhD training**, while the **Strauss–Corbin systematic procedure dominates as the hands-on coding workflow** in applied and student research — a division between the cited *philosophy* and the practiced *method* [5][4]. The Glaserian classic line, which "attempts to study social reality without preset classifications," is the smallest of the three strands in contemporary applied use [5]. Discipline-specific audits confirm the multi-version landscape: a 2023 study of three high-impact LIS journals (*Journal of Documentation*, *JASIST*, *Library & Information Science Research*) and Rieger's widely-cited (94 citations) *Nursing Inquiry* comparison both treat all three strands as live options researchers must choose among [6][9].
