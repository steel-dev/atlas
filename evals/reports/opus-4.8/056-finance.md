## ClearPoint Neuro (CLPT) — Financial Sustainability Through the Biologics/Drug-Delivery Transition (Q3 / 9M 2024)

**Bottom line.** ClearPoint entered Q4 2024 self-funded for its transition: nine-month operating cash burn of $7.7M and essentially zero net investing outflow produced free cash flow of roughly **–$7.7M**, against a **$21.6M** cash balance and **no debt** after the August repayment. The average ~$2.6M/quarter burn overstates the current run-rate — Q3 operational burn had fallen to **$1.2M**, a 33% YoY reduction — so on the trailing trajectory the company has multiple years of runway and management asserts at least twelve months. The business model evolution is showing up cleanly in the numbers: total revenue grew **41%** to $8.1M with the fastest-scaling segment being capital equipment (+133%), gross margin recovered to **60%** (from 57% a year earlier), and the equity-funded, debt-free balance sheet reflects a deliberate shift from leveraged to equity financing during a period of improving capital efficiency.

Free cash flow effectively equals the operating burn because investing activity was nominal — only $12K of equipment purchases, versus $9.1M of *inflows* in 9M 2023 when short-term investments matured ($10.0M) and the Carlsbad, California manufacturing site was being equipped [1]. That makes 2024 a cleaner read of underlying cash consumption, with no investment maturities flattering the figure.

**Burn rate.** Averaging the nine-month operating outflow gives ~**$2.57M per quarter** ($7,707K ÷ 3). But this is a declining series: management reported Q3 2024 operational cash burn of just **$1.2M**, down 33% YoY [2], implying the first-half quarters carried most of the consumption and the exit run-rate is roughly half the year's average. Runway is therefore wide and direction-dependent:

- At the 9M average (~$2.6M/qtr): ~8 quarters (~2 years) of cash.
- At the Q3 run-rate (~$1.2M/qtr): ~18 quarters of cash.
- Management's own statement: existing cash sufficient for **at least the next twelve months** [1].

**Capital efficiency and financing implications.** The pattern shows improving capital efficiency: revenue up 41% while quarterly burn fell by a third, and the full-year 2023 operating use of cash ($13.7M) is being roughly halved on an annualized basis in 2024 [1]. The March equity raise was the funding event of the year; the August repayment then *consumed* $10M of that cushion to extinguish leverage. Management chose balance-sheet de-risking (no debt, no covenants, collateral released) over hoarding the cash — a defensible trade given the reduced burn, but one that means the next major capital need will likely be met with equity rather than debt, exposing shareholders to further dilution if biologics partners' trials slip or activation-driven growth stalls. With burn trending toward ~$5M annualized and $21.6M on hand, ClearPoint does not face a near-term financing cliff, but it is not yet self-sustaining and will need either continued operating leverage or another raise before cash flow turns positive.

### March 2024 equity raise and August 2024 debt repayment

- **Equity (March 2024):** Public offering of **2,653,848 shares** — 2,307,694 base shares at **$6.50/share** plus 346,154 over-allotment shares at the same price — for net proceeds of **~$16.2M** ($16,183K) after underwriting discounts and expenses [1].
- **Debt (August 23, 2024):** Full repayment of the remaining **First Closing Note**, a 2020 senior secured convertible note under the 2020 securities purchase agreement: **$10M principal + ~$0.1M accrued interest**. The noteholder waived prepayment prohibitions and **all collateral was released**, leaving ClearPoint with **no outstanding debt** at quarter-end [1]. The original SPA raised $25M gross from two investors, of which $15M had previously converted to common stock [1].

|---|---|---|---|---|
| Biologics & Drug Delivery | $4.4M | $3.5M | **+27%** | Largest (~54% of revenue) |
| Neurosurgery Navigation & Therapy (disposables) | $2.9M | $1.9M | **+49%** | Mid-scale |
| Capital Equipment & Software | $0.8M | $0.4M | **+133%** | Smallest base |
| **Total** | **$8.1M** | **$5.8M** | **+41%** | — |

Source: [2]. Within biologics, a $1.6M increase in product (disposables) revenue as partners progress in trials was partially offset by a $0.7M decline in service/other revenue from fewer pre-clinical studies. Navigation/therapy growth was driven by newly activated accounts, higher case counts, and new products (SmartFrame OR, Prism Laser Therapy). Capital equipment growth reflects more placements of navigation/software and Prism laser units, with five new global centers activated in Q3 and **19 new centers YTD — roughly three times the historic activation rate** [2]. Overall product revenue (including biologics) more than doubled, growing 127% to $5.5M, while navigation/device revenue set a record at $2.9M [2].

**Strongest momentum — verdict.** On pure growth rate, **capital equipment & software (+133%)** is the standout, but off a $0.8M base it is not yet material to the top line. The more telling signal is the **record activation cadence** it represents: each capital placement seeds future recurring disposable (navigation) and biologics revenue, so it is the leading indicator of the franchise's forward momentum. **Navigation & Therapy (+49% to $2.9M)** is the strongest performer *at meaningful scale*. The verdict: capital equipment carries the strongest **strategic/forward** momentum (the installed base that drives everything downstream), while navigation/therapy is the strongest momentum *contributing today*. Biologics, though the largest segment, grew the slowest (+27%) and is the most volatile, since its services line swings with partners' trial timing.

### Gross margin trajectory

| Period | Gross margin | Change | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Q3 2024 vs Q3 2023 | 60% vs 57% | **+3 pts** | [2] |
| 9M 2024 vs 9M 2023 | 61% vs 56% | **+5 pts** | [1] |
| 6M 2023 vs 6M 2022 | 56% vs 63% | **–7 pts** | [3] |

The two windows tell opposite stories that together explain the transition's margin arc. The **2022→2023 first-half compression (–7 pts, to 56%)** was driven by a higher mix of lower-margin biologics/drug-delivery pre-clinical *services* and by the elevated costs of standing up the new Carlsbad, California manufacturing facility (lease commenced June 1, 2023, with the prior Irvine facility terminated in October 2023) [3][1]. The **Q3 2024 recovery (+3 pts, to 60%)** indicates the facility transition is now contributing operating leverage rather than absorbing cost, and that the mix is tilting back toward higher-margin product (disposables) revenue as partners move from low-margin studies into commercial trial volume.

### Strategic positioning and operational scalability

**What the divergent segment performance reveals.** The split between a fast-scaling, low-margin-services-heavy biologics line and a hyper-growth-but-tiny capital-equipment line maps directly onto ClearPoint's stated four-pillar, multi-vector strategy. The company is deliberately using the **device/capital business as the wedge** — record activations expand the installed base and the operating-room footprint (SmartFrame OR) — while the **biologics/drug-delivery business is the long-dated option**, dependent on partners' cell- and gene-therapy trials reaching commercial success. The contrast shows ClearPoint is not betting on a single revenue engine: capital placements de-risk near-term recurring revenue, while biologics provides the larger but partner-dependent upside. The slower 27% biologics growth, with services declining as fewer pre-clinical studies run, also exposes the core risk of the transition — biologics revenue is gated by external partners' timelines and FDA programs, not solely by ClearPoint's own commercial execution.

**Manufacturing transitions, volume, and future profitability.** The Carlsbad facility build-out was the cost that depressed 2022→2023 margins; with that transition substantially behind it, rising disposable volume is now passing through at improving margin (the 57%→60% Q3 move). Because the incremental product is largely disposables sold into an expanding installed base of activated accounts, the model has favorable operating leverage: fixed manufacturing capacity is being filled by higher case counts and more partners' trial volume, so each new placement and each trial that advances should lift utilization and gross margin without proportional cost. The combination of **revenue +41%, gross margin +3 pts, operating expenses up only modestly ($8.2M→$10.0M, mainly personnel and product development, offset by bad-debt recoveries), and quarterly burn down 33%** is the empirical signature of that scalability beginning to take hold [2]. The constraint on reaching profitability is no longer facility cost but volume — specifically whether activation momentum and biologics-partner trial progression continue at the current pace to fill the now-built capacity.
