# Acceptance and Cure of the Legal-Size Paper Delivery: A UCC Analysis on Summary Judgment

## Question Presented

On October 15, Buyer issued a purchase order specifying letter-size paper; on November 8, Vendor delivered 500 reams of "standard office paper," all in legal size. Buyer's office manager accepted delivery and stored the reams. Over the next three weeks, staff consumed 180 reams for internal drafts and overflow work (while complaining about reformatting), and Buyer printed its standard letterhead and logo on the remaining 320 reams. On November 30, Buyer demanded its money back and refused Vendor's offer to exchange remaining stock for letter size within five business days. The issue is whether, applying UCC acceptance and cure principles, Buyer rightfully rejected the goods or must accept them subject to Vendor's proposed cure — and how this resolves on summary judgment.

The short answer: Buyer almost certainly **cannot establish a rightful rejection** as to the 320 imprinted reams, and likely cannot as to the 180 consumed reams either, because both sets of acts are inconsistent with Vendor's ownership and therefore constitute acceptance. But Buyer is **not relegated to "accept subject to cure"** in the way Vendor frames it: once the goods were accepted (and altered), Vendor's late exchange offer is largely beside the point, and acceptance does not extinguish Buyer's residual damages claim — though Buyer's inability to quantify its losses cripples that claim. A court could grant Vendor partial summary judgment that acceptance occurred (especially as to the 320 imprinted reams), while genuine fact disputes over the meaning of "standard office paper" and over damages may survive to trial.

## Governing Framework

### Perfect tender, not substantial performance

The transaction is a sale of goods governed by UCC Article 2. The common-law doctrine of **substantial performance** — under which a party who substantially though not perfectly meets the contract's requirements avoids breach — does **not** apply to sales of goods; the UCC instead imposes the **perfect tender rule** [1]. Under § 2-601, if the goods "fail in any respect to conform to the contract," the buyer may (a) reject the whole, (b) accept the whole, or (c) accept any commercial unit and reject the rest, subject to the installment-contract exception and contractual limitations of remedy. This forecloses Vendor's core argument that legal-size paper is "equivalent" and therefore conforming. Equivalent commercial value is a substantial-performance concept; it is not a defense to nonconformity under the perfect tender rule [1].

That said, the perfect tender rule is "subject to" the rules on acceptance, cure, and the like — so identifying nonconformity is only the first step. The dispositive questions are whether Buyer **accepted** and whether Vendor could **cure**.

### Did the goods conform? The meaning of "standard office paper"

Conformity is measured against the contract terms. The October 15 purchase order specified **letter size**; the contract body recited "standard office paper" without dimensions. Under § 2-202, that written term may be "explained or supplemented" by course of dealing or usage of trade, and under § 1-303 a usage of trade — a practice of such regularity as to justify an expectation it will be observed — is relevant to the meaning of the agreement [2]. Crucially, however, § 1-303 requires that usage of trade "be proved as facts," and **express terms prevail over usage of trade when the two are inconsistent**. Here the purchase order's express "letter size" specification controls over any vaguer trade meaning of "standard office paper," so legal-size delivery is nonconforming. To the extent the parties dispute whether usage of trade made legal size "standard," that is a fact question — but it does not rescue Vendor, because the express letter-size term governs [2].

## Acceptance

### What constitutes acceptance

Under § 2-606(1), acceptance occurs when the buyer, after a reasonable opportunity to inspect, (a) signifies that the goods conform or that it will retain them despite nonconformity; (b) fails to make an effective rejection; or (c) **"does any act inconsistent with the seller's ownership"** [3]. Acceptance of part of any commercial unit is acceptance of the entire unit (§ 2-606(2)) [3]. A rejection, by contrast, is effective only if made within a reasonable time after delivery and with seasonable notice to the seller (§ 2-602(1)) [4].

The consequences of acceptance are stiff. Under § 2-607: the buyer must pay the contract rate for accepted goods; acceptance "precludes rejection of the goods accepted"; and the **burden is on the buyer** to establish any breach as to accepted goods [5].

### The 320 imprinted reams — acceptance as a matter of law

Printing the company's permanent letterhead and logo on the 320 remaining reams is the strongest fact in the case. It is squarely an "act inconsistent with the seller's ownership" under § 2-606(1)(c) [3], and it maps closely onto the leading installation cases:

- In **Cervitor Kitchens, Inc. v. Chapman**, 82 Wn.2d 673, 513 P.2d 25 (Wash. 1973), the buyer's installation of kitchen units was held inconsistent with the seller's ownership and thus an acceptance under § 2-606(1)(c), obligating the buyer to pay the contract price under § 2-607(1). The court held that "[i]f [the buyer] was to reject [the units] effectively, he had to do so before installation," and that installation of goods whose defects were readily observable was an acceptance **as a matter of law** [6].
- Cervitor relied on **United States v. Crawford**, 443 F.2d 611 (5th Cir. 1971) (installing fuel filter/separator units precluded revocation), and **Park County Implement Co. v. Craig**, 397 P.2d 800 (Wyo. 1964) (buyers accepted a truck chassis when they began installing a hoist and dump bed) [6].

Imprinting letterhead is analytically indistinguishable from installation: it physically and permanently appropriates the goods to Buyer's own use, dedicating them in a way the seller could never undo. The legal-versus-letter discrepancy was obvious on delivery — just as the Cervitor defects were "readily observable" before installation — so Buyer cannot characterize the printing as part of a reasonable inspection [6]. As to the 320 reams, acceptance is established, Buyer must pay the contract rate (§ 2-607(1)), and rejection is precluded (§ 2-607(2)) [5].

Revocation of acceptance under § 2-608 is also unavailable for these reams. Revocation must occur before any "substantial change in condition of the goods which is not caused by their own defects" [7]. Imprinting letterhead is exactly such a buyer-caused substantial change, which independently bars revocation [7].

### The 180 consumed reams — likely acceptance, but more fact-dependent

The 180 reams used over three weeks for drafts and overflow present a closer question, governed by § 2-606(1)(a) and (c). Use of goods can both exhaust the "reasonable opportunity to inspect" window (after which retention signifies acceptance) and itself be an act inconsistent with the seller's ownership [3]. Two features point strongly to acceptance: the nonconformity was apparent immediately (legal vs. letter is visible at a glance), and Buyer engaged in **consumptive** use of a substantial quantity over three weeks rather than a brief test.

The counterweight is that "a reasonable time for inspection of goods … is a question of fact if the facts are disputed," and only becomes a question of law where the facts are undisputed — the rule of **La Villa Fair v. Lewis Carpet Mills**, 219 Kan. 395, 548 P.2d 825 (Kan. 1976), where a nine-month inspection delay did *not* amount to acceptance given the specific storage-and-resale context [8]. La Villa Fair cautions that timing-based acceptance is context-sensitive. But its facts are far afield: there the goods were warehoused untouched awaiting the buyer's own purchaser, and the defect was latent. Here Buyer actively consumed the goods knowing of the size difference, which looks like retention "in spite of … non-conformity" under § 2-606(1)(a) rather than inspection [3]. On these facts a court would likely find acceptance of the 180 reams as well, though a judge applying La Villa Fair could treat "reasonable time" as a triable issue if Buyer genuinely disputes it [8].

### The complaints do not preserve rejection

Employee grumbling about reformatting is not a rejection. Rejection requires seasonable notice to the **seller** within a reasonable time (§ 2-602(1)) [4]; internal complaints are not notice to Vendor. And even a purported rejection would not help, because after rejection "any exercise of ownership by the buyer … is wrongful as against the seller" (§ 2-602(2)(a)) [4] — so continuing to use and imprint the reams would have been wrongful, not a vindication of rejection rights.

## Cure

Vendor's November 30 offer to exchange the remaining stock for letter size within five business days is a cure attempt under § 2-508. Because the time for performance had passed (the single November 8 delivery was the contract performance), only § 2-508(2) is available: where a buyer rejects a nonconforming tender that **"the seller had reasonable grounds to believe would be acceptable with or without money allowance,"** the seller, on seasonable notice, gets a **further reasonable time** to substitute a conforming tender [9]. (Section 2-508(1)'s within-the-contract-time cure does not apply because the performance time had expired [9].)

The cure offer fails for a more fundamental reason than timing: **there is little left to cure.** Cure under § 2-508 presupposes goods that were rejected (or are still rejectable). Here:

- The 320 imprinted reams are accepted and permanently altered; an "exchange for letter size" cannot restore them, and acceptance precludes rejection (§ 2-607(2)) [5]. They cannot be returned in original condition.
- The 180 consumed reams are gone.
- Vendor's offer to swap "remaining stock" therefore reaches goods that are already accepted/altered, not goods awaiting a conforming substitute.

Two further points cut against Vendor's cure theory. First, whether Vendor "had reasonable grounds to believe" legal size would be acceptable — the statutory predicate for § 2-508(2) cure — is itself a fact question; the express letter-size purchase order makes that belief hard to sustain [9]. Second, even a valid cure right operates only on rejected goods; it does not reverse an acceptance already worked by acts inconsistent with ownership. The net effect is not that Buyer "must accept subject to cure," but that Buyer **already accepted**, and the cure question is largely moot for the imprinted and consumed reams.

## Acceptance Does Not Bar a Damages Claim — But Buyer's Damages Are Unproven

Acceptance precludes rejection; it does not, by itself, impair other remedies for nonconformity (§ 2-607(2)) [5], and the installation cases recognize that acceptance leaves a damages action intact [6]. To preserve that claim, however, Buyer "must within a reasonable time after he discovers … any breach notify the seller of breach or be barred from any remedy," and Buyer carries the **burden** to establish the breach as to accepted goods (§ 2-607(3)–(4)) [5]. Buyer's November 30 demand likely satisfies notice, but on damages Buyer "cannot quantify hours lost" — meaning that even if it proves nonconformity, its disruption/wasted-time theory may recover little or nothing. The proper posture is therefore an accepted-goods damages claim (offset against the price), not rescission and return of payment.

## Summary Judgment Analysis

Summary judgment is proper only where "there is no genuine dispute as to any material fact and the movant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law" (Fed. R. Civ. P. 56(a)); the standard was unchanged by the 2010 amendment that substituted "dispute" for "issue," and a court draws reasonable inferences for the nonmovant and may not resolve credibility on paper [10]. Sorting the facts:

**Facts supporting judgment for Vendor as a matter of law:**
- Buyer's imprinting of letterhead on the 320 reams is undisputed and is an act inconsistent with Vendor's ownership → acceptance as a matter of law (Cervitor; § 2-606(1)(c)) [6][3].
- That acceptance triggers the price obligation and precludes rejection of those reams (§ 2-607(1)–(2)) and bars revocation given the substantial buyer-caused change in condition (§ 2-608(2)) [5][7].

**Disputed material facts that could preclude full summary judgment:**
| Issue | Why it may be a jury question | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning of "standard office paper" / whether usage of trade made legal size conforming | Usage of trade must be "proved as facts" under § 1-303; § 2-202 allows explanation by trade usage — but express "letter size" term in the PO controls | [2] |
| Whether the 180 reams' three-week use exceeded a "reasonable time to inspect" | "Reasonable time" is a fact question if facts are disputed | [8] |
| Whether Vendor "had reasonable grounds to believe" legal size acceptable (§ 2-508(2) predicate) | Fact-dependent; undermined by express PO term | [9] |
| Amount of Buyer's damages | Buyer cannot quantify hours lost; burden on Buyer | [5] |

**Facts that are largely irrelevant to the dispositive issues:**
- Vendor's incomplete delivery records and missing packing slips concern **four other accounts**, not this transaction. Delivery of the 500 reams to Buyer is admitted, so the missing slips create no genuine dispute on any material fact here [10].
- Employee complaints are not notice to the seller and do not constitute or preserve rejection (§ 2-602(1)) [4].

**Disposition.** A court is well positioned to grant Vendor **partial summary judgment** that Buyer accepted at least the 320 imprinted reams and must pay the contract rate for them, with rejection and revocation foreclosed [6][5][7]. Acceptance of the 180 consumed reams is highly likely on these facts but is more vulnerable to a La Villa Fair "reasonable time" argument and could be left for trial if genuinely disputed [8]. What survives is not Buyer's rescission-and-refund demand but, at most, a residual accepted-goods damages claim that Buyer must prove — and presently cannot quantify [5].

## Bottom Line

- **Conformity:** Legal size is nonconforming; the express letter-size purchase-order term controls over the vague "standard office paper" phrase, and the perfect tender rule — not substantial performance — applies, so Vendor's "equivalent value" argument fails [1][2].
- **Acceptance:** Imprinting letterhead on 320 reams is acceptance as a matter of law (act inconsistent with ownership); three weeks of consumptive use of 180 reams is very likely acceptance too [3][6][8].
- **Cure:** Vendor's five-business-day exchange offer cannot resurrect a rejection right Buyer never effectively exercised and cannot "cure" goods already accepted and permanently altered; § 2-508(2)'s predicate is also doubtful given the express PO term [9].
- **Remedy:** Buyer cannot rightfully reject or get its money back; it is limited to an accepted-goods damages claim it bears the burden to prove and has not quantified [5].
- **Summary judgment:** Vendor should win at least partial summary judgment on acceptance of the imprinted reams; conformity/usage-of-trade and damages questions, and possibly the timing of the 180-ream use, may remain for trial [10].
