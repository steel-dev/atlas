# Industrial Automation for Manufacturing in Saudi Arabia, 2015–2025

## Bottom line

Saudi Arabia is a **small but fast-growing, early-maturity** industrial-automation market. Independent estimates put the Saudi industrial-automation market at roughly **USD 1.0–2.0 billion in 2024/2025**, growing at a **~7.9%–10.4% CAGR**, with the higher figure (Data Bridge) covering broad "industrial automation" and the lower (IMARC) covering narrower "smart factory automation" [1][2]. Robotics penetration is the market's defining gap: the IFR does not publish a standalone Saudi robot-density figure (Saudi installations are too few to disclose under IFR compliance rules), and the **global average is 132 robots per 10,000 manufacturing employees in 2024** [3][4] — a benchmark Saudi manufacturing is well below. Vision 2030, chiefly through the **National Industrial Development and Logistics Program (NIDLP)** and its goal to enable **SAR 1.4 trillion of private-sector investment by 2030**, plus the **Future Factories** initiative at the Ministry of Industry & Mineral Resources, is the principal demand driver [5][6]. On giga-projects, the headline Siemens and ABB awards retrieved are **not factory/manufacturing robotics**: Siemens' NEOM work is **automated-crane/port logistics automation** at Oxagon's Port of NEOM [7], and ABB's Red Sea Global work is **electrification/EV charging**, not manufacturing automation [8].

## 1. Market size, CAGR, and robotics penetration

### Market size and growth — reconciling the estimates

There is no single official Saudi "industrial automation for manufacturing" figure; estimates vary by definitional scope (industrial automation vs. factory/smart-factory automation vs. robotics-only). The two Saudi-specific estimates retrieved:

| Source | Scope | Recent value | Projection | CAGR | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Data Bridge Market Research [1] | Industrial automation | USD 1.99 bn (2024) | USD 3.72 bn (2032) | 10.40% | 2025–2032 |
| IMARC Group [2] | Smart factory automation | USD 1,005.1 mn (2025) | USD 1,990.1 mn (2034) | 7.89% | 2026–2034 |

For regional context, Mordor Intelligence sizes the **Middle East & Africa industrial automation market at USD 5.28 bn (2025), reaching USD 7.44 bn by 2030 at a 7.10% CAGR** (study period 2019–2030), and reports a separate MEA factory-automation/industrial-controls CAGR of **6.80%** for 2025–2030. Saudi Arabia is the largest single national sub-market within that MEA total.

**Reconciliation:** The roughly 2× gap between Data Bridge (USD 1.99 bn) and IMARC (USD 1.0 bn) is mostly scope: "industrial automation" includes process-control systems (PLC/DCS/SCADA), field instrumentation/sensors, and drives across process industries (oil, gas, chemicals, utilities), whereas "smart factory automation" is narrower discrete/Industry-4.0 factory technology. Both forecast a **high-single-to-low-double-digit CAGR (~8–10%)**, well above the MEA regional average — consistent with Saudi Arabia being the diversification engine of the region. **A clean 2015 USD baseline is not published in the retrieved sources.** The earliest available indicative reference points are Data Bridge's chart starting in **2018** and IMARC's historical series starting in **2020** (both labelled indicative for the pre-2018 period, since neither publishes a 2015 value); both nonetheless attribute the growth trajectory directly to Vision 2030/NIDLP [1][2].

### Robotics penetration (robot density)

The question's core penetration metric — installed industrial robots per 10,000 manufacturing workers — **is not separately published for Saudi Arabia by the IFR**. The IFR World Robotics database lists Saudi Arabia (coded **SA-Saudi Arabia**) as a surveyed economy within the Asia / "Rest of Asia" grouping (alongside Iran, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar and the UAE), confirmed in both the WR2021 data-availability chart (columns 1993–2020) and the WR2025 database (1993–2024) [9]. However, **no Saudi-specific robot-density figure (robots per 10,000 manufacturing workers) is published by the IFR**, and no standalone Saudi annual-install or operational-stock number appears in the free executive summary or press release. Saudi data points fall into the IFR's coded category "surveyed, but no or very few installations reported (data usually hidden due to compliance rules from 2014 onwards)" [9] — meaning the metric is a documented dead-end in the public dataset (it requires the paid WR2025 database), and the same suppression applied across the WR2021 baseline period, indicating consistently low reported installation volumes rather than a recent decline. **Indicatively**, suppression under the "very few installations" rule places Saudi robot density far below the 132-per-10,000 global average rather than at any disclosed value.

What the IFR data does establish are the benchmarks against which the Saudi gap should be read (2024 data):

| Robot density benchmark (robots / 10,000 mfg employees, 2024) | Value |
|---|---|
| **Global average** | **132** [3] |
| Western Europe | 267 [3] |
| EU-27 | 231 [3] |
| North America | 204 [3] |
| Asia | 131 [3] |
| United States (8th worldwide) | 307 [3] |
| South Korea (#1) | 1,220 [3] |
| Singapore (#2) | 818 [3] |

Two IFR World Robotics 2025 documents report figures on slightly different bases and the two should not be conflated: the **robot-density press release [3]** gives a **global average of 132**, with Western Europe 267, North America 204, and Asia 131; the **executive summary [4]** reports a **global manufacturing-industry average of 177**, with Asia at 204 units (+12% CAGR 2019–2024), Europe 148, and the Americas 131. Either way, Saudi Arabia sits far below the global average.

Global installation context: **542,076 industrial robots were installed worldwide in 2024** (second-highest ever; 2022 record 552,946; 2023 541,302), with **Asia taking 74% of deployments** and China alone 54% (295,000 units, ~2 million operational stock) [4][3]. Saudi Arabia's installations are small enough to be suppressed in this dataset — itself the clearest evidence that **Saudi robot density sits far below the 132-unit global average**, marking an early-maturity market with a large penetration gap and, by extension, large headroom.

## 2. How Vision 2030 drives automation/robotics demand

Vision 2030's causal chain to automation demand runs through three reinforcing mechanisms — diversification away from oil, localization mandates, and giga-project build-out — operationalized mainly through the NIDLP and ministry-level industrial programs.

**National Industrial Development and Logistics Program (NIDLP)** — a Vision 2030 Realization Program launched in early 2019, covering four sectors (energy, mining, industry, logistics) and built on **two explicit pillars: Local Content and the Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR)** [5]. The 4IR pillar names "Automation and Robots," AI, IoT, additive manufacturing, smart sensors and flexible machines as its core technologies — i.e., automation demand is written directly into the program design [5].

**Quantified targets and progress (NIDLP Delivery Plan 2021–2025)** [5]:
- **Enable private-sector investment of SAR 1.4 trillion by 2030** (the headline investment target, spanning energy, mining, industry, logistics).
- Vision 2030 goal that **non-oil GDP roughly doubles by 2030**, requiring faster-than-average growth in NIDLP sectors.
- **SAR 9 billion** invested in developing industrial infrastructure; **9,800** existing/under-construction industrial factories (9.6% growth); **114** factories completed under the ready-factories initiative.
- 4IR transformation progress: **agreements signed for 22 factories**, with **4IR transformation plans implemented in 5+ factories**; the **National Productivity Program** (with the Saudi Industrial Cities Authority) targets productivity gains across **22 factories**.
- **National Digital Transformation Strategy** targets adoption of 4IR technologies across main sectors by **2025** [5].
- Mining is targeted as the "third pillar" of Saudi industry, with >SAR 100 billion of mineral-value-chain opportunity and a value chain that could add SAR 281 billion to GDP and 256,000 jobs [5].

**Localization (Local Content pillar).** NIDLP's Local Content track, modeled on the oil-and-gas **IKTVA** program, drove local market share in the oil & gas sector from roughly 25% (2015) up through 35%/37%/45%/52% to **56% by 2018** [5]. This localization logic — favoring suppliers that manufacture/integrate domestically — is the policy lever that most directly shapes how automation vendors must compete (see §4 and §5).

**Causal synthesis.** The mechanism is: (a) diversification away from oil forces the build-out of non-oil manufacturing (chemicals/plastics, food & beverage, metals, mining downstream), creating greenfield demand for process and discrete automation; (b) the SAR 1.4 trillion investment target and giga-projects (NEOM/Oxagon, Red Sea) fund that capacity at scale; and (c) localization mandates push that demand toward vendors willing to localize content and integration, while 4IR/Future Factories subsidies lower the adoption barrier for existing factories. Together these convert national policy into concrete automation and robotics procurement.

## 3. Regulatory and institutional changes supporting automation

- **Future Factories Program / Initiative — Ministry of Industry & Mineral Resources (MIM).** The "Main Digitization Track" is a sub-track that raises factory productivity, competitiveness and operational efficiency through digitization and automation solutions (digitizing procedures, planning and communication systems, production-line control, material handling/packaging) that "do not require large capital," with the Ministry covering part of the cost; stated objectives include enhanced performance follow-up, higher digital maturity, and job creation [6]. This is the primary government channel subsidizing automation uptake in existing Saudi factories.
- **Saudi Industrial Cities Authority (MODON) / industrial-cities framework.** NIDLP works through the Saudi Industrial Cities Authority on the National Productivity Program (22 factories) and the ready-factories ("plug and play") initiative, providing the physical and institutional base for automated plant deployment [5].
- **National Digital Transformation Strategy** (4IR adoption by 2025) and the **Tanafusiya** competitiveness program (with the Saudi Industrial Development Fund and KACST) round out the institutional support stack [5].

## 4. Siemens and ABB named giga-project awards since 2021 — and a scope correction

A central analytical finding: **the prominent Siemens and ABB engagements at NEOM and the Red Sea project are not manufacturing/factory robotics.** They are adjacent industrial automation (port/crane) and electrification work, respectively. A sales rep should not treat these as evidence of a mature factory-robotics procurement pipeline.

| Vendor | Project / contract | Location | Scope | Manufacturing-robotics? | Value / date | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Siemens** | Crane automation at **Port of NEOM** (with Mevea digital-twin & simulation) — 10 automated Ship-to-Shore (STS) gantry cranes + 30 automated electric Rubber-Tyred Gantry (RTG) cranes | Oxagon, NEOM | Port/logistics automation, digital twin, remote crane control | **No** — port-logistics automation, not factory robotics | Announced **July 22, 2024**; contract value not disclosed | [7] |
| **ABB** | **Red Sea Global** sustainable-tourism development — electrification, including an extensive EV-charging infrastructure | Red Sea Project | Electrification / power distribution / EV charging | **No** — electrification, not manufacturing automation | Story dated **Feb 10, 2025**; value not disclosed; resort opens 2030 | [8] |

**Scope verification.** Siemens' NEOM role is genuinely industrial automation (digital-twin-driven autonomous cranes), but it sits in port/terminal operations at Oxagon, not in a manufacturing line; the primary trade source dates the announcement **July 22, 2024** and discloses no contract value [7]. ABB's Red Sea role is power/electrification for a tourism development — outside manufacturing automation entirely [8]. Neither vendor's giga-project award is a factory-robotics contract, and the contract values are not publicly disclosed in these sources. **Beyond the Red Sea Global electrification engagement, no other named ABB contract or award at NEOM/Oxagon or the Red Sea project since 2021 is documented** — NEOM/Oxagon industrial procurement disclosures (e.g., the green-hydrogen and Oxagon MoU announcements) name no ABB factory or automation award [10]. The broader Oxagon thesis — an Industry-4.0 "reimagined industrial city" embedding robotics, automation, AI and IoT, and home to the **USD 8.4 billion** NEOM Green Hydrogen plant (ACWA Power / Air Products / NEOM JV) — signals where future factory-automation demand will concentrate, but that is forward-looking pipeline rather than an awarded manufacturing-robotics contract [7].

## 5. Local-content and procurement requirements a vendor must navigate

Automation vendors selling into Saudi manufacturing — especially giga-projects and government-linked buyers — must navigate localization rules rooted in NIDLP's Local Content pillar and the IKTVA model that lifted oil-and-gas local content to 56% by 2018 [5]. In practice this means procurement scoring and preferences favor suppliers that localize content, manufacturing, and integration in-Kingdom; vendors that establish local assembly, service, and system-integration capacity are advantaged versus pure importers. (The specific current LCGPA/Local Content & Government Procurement Authority percentage thresholds are administered under this same localization framework; the program's direction — rising local-content requirements over time — is documented in the NIDLP plan [5].)

## 6. Three practical implications for a sales rep targeting Saudi manufacturing

1. **Sell the penetration gap, not a mature replacement cycle.** With Saudi robot density below the **132/10,000 global average** and installations small enough to be suppressed in IFR data [3][4], this is a greenfield-adoption market. Lead with first-time automation/Industry-4.0 packages and tie financing to the **Future Factories** cost-share program [6] and the **National Productivity Program** [5], rather than assuming buyers already run robot fleets.

2. **Localize to win — content, integration, and service in-Kingdom.** Procurement preferences flow from the NIDLP Local Content pillar / IKTVA model (oil & gas local content reached 56% by 2018) [5]. A vendor with local assembly, a local system-integrator network, and in-Kingdom service will outscore importers; build (or partner for) that footprint before bidding, especially on government-linked and giga-project work.

3. **Follow the SAR 1.4 trillion and the giga-projects, but qualify the scope.** The investment pipeline is real — **SAR 1.4 trillion private-sector target by 2030** [5], Oxagon's Industry-4.0 industrial city, and the **USD 8.4 bn** NEOM green-hydrogen plant [7]. But current marquee NEOM/Red Sea awards are port automation (Siemens) and electrification (ABB), not factory robotics [7][8]; target the downstream manufacturing tenants (chemicals/plastics, food & beverage, metals/mining downstream, hydrogen-adjacent industry) where discrete and process automation will actually be specified, and time engagement to Oxagon's manufacturing build-out.
