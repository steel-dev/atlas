# Autism Diagnostic Criteria in DSM-5 (2013) vs. ICD-11 (2019): Criteria, Sensory Weighting, Evidence, Guidelines, Advocacy, Safety, and Coding

## Overview

DSM-5 (American Psychiatric Association, 2013) and ICD-11 (WHO; released June 2018, adopted by the World Health Assembly in 2019, in force since 1 January 2022) converged on a single, dimensional "autism spectrum disorder" category built on **two core symptom domains**: persistent social-communication/interaction deficits and restricted, repetitive, inflexible patterns of behaviour, interests, or activities (RRBs). Critically for this question, **both manuals now place sensory atypicalities inside the RRB domain as one of several optional example features — neither treats sensory difference as a separate, mandatory criterion.** DSM-5 was the first manual to elevate sensory symptoms to diagnostic-criterion status (item B4); ICD-11 followed, marking a change from ICD-10, where sensory processing was not a core feature [1][2][3].

## (A) Side-by-Side Criteria Table

| Dimension | DSM-5 (2013), code 299.00 / F84.0 | ICD-11 (2019), code 6A02 |
|---|---|---|
| **Core social-communication criteria** | Domain A: persistent deficits in social communication and social interaction across multiple contexts — **all 3 sub-criteria required**: (1) social-emotional reciprocity; (2) nonverbal communicative behaviors; (3) developing/maintaining/understanding relationships [4] | Persistent deficits in the ability to **initiate and sustain reciprocal social interaction and social communication** [2] |
| **Restricted/repetitive domain** | Domain B: **≥2 of 4**: (B1) stereotyped/repetitive motor movements, use of objects, or speech; (B2) insistence on sameness/inflexible routines; (B3) highly restricted, fixated interests; (B4) **sensory** [4] | "A range of restricted, repetitive, and inflexible patterns of behaviour, interests or activities" atypical/excessive for age and sociocultural context; example features listed include repetitive motor movements, special interests/attachments, ritualized behaviour, and **sensory** [2] |
| **Sensory processing mention / weighting** | **Item B4**: "Hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory input or unusual interest in sensory aspects of the environment (e.g., apparent indifference to pain/temperature, adverse response to specific sounds or textures, excessive smelling or touching of objects, visual fascination with lights or movement)." One of four RRB items; **new in DSM-5** (absent from DSM-IV) [4][3] | Listed as one RRB example feature: "lifelong excessive and persistent **hypersensitivity or hyposensitivity to sensory stimuli** or unusual interest in a sensory stimulus, which may include … sounds, light, textures (especially clothing and food), odours and tastes, heat, cold or pain." New relative to ICD-10 [2][1] |
| **Severity specifiers** | **Three levels, rated separately for each domain**: Level 1 "Requiring support," Level 2 "Requiring substantial support," Level 3 "Requiring very substantial support." Plus specifiers: with/without intellectual impairment; with/without language impairment; associated medical/genetic condition [4] | **No 3-level support scale.** Qualifiers describe subtype by **intellectual development AND functional language**: 6A02.0 (no ID, mild/no language impairment), 6A02.1 (ID, mild/no language impairment), 6A02.2 (no ID, impaired language), 6A02.3 (ID, impaired language), 6A02.5 (ID, absence of functional language) [1][5] |
| **Age / onset requirement** | "Symptoms must be present in the **early developmental period** (but may not become fully manifest until social demands exceed limited capacities, or may be masked by learned strategies in later life)" [4] | Onset "occurs **during the developmental period – typically in early childhood** – but characteristic symptoms may not become fully manifest until later, when social demands exceed limited capacities" [2] |
| **Functional impairment threshold** | "Symptoms cause **clinically significant impairment** in social, occupational, or other important areas of current functioning" [4] | "Symptoms result in **significant impairment in personal, family, social, educational, occupational** or other important areas of functioning"; diagnosis still appropriate where exceptional effort masks deficits [2] |

**Analytical reading of the table.** The two manuals are substantively aligned on the two-domain structure, onset wording, and impairment requirement. The principal divergences are: (i) **severity** — DSM-5's behaviourally framed three-tier "support" scale vs. ICD-11's descriptive intellectual-development/functional-language qualifiers [4][1]; and (ii) DSM-5's more explicit, enumerated sub-criterion structure (3-of-3 social + 2-of-4 RRB) vs. ICD-11's narrative "essential features" format [4][2].

### How each manual *weights* sensory differences

- **DSM-5 (2013)** was the first edition to make sensory symptoms a **diagnostic criterion** (B4), reflecting recognition that sensory hyper-/hyporeactivity is integral to autism rather than an associated feature [4][3]. The AAP explicitly notes that "the recognition of symptoms of ASD related to sensory processing led to the inclusion of sensory symptoms … in the DSM-5" [3].
- **ICD-11** subsequently added sensory atypicality to the RRB domain — a change from ICD-10, "where unusual sensory processing was not yet considered a core (diagnostic) feature" [1].
- **Weighting is equivalent in both manuals**: in each, sensory difference is **one optional manifestation within the RRB domain, not a stand-alone or mandatory requirement.** Under DSM-5 a person can meet criteria without any sensory symptom (B4 is one of four, two of which suffice); the same holds in ICD-11, where it is one example feature among several [4][2]. Neither manual recognizes a free-standing "Sensory Processing Disorder," and the AAP states plainly that "the DSM-5 does not include sensory processing disorder as a discrete diagnosis" [3].

## (B) Evidence Synthesis: DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 Diagnostic Accuracy / Utility

**Important coverage caveat.** The published quantitative literature overwhelmingly compares **DSM-5 against DSM-IV/DSM-IV-TR**, not directly DSM-5 against ICD-11. Despite targeted searching, no peer-reviewed systematic review or meta-analysis reporting pooled sensitivity/specificity for **DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 head-to-head** was located; this is a genuine gap in the evidence base as of this review. The strongest available quantitative syntheses (Kulage 2014, 2019) quantify how the move to a unified two-domain DSM-5 model — the same structural shift ICD-11 later adopted — changes diagnosis. The Greaves-Lord et al. review is the best available direct conceptual/criterion-level DSM-5–ICD-11 comparison.

| Source | Design | Sample | Key statistics | GRADE quality* |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Kulage, Smaldone & Cohn 2014** (J Autism Dev Disord 44:1918–32) [6] | Systematic review + meta-analysis (DSM-5 vs DSM-IV-TR) | 418 studies screened, **14 included** | Pooled **decrease in ASD diagnosis 31% (95% CI 20–44), p=0.006**; Autistic disorder −22% (16–29, p<0.001); PDD-NOS −70% (55–82, p=0.01); Asperger's −70% (26–94, p=0.38, NS); reported study-level range 7.3–68.4% | **Moderate** (consistent direction, but high between-study heterogeneity and wide CIs) |
| **Kulage, Goldberg, Usseglio, Romero, Bain & Smaldone 2020** (J Autism Dev Disord 50:2102–27) [7] | 5-year follow-up systematic review + meta-analysis (DSM-5 vs DSM-IV-TR) | **33 included studies** | Pooled **decrease in ASD 20.8% (16.0–26.7), p<0.001**; Autistic Disorder −10.1% (6.2–16.0, p<0.001); Asperger's −23.3% (12.9–38.5, p=0.001); PDD-NOS −46.1% (34.6–58.0, p=0.52, NS). Only **28.8% (13.9–50.5, p=0.06)** of those losing an ASD diagnosis would qualify for Social Communication Disorder | **Moderate** (larger evidence base, narrower estimate than 2014, but still indirect to ICD-11) |
| **Greaves-Lord, Skuse & Mandy 2022** (Clinical Psychology in Europe 4(SI):e10005) [1] | Narrative/critical review of DSM-5 vs ICD-11 autism criteria (criterion-level comparison) | Conceptual (no diagnostic sample) | Documents that ICD-11 adds sensory atypicality to the diagnostic requirements (vs ICD-10) and is less ID-anchored than DSM-5; supports broad convergence of the two manuals | **Low–Moderate** (expert review; no primary accuracy data) |

\*GRADE ratings are this report's appraisal; **none of the source abstracts reported their own GRADE rating.** The Kulage syntheses are downgraded for indirectness (they measure DSM-5 vs DSM-IV, a proxy for the structural change ICD-11 also made) and heterogeneity; they are not direct DSM-5-vs-ICD-11 accuracy estimates.

**Synthesis.** Both meta-analyses converge on the finding that adopting a unified two-domain model **reduces the number of people meeting ASD criteria** relative to the older DSM-IV/ICD-10 fragmented categories, with the largest loss in the PDD-NOS/milder-presentation group and a substantial share of those displaced **not** captured by an alternative diagnosis [6][7]. Because ICD-11 made the same two-domain consolidation, similar (and arguably milder, given ICD-11's more flexible narrative criteria) attenuation would be expected — but direct ICD-11 sensitivity/specificity data are not available in the sources reviewed.

## (C) Clinical Guideline Positions

- **WHO** authored ICD-11 itself; the official *Clinical Descriptions and Diagnostic Requirements (CDDR)* (2024) sets out the 6A02 criteria, including the sensory example feature, and reflects WHO's position that sensory atypicality belongs within the RRB domain. ICD-11 has been in force for member-state reporting since 1 January 2022 [2].
- **APA** maintains DSM-5 (and DSM-5-TR) as the US clinical standard; sensory symptoms remain criterion B4 [4].
- **American Academy of Pediatrics (2020 clinical report, Hyman/Levy/Myers, *Pediatrics* 145(1):e20193447; reaffirmed Oct 2025)** operationalizes diagnosis on **DSM-5** criteria, describing the same two core domains and explicitly incorporating sensory symptoms. It does not adopt or endorse ICD-11 criteria (US practice uses DSM-5 with ICD-10-CM coding). On sensory weighting it is cautious about *intervention*: it states common sensory-based interventions (skin brushing, weighted vests, swinging) "are not yet supported in the peer-reviewed literature," and sensory integration therapy has only "modest" evidence — while affirming that sensory status assessment is part of diagnostic work-up [3].
- **NICE** (England) guidelines historically frame autism on **ICD-10/DSM** criteria (CG128 in under-19s; CG142 in adults), with CG142 recommending tools such as the AQ-10 for initial identification. The NICE guidance reviewed here does not specifically mandate ICD-11 adoption or assign special diagnostic weight to sensory processing as a stand-alone criterion . *(NICE source text on sensory weighting was not retrieved in full; treat the precise CG142/CG128 wording on ICD-11 adoption as not fully established here.)*

## (D) Autism Advocacy Organisation Positions on Neurodiversity-Affirmation

The available sourcing is strongest for **ASAN**; positions of the National Autistic Society and Autism Speaks were not retrieved in citable primary form and should be treated as **not established** here.

- **Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN):** Produced two policy briefs analysing DSM-5 autism criteria — *"What Are the Stakes? An Analysis of the Impact of the DSM-5 Draft Autism Criteria on Law, Policy and Service Provision"* and *"ASD in DSM-5: What the Research Shows and Recommendations for Change"* (2012, via ASAN's Program on Ethical, Legal and Social Implications of Autism Research, AIDD-funded). ASAN engaged **critically** with the DSM-5 narrowing of criteria, warning it could strip some autistic people of eligibility for services, supports, and accommodations, and recommending changes for better community representation [8]. The briefs frame DSM-5's consolidation as a risk to under-recognized presentations rather than as a neurodiversity gain.
- **National Autistic Society (UK)** and **Autism Speaks (US):** specific cited statements on which manual is "more neurodiversity-affirming" were **not located** in the retrieved sources. No claim should be asserted on their behalf without that sourcing.

**Analytical note:** The neurodiversity critique in the retrieved material centres less on DSM-5-vs-ICD-11 wording and more on the *narrowing/gatekeeping* effect both manuals' unified criteria can have, and on deficit-based language — concerns that apply to both manuals [8].

## (E) Diagnostic Safety Risks (with attention to sensory-dominant presentations)

- **Under-diagnosis / displaced cases from criteria tightening.** The unified two-domain model reduces ASD diagnoses by ~21–31% relative to older criteria, with the steepest loss among milder/PDD-NOS presentations; under DSM-5, only ~29% of those losing an ASD diagnosis would qualify for the alternative Social Communication Disorder, leaving a substantial group impaired but undiagnosed [6][7]. Since ICD-11 adopted the same consolidation, an analogous (if attenuated) risk applies.
- **Sensory "overshadowing."** WHO's CDDR explicitly warns that in preschool children "sensory sensitivities to everyday sounds, or to foods, may overshadow the underlying social communication deficits" — i.e., a prominent sensory presentation can mask the social-communication signs and lead to **misattribution or delayed diagnosis** [2].
- **No "Sensory Processing Disorder" category in either manual.** Because neither DSM-5 nor ICD-11 recognizes SPD as a discrete diagnosis [3], children whose presentation is dominated by sensory features but who do not meet full social-communication criteria may fall between categories — risking either inappropriate non-recognition or referral to unsupported sensory-specific therapies. The AAP notes sensory-based interventions and sensory integration therapy lack robust evidence, raising the risk of **inappropriate intervention** if sensory features are over-weighted clinically [3].
- **Behavioural mislabelling.** ICD-11 notes that "demand avoidant"/oppositional behaviour and aggressive outbursts in autistic individuals are often triggered by aversive sensory stimulation rather than defiance — a presentation that can be misdiagnosed as a disruptive behaviour disorder if the sensory driver is missed [2].

## (F) Reimbursement / Coding Summary

- **United States:** Clinical diagnosis uses **DSM-5** criteria, but billing/reimbursement uses **ICD-10-CM code F84.0** (Autistic disorder) — the operative claims code. US payers and the AAP-described pathway run on the DSM-5 + ICD-10-CM framework; the 2020 AAP report does not reference ICD-11 coding [3][4]. The US has **not** transitioned clinical billing to ICD-11.
- **ICD-11:** Autism maps to **code 6A02** (with .0–.5 subtype qualifiers) [1][5][2]. ICD-11 has been in force for WHO member-state morbidity/mortality reporting since 1 January 2022 [2], but adoption into national reimbursement systems lags.
- **EU / international:** ICD has long been the basis for European clinical coding; jurisdictions are at varying stages of ICD-11 implementation, generally still operating ICD-10/ICD-10-equivalent codes for reimbursement.
- **Coverage impact of the criteria change:** Because both manuals share the same two-domain core and ICD-11's narrative criteria are if anything *more* flexible, the criterion content itself is unlikely to be the decisive factor in coverage. The documented coverage risk arises instead from the **case-attenuation effect** — individuals displaced from an ASD diagnosis (and not captured by Social Communication Disorder) may lose access to diagnosis-contingent services and reimbursement, irrespective of which manual is used [6][7]. Specific, current (2026) payer-by-payer determinations on ICD-11 adoption and reimbursement were **not established** in the retrieved sources and should be verified against payer policy directly.

## Bottom line

DSM-5 and ICD-11 describe autism in closely parallel two-domain terms and **weight sensory differences identically — as one optional example feature within the restricted/repetitive-behaviour domain, not a mandatory or stand-alone criterion** [4][2]. DSM-5 was the historical first-mover in elevating sensory symptoms to criterion status (B4); ICD-11 followed, departing from ICD-10 [4][1][3]. The clearest divergence is structural: DSM-5's three-tier support-based severity scale vs. ICD-11's intellectual-development/functional-language qualifiers [4][1]. Direct head-to-head diagnostic-accuracy evidence for DSM-5 vs. ICD-11 is largely absent; the best available syntheses show the shared unified model reduces ASD diagnoses by roughly one-fifth to one-third, with under-diagnosis of milder/sensory-overshadowed presentations the dominant documented safety risk [6][7][2].
