## Drying performance — wool, cotton, synthetics (AS/NZS 2442)

The Australian energy label and minimum standards for clothes dryers run under **AS/NZS 2442.2** (energy performance); the Bosch sheet states its rating was determined on that standard's Cottons programme [1]. **Model-specific AS/NZS 2442.1 fabric-by-fabric drying-performance scores (wool, cotton, synthetics) are not publicly published for any of these three models** — the Australian standard's performance results are not carried on manufacturer spec sheets, and CHOICE's lab scores sit behind membership. In the absence of published per-fabric results, the best available proxies are **(i) CHOICE brand standing** (LG = CHOICE "Australia's Best Dryer Brand 2025" [2]) and **(ii) the sensor-dry and dedicated fabric programmes each model offers** — both *proxies only*, not measured drying-quality scores. Confirmed cycle features:

- **LG** — 13 programmes including **Cotton, Wool, Delicates**, Easy Care, Sportswear, Allergy Care, Mixed Fabric, Quick 30 and Rack Dry; sensor (Auto) dry levels Iron/Cupboard/Extra [2][3].
- **Samsung** — OptimalDry moisture-sensing across cotton/synthetics/delicate programmes [4].

All three are gentle heat-pump dryers (lower drum temperatures than vented/condenser-only units), suitable for wool and synthetics; the Bosch and LG both have a named **Wool** programme, the LG additionally a **Delicates** programme [2][3][1].

## Noise and shared-wall compliance

- **Bosch WTX88M20AU**: ~**62 dB(A)** during drying, with ActiveAir heat-pump and an explicit Anti-Vibration design that limits structure-borne noise [1].
- **LG DVH9-09W**: inverter-compressor design marketed as quiet; a numeric dB(A) is not published on the LG Australia page [2].
- **Samsung DV90T6240LH**: ~**63–64 dB(A)**, acoustic emission class B on its EU label [4].

Victoria does **not** set a fixed dB(A) cap for domestic appliances. Under EPA Victoria's *Noise guideline: Assessing noise from residential equipment* (Publication 1973) and the **Environment Protection Regulations 2021 (reg. 114)**, noise from fixed residential equipment is **"unreasonable" at night (prohibited hours) if it can be heard in a habitable room of any other residence**, doors/windows open or not; by day/evening it is unreasonable if it recurringly interferes, especially via "clearly noticeable rattles or vibrations" [6]. For a shared-wall terrace the practical controls are therefore **not running the dryer overnight** and **minimising structure-borne vibration** — where the Bosch's explicit anti-vibration mounting is an advantage [1][6]. Body-corporate by-laws typically mirror this audibility/nuisance test rather than a dB number.

## Installation in a heritage terrace

All three are **condenser heat-pump dryers that vent no moist air or duct externally** — water is collected internally (tank or drain) and there is no wall penetration [1][2][3][4]. This matters twice over:

- **Ventilation fit**: with no external vent required, they suit the **limited-ventilation** terrace far better than a vented dryer; only modest room airflow is needed to shed the small amount of heat they emit.
- **Heritage Overlay (Clause 43.01, Victorian Planning Provisions, VC160)**: a planning permit is required to demolish/remove a building or to "construct a building or construct or carry out works," explicitly including **domestic services that are *visible from a street or public park***. An internally placed, free-standing heat-pump dryer involves **no external buildings/works and no street-visible service**, so **no Heritage Overlay planning permit is needed** to install it; internal-alteration controls only bite where a property's HO schedule specifically applies them. A vented dryer requiring an external wall vent would, by contrast, risk triggering the permit. The vent-free heat-pump format is thus the right category for a heritage-listed terrace.

## Condenser and lint maintenance

- **Bosch** — **self-cleaning condenser**: the unit flushes the heat-exchanger automatically, so there is **no manual condenser cleaning**; only the lint filter is cleaned each cycle and the water tank emptied [1].

## Victorian Energy Upgrades (VEU) rebate

Energy-efficient clothes dryers **are an eligible VEU activity** (Essential Services Commission **Activity 25**). To qualify the dryer must be **≥7 stars and ≥5 kg capacity** and listed on the GEMS and ESC product registers; each household can claim **once**. The **indicative discount is ~$70**, with the final value set by the accredited provider and varying with the VEEC certificate price [8]. The **Bosch (10-star)** and **LG (9-star)** both clearly qualify; the **Samsung** would need an Australian ≥7-star registration it does not have, so it cannot access the rebate as an import.

## Recommendation

**Buy the Bosch Serie 8 WTX88M20AU.** It is the most efficient (10-star, ~1.88 kWh/cycle), the quietest measured unit (62 dB(A)) with explicit anti-vibration mounting that suits a shared wall under EPA Victoria's audibility-based noise rule [1][6], the lowest-maintenance (self-cleaning condenser, lint-only servicing) [1], fully vent-free so it needs no Heritage Overlay permit and fits the limited-ventilation terrace [3], VEU-eligible (~$70) [8], and it carries the **lowest 10-year TCO (~$3,924)**. Its only real trade-off is the **8 kg drum** (vs 9 kg) and a standard 2-year warranty.

**Choose the LG DVH9-09W instead if** you want the **larger 9 kg capacity** (fewer loads for the 6–8/week household), the reassurance of the **10-year inverter-compressor parts warranty** [2], CHOICE "Best Dryer Brand 2025" standing, and the lowest **upfront** price (~$1,435–$1,499) [3] — accepting a somewhat higher 9-star running cost and 10-year TCO (~$4,255).

**Do not buy the Samsung DV90T6240LH for this scenario.** It is a UK/EU model without an Australian energy-label/star rating, is not sold through mainstream Australian retailers, cannot access the VEU rebate, and lacks assured local warranty service and sealed-system parts — leaving it without the energy-label, reliability and serviceability foundations the heritage-terrace household needs over a 10-year horizon [4].
