# Can Jordan Hayes Enforce the Remote-Work Promise on Summary Judgment? A Texas Promissory-Estoppel Analysis

## Bottom line

Hayes is unlikely to defeat the company's motion for summary judgment and almost certainly cannot obtain an order *enforcing* full remote work. Two independent obstacles each likely defeat the promissory-estoppel claim **as a matter of law**: (1) the bolded "Integration and Merger" clause Hayes signed ("This agreement supersedes all prior verbal representations"), combined with the offer letter's contrary "hybrid schedule per company policy" term, makes reliance on the prior oral "full remote" promise unjustified as a matter of law and forecloses promissory estoppel under *Freedom Equity Group v. MTL* [1]; and (2) in the employment context, Texas confines promissory estoppel to a promise to sign a *presently existing written agreement*, and treats reliance on an oral promise that does not limit the employer's at-will freedom as unreasonable as a matter of law (*Lotito*; *Collins*) [2].

Even if some element survived, the remedy mismatch is dispositive of the question actually asked: Texas promissory estoppel yields **reliance (out-of-pocket) damages**, not specific performance, so Hayes could at most recover relocation losses such as the **$40,000 loss on the Seattle home sale** and lease costs — he cannot compel the company to honor remote work itself [3][4]. The element on which the claim most plausibly *survives* to trial is substantial detrimental reliance (the home loss and the May 15 lease are concrete, foreseeable expenditures); the elements on which it most likely *fails* on summary judgment are the clear-and-definite-promise and reasonable-reliance prongs.

## 1. The promissory-estoppel elements under Texas law

Texas adopted promissory estoppel from Restatement (First/Second) of Contracts §90 in **Wheeler v. White, 398 S.W.2d 93 (Tex. 1965)**, which recognized that "where there is actually no contract the promissory estoppel theory may be invoked, thereby supplying a remedy which will enable the injured party to be compensated for his foreseeable, definite and substantial reliance" [2]. As subsequently stated, the elements are: (1) a **promise**; (2) **foreseeability** of reliance by the promisor; and (3) **substantial, reasonable, and justifiable detrimental reliance** by the promisee (*Frost Crushed Stone Co. v. Odell Geer Constr. Co.*, 110 S.W.3d 41, 44; *English v. Fischer*, 660 S.W.2d 521, 524 (Tex. 1983)) [3][2]. Texas authorities also require that the **promise not be covered by a valid contract**, and a clear and unambiguous promise; a promise that is not "clear and unambiguous" will not support the claim (*Esty v. Beal Bank*, 298 S.W.3d 280, 305) [3]. The "injustice avoidable only by enforcement" idea from §90 functions, in Texas practice, as the equitable rationale that operates *only where there is no enforceable contract* covering the subject [3][2].

## 2. Summary-judgment standard

Under Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 166a (functionally analogous to Fed. R. Civ. P. 56), summary judgment is reviewed de novo and is proper where there is no genuine issue of material fact; a defendant may obtain a **traditional** summary judgment by establishing that an element fails as a matter of law (as in *Freedom Equity Group*, *Lotito*, and *Collins*, all decided on summary judgment) [1][2][5]. Critically, several of the dispositive PE issues here — whether reliance is "reasonable as a matter of law" and whether an oral promise contradicts an unambiguous writing — are routinely resolved *on summary judgment*, not reserved for the jury [1][2]. Conversely, *whether the company unequivocally notified Hayes of a modification* and whether he accepted it can be fact questions (*Hathaway*) [6]. The defendant therefore has the stronger summary-judgment posture on the core PE elements; Hayes's best argument for a fact issue lies in detriment and in the comparator/disparate-treatment evidence, neither of which cures the legal defects.

## 3. Clear and definite promise — undermined by the contradictory "hybrid" term

The manager's oral "full remote work" assurance is facially a promise, but Texas requires it to be **clear and unambiguous** and not contradicted by the signed writing [3]. The offer letter says "hybrid schedule per company policy" — itself undefined as to remote frequency — which is the opposite of "full remote." Where an oral representation is "directly contradicted by the express, unambiguous terms of a written agreement between the parties," it cannot support promissory estoppel (*DRC Parts & Accessories v. VM Motori*, 112 S.W.3d 854, 858), as applied in *Freedom Equity Group* [1]. The clear-and-definite-promise prong is thus weak for Hayes precisely because the written instrument says something different.

## 4. The integration/merger clause and its effect on the oral promise

The bolded clause — "This agreement supersedes all prior verbal representations" — is a merger/integration clause, and Texas law gives it decisive effect against pre-signing oral promises. In **Freedom Equity Group, Inc. v. MTL Insurance Co.** (Tex. App.—Houston [1st Dist.] 2015, No. 01-14-00210-CV), the court held that "where the parties' agreement states that it contains the entire agreement between the parties or contains a similarly-worded merger provision, alleged oral promises made before the agreement was signed that conflict with the agreement cannot support a promissory estoppel claim," and **affirmed summary judgment** for the employer-equivalent on the PE (and fraud) claims [1]. The court relied on *2001 Trinity Fund v. Carrizo Oil & Gas*, 393 S.W.3d 442, 457 (merger clause foreclosed a PE claim based on oral promises before execution) [1]. Independently, a **valid contract covering the subject matter of the dispute precludes recovery in promissory estoppel** (*Doctors Hosp. 1997*, 154 S.W.3d 636; *BP Am. v. Zaffirini*) [3]. Because the offer letter expressly addresses work schedule, the remote-work subject is covered by the signed contract, which both bars PE on the "covered by a contract" rationale and supplies the contradicting term.

## 5. Can promissory estoppel override the integration clause / parol evidence rule?

No, on these facts. Hayes might invoke *Italian Cowboy Partners v. Prudential*, 341 S.W.3d 323 (Tex. 2011), for the proposition that a merger clause does not always defeat a claim premised on prior representations — but *Freedom Equity Group* distinguished *Italian Cowboy* as a **fraudulent-inducement** case turning on disclaimer-of-representations language, and held that where the plaintiff does *not* claim fraudulent inducement and the clause states the writing is the entire agreement, the merger clause forecloses promissory estoppel [1]. Reliance on an oral promise contradicted by the unambiguous written agreement is "unreasonable and unjustified as a matter of law" [1]. Thus, absent a fraudulent-inducement theory (and the facts give no indication Hayes pleaded fraud), the integration clause and parol-evidence principle bar Hayes's reliance on the prior verbal "full remote" representation.

## 6. The manager's lack of actual authority and whether apparent authority binds the company

HR records show the manager **lacked actual authority** to modify standard terms. Under Texas agency law, the company can still be bound by **apparent authority**, but the test focuses on the **principal's conduct**, not the agent's representations: apparent authority exists only where the principal's own actions lead a third party reasonably to believe the agent is authorized (*Gaines v. Kelly*, 235 S.W.3d 179, 182 (Tex. 2007); Restatement (Third) of Agency §2.03) [7]. "[T]he agent's representations are largely immaterial; the relevant considerations are the actions taken by the principal toward the agent" [7]. A principal may manifest authority by knowingly permitting the agent to hold himself out, or by failing to use ordinary care to prevent the indicia of authority [7]. A hiring manager's *job title* might "customarily carry specific authority" sufficient to create apparent authority for routine hiring decisions [7], but several facts cut against Hayes: the standard offer letter contained a contrary written term and an integration clause (a circumstance "of an unusual or extraordinary nature" that should prompt inquiry where the oral side-deal conflicts with the standard contract) [7], and the company never manifested assent to a non-standard remote arrangement. Apparent authority is at best a contested fact issue and does not rescue the claim from the merger-clause and at-will defects.

## 7. Was Hayes's reliance reasonable as a matter of law?

Likely **no**, for two converging reasons. First, as above, reliance on an oral promise "directly contradicted by the express, unambiguous terms" of the signed offer letter (and its integration clause) is unjustified as a matter of law [1]. Second, in the employment context, **Lotito v. Knife River Corp.**, 391 S.W.3d 226 (Tex. App.—Waco 2012), held that reliance on an oral promise of multi-year employment "without any limit on [the employer's] freedom to terminate" was "unreasonable and unjustified as a matter of law," affirming summary judgment for the employer (citing *Collins v. Allied Pharmacy Mgmt.*, 871 S.W.2d 929, 938) [2]. A promise about *how* an at-will employee will work (remote vs. hybrid) is a modifiable condition of at-will employment, so reliance on its permanence faces the same problem.

## 8. At-will employment: enforceable term or modifiable condition?

Texas at-will doctrine is central. An at-will employer may **unilaterally modify** the terms of employment prospectively: "either party may impose modifications to the employment terms as a condition of continued employment," and if the employer **unequivocally notifies** the employee of definite changes and the employee continues working with knowledge, acceptance occurs as a matter of law (*Hathaway v. General Mills*, 711 S.W.2d 227 (Tex. 1986), citing *L.G. Balfour Co. v. Brown*) [6]. The remote-work arrangement is therefore best characterized not as a vested, enforceable term but as a **modifiable condition** of at-will employment. The company's **July 1 policy requiring three office days weekly** is precisely such a prospective modification; under *Hathaway*, whether Hayes "accepted" it is a fact question (he refused on July 8 and worked under protest), but his refusal does not entitle him to *enforce* the old remote condition — it at most preserves a damages theory or lets him decline and leave [6]. The Texas Supreme Court has stressed reluctance to impose duties that "alter or conflict with the at-will relationship" (*Sawyer v. E.I. du Pont*, Tex. 2014) [4].

## 9. Substantial detriment: the $40,000 home loss and the May 15 lease

This is Hayes's strongest element. Wheeler/§90 protect "foreseeable, definite and substantial reliance" [2], and a **$40,000 loss on the Seattle home sale** plus a **Texas residential lease signed May 15, 2024** are concrete, quantifiable, out-of-pocket expenditures of exactly the kind reliance damages restore. *Sawyer* confirms that **expenses incurred in reliance on a promise of prospective employment are recoverable "because neither the injury nor the recovery depends on continued employment,"** even where the relationship is at-will [4]; it cites Restatement (Third) of Employment Law §6.05 cmt. f that an employer who induces is "liable for pecuniary losses incurred by the employee in reliance" but **not** for fulfilling any promise of continued employment [4]. So the detriment is real and likely creates a fact issue on this element — but it supports a *damages* recovery, not enforcement, and only if Hayes clears the promise/reasonable-reliance hurdles he is likely to fail.

## 10. The June confirmation email: writing or restatement of an unauthorized promise?

The manager's June email confirming the remote promise does **not** convert the oral promise into a binding modification. Under *Hathaway*, a contract modification requires a meeting of the minds and (for at-will terms) unequivocal notice plus acceptance — and the modifying actor must have **authority** [6]. The manager lacked actual authority to modify standard terms, so the email "merely restates an unauthorized prior promise" rather than effecting an authorized change. It is also not a "promise to sign a presently existing written agreement," which is the narrow employment-context predicate Texas requires for PE to escape the writing problem (*Lotito*; *Collins*; *Leach v. Conoco*; *Webber v. M.W. Kellogg*) [2]. The email does have **evidentiary** value: it corroborates that a definite oral promise was made and that reliance was foreseeable, helping Hayes survive a *no-evidence* motion on the existence of a promise — but it does not overcome the merger clause or the authority defect on a traditional motion.

## 11. The two comparator engineers with written remote approvals

The two simultaneously hired engineers who received **written** remote approvals cut against Hayes more than for him on the legal merits. They illustrate the company's *standard mechanism* — written approval — for granting remote work, underscoring that an oral side-promise contradicting the signed "hybrid" term was an unusual arrangement that a reasonable person should have questioned and reduced to writing [7]. The comparators may support an *injustice* narrative (Hayes was treated differently) and could be relevant to a separate **discrimination** theory not presented here, but disparate treatment alone does not satisfy promissory estoppel's clear-promise and reasonable-reliance elements, and Texas does not recognize a general good-faith exception to at-will employment that would convert unfairness into enforceability [4]. The comparator evidence is thus more probative of what reasonable reliance required (a writing) than of injustice excusing its absence.

## 12. Timing of reliance and the injustice prong

Hayes's reliance acts — selling the home (at the $40,000 loss) and signing the May 15 lease — occurred **before** the July 1 policy and while he worked fully remote through June without objection. That sequence strengthens the *detriment* and *foreseeability* showing (the company accepted remote work during the period the promise was operative) and is relevant to the injustice prong. But the July 1 policy is a lawful prospective modification of an at-will condition (*Hathaway*) [6], and the absence of objection in June does not create a vested right to remote work going forward. The injustice analysis is further weakened because §90/Wheeler's "injustice" rationale applies "where there is actually no contract" [2]; here a signed contract (with a merger clause) covers the schedule subject, channeling any remedy into reliance damages rather than enforcement.

## 13. Remedy: reliance damages, not specific performance

Decisively for the framing of the question, Texas limits promissory-estoppel recovery to **reliance damages** — "the amount necessary to restore the plaintiff to its former position," i.e., out-of-pocket loss — and **not** expectation/lost-profit damages (*Sun Oil Co. v. Madeley*, 626 S.W.2d 726, 734 (Tex. 1981), citing *Wheeler v. White*; *Fretz Constr. v. S. Nat'l Bank*, 626 S.W.2d 478, 483) [3]. A prevailing plaintiff may also recover attorneys' fees and pre/post-judgment interest [3]. *Sawyer* reinforces that for prospective-employment promises the recoverable harm is the employee's pecuniary reliance losses, not fulfillment of the promise [4]. Promissory estoppel therefore **cannot compel the company to let Hayes work remotely**; at most it could compensate the $40,000 home loss and lease/relocation costs — and only if the claim cleared the elements it is likely to fail. The four-year limitations period (Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code §16.051) is not an obstacle [3].

## 14. Statute of frauds (peripheral)

The statute of frauds (Tex. Bus. & Com. Code §26.01(a),(b)(6)) bars an oral agreement not performable within one year [2]. A remote-work condition of *at-will* employment is generally terminable/performable within a year, so it likely does **not** fall within the statute — meaning the statute of frauds is probably not the company's best defense (the merger clause and at-will/authority defenses are stronger). To the extent the statute did apply, Texas allows promissory estoppel to bar the statute only narrowly: as a counter-defense, the plaintiff must prove the PE elements *and* show "there is an existing written contract that the defendant promised to sign" (*Blackstone Med.*, 470 S.W.3d 636, 654-56; *Carpenter v. Phelps*) — and in the employment context PE applies "only if there was a promise to execute a presently existing written agreement" (*Lotito*; *Collins*; *Sonnichsen v. Baylor Univ.*, 47 S.W.3d 122, 125) [3][2]. There is no allegation the manager promised to sign an existing written remote-work agreement, so even if the statute applied, the estoppel escape hatch would not open.

## 15. Synthesis — who prevails on summary judgment

The **company is likely to prevail on summary judgment** on the promissory-estoppel claim. The claim most likely **fails** on (a) the clear-and-definite-promise element, because the signed "hybrid schedule per company policy" term contradicts the oral "full remote" promise [3][1], and (b) the reasonable-reliance element, because reliance on an oral promise contradicted by an unambiguous written agreement carrying a merger clause is unjustified as a matter of law [1], compounded by the rule that reliance not limiting the employer's at-will freedom is unreasonable [2] and by the manager's lack of authority [7][6]. The claim is also independently barred because a valid contract covers the subject matter [3].

The element on which Hayes most plausibly raises a fact issue is **substantial detriment** — the $40,000 home-sale loss and the May 15 lease are exactly the foreseeable, definite reliance losses §90 protects, and *Sawyer* confirms such relocation expenses are recoverable notwithstanding at-will status [4]. The June email and comparator evidence keep the *existence of a promise* and *foreseeability* alive enough to resist a no-evidence motion. But none of that lets Hayes **enforce the remote-work arrangement**: the Texas remedy is reliance damages, not specific performance [3][4], and the July 1 policy is a lawful prospective modification of a modifiable at-will condition [6]. The realistic best case for Hayes is not enforcement of remote work but a damages claim — most viably reframed as **fraudulent inducement** (which can survive a merger clause under *Italian Cowboy* where the relocation losses do not depend on continued employment) [1][4] — to recover the $40,000 and relocation costs.
