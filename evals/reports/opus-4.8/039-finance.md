# Premium Resources Ltd. (now NexMetals Mining Corp.): Financial Sustainability and Capital Structure Risk Assessment

## Bottom line

Premium Resources Ltd.—renamed **NexMetals Mining Corp. (Nasdaq/TSXV: NEXM)** on June 11, 2025—saw its shareholders' equity collapse from **C$9,745,686 at December 31, 2023 to a deficiency of C$(3,447,060) at December 31, 2024**, a swing of **C$13.19 million, or roughly a 135% decline that wiped out book equity entirely and turned it negative** [1]. The company carries a **material-uncertainty going-concern qualification** and is funded entirely by external capital, with no operating cash flow [1]. Its single largest near-term pressure is not its modest 12-month capital-expenditure and contractual obligations (only **C$7,180** of committed capex and roughly **C$4.99 million** of contractual obligations due within a year), but the **US$25 million (C$34,107,500) second Selebi APA instalment due January 31, 2026**, which sits outside that table and which management states it cannot currently fund [2]. Against that, the company held **C$26,463,957 in cash at June 30, 2025**—materially improved from C$6.1 million at year-end 2024 after a recapitalization—but still short of the January 2026 obligation alone [2]. Potential dilution is high: on the June 30, 2025 capital structure, exercise of all **9,428,996 outstanding warrants** would represent **~30.5% of the fully diluted base**, a level well into "material dilution" territory [2]. The combination of eroded/negative equity, going-concern doubt, near-term milestone payments to the BCL liquidators, and high dilution overhang makes **refinancing urgency high and the Botswana restart timeline contingent on repeated capital raises**.

## 1. Equity erosion: December 2023 → December 2024

On a consistent audited US-GAAP basis (financial statements stated in Canadian dollars, audited for the two-year period ended December 31, 2024):

| Item | Dec 31, 2023 | Dec 31, 2024 | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Additional paid-in capital | C$116,069,973 | C$145,025,333 | +C$28,955,360 |
| Accumulated deficit | C$(104,566,816) | C$(146,987,099) | −C$42,420,283 |
| Accumulated other comprehensive loss | C$(1,788,987) | C$(1,516,810) | +C$272,177 |
| Preferred shares | C$31,516 | C$31,516 | — |
| **Total shareholders' equity (deficiency)** | **C$9,745,686** | **C$(3,447,060)** | **−C$13,192,746** |

[1]

The **absolute decline was C$13,192,746**, a **~135.4% reduction** that took equity below zero into a deficiency [1]. The driver is the year's **net loss of C$42.42 million** (matching the C$42.4 million increase in accumulated deficit), only partially offset by ~C$29 million of new paid-in capital from equity issuance [1]. In other words, the company spent and lost more than it raised, and its cumulative losses (C$147.0 million deficit) now exceed its lifetime paid-in capital base. A negative book equity in an exploration-stage company with no revenue is a direct signal that the enterprise cannot self-fund and is structurally dependent on continual external financing—precisely the condition the auditors flag as **substantial doubt about the ability to continue as a going concern** [1].

Equity subsequently recovered to **C$36,555,627 at June 30, 2025** [2], but this recovery was financed, not earned: it reflects a large 2025 equity placement and the conversion of the Term Loan to equity, even as the accumulated deficit deepened further to C$(177,304,175) and the company booked another C$30.7 million net loss in the first half of 2025 [2]. The equity line is therefore a function of how recently the company raised money, not of any improvement in cash generation.

## 2. Dilution risk from outstanding warrants

As of the latest filing (Q2 2025, June 30, 2025), and reflecting the **20:1 share consolidation effective June 11, 2025**, the company had **9,428,996 common-share purchase warrants outstanding at a weighted-average exercise price of C$12.58** [2]. The warrant book by tranche:

| Warrants | Exercise price | Expiry |
|---|---|---|
| 301,200 | C$28.75 | June 28, 2026 |
| 1,012,981 | C$22.00 | June 14, 2029 |
| 801,089 | C$22.00 | June 21, 2029 |
| 3,833,334 | C$11.00 | March 18, 2028 |
| 3,480,392 Settlement Warrants (to Cymbria) | C$8.00 | March 18, 2028 |

[2]

The Settlement Warrants arose from the conversion of the Term Loan: NexMetals issued Cymbria 3,480,392 Settlement Units (one share + one C$8.00 warrant each) to satisfy C$20,882,353 of Term Loan principal [2].

**Potential dilution = warrants / (shares outstanding + warrants).** On the June 30, 2025 base of **21,449,318 shares outstanding** [2]:

- 9,428,996 / (21,449,318 + 9,428,996) = **30.54%** potential dilution from warrants alone.

The **current shares outstanding** are **35,512,606 common shares issued and outstanding**, per NexMetals' investor page dated June 17, 2026 [3] — this is the operative base for assessing dilution today, and on it warrant-only dilution is **20.98%** (9,428,996 / (35,512,606 + 9,428,996)) [3]. On this current base, the picture shifts further because of additional "tradeable warrants": the company then reported 9,428,996 plain warrants plus **14,035,100 tradeable warrants exercisable at C$8.00** [3]. Including both classes:

- (9,428,996 + 14,035,100) / (35,512,606 + 23,464,096) = **39.79%** potential dilution from all warrants.

NexMetals' own disclosed **fully diluted share count is 60,612,315** versus 35,512,606 outstanding—meaning roughly **41% of fully diluted shares would come from instruments not yet outstanding** (warrants, 920,871 options, 487,640 RSUs, 90,887 DSUs) [3].

**Interpretation.** Potential dilution in the **20–40% range is well above the ~10% threshold commonly treated as "material" dilution and the ~20% level that typically requires shareholder approval and signals heavy reliance on convertible/warrant-linked financing** [2][3]. A warrant overhang of this size has two compounding effects: it caps share-price upside (warrant holders supply stock into any rally), and it confirms that prior financings were priced with substantial sweeteners—evidence of a high cost of capital and constrained access to clean equity. The weighted-average warrant strike of C$12.58 also sits above several recent issuance prices, so the warrants are partly a contingent future capital source only if the shares re-rate sharply.

Including that instalment, total cash demands within ~12 months exceed **C$39 million**, against C$26.5 million of cash.

## 4. Contingent milestone payments to the BCL liquidators

The Selebi and Selkirk mines were acquired from the **BCL liquidator** under separate asset purchase agreements (the Selebi APA and Selkirk APA), closed January and August 2022 respectively [2].

- **Selebi APA aggregate purchase price: US$56,750,000**, payable in three instalments [2]:
 - **US$1,750,000** (C$2,086,830) paid at closing, plus **US$5,178,747** (C$6,164,688) care-and-maintenance funding contribution [2];
 - **US$25,000,000** (C$34,107,500) due upon the earlier of MMRGTES approval of the Section 42/Section 43 applications—**due January 31, 2026** [2];
 - a third instalment of **US$30,000,000 (C$40,929,000)** payable on completion of mine construction and production start-up by January 31, 2030 (no later than four years after Section 42/43 approval); if Selkirk is commissioned before Selebi, this payment triggers on Selkirk's commission date [2].
- **Total remaining contingent post-closing milestone payments across both mines: ≈ US$55 million**, comprising **(i) US$25 million due January 31, 2026, and (ii) US$30 million due upon the earlier of commissioning/start of production or four years from the mining-licence renewal date for either the Selebi Mines or the Selkirk Mine** [2].

As of June 30, 2025, the conditions for the second and third instalments had not been met, so these amounts are **not accrued** on the balance sheet [2]. Failure to satisfy the milestone payments and study-phase requirements would cause the **mines to revert to the liquidators** [2]. Each mine carries additional contingent obligations beyond the headline milestones: the Selebi purchase is subject to a **2% NSR royalty** (50% buy-back right) and a contingent-consideration agreement (sliding-scale US$0.50–1.40/tonne on newly discovered deposits over 25 Mt, plus 15% price-participation on post-tax earnings tied to commodity-price increases of ≥25%); the Selkirk purchase carries a **1% NSR royalty** (full buy-back right) on similar contingent terms [2]. Cymbria paid C$2,750,000 for the right to participate in those NSR buy-backs (the NSR option liability on the balance sheet) [2]. The company also let lapse, and on August 11, 2025 formally abandoned, a planned acquisition of the adjacent Phikwe South and Southeast Extension deposits [2]. The **Selkirk APA's three-year study phase expired August 17, 2025**; the company submitted a one-year extension notice and was awaiting formal acceptance from the liquidator as of the report date [2].

## 5. Liquidity, runway, and refinancing urgency

| Metric | Value | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Cash & equivalents, Jun 30, 2025 | C$26,463,957 | [2] |
| Cash & equivalents, Dec 31, 2024 | C$6,105,933 | [2] |
| H1 2025 net loss | C$30,707,396 | [2] |
| Total liabilities, Jun 30, 2025 | C$10,498,247 | [2] |
| Total assets, Jun 30, 2025 | C$47,053,874 | [2] |
| Selebi 2nd instalment due Jan 31, 2026 | C$34,107,500 (US$25M) | [2] |

The **C$26.5 million cash balance does not, on its own, cover the C$34.1 million January 2026 Selebi instalment**, let alone the additional ~C$5 million of other contractual obligations due within a year [2]. Management explicitly states the company "does not currently have sufficient funds to meet this obligation" and "will need to use its existing working capital and raise additional amounts as needed" [2]. With **no source of operating cash flow** and a half-year cash burn run-rate implied by a C$30.7 million net loss, the runway is on the order of months absent further financing. The interim report (MD&A dated August 13, 2025) reaffirms the going-concern position: management states the financial statements are prepared on a going-concern basis but that "material uncertainties cast substantial doubt about the Company's ability to continue as a going concern," given negative operating cash flow, no significant credit lines, and full dependence on external financing [4]. The **most recent disclosed financing arrangement is the March 18, 2025 Financing** (the C$46.0 million private placement plus the C$20.9 million Cymbria/EdgePoint term-loan conversion); no further financing was disclosed as a subsequent event through the August 13, 2025 report date, leaving the US$25 million January 2026 milestone unfunded as of the latest disclosure [4].

**Refinancing urgency is therefore high.** The combination of (a) book equity that fell into deficiency at end-2024, (b) a fixed, large, near-dated US$25 million milestone, and (c) cash that recovered only because of a 2025 equity-plus-debt-conversion recapitalization means the company must raise capital again before January 2026 or restructure/defer the Selebi obligation. Its scope to do so cleanly is constrained by the existing warrant overhang and by the fact that the Term Loan was already converted to equity rather than refinanced as debt—evidence that debt capacity is limited and that equity (and warrant-laden units) is the financing channel of last resort [2].

The principal mitigant is the **March 2025 recapitalization** (closed March 18, 2025): a **C$46.0 million non-brokered equity private placement** plus the **C$20.9 million Cymbria Term Loan debt-to-equity conversion**, which deleveraged the balance sheet, introduced a **new strategic investor group**, and installed new leadership (Morgan Lekstrom as CEO, March 20, 2025; Paul Martin as Chairman) [2][1]. That financing—not operating performance—is what lifted cash from C$6.1 million to C$26.5 million and restored positive book equity by mid-2025 [2]. It demonstrates the company retains access to risk capital, but it also illustrates the model: each advance is financed by issuing equity and warrants, perpetuating the dilution overhang. The continued going-concern qualification and the unfunded US$25 million January 2026 milestone mean a further raise of comparable scale is required within roughly six months of the June 30, 2025 balance date [2][1].

## 6. Combined impact on funding and the Botswana development timeline

The three pressures reinforce one another:

- **Equity erosion / going concern** removes any debt-based buffer—negative book equity at end-2024 and recurring losses mean lenders price the company as distressed, pushing it toward dilutive equity [1].
- **Dilution overhang (30.5% on the June 2025 base; ~40% including tradeable warrants)** raises the cost of each new raise: existing warrants and the depressed share price mean new equity must be sold cheaply or with further sweeteners, deepening the overhang [2][3].
- **Milestone payments (US$25M in Jan 2026, US$30M at commissioning) and the study-phase deadlines** are hard, externally enforced gates: missing them returns the Selebi/Selkirk mines to the liquidators, so the company cannot simply slow spending to preserve cash without risking the assets themselves [2].

The practical consequence is that **NexMetals' ability to fund operations and to hit the Botswana restart milestones is contingent on successfully raising tens of millions of dollars on short notice and on a recurring basis.** The development timeline—Section 42/43 approvals for licence extension/conversion driving the US$25 million January 2026 payment, and commissioning by January 31, 2030 (within four years of approval) driving the US$30 million payment—is effectively a financing schedule the company has not yet funded [2]. Each milestone both requires cash and, once met, triggers a further cash demand, so the financing pressure does not abate as the project advances; it intensifies. The C$26.5 million cash position buys time for near-term work but is insufficient for the January 2026 milestone alone, making at least one further substantial capital raise a prerequisite for keeping the projects [2].

The company is positioned as a Botswana redevelopment story—restarting the **previously producing Selebi (Ni-Cu-Co) and Selkirk (Ni-Cu-Co-PGE) mines** [2][3]—and retains research coverage from ATB Cormark, SCP Resource Finance, and Raymond James, indicating continued capital-markets engagement that supports its reliance on serial equity issuance [3]. But on the evidence, the financial structure is fragile: equity wiped out and reconstituted only by dilutive recapitalization, a heavy warrant overhang, and a near-dated milestone it cannot currently fund, all under an active going-concern qualification [2][1].
