## Fortive's Evolving Portfolio Strategy After the Ralliant (Precision Technologies) Separation

### Correcting the premise: Precision Technologies WAS the spun-off business

A foundational finding reframes this analysis. Precision Technologies (PT) was not a *retained* segment — it **was** the business Fortive separated. On **June 28, 2025**, Fortive completed the spin-off of its Precision Technologies segment into an independent, publicly traded company, **Ralliant Corporation (NYSE: RAL)**, distributing **one Ralliant share for every three Fortive shares** held as of the June 16, 2025 record date [1]. Coincident with the separation, **Olumide Soroye became President and CEO of Fortive, succeeding James A. Lico**, who retired [1].

After the separation Fortive reports **two** strategic segments, not three:
- **Intelligent Operating Solutions (IOS)**
- **Advanced Healthcare Solutions (AHS)**

Before the separation, Fortive had three segments — IOS, **Precision Technologies**, and AHS [2]. The question's premise of "three remaining segments" describes the *pre-separation* structure; PT/Ralliant is the entity that left. Accordingly, the analysis below evaluates the two continuing segments (IOS, AHS) plus PT in its final quarters inside Fortive, because PT's relative performance is precisely what the separation logic turns on. Beginning in Q3 2025, Fortive reclassifies Ralliant as a discontinued operation [1].

### Strategic rationale for the separation

Fortive announced the spin on **September 4, 2024** [2]. Management framed it as creating two more strategically focused companies [2]:
- **Continuing Fortive** — a "technology solutions company, retaining the Fortive name," combining the IOS and AHS brands, focused on **"resilient, high-quality recurring growth by delivering productivity and safety to customers"** [2].
- **Ralliant** — a "global technology company" of the Precision Technologies brands, focused on **"precision instruments and highly engineered products essential for breakthrough innovation,"** aligned to "powerful secular trends" [2].

The transaction was structured to qualify as a **tax-free spin-off** for U.S. federal income tax purposes [2]. CEO Soroye tied the post-separation strategy to the "Fortive Accelerated strategy, including our disciplined capital allocation approach and focus on building investor trust" [1]. In short: separate the more cyclical, hardware-oriented precision-instrument business from the recurring-revenue software/healthcare core so each can pursue its own capital allocation and growth strategy.

Fortive's Q2 2025 release presents two views. The **reported/consolidated** view still includes PT (since the spin closed at quarter-end); the **continuing-operations** view treats PT as discontinued and shows only IOS and AHS.

[1] (margins shown in the source: IOS 24.7%, PT 17.8% [1])

**Continuing-operations view — Q2 2025 vs Q2 2024 (IOS + AHS only; PT as discontinued):**

|---|---|---|---|---|---|

(The continuing-ops view restates IOS revenue higher — $696.9M vs the reported $675.7M — reflecting reclassifications tied to discontinued-operations accounting.)

**Ranking unit economics in Q2 2025:** IOS has by far the strongest margin (~24.5–24.7%), roughly double AHS (~11–12%), with PT in between (17.8%) before its departure [1]. On *trajectory*, AHS was the only continuing segment to expand margin year over year (+20 bps to 11.2%), while IOS slipped ~40 bps on flat revenue [1]. IOS therefore has the **strongest absolute unit economics**, while AHS shows the **best margin direction** among continuing segments in the quarter — though AHS revenue declined 1.3% (core −1.9%) and IOS was essentially flat (+0.1% reported) [1].

### Q1 2025 vs Q1 2024 segment and company margins (computed)

The Q1 release (three-segment, pre-separation) is the clearest window into *why* PT was separated:

| Segment | Q1'25 Margin | Q1'24 Margin | Δ |
|---|---|---|---|
| IOS | 25.9% (op $173.7M / rev $671.4M) | 24.7% | **+120 bps** |
| Precision Technologies | 17.4% (op $87.3M / rev $500.6M) | 26.7% | **−930 bps** |
| AHS | 8.7% (op $26.3M / rev $302.2M) | 9.2% | −50 bps |
| **Company total** | **15.8%** (op $233.6M / rev $1,474.2M) | **19.8%** | −400 bps |

[2]

The reported company-margin drop (15.8% vs 19.8%) is heavily distorted by a **$63.1M gain on sale of property recorded in Q1 2024** that inflated the prior-year base [2]. On an **adjusted** basis, Q1 2025 operating margin was **25.3%, up 20 bps year over year, led by IOS** [2].

The PT story is stark: its Q1 2025 margin collapsed to 17.4% from 26.7% a year earlier (−930 bps), with revenue down 10.4% ($500.6M vs $559.0M), as test-and-measurement customers delayed investments amid macro and tariff uncertainty [2]. By contrast IOS expanded margin (+120 bps to 25.9%) on slightly higher revenue [2]. Management explicitly updated 2025 guidance to "reflect the delayed recovery in Precision Technologies" [2].

The consistent signal is that **PT was already the laggard in FY2024 (flat revenue, contracting margin) and then fell into material decline in 2025 (−10.4% then −5.1%)**, while IOS and AHS were the growth-and-margin engines in FY2024 before flattening in the 2025 quarters [1][2][3]. PT was both the slowest-growing and most margin-volatile segment — the natural candidate to separate.

### Synthesis: what the metrics reveal about portfolio prioritization

The combined evidence points to a coherent prioritization logic:

1. **PT was the drag the separation removes.** Across Q1 and Q2 2025, PT showed double-digit-then-mid-single-digit revenue decline and a sharp Q1 margin collapse (26.7%→17.4%), driven by delayed test-and-measurement demand [2][1]. Spinning it off as Ralliant lifts a cyclical, hardware-exposed drag off Fortive's growth and margin profile.

2. **The retained core is the higher-margin, recurring-revenue business.** IOS runs at ~24–26% operating margins and AHS at ~11–12%; both are positioned as "resilient, high-quality recurring growth" franchises in productivity, safety, and healthcare [2][1]. Removing PT lets Fortive present a cleaner recurring-revenue story.

3. **Underlying profitability is stable-to-improving despite soft top line.** Although reported company margin fell year over year in both quarters, that decline is an artifact (the Q1 2024 $63.1M property gain) and of PT's weakness; on an adjusted basis Q1 2025 operating margin rose 20 bps to 25.3%, led by IOS [2]. Continuing-operations adjusted EBITDA margin in Q2 2025 was 28.4% [1].

4. **Capital allocation toward the retained units.** Fortive deployed $345M to share repurchases in the first half of 2025 and ended Q2 2025 with $1,827.4M cash (vs $808.1M at year-end 2024) [1], consistent with the "disciplined capital allocation" thesis for the focused, post-spin company [1].

**Did the separation strengthen the remaining units' competitive positioning?** The sources support a qualified yes on *portfolio quality* — Fortive emerges weighted toward higher-margin (IOS ~24.5%) and margin-improving (AHS +20 bps) recurring-revenue businesses, shedding the segment with the steepest revenue decline and most volatile margins [1][2]. They do **not**, however, demonstrate accelerating *growth* in the retained units: IOS and AHS were roughly flat-to-down in both 2025 quarters [1][2]. The separation's near-term benefit is thus a cleaner, higher-margin, more focused portfolio with better unit economics, rather than evidence of a step-change in revenue momentum.

### Evidence gaps (stated plainly)

- FY2024 or FY2023 full-year **segment-level** revenue and operating profit — so FY2024 segment operating margins and the **2023→2024 annual segment growth rates** the question requests cannot be derived from this evidence and are not estimated here.
