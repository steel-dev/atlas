# Carbon Pricing in California, the EU, and China, 2020–2024

## Bottom line

Across 2020–2024 the three largest compliance carbon markets diverged sharply in price, stringency, and measured emissions outcomes. The **EU ETS** is the most mature and stringent system: it carries an absolute, declining cap, prices that peaked near **€100/tCO2 in early 2023** before settling to an annual average of **€65/t in 2024**, a **historic 16.5% one-year drop** in stationary-installation emissions in 2023, near-universal compliance, and the only operating carbon border adjustment (CBAM). **California's Cap-and-Trade** is a moderate-price, absolute-cap system (settlement prices rising from the high-teens in 2020 to **$31.91 in November 2024**, having touched ~$42 in February 2024) with deep statutory earmarking of revenue. **China's national ETS** is the largest by covered emissions (~5.1 billion tCO2 from the power sector, the world's largest single market) but is the least stringent: it is **intensity-based, not an absolute cap**, allowances are free, prices are low (~¥79–100/t, roughly USD 10–14), and it produces efficiency gains rather than absolute reductions. Price level, cap design (absolute vs. intensity), and design maturity together explain why the EU shows the largest verified absolute cuts, California a moderate contribution, and China primarily intensity improvement.

## Allowance price trajectories

**California.** Settlement prices began the program at the **$10 floor (2013)** and hovered near the floor until 2021, then rose above it, exceeding **$30/MTCO2e in 2023** [2]. In 2024 prices peaked near **$42/MTCO2e at the February auction**, fell to **$38.35 in May** (with the floor at $24.04), and settled at **$31.91 for current-vintage allowances at the November 2024 auction** (advance vintage $30.16) [1][2]. The system's **hard price ceiling was $88.22 in 2024**, rising 5% plus inflation annually; no auction has ever cleared at the ceiling, and the two **Allowance Price Containment Reserve tiers were $56.20 (Tier 1) and $72.21 (Tier 2)** in 2024, with no reserve sale ever held [2].

**EU ETS.** EUA prices rose from roughly €25/t in 2020 to a **peak near €100/t in early 2023**, then eased. The annual average spot price was **€83/t in 2023 and €65/t in 2024** (a 22% year-on-year fall), reaching a **low of €51/t in February 2024** before partially recovering; the price decline was driven by weak EUA demand amid continued power-sector decarbonisation.

**China.** The national market opened in **July 2021**. During the second compliance cycle the composite closing price fluctuated between **¥50 and ¥82/t**, closing 2023 at **¥79.42/t** — up 65.46% on the first trading day's opening price and 46.48% above the first-cycle close — and exceeded **¥100/t for the first time on 24 April 2024** [3]. The average secondary-market price in 2025 was about **¥70.78/t (USD 9.85)**, an order of magnitude below EU and California prices [4].

## Emissions outcomes vs. baseline

**EU ETS** delivered the largest verified absolute reductions. In 2023 emissions from stationary installations fell a **record 16.5%**, driven by the power sector as renewables (mainly wind and solar) expanded and gas displaced coal; installation emissions are now **~47.6% below 2005 levels** and on track for the **2030 target of −62%** [5]. Verified emissions fell further to **1,018 MtCO2e in 2024 from 1,150 MtCO2e in 2023**. Intra-EU aviation emissions, by contrast, rose ~10% in 2023 [5]. The Fit-for-55 revision tightened the cap (the 2024 cap was cut by 90 million allowances) and extended scope to maritime transport [5].

**California** met its **2020 statewide target (return to the 1990 level, ~431 MMTCO2e) ahead of schedule**; the binding forward target under **SB 32 is −40% below 1990 by 2030**, with AB 1279 setting −85% below 1990 and net-zero by 2045 [2]. The share of those reductions attributable to cap-and-trade alone is contested, since complementary policies operate in parallel; reductions from projects funded by auction proceeds total roughly **110 million metric tons CO2e** cumulatively [2].

**China's** system is **intensity-based** rather than an absolute cap: allowances are allocated free against output benchmarks, so the program rewards efficiency rather than capping total tonnes. Over the second cycle the **carbon intensity of electricity generation (CO2 per unit of electricity generated) fell 8.78% versus 2018**, while the intensity of thermal power generation specifically fell 2.38% versus 2018 [3]. The official progress report publishes no absolute emissions reduction estimate against a counterfactual or business-as-usual baseline — consistent with the system's intensity-based design, which targets emissions per unit of output rather than a total emissions cap — so this intensity decline stands as the indicative proxy for the program's mitigation effect rather than a quantified absolute reduction [3]. The market covers **~5.1 billion tCO2 from 2,257 power-sector entities**, the world's largest covered volume and more than 40% of national CO2; China's economy-wide GHG total was **14,314 MtCO2e in 2021** [3][4]. China's updated NDC commits to peaking CO2 before 2030 and cutting CO2 intensity per GDP by over 65% from 2005, with its first absolute economy-wide GHG reduction commitment (7–10% from peak by 2035) in the NDC 3.0 [4].

## Revenue generation and allocation

**California** has raised roughly **$28 billion in cumulative auction proceeds** [2]. Proceeds flow to the Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund (GGRF) and California Climate Investments. **AB 1550 (2016) requires a minimum 35% of available funds benefit priority populations** — disadvantaged communities, low-income communities, and low-income households [6]. Major recipients include the High-Speed Rail project and the Affordable Housing and Sustainable Communities program, which has invested over $4 billion in cap-and-trade funds [6][2].

**EU ETS** auction revenue was **€43.6 billion in 2023**, with cumulative revenue exceeding **€258 billion (2013–2025)** [5][7]. Revenue is split between Member-State national budgets, the **Innovation Fund**, the **Modernisation Fund**, and the Recovery and Resilience Facility for REPowerEU [5][7]. Since **June 2023, Member States must use 100% of relevant ETS revenue (or an equivalent amount) for climate and energy purposes** under Fit-for-55; the Innovation Fund was enlarged from 450 to 650 million allowances [7].

**China** does not yet auction allowances — allocation is free — so the system generates no auction revenue; cumulative CEA secondary trading turnover reached **¥24.92 billion by end-2023** [3][4].

## Compliance

- **EU ETS:** near-100% surrender compliance, backed by penalties; the system is described as stable and well-functioning [5].
- **China:** compliance rates of **99.61% (2021) and 99.88% (2022)** in the second cycle, improving on the first; **202 entities used flexible compliance mechanisms** and 3.72 million tonnes of CCER offsets were surrendered [3].
- **California:** covered entities surrender compliance instruments against multi-year obligations at high rates; no auction has triggered the price ceiling or a reserve sale, indicating ample compliance supply [2].

## COVID-19 handling and banking/allowance adjustments

The **EU ETS Market Stability Reserve (MSR)** absorbed the surplus that built up during the pandemic-era demand drop, continuing to **remove surplus allowances from circulation and invalidate allowances held in the reserve** [5]. The MSR's elevated intake rate cushioned price volatility.

**California's** design contained pandemic disruption through its **price floor and Allowance Price Containment Reserve**: when 2020 demand weakened, allowances that went unsold were held back and only gradually released after two consecutive auctions cleared above the floor, preventing oversupply [2]. **Banking is permitted** (allowances do not expire) but is bounded by a **holding limit** on the maximum allowances an entity or corporate group may hold [2].

**China** launched its national market in **July 2021**, after the worst of the pandemic, beginning trading in the power sector and completing its first two compliance cycles (2019–2020 and 2021–2022) over the period [3].

## CBAM and leakage mitigation

The three systems take divergent approaches to carbon leakage:

- **EU — CBAM plus free-allocation phase-out.** The **Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism entered its transitional phase on 1 October 2023** (first reporting period ending 31 January 2024), with the **definitive regime from 2026**. It covers **cement, iron and steel, aluminium, fertilisers, electricity, and hydrogen** — the goods at greatest leakage risk — and, when fully phased in, will capture more than 50% of emissions in ETS sectors. CBAM is explicitly aligned with the phase-out of free ETS allowances to those sectors [8].
- **California — free allocation and resource-shuffling rules.** California addresses leakage by allocating free allowances to leakage-exposed industry and by anti-"resource shuffling" rules in its electricity sector (preventing utilities from nominally re-sourcing cleaner power to evade obligations), rather than a border charge [2].
- **China — no border measure.** China's system has no border adjustment; its intensity-based free allocation effectively shields covered industry from competitiveness loss [3].

## Interaction with complementary policies and the waterbed effect

In each jurisdiction, renewable mandates and fuel-switching reduce demand for allowances, which in an absolute-cap system would simply free up allowances elsewhere — the "waterbed" effect. The **EU directly neutralises this through the MSR**, which withdraws and cancels surplus allowances so that complementary policies translate into permanently lower supply rather than merely cheaper allowances [5]. In 2023 the EU's renewable expansion and coal-to-gas switching both drove the record emissions drop and depressed EUA demand, contributing to the 2024 price fall [5]. **California** layers cap-and-trade beneath a Renewables Portfolio Standard (SB 100: 60% renewable electricity by 2030 and 100% clean — renewable plus zero-carbon — electricity by 2045) and related clean-energy mandates; because the cap is absolute and prices have largely sat near the floor with no reserve drawdown, complementary policies have done much of the heavy lifting while keeping allowance prices moderate [2]. **China's intensity design avoids the classic waterbed problem** because there is no fixed pool of allowances to redistribute — but for the same reason, efficiency mandates and coal controls show up as intensity improvement rather than a tightening absolute cap [3].

## Comparative synthesis

Ranking the systems on stringency and measured outcomes: the **EU ETS** leads on price, absolute cap stringency, verified absolute reductions, compliance, revenue scale, 100% earmarking, and the only operating border measure. **California** sits in the middle — an absolute cap with moderate prices, strong statutory earmarking (35% floor to priual populations), but reductions hard to isolate from complementary mandates. **China** is largest by covered volume but least stringent: intensity-based, free allocation, low prices, and efficiency-rather-than-absolute outcomes, with no border adjustment. The divergence is explained by the interaction of **price level** (EU highest, China lowest), **cap design** (absolute caps in the EU and California create scarcity that intensity benchmarking in China does not), and **design maturity** (the EU's two-decade-old market with an MSR and CBAM versus China's market only three years into operation).
