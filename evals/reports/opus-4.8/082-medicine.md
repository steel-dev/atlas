# Monitoring Strategy and Authorization Pathway for Suspected Intermittent High-Grade AV Block

## Bottom line

The clinical picture — a 68-year-old man with recurrent lightheadedness/fatigue, resting heart rate 52, **first-degree AV block with PR prolonging from 200 ms (3 weeks ago) to 240 ms now, plus undocumented "dropped beats"**, all beginning roughly 8 weeks after metoprolol 50 mg twice daily was started — points to **beta-blocker–associated AV conduction disease as the most likely reversible driver**, layered on possible intrinsic conduction disease that the drug has unmasked. The operative management moves are:

1. **Hold or stop metoprolol now** and substitute a non-AV-nodal-blocking antihypertensive (ACE inhibitor, ARB, dihydropyridine calcium-channel blocker, or thiazide), because the 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Bradycardia Guideline requires that reversible/drug causes be addressed **before** committing to a permanent pacemaker [1].
2. **Capture the rhythm with a real-time, alerting extended monitor — mobile cardiovascular telemetry (MCOT, CPT 93228–93229) or an extended external patch — not a 24–48 h Holter**, because his episodes are roughly weekly and the diagnostic priority is to characterize the dropped beats as Mobitz I (benign, AV-nodal) versus Mobitz II / high-grade block (dangerous, infranodal) [1][2].
3. **Do not simply discharge him to a 3-month outpatient queue.** Because he lives alone, drives daily, and has had four presentations with bradycardia and near-syncopal symptoms with as-yet-uncaptured dropped beats, the safest bridge is **inpatient/observation telemetry on this presentation** (or urgent ED-to-electrophysiology referral) plus **driving-restriction counseling**.
4. **Escalate immediately to a pacemaker/EP consult — not outpatient workup — if any of the following are captured: symptomatic Mobitz II, high-grade or 2:1 AV block, complete (third-degree) heart block, syncope with bradyarrhythmia, or pauses >3 seconds.** Per the guideline, Mobitz II/high-grade/third-degree block not attributable to a reversible cause warrants permanent pacing **regardless of symptoms** [1].

## 1. Why this is most likely drug-induced — and why that changes the pathway

The temporal sequence is the central analytical point. Metoprolol was started ~8 weeks ago when his prior cardiologist retired; symptom onset, resting HR 52, and progressive PR prolongation (200 → 240 ms over 3 weeks) all track with the drug. The 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Guideline lists beta-adrenergic receptor blockers first among medications that induce or exacerbate bradycardia and conduction disorders [1].

The reversible-cause workup is already largely complete and **negative**, which isolates metoprolol as the operative variable:

| Reversible cause | Value | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Potassium | 4.3 mmol/L | Normal — no hyperkalemia |
| Magnesium | 2.0 mg/dL | Normal |
| TSH | 2.8 mIU/L | Normal — hypothyroidism excluded |
| Creatinine | 1.1 mg/dL | Normal renal function (no drug-accumulation driver) |
| Ischemia | Serial troponins negative ×4 visits | Acute MI excluded |

With electrolytes, thyroid, renal function, and ischemia excluded, **the medication is the remaining modifiable variable** [1].

**The guideline's reversible-vs-irreversible logic (the crux):**
- COR I (B-NR): "Patients with transient or reversible causes of AV block, such as Lyme carditis or drug toxicity, should have medical therapy and supportive care, including temporary transvenous pacing if necessary, **before** determination of need for permanent pacing" [1].
- For sinus bradycardia/conduction disease from a non-essential drug, "a beta-blocking drug that is used solely to control hypertension but is causing significant bradycardia could be switched to a diuretic, angiotensin converting enzyme inhibitor, or an angiotensin receptor blocking drug that are devoid of negative chronotropic effect" [1].
- **Important caveat against over-attributing to the drug:** the guideline notes that *therapeutic* (not overdose) doses of beta-blockers/CCBs are "not commonly responsible for new AV block, and most patients in this scenario ultimately require permanent pacing" [1]. So withdrawal is necessary but may not be sufficient — if block persists or progresses after the drug is cleared, the conduction disease is intrinsic and pacing is indicated.
- And if a beta-blocker were genuinely required with no alternative, symptomatic AV block from guideline-directed therapy "for which there is no alternative treatment and continued treatment is clinically necessary" is itself a COR I indication for permanent pacing [1]. Here metoprolol is being used only for hypertension, where multiple non-chronotropic alternatives exist — so the correct first step is substitution, then monitoring.

**Practical move:** Stop or substitute metoprolol (e.g., amlodipine, lisinopril/losartan, or a thiazide — none of which appear on the guideline's bradycardia-inducing medication list, which does include non-dihydropyridine CCBs, clonidine, methyldopa, digoxin, amiodarone, and sotalol) and re-assess conduction off the drug [1].

## 2. Reading the rhythm: why the "dropped beats" raise the stakes

First-degree AV block (PR 240 ms) and Mobitz I (Wenckebach) are typically AV-nodal and **benign**; isolated, asymptomatic first-degree/Mobitz I/2:1 nodal block carries a COR III (Harm) recommendation against pacing [1]. The danger lies in **Mobitz II**, which is usually infranodal:

| Feature | First-degree | Mobitz I (Wenckebach) | Mobitz II |
|---|---|---|---|
| ECG hallmark | Fixed prolonged PR (>200 ms; "severe" >300 ms) | Progressive PR lengthening then a dropped beat | Constant PR with sudden non-conducted P, no warning |
| Level of block | AV node | AV node | Infranodal (His-Purkinje) |
| Progression to complete block | Low | Low | High |
| Pacing if symptomatic/irreversible | Reasonable only if marked + clearly symptomatic (COR IIa) | Reasonable only if clearly symptomatic (COR IIa) | Recommended regardless of symptoms if not reversible (COR I) [1] |

Mobitz II is "much more likely than Mobitz I to be associated with haemodynamic compromise, severe bradycardia and progression to 3rd degree heart block," its instability "may be sudden and unexpected, causing syncope (Stokes-Adams attacks) or sudden cardiac death," and the **risk of asystole is around 35% per year**; it "mandates immediate admission for cardiac monitoring, backup temporary pacing and ultimately insertion of a permanent pacemaker" [3].

The clinical problem is that his **dropped beats have never been captured on a telemetry strip**, so we do not yet know whether they are benign (Mobitz I) or dangerous (Mobitz II/high-grade). Their presence superimposed on first-degree block, in a symptomatic, bradycardic patient with PR that is actively progressing, **raises the pretest probability of advancing infranodal block** and is precisely why characterizing the dropped beats is the diagnostic objective — and why he should not be left undiagnosed in a 3-month queue.

## 3. Monitoring modality selection — match the device to ~weekly episodes

The guideline's COR I (B-NR) recommendation is to use rhythm monitoring "to establish correlation between heart rate or conduction abnormalities with symptoms, with the specific type of cardiac monitor chosen based on the frequency and nature of symptoms" [1]. Device choice is driven by symptom frequency:

| Modality | Recording window | Best fit | Fit for this patient |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Holter** (CPT 93224–93227) | ≤48 h | Daily/continuous symptoms | **Poor** — episodes are ~weekly; Holter yield for bradyarrhythmia is low (4% in 518 monitors, none showed advanced AV block) [1][2]. This is also the modality the insurer denies. |
| Extended external ECG (CPT 93241–93248) | >48 h–15 days | Less frequent symptoms | Reasonable |
| External patch | 2–14 days, leadless, single-lead, auto- + patient-trigger | Less frequent, ambulatory, compliance-sensitive | Good |
| External event monitor (CPT 93268–93272) | up to 30 days, loop memory | Infrequent symptoms | Good but patient-trigger dependent; the modality requiring prior auth |
| **Mobile cardiovascular telemetry / MCOT** (CPT 93228–93229) | up to 30 days, **real-time analysis, auto-detection, transmitted to a 24 h attended surveillance center** | "Spontaneous symptoms too brief, too subtle, or too infrequent to be documented with patient-activated monitors" and "high-risk patients whose rhythm requires real-time monitoring" [1][2] | **Preferred** |
| **Implantable loop recorder / ICM** (insertion CPT 33285) | up to ~3 years (battery 2–3 y), auto-detect + remote, no patient action required | "Infrequent symptoms (>30 days between symptoms)" if non-invasive workup non-diagnostic (COR IIa) [1] | Reserve for later if external monitoring is non-diagnostic |

**Recommendation:** **MCOT (or an extended alerting patch) is the modality of choice.** Its real-time auto-detection and 24/7 attended monitoring station give immediate alerting on high-grade block or pauses — the decisive advantage for a man who **lives alone, has no patient-activation reliability guarantee, and cannot make frequent clinic visits** [1][2]. The 30-day window comfortably brackets his roughly weekly events.

The **implantable loop recorder** is the modality reserved for genuinely *infrequent* events: the guideline supports an ICM (COR IIa, C-LD) "in patients with infrequent symptoms (>30 days between symptoms) suspected to be caused by bradycardia… if initial noninvasive evaluation is nondiagnostic," with up to ~3 years of continuous, auto-alerting, patient-effort-free monitoring [1]. Because his episodes are *more* frequent than monthly, the ILR is not first-line **now**, but it is the correct escalation if a 30-day external monitor is non-diagnostic — and it has a separate procedural CPT/coverage pathway (insertion CPT 33285, subcutaneous cardiac rhythm monitor [4]). Coverage of subcutaneous cardiac rhythm monitors is *addressed in* CMS NCD 20.15 (Electrocardiographic Services; NCDId 179, version 2), but that NCD treats ambulatory cardiac monitoring separately and there is **no NCD specific to subcutaneous cardiac rhythm monitors** — so per the Medtronic reimbursement guide, coverage is determined by local Medicare Administrative Contractor (MAC) discretion with documentation of medical necessity required, rather than by a national rule guaranteeing ILR coverage for unexplained syncope [4]. This pathway runs separately from the external-monitor authorization channel [2][4].

## 4. Authorization pathway — overturning the Holter denial

The denial logic ("Holter denied without documented arrhythmia; event monitors require prior auth") is addressable on its own terms. The governing Medicare policy is **LCD L39490 (Ambulatory Electrocardiograph (AECG) Monitoring)**, with coding guidance in **CMS Billing and Coding Article A59268**, both tied to **NCD 20.15 – Electrocardiographic Services (NCDId 179, version 2)** [2][5]. Article A59268's "Table of Various AECG Monitors" sets the exact codes: **Holter (≤48 h) = CPT 93224–93227**; extended external ECG >48 h–7 days = 93241–93244 and >7–15 days = 93245–93248; **external patient-/auto-activated event recorders (loop memory, remote download or 24-h attended, up to 30 days) = CPT 93268–93272**; **MCOT = CPT 93228–93229**; subcutaneous cardiac rhythm monitor = 93298 [2]. Article A59268 states that coverage requires the record to "support the presence of specific symptoms such as **syncope, dizziness, shortness of breath, chest discomfort, palpitations, other abnormal test results which would indicate the need for extended monitoring, or an underlying history of cardiac disease**," and explicitly requires documentation supporting "the medical necessity for greater than 24-hour monitoring and… the type of monitoring utilized" [2].

This patient already satisfies those criteria, and the documentation move is to **make the record carry the values that meet medical necessity**:
- **Symptomatic bradycardia** (recurrent lightheadedness/fatigue; HR 52) and near-syncope;
- **Documented conduction abnormality**: first-degree AV block with PR prolongation **200 → 240 ms**, plus observed dropped beats — an "abnormal test result indicating the need for extended monitoring";
- **Four ED presentations** establishing recurrence and an underlying conduction disorder.

The coverage standard rewards **extended, type-appropriate monitoring**, not a 24–48 h Holter. The strongest authorization request therefore is **not** to re-fight for the denied Holter but to **request MCOT (93228–93229) or an extended patch directly**, with the chart documenting symptomatic bradycardia + the captured PR prolongation/dropped beats + the rationale that a Holter is inadequate to his episode frequency and that real-time alerting is medically necessary given he lives alone. CMS notes 93228/93229 are reported once per 30 days [2]. The captured abnormal ECG, properly documented, is exactly the "documented arrhythmia"/abnormal test result the payer wants to see.

## 5. Safety bridge — do not discharge into a 3-month queue

Two safety issues make another simple discharge inappropriate.

**(a) Capture the rhythm now, in a monitored setting.** Given four prior negative discharges, an actively progressing PR interval, bradycardia, and uncaptured dropped beats of unknown type, **inpatient admission or observation-status telemetry on this visit** is the highest-yield and safest way to characterize the dropped beats before he goes home — and it sidesteps the outpatient authorization delay entirely. If Mobitz II, high-grade/2:1 block, third-degree block, or pauses are captured, he is already in the right place for temporary pacing and pacemaker consultation [1][3].

**(b) Driving restriction counseling.** He drives daily and lives alone, so an unwitnessed syncopal event carries third-party risk. State driver-fitness guidance is explicit: **"Syncope due to bradyarrhythmia… untreated bradycardia-induced syncope precludes the operation of private, cargo transport and passenger transport vehicles,"** and driving may be re-evaluated only "after successful treatment is accomplished either by removal of the medication causing bradycardic syncope or establishment of appropriate pacemaker function" [6]. The same guide imposes a **6-month no-drive period after a single episode of unexplained syncope**, lifted if the cause is found and corrected, and a **1-year** period for recurrent non-vasovagal syncope (≥2 episodes in 6 months) [6]. (Thresholds are from the Texas Medical Advisory Board guide; specific durations vary by state DMV, and the 2017 ACC/AHA/HRS Syncope Guideline contains the corresponding driving-and-syncope recommendation [6].) The practical counseling: advise him not to drive until the rhythm is characterized and the reversible driver (metoprolol) is removed or the conduction disease is treated.

## 6. Expedited cardiology/EP access — converting routine to urgent

The 3-month new-patient wait reflects *routine* triage; this patient's documentation should reclassify him as **urgent**:
- **ED-to-EP direct referral / urgent electrophysiology consult** from the current presentation, rather than a routine outpatient cardiology referral, justified by recurrent symptomatic bradycardia, progressive conduction disease, and uncaptured high-grade-block-suspicious dropped beats.
- If admitted/observed, the **inpatient EP consult** occurs in-house, bypassing the outpatient queue.
- The documentation that drives this is the same that supports monitor authorization: HR 52, PR 200→240 ms, dropped beats, four ED visits, negative reversible-cause workup — a record that reads as evolving conduction disease, not a stable referral.

## 7. Thresholds: immediate pacemaker/EP consult vs. outpatient workup

| Finding | Disposition | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Mobitz II AV block** (symptomatic) | **Immediate** admission, telemetry, backup temporary pacing, pacemaker consult | High asystole risk (~35%/yr); pacing recommended regardless of symptoms if not reversible [1][3] |
| **High-grade or 2:1 AV block** | **Immediate** consult | COR I pacing if not reversible [1] |
| **Complete (third-degree) heart block** | **Immediate** consult | COR I pacing if not reversible [1] |
| **Syncope with documented bradyarrhythmia** | **Immediate** consult + driving restriction | Symptomatic bradycardia indication; driving precluded [1][6] |
| **Pauses >3 seconds** | **Immediate** consult | High-risk finding requiring real-time monitoring/escalation [1] |
| First-degree AV block, asymptomatic | Outpatient; do not pace | COR III: Harm [1] |
| Mobitz I (Wenckebach), asymptomatic or symptoms not temporally correlated | Outpatient; do not pace | COR III: Harm [1] |
| Marked first-degree or Mobitz I **with symptoms clearly attributable** | Outpatient EP evaluation; pacing reasonable (COR IIa) after reversible causes addressed | [1] |

The unifying rule from the 2018 guideline: **acquired Mobitz II, high-grade, or third-degree AV block not attributable to a reversible or physiologic cause warrants permanent pacing regardless of symptoms** [1]. Everything in this patient's plan — stopping metoprolol, alerting telemetry, monitored capture — exists to determine which side of that line his dropped beats fall on, safely and quickly.
