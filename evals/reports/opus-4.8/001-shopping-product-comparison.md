# Temperature-Controlled Vaccine Transport for a West African Cold Chain: Comparative Evaluation

## Executive summary

For a pharmaceutical cold chain spanning Accra, Lagos, Dakar and Abidjan, the three "vendor families" in scope solve fundamentally different problems and are only partly substitutable:

- **Thermo King** and **Carrier Transicold** make **active, engine- or battery-powered transport refrigeration** for trucks, trailers and last-mile vans. They are engineered to **Good Distribution Practice (GDP)** standards, carry CE/ATP/TÜV certification, and offer sophisticated telematics — but **none of their products appear on the WHO PQS list** [1]. They depend on diesel, the vehicle alternator, or onboard batteries, and so their "no-electricity" autonomy is finite (≈12 h on one battery for a Thermo King ColdCube; longer for the Carrier Vector eCool) [2][3].
- **Off-grid / passive alternatives** — WHO-PQS passive cold boxes and vaccine carriers (B Medical, AOV, Nilkamal, Apex, Blowkings, Qingdao Aucma ARKTEK), PCM rolling containers (Aircontainer Bigbox, Dometic PCM packs), and solar direct-drive (SDD) refrigerators (B Medical, Sure Chill/Godrej) — are the only category that is actually **WHO PQS-listed**, that needs **no electricity at all during transit**, and that routinely exceeds **12 h of holdover even at +43 °C** [1][4][5].
- For **maintaining WHO Prequalification standards on a 12+ hour journey with no electricity**, the passive PQS boxes are the natural backbone, paired with a **WHO-PQS temperature-monitoring device** such as Nexleaf **ColdTrace Transport** (E006-102, prequalified Aug 2025) [6][7]. The active reefers are best understood as the **upstream bulk-haul layer** (regional warehouse to city hub), not the last leg into power-poor areas.

The evidence base supports a **hybrid recommendation**: active reefers (with Carrier's dual cellular/satellite telematics as the strongest connectivity option) for trunk routes, and **WHO-PQS passive boxes plus a PQS logger** for the off-grid last mile, where controlled-temperature-chain (CTC) economics show the lowest cost per dose [8][9].

## 1. Thermo King — pharmaceutical/last-mile offering

Thermo King markets pharmaceutical transport under its **TK PharmaSolutions** platform, which is built around **GDP compliance** plus application know-how and qualification services [2]. The product most relevant to off-grid last-mile delivery is the **ColdCube** portable container range [2]:

| Attribute | ColdCube specification |
|---|---|
| Models | 5 total — horizontal 140 L / 330 L / 720 L; vertical 565 L / 760 L (front-opening) |
| Temperature control | Freeze / cool / heat, precision to **1 °C**; insulation up to 130 mm; R134a or R404A |
| **Off-grid autonomy** | Battery-powered: **up to 12 h on one battery, up to 99 h with two batteries**; vehicle-powered and hybrid options also available |
| Certification | **GDP, ATP, CE, TÜV** (model-dependent FRCX/FRAX/BRBX); 2-yr parts & labour warranty |
| Monitoring | TracKing + TK Reefer telematics: real-time temperature, GPS position, out-of-range alerts; Bluetooth + TouchLog data logging |

Critically, ColdCube and the wider TK PharmaSolutions range are **GDP/ATP-certified, not WHO PQS-listed** — no Thermo King product appears in the WHO PQS E004 catalogue [1]. The 12 h single-battery figure exactly meets, but does not comfortably exceed, the 12+ hour no-electricity requirement; the two-battery (99 h) configuration is needed for genuine margin in West African heat [2].

## 2. Carrier Transicold — diesel, electric and autonomous units

Carrier Transicold's range is truck/trailer-scale active refrigeration [3]:

- **Supra diesel truck family** — vehicle-powered, direct-drive units.
- **Vector** trailer units — Carrier's all-electric *architecture* (diesel-electric).
- **eCool** suite — low-to-ultra-low-emission electric truck/trailer units.
- **Vector eCool** — described as the *first fully autonomous, all-electric, engineless refrigerated trailer*, charged from the trailer axle, i.e. it can run without an idling engine [3].

These are pitched for GDP-compliant pharmaceutical cold chains, but like Thermo King they are **not WHO PQS-listed** — truck reefers are outside the PQS device categories [1]. Their "no-electricity" story is conditional: diesel units need fuel; electric/eCool units need the towing vehicle's motion or grid charging. None offers the days-long passive holdover of a PQS cold box. Carrier's autonomy advantage over Thermo King is the engineless Vector eCool for trailer-scale loads, not last-mile vans.

## 3. Innovative off-grid alternatives

The off-grid category is where WHO PQS coverage and true zero-electricity operation actually live. Four sub-types are evidenced:

**(a) WHO-PQS passive cold boxes and vaccine carriers (category E004).** 74 prequalified products are listed [1]. Representative cold life at **+43 °C** ambient (the relevant West African worst case):

| PQS code | Manufacturer / model | Capacity (L) | Cold life @ +43 °C |
|---|---|---|---|
| E004-041 | Qingdao Aucma **ARKTEK YBC-5** | 5.4 | **840 h (~35 days)** |
| E004-046 | Apex AICB-316L | 22.4 | 153:05 |
| E004-018 | Blowkings CB-12-CF | 12.0 | 156:00 |
| E004-036 | Nilkamal RCB 444L-A | 20.25 | 152:28 |
| E004-005 | B Medical **RCW25** | 20.0 | 134:40 |
| E004-004 | B Medical RCW12 | 7.0 | 114:50 |
| E004-009 | AOV AVC-46 (carrier) | 2.5 | 50:12 |
| E004-008 | AOV AVC-44 (carrier) | 1.35 | 40:02 |

Even the small vaccine *carriers* (40–51 h) and certainly the large cold boxes (110–156 h) vastly exceed a 12 h no-electricity journey at full +43 °C ambient [1].

**(b) PCM-based passive coolers.** B Medical's transport boxes can run on **+5 °C PCM** elements; at +43 °C with a 20 L load, cold life is ~10 h (14 elements), 24.3 h (18) or 40.7 h (24) in the +2–8 °C band — so ≥18 elements clears a 12 h trip in peak heat [4]. They conform to ADR/RID/IMDG/ICAO-TI/IATA-DGR and are marketed as WHO-PQS-certified (E004/005) [4]. The commercial **VeriCor Cool Cube 96 (VT-96)** holds 2–8 °C for **126+ hours** with no electricity, ice or buffering, at **US$2,399**, with an optional dual-zone data-logger kit ($239) [10]. PATH's Optimize work documents Dometic PCM packs (Tunisia) and the **Aircontainer Bigbox** 161 L rolling PCM container used on Senegal's "moving warehouses," which keep vaccines in range for a **work week** and have **lower maintenance burden and higher reliability than active-refrigerated trucks on poor roads** [5].

**(c) Dry-ice systems.** B Medical's RCW25 can be loaded with ~23 kg dry ice to carry frozen/ultra-cold products (e.g., Pfizer-format boxes) for multi-day holdover [4].

**(d) Solar direct-drive (SDD) refrigerators/freezers** — for fixed nodes along the route, not the vehicle:
- B Medical **TCW 40R SDD** fridge: 36 L, **autonomy 81 h54 / hold-over 93 h24 at +43 °C**, PQS E003/073, Grade A freeze protection, 10-yr warranty, integrated digital monitoring, 2×200 W panels [4]; the matching **TFW 40 SDD** freezer gives 120 h autonomy at +43 °C.
- **Sure Chill** (water-at-4 °C density principle): WHO-PQS **Godrej GVR 100 DC (E003/050)**, hot-climate-zone SDD; the technology delivers **>10 days holdover** with as little as 4 h electricity/day, eliminating backup generators where the grid gives ≥8 h/day [5].

## 4. Reliability and holdover over a 12+ hour, no-electricity journey at West African ambient

| Solution | No-grid mechanism | 12 h @ ~+43 °C? | Failure/reliability notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| TK ColdCube | Battery (1×=12 h, 2×=99 h) | Marginal on 1 battery; comfortable on 2 | Depends on battery state-of-charge; precision ±1 °C [2] |
| Carrier Supra/Vector | Diesel / engine / axle | Yes while fuelled/moving | Fuel supply, generator maintenance are documented weak points in the region [3][5] |
| PCM cold box (B Medical, ≥18 elements) | Latent heat of PCM | Yes (24–41 h) | No moving parts; rugged rotomoulded shell [4] |
| WHO-PQS cold box (e.g., RCW25, ARKTEK) | Ice/thermal mass | Easily (115 h–35 days) | Field-proven; passive = high reliability on poor roads [1][5] |
| Aircontainer Bigbox (PCM, 161 L) | Pre-chilled PCM panels | Yes (≈1 work week) | Explicitly **more reliable than active reefer trucks on bad roads** [5] |

The structural finding: **passive PQS/PCM solutions dominate on no-electricity reliability** because they have no engine, fuel line, alternator or charge state to fail; active reefers introduce exactly the failure modes (fuel, generators, electronics) that PATH flags as problematic in intermittent-power West African contexts [5]. The Togo field data underline the stakes: ~30 % of Central Region health centres had **no refrigerator** and ~19 % had **broken/unused** units [8].

## 5. Real-time temperature monitoring

| Provider | Channels | Features | PQS status |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Carrier Lynx Fleet** | **4G LTE + satellite (auto-switching)** | Real-time GPS, remote setpoint control (APX), exception alerts (temp/geofence/low fuel-battery), digital records, predictive maintenance; backup battery + solar; works with other makers' units; tiered monthly plans (Monitor / +Control / +Enhanced) with 1-way data at 15-min intervals baseline | Not PQS (GDP/FSMA-oriented) [9] |
| **Thermo King TracKing / TK Reefer** | Cellular + Bluetooth | Real-time temperature, GPS, out-of-range alerts, TouchLog logging | Not PQS [2] |
| **Passive boxes** | None built in | Must pair with an external logger | n/a [1] |
| **Nexleaf ColdTrace Transport** | Cellular | Real-time transport temperature monitoring, driver alerts mid-trip, app + platform; piloted/scaling in Tanzania with Gavi | **WHO-PQS E006-102, prequalified 05 Aug 2025** [6][7] |

For the specified cities, **Carrier's dual cellular/satellite Lynx is the strongest answer to West Africa's connectivity dead zones**, but it is a GDP/fleet tool, not a PQS device. To remain **WHO-PQS-compliant** on the monitored leg, the passive boxes should carry a **PQS E006 logger such as ColdTrace Transport**, which is purpose-built for low-infrastructure transport and is the only monitoring product here actually on the PQS list [6][7][9].

## 6. Service, parts and technician networks in Accra, Lagos, Dakar, Abidjan

The sources confirm both OEMs run **large dealer/franchise networks** but do not confirm a dedicated pharma/PQS service desk in each of the four target cities:

- **Carrier Transicold**: "**over 900 locations throughout Europe, Middle East and Africa**," billed as the largest refrigerated truck/trailer service network, with **OneCALL 24/7 assistance** and **BluEdge** service agreements; coverage is found via the ttdealers locator [3]. Carrier's official Ghana distributor **Coolink Ltd (Hitti Group, 60+ years)** is identified, though primarily for air-conditioning rather than Transicold reefers.
- **Thermo King**: an EMEA **Dealer Service Network** emphasising proactive maintenance, consistent quality and rapid response, accessed through its dealer locator; in **Nigeria**, **Integrated Motors Industries (IMI)** — a 20+-year cold-chain firm with a 2-acre facility at **Oshodi, Lagos** — sells, services and repairs Thermo King V-series (direct-drive) and T-series (independent-engine) units, both at its premises and on the customer's site, with an electric-standby option [11]. IMI's stated focus is perishable food transport (meat, poultry, vegetables) rather than vaccine/PQS work, so vaccine-grade qualification would need confirming [11].
- **Passive PQS boxes and SDD fridges** carry low field-maintenance burden by design (no engine/refrigerant circuit on passive units; B Medical SDD has a **10-year warranty**), which PATH notes is a reliability advantage on poor roads [4][5].

**Gap/limitation:** the fetched sources do **not** give city-by-city technician counts or contractual response times for Accra, Lagos, Dakar and Abidjan. This should be verified directly against the live Carrier and Thermo King dealer locators and local distributor contracts before procurement.

## 7. WHO Prequalification (PQS) standards and which products comply

WHO's **Performance, Quality and Safety (PQS)** programme sets equipment standards and type-testing protocols for vaccine cold-chain devices, and WHO recommends purchasing PQS-prequalified equipment [5]. Relevant categories and defined metrics:

- **E003** — refrigerators/freezers (incl. electric, ice-lined, gas/kerosene, **solar**) [5].
- **E004** — **passive cold boxes and vaccine carriers**; 74 listed products, rated by capacity, **cold life at +43 °C**, and minimum rated ambient [1].
- **E006** — temperature-monitoring devices [6].
- B Medical defines the governing metrics: **hold-over time** (hours all points stay +2 to +8 °C at max rated ambient after power loss), **cold life** (lid-close until warmest point hits +10 °C at constant +43 °C), and solar **autonomy** (days held under low solar radiation) [4].

**PQS-listed in this evaluation:** B Medical RCW4/RCW8/RCW12/RCW25 cold boxes and TCW40R SDD (E003/073) [1][4]; AOV, Nilkamal, Apex, Blowkings, Giostyle, EBAC, Paracoat, Qingdao carriers/boxes including ARKTEK YBC-5 (E004-041) [1]; Sure Chill/Godrej GVR 100 DC (E003/050) [5]; Nexleaf ColdTrace Transport (E006-102) [6].
**Not PQS-listed:** all Thermo King and Carrier Transicold transport units and their telematics (GDP/ATP/CE/TÜV/FSMA instead) [2][3][1][9].

## 8. Total cost per vaccine dose delivered

No source gives a like-for-like delivered-cost-per-dose across the three OEM families; the strongest cost evidence is the PATH/WHO **Togo MenAfriVac field study (2014 USD)** on controlled-temperature-chain vs full cold-chain logistics [8]:

| Approach | Logistics cost per dose (2014 USD) |
|---|---|
| **CTC** (vaccine held ≤+40 °C for ≤4 days before use) | **$0.026 ± 0.032** |
| **Full cold-chain logistics (CCL)** | **$0.029 ± 0.054** (not statistically different) |
| Hypothetical daily refrigerated deliveries (no CTC) for fridge-less facilities | **$0.063** (range $0.007–$0.33), worst for remote sites |

The takeaway: **for remote, power-poor facilities, off-grid/passive CTC-style delivery is the cheapest per dose**, and forcing an active-refrigeration / daily-delivery model roughly **doubles or more** the last-mile cost [8]. Component cost drivers to weigh for the four-city network:

- **Capital**: a passive PCM cooler is ~US$2,399 (Cool Cube 96) [10] versus a far higher (unquoted) capital outlay for ColdCube units and truck/trailer reefers.
- **Energy/fuel**: zero for passive boxes; diesel/electric recurring cost for Carrier/TK active units [3].
- **Monitoring fees**: Carrier Lynx requires a **recurring monthly service plan** (tiered) [9]; PQS loggers are a one-off device cost.
- **Maintenance**: passive boxes/SDD have low burden and long warranties (B Medical 10-yr) [4]; active reefers carry generator/refrigerant servicing and fuel-supply risk [5].
- **Wastage/spoilage**: driven by reliability and monitoring; the Togo study also compared closed-vial wastage between CTC and CCL, and PATH documents large losses from poorly monitored domestic refrigerators — reinforcing that **monitoring + reliable holdover protect dose value** more than headline equipment cost [8][5].

## Bottom line and ranking against your four criteria

1. **12+ h no-electricity reliability** — **Passive WHO-PQS / PCM boxes win decisively** (40 h–35 days holdover at +43 °C, no failure-prone engine); Carrier Vector eCool > TK ColdCube (2-battery) > TK ColdCube (1-battery, only marginal) among active units [1][4][2][3][5].
2. **Real-time monitoring in patchy networks** — **Carrier Lynx (cellular+satellite)** is technically strongest but not PQS; **Nexleaf ColdTrace Transport** is the PQS-compliant logger to pair with passive boxes [9][6][7].
3. **Maintenance in the four cities** — both OEMs have broad EMEA dealer networks (Carrier 900+ sites, OneCALL 24/7; Thermo King via IMI in Nigeria); passive/solar gear has the lowest maintenance need — **but city-level response times remain unverified in these sources** [3][4][5].
4. **Cost per dose at WHO-PQS standard** — **off-grid/CTC passive delivery is cheapest per dose (~$0.026) and is the only PQS-listed path**; active reefers add fuel and recurring monitoring fees and are not PQS [8][1][9].

**Recommended architecture:** Carrier (Vector/Supra with Lynx) or Thermo King reefers for bulk trunk legs between city hubs, then **WHO-PQS passive cold boxes (e.g., B Medical RCW25 / ARKTEK / PCM Bigbox-style containers) plus a PQS E006 logger (ColdTrace Transport)** for every 12+ hour off-grid last leg, with B Medical/Sure Chill **SDD refrigerators** anchoring storage at fridge-less nodes.
