# This sounds like a possible bowel obstruction — go to the ER now, do not ride it out at home

**Short answer: Based on what you describe, this warrants emergency evaluation now, not home management with clear liquids.** The combination you describe — a known intestinal narrowing (stricture) being watched, intense doubled-over cramping, more than 24 hours of vomiting up everything you eat or drink, and signs of dehydration (very dry mouth, lightheaded on standing) — fits the classic picture of a *bowel obstruction*, which is a recognized surgical emergency in Crohn's disease, not a typical inflammatory flare [1][2][3]. This is general information and not a substitute for being evaluated in person; given an acute presentation like this, the safe move is to be seen urgently.

## Why this looks different from your usual flare

You already sense this "feels different," and that instinct is worth acting on. Guidance for people with Crohn's explicitly notes that patients "usually know their pattern of symptoms" and should treat new or worse symptoms as a reason to seek help [4]. Several features here point toward mechanical obstruction rather than ordinary inflammation:

- **You have a known stricture.** Crohn's inflammation produces scar tissue that narrows the bowel into a stricture; if a stricture becomes too narrow — or if food that is hard to digest gets stuck at it — it can block the intestine and cause a bowel obstruction [1]. A scope six months ago showing "narrowing they were watching" means you already have the anatomical setup for obstruction, which raises the likelihood that this episode is an obstruction rather than a routine flare.
- **The symptom triad matches.** Small bowel obstruction classically presents with crampy/colicky abdominal pain that can become constant and severe, vomiting, abdominal distension, and reduced passage of gas/stool [2]. Obstructive symptoms from a Crohn's stricture often come on shortly after eating [1].
- **Persistent vomiting plus dehydration is itself a danger sign.** With obstruction, "persistent vomiting can lead to dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, and metabolic alkalosis," and a proximal (higher) obstruction in particular causes significant vomiting with "rapid onset of dehydration and electrolyte imbalances" [2].

Healthline's Crohn's obstruction guidance states that "partial or complete blockages typically require urgent medical attention," that "an intestinal obstruction can be a medical emergency," and lists *intractable vomiting*, high fever, and inability to pass gas or stool as indicators [1].

## Your dehydration signs are the moderate-to-severe kind

The specific symptoms you mention are the ones clinicians use to flag dehydration that needs urgent treatment:

- **Dry mouth** and **dizziness/lightheadedness on standing that doesn't go away** are listed as signs of *severe* dehydration [5].
- Moderate-to-severe dehydration also includes dizziness, raised heart rate, low blood pressure, and decreased urination [6].
- Untreated, severe dehydration "needs urgent treatment" and can cause complications including kidney problems and low blood pressure [5].

A practical self-check on the way: reduced urination / dark urine is another marker of significant fluid loss [6][5].

## Why "clear liquids and ride it out" is the wrong and potentially dangerous plan here

1. **You can't keep anything down, so oral rehydration cannot work.** Oral rehydration solutions and clear fluids only help if you can retain them — the advice is to "keep drinking small amounts of fluids regularly" [5], and ORS is the recommended route only for those who can take fluids by mouth [6]. With more than 24 hours of vomiting everything, that route has already failed, and you are losing fluid you cannot replace. That is precisely the situation that needs **IV fluids** instead [2].
2. **Eating/drinking can worsen a suspected obstruction.** If you have a stricture, food that's hard to digest can lodge at the narrowing and trigger the blockage [1], and obstructive symptoms characteristically flare after meals [1]. The treatment for obstruction is the opposite of oral intake — **bowel rest and nasogastric (NG) decompression** [1][2][3].
3. **An obstruction can progress while you wait.** Even a partial bowel obstruction "is a serious problem that requires immediate medical attention" [1]. Without prompt treatment, the blocked bowel "can start to die," leading to serious complications including sepsis; a complete obstruction can also cause perforation (a hole in the intestinal wall), which can be fatal [1]. Mechanistically, rising pressure behind the blockage compromises blood flow, causing ischemia, necrosis, and — if untreated — perforation, peritonitis, sepsis, and potentially death [2].

This is the time-sensitivity that makes "ER now" the right call rather than waiting.

## What the ER is likely to do (so you know what to expect)

For someone presenting with concern for bowel obstruction, the standard workup and initial treatment are well established:

- **Labs:** CBC, basic metabolic panel (electrolytes/kidney function), and lactate; abnormal values such as leukocytosis or lactic acidosis raise concern for bowel ischemia [7]. Persistent vomiting can derange electrolytes and cause metabolic alkalosis [2].
- **Imaging:** CT of the abdomen and pelvis with IV contrast is the standard study; CT is the "gold standard" for identifying the transition point and detecting ischemia or perforation [2][7].
- **IV fluid and electrolyte resuscitation** to restore intravascular volume and correct imbalances — the rehydration route you can't achieve by mouth right now [2][7].
- **NG tube decompression and bowel rest** to relieve distension and vomiting and reduce aspiration risk [1][2][3]. Many short-lived small bowel obstructions improve within about 48 hours of NG tube placement [1].
- **Surgical consultation** — for Crohn's/IBD, guidance is to involve colorectal surgery [7]. Urgent surgery is indicated for signs of ischemia/strangulation, peritonitis, or an obstruction that doesn't resolve [2][7]. In stricturing Crohn's specifically, initial management once abscess/perforation are excluded is often medical (NG decompression and steroids), but a large share of patients ultimately need an intervention — roughly two-thirds of stricturing-Crohn's obstructions don't resolve with medical therapy alone, and about 75% of acute Crohn's strictures eventually require endoscopic or surgical treatment [3].

Note that your known stricture also changes routine self-care advice: anti-diarrheal medicines may be unsafe when you have a bowel narrowing, so this is not a situation to self-medicate through [5].

## How to get there — drive or call 911?

Lightheadedness when you stand that doesn't resolve is a sign of significant dehydration [5]. If you feel faint, near to passing out, or otherwise can't safely transport yourself, call 911/an ambulance rather than driving — actual loss of consciousness is an explicit "call 911" trigger [4]. If you can be driven safely by someone else, that is reasonable, but do not drive yourself if you are feeling faint. Either way, go now rather than continuing to try clear liquids at home.

## Bottom line

- The picture of **known stricture + severe cramping + 24+ hours of vomiting everything + dehydration signs** strongly suggests a **bowel obstruction**, a Crohn's surgical emergency — not your usual flare [1][2][3].
- **Clear liquids at home will not fix this and may make it worse:** you can't retain fluids, oral intake can aggravate an obstruction, and the needed treatments (IV fluids, NG decompression, bowel rest, urgent imaging) are only available in the hospital [1][6][5][2].
- **Waiting risks progression** to complete obstruction, bowel ischemia, perforation, and sepsis [1][2].
- **Go to the ER now.** Call 911 if you feel faint, nearly pass out, or can't get there safely [4][5].

*This is general health information, not a diagnosis or a substitute for in-person emergency evaluation. Given the acuity of what you describe, please be seen now.*
