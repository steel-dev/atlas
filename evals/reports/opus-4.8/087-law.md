# Vicarious Liability of the Delivery Platform for the Courier's Collision: Summary-Judgment Analysis

## Bottom line

**Summary judgment for the company should be denied.** The facts present genuine disputes on the two controlling questions — whether the courier was an employee rather than an independent contractor, and whether the collision occurred within the scope of his work — and under Fed. R. Civ. P. 56(a) a court may grant judgment only where "there is no genuine dispute as to any material fact" and the movant "is entitled to judgment as a matter of law" [1]. Worker classification is "a mixed question of law and fact that must typically be resolved by a jury," and a moving defendant prevails only if every material fact and inference is undisputed and a reasonable jury "could reach but one conclusion" [2]. Here the control indicia point in opposing directions, so the vicarious-liability question goes to the factfinder. This tracks the dispositions in the two leading gig-courier cases: *O'Connor v. Uber* (N.D. Cal. 2015) denied the platform's summary-judgment motion [2], and *Razak v. Uber* (3d Cir. 2020) vacated a grant of summary judgment that had classified couriers as contractors [3].

Because these indicia "point in opposing directions" and several rest on disputed facts about the platform's control over the "manner and means" of performance, neither party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law [2].

## 1. Respondeat superior and the employee/contractor line

Under respondeat superior, an employer or principal is vicariously liable for the wrongful acts of an employee or agent where the act (1) occurs within the scope of employment and (2) confers some benefit on the employer; the doctrine applies to employees but **not** to independent contractors [6]. A plaintiff must establish both a master–servant relationship and that the negligent conduct fell within the scope of employment [4]. Classification is therefore the threshold issue: if the courier is a contractor, the platform is generally not vicariously liable for his collision.

## 2. The control test (Restatement (Second) of Agency §220)

The common-law test turns on control. A "servant" is one whose physical conduct in performing services is subject to the other's "control or right to control" (§220(1)) [4]. Section 220(2) lists ten factors, none dispositive: (a) extent of control the master may exercise over the details of the work; (b) whether the worker is engaged in a distinct occupation or business; (c) whether the work is usually done under the employer's direction or by a specialist without supervision; (d) skill required; (e) who supplies the instrumentalities, tools, and place of work; (f) length of time employed; (g) method of payment (by time or by job); (h) whether the work is part of the employer's regular business; (i) whether the parties believe they are creating a master–servant relation; and (j) whether the principal is in business [4]. The "most significant consideration" is the right to control work details, and courts ask "**not how much control a hirer exercises, but how much control the hirer retains the right to exercise**" [2].

Applied here, the control factors are precisely the kind that defeat summary judgment:

- **App-mandated routes** and **no pricing discretion** go directly to control over the means of performance. In *Razak*, the Third Circuit reversed summary judgment in part because Uber controlled drivers' territory, fare cancellation, and "advertising and price setting" — "ost of the factors that determine an UberBLACK driver's Uber-profit … are controlled by Uber" — making a reasonable verdict for the workers possible [3]. A courier who must follow algorithmically dictated routes and has no pricing discretion presents the same retained control.
- **Algorithmic auto-suspension for declining two consecutive orders** and **automated acceptance-rate warnings affecting account standing** are the modern analogue of the right to discharge or discipline. The right to terminate at will is "perhaps the strongest evidence of the right to control" and the "most important" factor [2]; in *O'Connor* the court treated Uber's practice of deactivating underperforming drivers as strong control evidence [2]. An algorithm that forcibly suspends a courier for two declined orders is at least as coercive a control mechanism.
- **Company-branded gear** supports employee status as both a tool/instrumentality and an indicium of integration into the business [4].
- **Setting his own schedule** favors contractor status, but only weakly: "the fact that a certain amount of freedom is allowed or is inherent in the nature of the work … does not preclude a finding of employment status," and discretion over when to work is "not inconsistent" with employee status [2].
- **Personal vehicle** favors contractor status but is **not dispositive** — FedEx and several courier defendants required drivers to supply their own vehicles yet the workers were (or could be) found employees [2].

## 3. The label is not dispositive; courts look to operational and economic reality

The independent-contractor agreement's prohibition on "employees" does not control. "The label placed by the parties on their relationship is not dispositive, and subterfuges are not countenanced" (*Borello*), and " party's use of a label to describe a relationship with a worker … will be ignored where the evidence of the parties' actual conduct establishes that a different relationship exists" — even a contract expressly disclaiming employment and presented on a take-it-or-leave-it basis [5][2]. Courts instead examine "the circumstances of the whole activity" to decide whether, "as a matter of economic reality, the individuals are dependent upon the business to which they render service" [3]. The courier's **55-hour weekly workload** supplies the permanence/economic-dependence and "integral to the business" factors of that economic-reality inquiry [3]: a worker devoting that many hours is plausibly dependent on the platform, and delivery is the platform's core service — as the *Razak* district court put it of Uber, "if could not find drivers, would not be able to function" [3].

## 4. Gig-economy / ABC analogues and algorithmic control

In jurisdictions applying the **ABC test** adopted in *Dynamex Operations West v. Superior Court* (and codified in California's AB5), the worker is **presumed an employee** unless the hiring entity proves all three prongs: (A) the worker is free from the entity's control and direction in fact and under contract; (B) the work is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business; and (C) the worker is customarily engaged in an independently established business of the same nature [7]. Two prongs are fatal here on these facts: a delivery platform cannot show prong B because delivery **is** its usual course of business, and the app-mandated routes, suspension algorithm, and acceptance-rate enforcement undercut prong A's "free from control" requirement [7]. Because the hiring entity bears the burden and must satisfy every prong, the ABC framework makes employee status still more likely than under the common-law balancing test [7]. Even under the federal **economic-reality** standard, the analysis is comparable and no single factor is determinative [3][8]. Algorithmic management — automated routing, automated discipline, automated acceptance-rate scoring — is best understood as evidence of the right to control the manner and means of work, the heart of both the common-law control test and ABC prong A [2][7].

## 5. Scope of employment and benefit to the employer

Even assuming employee status, vicarious liability requires that the collision fall within the scope of employment. Under Restatement (Second) of Agency §228, conduct is within scope only if (a) it is of the kind the worker is employed to perform, (b) it occurs substantially within authorized time and space limits, and (c) it is "actuated, at least in part, by a purpose to serve the master"; conduct falls outside scope only if it is "different in kind," "far beyond" the time/space limits, or "too little actuated by a purpose to serve the master" [4]. The Restatement (Third) §7.07 asks the same core question: was the worker performing assigned work subject to the employer's control, or pursuing an independent course not intended to serve any employer purpose? [9]

These facts fit comfortably within scope. The courier was **rushing to complete an order** during a **platform surge-pricing period** while **three deliveries behind quota** under automated acceptance-rate pressure. Driving to complete a delivery is exactly the kind of conduct he was engaged to perform, it occurred during active work, and it was plainly "actuated … by a purpose to serve" the platform [4]. The **benefit-to-employer** element is satisfied for the same reason: surge pricing and the quota show the rushing directly served the platform's commercial interest — completed orders during surge pricing generate revenue for the platform [6][4]. At minimum, whether the collision was within scope is itself a jury question on this record.

## 6. Issues that do not change the outcome

- **Borrowed-servant doctrine.** The borrowed-servant rule allocates vicarious liability between two potential masters when one employer lends a servant to another, asking which principal had the right to control the servant's physical conduct at the relevant time. It is **not implicated here.** This courier worked through a single platform; there is no lending employer and no second principal competing for the right to control. The only liability question is the ordinary one — whether the platform itself is the master under §220's right-to-control test [4] — not which of two masters controlled a borrowed worker. The doctrine adds nothing to the analysis on these facts.\n- **Lapsed liability insurance.** The courier's personal liability policy lapsed two weeks before the accident. That bears on his own coverage and the company's contractual right to require insurance, but it neither establishes nor defeats the platform's vicarious liability — respondeat superior turns on the employment relationship and scope of employment [6][4], not on whether the tortfeasor carried his own insurance.
- **Hiring the teenage son.** The courier occasionally hired his teenage son for large orders, paying him directly off-platform. The ability to "employ helpers" is a §220 factor — bearing on whether the worker is "engaged in a distinct occupation or business" (§220(2)(b)–(c)) and on the DialAmerica "employment of helpers" / investment factor (factor 3) — and nominally weighs toward independent-enterprise status [4][3]; in *O'Connor* the comparable point that drivers "may employ other drivers to drive on their behalf" was among the secondary factors supporting contractor status [2]. On these facts, however, the factor carries little weight. The question under §220(2)(c) (whether the work is usually done by a specialist without supervision) and DialAmerica factor 3 is whether the worker is genuinely running an independently established delivery enterprise distinct from the platform. Informal, occasional, off-platform help from a **teenage family member** for the occasional large order is not the hallmark of such a business: it shows neither a sustained, separately held trade, nor meaningful capital investment, nor the kind of staffing that signals an enterprise rather than a single worker accepting overflow help. The son's minority and the casual, family nature of the arrangement make it marginal evidence at best
- **Spoliation of training records.** The safety-protocol training records were lost during a system migration, though the onboarding checklist references their completion. Where evidence is lost, a court has broad discretion to impose an **adverse inference** — which may be **permissive** (the jury *may* infer the missing evidence was unfavorable) or **mandatory** (the jury *must* so infer) — but only where the missing evidence is **material** [10][11]. The inference rests on the premise that a party's intentional loss or destruction of evidence gives rise to a reasonable inference the evidence was unfavorable [11]. Two limits make this far from a clean point against the company. First, because the lost training records are electronically stored information, Fed. R. Civ. P. 37(e)(2) permits the four severest sanctions — presuming the lost information unfavorable, a mandatory or permissive adverse-inference instruction, dismissal, or default — **only "upon a finding that the party acted with the intent to deprive another party of the information's use in the litigation"**; most circuits independently apply a bad-faith test (*Bull v. UPS*, 665 F.3d 68, 79 (3d Cir. 2012): "a finding of bad faith is pivotal"), and mere negligence is insufficient [11]. A routine **system-migration loss is plausibly only negligent**, not intentional, so the strongest adverse-inference sanction may be unavailable; a lesser remedy under Rule 37(e)(1) (measures no greater than necessary to cure prejudice) or simply a residual fact dispute about whether training occurred may still preclude summary judgment, but the company is not automatically saddled with an adverse inference. Second, the **duty to preserve** attaches only when litigation is "pending or reasonably foreseeable" [11]; if the system migration predated any foreseeable claim arising from this collision, no preservation duty had been triggered when the records were lost, and the adverse inference would not apply at all. The training-records loss therefore bears on the disputed control issue (whether safety protocols were imposed — itself an indicium of control over the manner of work) only if the timing and intent prerequisites are met; even without any adverse inference, the underlying factual dispute over imposed safety protocols independently supports denying summary judgment.

## 7. Synthesis

Classification is "a mixed question of law and fact that must typically be resolved by a jury," and the defendant's path to summary judgment — every material fact undisputed and only one reasonable conclusion available — is "a pretty tough standard to meet" [2]. Here it is not met. Strong control indicia (app-mandated routes, no pricing discretion, branded gear, algorithmic suspension and acceptance-rate discipline, 55-hour workload, delivery as the platform's core business) point toward employee status; a handful of factors (own schedule, personal vehicle, the IC label, the off-platform son sub-hire) point the other way but are individually non-dispositive and partly disputed [4][2][3][7]. The scope-of-employment and benefit elements are satisfied or at least triable on the rushing-during-surge-pricing facts [4][6]. As in both *O'Connor* and *Razak*, those competing, fact-laden indicia must be resolved by the factfinder, not on summary judgment [2][3]. **A court would deny the company's motion and send the vicarious-liability question to the jury** — and, given the strength of the control evidence, the company would face a meaningfully harder task obtaining summary judgment in its favor than the plaintiff would in defeating it.
