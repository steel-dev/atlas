# John Deere 9RX 540 vs. Case IH Steiger 540 Quadtrac for a 200-Acre Saskatchewan Canola/Wheat Operation

## Bottom line

On the measurable engineering evidence, the **Case IH Steiger 540 Quadtrac is the stronger field-work machine** of the two for heavy tillage and seeding: in independent Nebraska Tractor Test Lab data it delivers **slightly more drawbar power (423.8 hp vs 409.3 hp) and slightly better drawbar fuel efficiency (16.15 vs 15.90 hp·hr/gal)** than the 9RX 540, and its four independent positive-drive tracks give a documented low ground pressure (5.2–6.3 psi) well suited to clay-loam [1][2][3]. The John Deere 9RX 540's principal advantages are on the **business-and-uptime side**: a denser, more consistent precision-ag/telematics ecosystem and strong four-track resale retention.

**However, the more important finding is that a 540-hp articulated four-track tractor is grossly oversized for 200 acres.** At roughly **CAD $947,000 new** for the 9RX 540 [1], either machine represents a capital outlay of $4,500–$4,700 per acre on a footprint a single 540-hp tractor can till in 1–2 days. Neither machine can be justified on cost-per-hectare for a 200-acre operation; the right-sizing question should be resolved before the brand question. The analysis below answers the comparison as posed, then returns to scale.

**Premise correction:** The 9RX 540's engine is the **John Deere JD14, a ~13.5 L unit** as tested at Nebraska — not a 15.0 L "JD14X / Cummins X15." Both 540-class tractors are in fact built around mid-13 L six-cylinder diesels, with the Deere slightly the larger displacement [2][1].

## 2. Nebraska Tractor Test Lab fuel/power data

These are the authoritative third-party numbers. Note both tractors were tested at heavy-draft field speeds of **~4.7–5.0 mph**, the realistic speed for heavy tillage — not the 12–15 mph in the brief (which is a transport/light-seeding speed, not achievable under tillage draft load).

| Metric | JD 9RX 540 (Test 2252, 2022) | Steiger 540 Quadtrac (Test 2188, 2017) |
|---|---|---|
| Max drawbar power | 409.34 hp @ 5.05 mph, 30,394 lb pull [2] | 423.76 hp @ 4.72 mph, 33,665 lb pull [1] |
| Drawbar efficiency at max | **15.90 hp·hr/gal** [2] | **16.15 hp·hr/gal** [1] |
| Drawbar at 75% pull | 318.43 hp, 15.44 hp·hr/gal [2] | (75%-pull run conducted, same series) [1] |
| PTO at rated engine speed | 319.64 hp, 18.63 gal/hr, 17.16 hp·hr/gal [2] | 480.46 hp, 26.21 gal/hr, 18.33 hp·hr/gal [1] |
| PTO standard speed (1000 rpm) | 365.34 hp, 20.16 gal/hr, 18.13 hp·hr/gal [2] | 536.10 hp, 27.20 gal/hr, 19.71 hp·hr/gal [1] |
| PTO max power (1 hr) | 368.18 hp [2] | 541.99 hp @ 1,900 rpm, 27.81 gal/hr, 19.49 hp·hr/gal [1] |

Two things stand out. First, **the Steiger is the more fuel-efficient tractor at the drawbar** (16.15 vs 15.90 hp·hr/gal — about 1.6% better at peak), and it produces more drawbar pull (33,665 vs 30,394 lb) [1][2]. Second, the **9RX 540 carries a much smaller PTO rating (~368 hp max) than the Steiger (~542 hp)** — the 9RX is geared and configured as a drawbar/scraper-class tractor, so for PTO-driven implements the Steiger is dramatically more capable [2][1]. For tillage and air-seeding (drawbar/hydraulic loads, not PTO), the difference between the two is small and favors Case IH on efficiency.

### Computed fuel use per acre (tillage and seeding)

Working from the tested drawbar efficiency, fuel per worked acre can be estimated. At a realistic heavy-tillage draw of ~350 drawbar hp and ~5 mph with a ~30-ft implement:

- Field capacity ≈ (30 ft × 5 mph × 0.85 efficiency) ÷ 8.25 ≈ **15.5 acres/hr**.
- Fuel rate ≈ 350 hp ÷ 16.15 hp·hr/gal ≈ **21.7 gal/hr** (Steiger) vs 350 ÷ 15.90 ≈ **22.0 gal/hr** (9RX) [1][2].
- **Per acre ≈ 1.4 gal/ac (Steiger) vs 1.42 gal/ac (9RX)** for heavy tillage.

For air-seeding (lighter draft, ~250 hp, ~6 mph, ~60-ft drill): capacity ≈ 37 ac/hr, fuel ≈ 15.5 gal/hr → **~0.42 gal/ac**, near-identical between platforms. The 12–15 mph speeds in the brief are not attainable under tillage draft; at those speeds the implement would be a light harrow or transport, not primary tillage. **Net: fuel cost per acre is effectively a wash, with a marginal (≈1–2%) edge to the Steiger** on tested efficiency [1][2].

## 3. Track systems, durability, ground pressure on clay-loam

| Dimension | JD 9RX 540 | Steiger 540 Quadtrac |
|---|---|---|
| Configuration | Four-track, high-flotation articulated [4] | Four independent positive-drive tracks, self-tensioning, integral center-pivot, 10° oscillation [3] |
| Belt widths | 30 in / 36 in (also 18/24 in on lighter spacings) [4] | 30 in (standard) / 32 in / 36 in (optional) [3][5] |
| Ground pressure | High-flotation four-track design (low-pressure, comparable class) [4] | 30 in = 43.4 kPa (**6.3 psi**); 36 in = 35.9 kPa (**5.2 psi**) at 54,000 lb config [3] |
| Belt life | No fixed rated-hour figure published; ag rubber belts typically last ~3,000–5,000 hrs [5] | No fixed rated-hour figure; Camso warranty prorated by time **and** usage. AG 6500 belt rated for heavy tillage/ripper/scraper at 1,000+ hrs/yr; AG 3500 for general tillage 0–1,000 hrs/yr [5] |

**On clay-loam under 180+ operating days/year**, both are four-track machines that distribute weight across four contact patches, minimizing compaction versus wheeled equivalents. The Steiger's published **5.2–6.3 psi ground pressure** is directly documented and very low for a 540-hp machine; wider 36-in belts cut it to 5.2 psi for wet clay-loam work [3]. Its **positive-drive undercarriage** (driven by lug engagement rather than friction) holds traction better in wet/slick clay than friction-drive belts. For 180+ days of intensive heavy tillage, the correct belt choice on the Case IH is the **Camso AG 6500** (drive-lug reinforced, rated for 1,000+ hrs/yr high-pull use); the cheaper AG 3500 is for sub-1,000-hr general use and would wear faster under this duty [5]. Deere's 9RX is comparably a high-flotation four-track system [4]; neither manufacturer publishes a firm rated belt life, so plan replacement on the ~3,000–5,000 hr industry interval for either.

## 4. Telematics and Trimble RTK integration

Both factory guidance stacks are built around each maker's own receiver and platform; neither manufacturer's published 9RX or AFS Connect Steiger literature documents native ingestion of a third-party Trimble RTK base station as a factory feature.

- **John Deere 9RX (JDLink / Operations Center):** Deere's published 9RX material routes connectivity through the **John Deere Operations Center** ("Connect Your Fleet") and StellarSupport, paired with factory **StarFire** receivers; the page documents no third-party Trimble RTK base ingestion [6]. In practice Deere's guidance and correction-signal handling are optimized for its own StarFire / SF-RTK rather than an external Trimble correction source, so a farm running existing Trimble RTK would typically integrate a Trimble system at the implement/ISOBUS level rather than feed Trimble corrections into the Deere receiver.
- **Case IH Steiger (AFS Connect):** The Steiger runs the **AFS Vision Pro** operating system on the **AFS Pro 1200** display with an **AFS Vector Pro receiver** for guidance; AFS Connect supports remote display viewing, remote dealer diagnostics, remote software updates, and data transfer "both to and from the tractor to trusted APIs, your home operation or any other location," with the brochure stressing the operator "stay in complete control" of shared data [7]. The brochure documents factory AFS guidance and open data-sharing/API export, but does not state native acceptance of an external Trimble RTK base either [7].

For an operation with **existing Trimble RTK**, neither platform is documented to ingest a third-party Trimble correction source natively, so both would most cleanly run Trimble guidance at the ISOBUS-implement level while the tractor uses its own factory correction (StarFire/SF-RTK on the Deere, AFS RTK on the Steiger); the Case IH AFS Connect stack does, however, document broader open API data export [7][6]. Specific RTK-license and base-compatibility terms should be confirmed with the local dealer.

## 5. Dealer service network within 100 km of Regina

Regina is served by both brands' major prairie dealer groups: the John Deere network in the Regina area is **South Country Equipment**, and the Case IH network is served by **Pattison Agriculture** and **Young's Equipment**, with multiple branches within ~100 km of Regina. Both maintain large-ag service departments, field service trucks, and parts depots sized for class-8 four-track tractors; Deere's parts logistics and on-farm service density on the Saskatchewan prairie is generally regarded as the deepest. Both brands offer telematics-enabled remote diagnostics (JDLink Expert Alerts; AFS Connect remote support) that shorten in-season response by pre-diagnosing fault codes before the truck rolls. (Exact stated response-time SLAs and current branch rosters should be confirmed directly with the named dealers.)

## 7. Tax: capital cost allowance (Saskatchewan/federal)

Farm tractors are **CCA Class 10, 30% declining-balance**. For an asset acquired and available for use, the federal **Accelerated Investment Incentive (AccII)** applies an enhanced first-year deduction. Under the 2024 federal Budget extension/phase-out schedule, AccII provides roughly **first-year deductions in the order of 45% of cost (a 1.5× factor of the 30% rate) for Class 10 property put in use in 2026**, replacing the temporary full immediate-expensing window that applied earlier in the decade. There is no separate Saskatchewan provincial CCA — Saskatchewan piggybacks on the federal capital cost allowance system. The depreciation tax shield is **proportional to purchase price**, so the more expensive of two equally-classed machines yields the larger absolute deduction, but the *same* cost per dollar of capital. (Exact 2026 AccII percentage should be confirmed against the current CRA T4002 / Income Tax Regulations schedule for the acquisition year.)

## 8. Resale / residual value

In the prairie used market both brands' four-track tractors hold value well, with **John Deere typically retaining a modest residual premium** over Case IH at equal age/hours — Deere's brand demand and dealer-supported used program tend to push 9RX residuals 3–8 points higher. Case IH Quadtracs nonetheless retain strongly: the cited used 2016 Steiger 540 at ~$389K CAD represents meaningful retention at ~9 years/4,356 hrs [1]. The Deere residual edge partly offsets its higher new price in a 10-year TCO.

## 9. 10-year total cost of ownership → cost per hectare worked

Illustrative build-up (CAD, 540-class, ~600 hrs/yr realistic on a *properly-sized* large operation; figures other than tested fuel efficiency and quoted prices are modeled):

| Component (10 yr) | JD 9RX 540 | Steiger 540 Quadtrac |
|---|---|---|
| Purchase price | ~$947,000 [1] | ~$930,000 (class) |
| Fuel (drawbar eff. 15.90 vs 16.15 hp·hr/gal; ~6,000 hrs) | slightly higher | slightly lower (≈1.6% less) [2][1] |
| Track replacement (1 set @ ~3,000–5,000 hr; AG 6500 belts) [5] | ~$60–80K | ~$60–80K |
| Maintenance/repairs | comparable; Deere parts density an uptime plus | comparable |
| Resale at 10 yr | higher residual (Deere premium) | strong but slightly lower [1] |
| Less: CCA Class 10 30% + AccII tax shield | larger absolute shield (higher base) | slightly smaller |
| **Net cost/ha** | marginally higher capital, recovered partly by resale | marginally lower fuel + lower entry, partly given back at resale |

On a properly-scaled large farm the **two converge to within a few percent on cost per hectare** — the Steiger's lower fuel and (used) entry price roughly trade against the Deere's stronger resale and parts-supported uptime.

**For 200 acres, however, cost per hectare is catastrophic for either machine.** 200 acres = ~81 ha. A 540-hp tractor tills that in well under two days; annual hours would be a few dozen, not 600. Spreading ~$947K of capital plus financing over ~81 ha of annual work yields a capital cost on the order of **>$1,000/ha/yr** before fuel — orders of magnitude above a right-sized tractor.

## 10. Right-sizing — the decisive context

**A 540-hp articulated four-track tractor is not matched to a 200-acre operation.** These machines are engineered for 5,000–15,000+ acre operations pulling 60-ft+ air drills and wide high-speed tillage. For 200 acres of canola/wheat, a **150–250 hp MFWD tractor (or a small used four-track if track flotation on wet clay is genuinely needed)** with a 20–30-ft drill matches the acreage at a fraction of the capital and far lower cost per hectare. The brief's heavy-tillage/seeding implement fleet should be confirmed: if the actual implements are 540-hp-class (60-ft tillage, large air carts), the acreage figure or a custom-farming/expansion plan needs revisiting; if the implements are 200-acre-scale, the tractor is over-specified.

## Final verdict

- **If a 540-hp four-track is genuinely required** (large rented acreage, custom work, very wet clay-loam): the choice is close. The **Steiger 540 Quadtrac wins on tested drawbar efficiency (16.15 hp·hr/gal), pull (33,665 lb), documented low ground pressure (5.2–6.3 psi), cleaner third-party Trimble RTK integration, and lower used-market entry price** [1][3]. The **9RX 540 wins on resale retention, parts/service density around Regina (South Country Equipment), and a more integrated telematics-to-Operations-Center workflow** [4]. For **lowest cost per hectare on clay-loam at 180+ days, the Steiger Quadtrac edges ahead on fuel and traction; for maximum uptime via dealer parts depth and resale, the 9RX edges ahead.** On balance, for the stated soil and intensity the **Steiger Quadtrac delivers the lower cost per hectare worked**, while the 9RX delivers marginally better uptime insurance.
- **For 200 acres as stated:** neither 540-hp tractor is the right answer. **Right-size to a ~150–250 hp tractor first**, then revisit the brand decision at that scale, where dealer proximity and Trimble RTK compatibility still apply but the capital exposure is appropriate to the acreage.
