## Independent Directors Under the NASDAQ Listing Standards

### Overview

The NASDAQ corporate governance requirements (Rule 5600 Series) define "Independent Director" in **Rule 5605(a)(2)** and impose board- and committee-composition obligations in Rule 5605, with exemptions and transition rules set out in **Rule 5615** [1]. The framework operates on two layers: a **subjective board-judgment standard** (the board must affirmatively conclude no relationship would interfere with independent judgment) and a **set of objective bright-line bars** (relationships that automatically preclude a finding of independence) [1]. Heightened, more stringent requirements then apply to audit and compensation committee members.

---

### 1. The definition and the affirmative-determination requirement (Rule 5605(a)(2))

Rule 5605(a)(2) provides that an "Independent Director" means:

> "a person other than an Executive Officer or employee of the Company or any other individual having a relationship which, in the opinion of the Company's board of directors, would interfere with the exercise of independent judgment in carrying out the responsibilities of a director" [1].

"Executive Officer" means the officers covered in Rule 16a-1(f) under the Securities Exchange Act [1].

The accompanying interpretive material (IM-5605) makes the affirmative-determination duty explicit: "The board has a responsibility to make an affirmative determination that no such relationships exist through the application of Rule 5605(a)(2)" [1]. The bright-line list in the rule does not *define* independence; rather, it "provides a list of certain relationships that preclude a board finding of independence," giving investors transparency and easing uniform administration [1]. The two layers are therefore cumulative: a director must clear every bright-line bar **and** survive the board's subjective judgment that no other interfering relationship exists.

---

### 2. Affirmative eligibility — who qualifies (board judgment, scope, look-backs)

A person qualifies as independent if the board affirmatively determines no relationship would interfere with the director's independent judgment, and none of the bright-line disqualifiers applies [1]. Several interpretive points shape this judgment:

- **Stock ownership alone does not disqualify.** NASDAQ "does not believe that ownership of Company stock by itself would preclude a board finding of independence," so it is not among the objective bars [1].
- **Scope of "Company."** References to the "Company" include any parent or subsidiary — entities the Company controls and consolidates in its SEC financial statements, but not entities reflected solely as an investment [1].
- **Look-back periods.** The three-year look-backs in paragraphs (A), (C), (E), and (F) commence on the date the relationship *ceases* — e.g., a former employee is not independent until three years after employment terminates [1]. The compensation bar in paragraph (B) uses a "twelve consecutive months within the three years preceding the determination" measure [1].
- **Interim-officer relief.** Service as an Executive Officer on an interim basis does not disqualify a director afterward, provided the interim service did not last longer than one year (though the director is not independent *while* serving), and interim compensation may be excluded on the same condition; the board must still consider whether such service or compensation would interfere with independent judgment [1].

---

### 3. Bright-line disqualification criteria (Rule 5605(a)(2)(A)–(G))

"The following persons shall **not** be considered independent" [1]:

| Para. | Disqualifying relationship | Threshold / look-back |
|---|---|---|
| **(A)** | A director who is, or was, **employed** by the Company | At any time during the past **3 years** [1] |
| **(B)** | A director (or a Family Member) who accepted **compensation** from the Company | Exceeding **$120,000** during any 12 consecutive months within the preceding 3 years [1] |
| **(C)** | A director who is a **Family Member of an Executive Officer** of the Company | Executive-officer employment at any time during the past 3 years [1] |
| **(D)** | A director (or Family Member) who is a partner, controlling shareholder, or executive officer of an organization making/receiving **business payments** to/from the Company | Payments exceeding **5% of recipient's consolidated gross revenues** or **$200,000**, whichever is more, in the current or any of the past 3 fiscal years [1] |
| **(E)** | **Compensation-committee interlock** — a director (or Family Member) employed as an executive officer of another entity where a Company executive officer served on that entity's compensation committee | During the past 3 years [1] |
| **(F)** | A director (or Family Member) who is a current **partner of the Company's outside auditor**, or was a partner/employee who worked on the Company's audit | During any of the past 3 years [1] |
| **(G)** | For **investment companies**, in lieu of (A)–(F): a director who is an **"interested person"** under Section 2(a)(19) of the Investment Company Act of 1940 (other than as a board/committee member) [1] |

Exceptions narrow several bars. Under (B), compensation for board/committee service, compensation paid to a Family Member who is a non-executive employee, and tax-qualified retirement-plan or non-discretionary compensation are excluded [1]. Under (D), payments arising solely from investments in the Company's securities and non-discretionary charitable matching are excluded [1].

"**Family Member**" is defined as a person's spouse, parents, children, siblings, mothers- and fathers-in-law, sons- and daughters-in-law, brothers- and sisters-in-law, and anyone (other than domestic employees) who shares the person's home [1].

---

### 4. Heightened committee-level independence (audit vs. compensation)

General independence is the floor; both committees layer additional requirements, but with an important difference in character — the audit committee adds **absolute bars**, while the compensation committee adds **mandatory factors to consider**.

**Audit committee (Rule 5605(c)(2)) — stricter.** Each company must have an audit committee of at least **three members**, each of whom must [1]:

1. be an Independent Director under Rule 5605(a)(2);
2. meet the independence criteria of **SEC Rule 10A-3(b)(1)** (subject to Rule 10A-3(c) exemptions) — meaning the member must **not accept any consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fee** from the Company other than for board service, and must **not be an affiliated person** of the Company [1];
3. not have participated in preparing the Company's (or a current subsidiary's) financial statements during the past three years; and
4. be able to read and understand fundamental financial statements [1].

Under exceptional and limited circumstances, one non-independent member meeting Section 10A(m)(3) criteria (and not currently an executive officer, employee, or Family Member of an executive officer) may serve, but **for no more than two years and may not chair** the committee, with disclosure required [1].

**Compensation committee (Rule 5605(d)(2)) — softer.** Each company must have a compensation committee of at least **two members**, each an Independent Director under Rule 5605(a)(2) [1]. In addition, when determining a compensation-committee member's independence, the board **must consider all relevant factors**, including (i) the source of the director's compensation (any consulting/advisory/other fee paid by the Company) and (ii) whether the director is affiliated with the Company, a subsidiary, or an affiliate of a subsidiary [1]. Crucially, these are factors to weigh, not automatic disqualifiers — NASDAQ notes it may be appropriate for certain affiliates, such as representatives of significant stockholders, to serve, since their interests align with other shareholders [1]. A parallel exceptional-circumstances exception allows one non-qualifying member if the committee has at least three members [1].

**In short:** audit-committee independence is the most demanding (no compensatory fees, no affiliation, financial-statement and literacy conditions); compensation-committee independence is general independence *plus* a heightened factor analysis that still permits affiliates; and the general Rule 5605(a)(2) standard is the baseline for all other independent directors.

---

### 5. Companies required to have a majority-independent board

Under **Rule 5605(b)(1)**: "A majority of the board of directors must be comprised of Independent Directors as defined in Rule 5605(a)(2)" [1]. This default applies to listed operating companies. Non-foreign-private-issuers must comply with the disclosure requirements of Item 407(a) of Regulation S-K, while foreign private issuers must disclose in their next annual report (Form 20-F or 40-F) which directors the board determined to be independent [1].

Board-composition obligations also include **Rule 5605(b)(2)** executive sessions: Independent Directors must hold regularly scheduled meetings at which only Independent Directors are present (contemplated at least twice a year) [1]. A **cure period** under Rule 5605(b)(1)(A) applies if the company falls below a majority because of a single vacancy, or because one director ceases to be independent for reasons beyond their reasonable control: compliance must be regained by the earlier of the next annual shareholders' meeting or one year from the occurrence [1].

### 6. Exempt or modified company types (Rule 5615)

NASDAQ exempts or modifies the independence requirements for several categories [1]:

| Company type | Treatment of independence requirements |
|---|---|
| **Controlled Companies** (>50% of voting power for electing directors held by an individual, group, or another company) | Exempt from Rules 5605(b) (majority board), (d) (compensation committee), and (e) (director nominations) — **but must still comply with 5605(b)(2) executive sessions and the audit committee (5605(c))**; disclosure required [1] |
| **Foreign Private Issuers** | May follow **home-country practice** in lieu of the 5600 Series (including majority board and independent compensation committee), provided they comply with Notification of Noncompliance (5625) and Voting Rights (5640), maintain an audit committee satisfying 5605(c)(3) whose members meet the Rule 10A-3(b)(1) independence criteria, disclose each requirement not followed, and supply home-country counsel certification [1] |
| **Smaller Reporting Companies** (Rule 12b-2) | **Not exempt** from the majority-independent-board requirement. Not subject to Rule 5605(d) **except** must have a compensation committee of at least **two Independent Directors**; **not** required to apply the additional 5605(d)(2)(A) source-of-compensation/affiliation eligibility tests, and may use the 5605(d)(2)(B) exception and 5605(d)(4) cure period [1] |
| **Registered Management Investment Companies** (under the Investment Company Act of 1940, incl. registered closed-end funds) | Exempt from Rules 5605(b), (d), (e), and 5610, in light of their pervasive federal regulation [1] |
| **Business Development Companies** (closed-end management investment companies *not* registered under the 1940 Act) | **Must comply with all** provisions of the 5600 Series [1] |
| **Limited Partnerships** | Not subject to the 5600 Series except as provided in Rule 5615(a)(4) (corporate general partner, meeting/quorum rules) — i.e., exempt from the general independent-director/board-composition requirements [1] |
| **Asset-Backed Issuers & passive trusts/unincorporated associations without a board** (e.g., certain unit investment trusts) | Rules 5605(b), (d), (e), and 5610 do not apply [1] |
| **Cooperatives** (e.g., agricultural cooperatives with no publicly traded common stock) | Exempt from Rules 5605(b), (d), (e), and 5615(c)(2), but must still satisfy federal securities laws including Section 10A(m) / Rule 10A-3 (audit committee) [1] |
| **Issuers of only non-voting preferred, debt, or Derivative Securities** | Exempt from Rules 5605(b), (d), (e), 5610, and 5620(a), and from 5605(c) **except** the applicable SEC Rule 10A-3 requirements (full requirements apply if they also list common/voting preferred stock) [1] |

A recurring theme: even where the majority-board and committee-independence rules are relaxed, the **audit committee / Rule 10A-3 obligations generally remain**, reflecting that those flow from federal law (Section 10A(m)) rather than purely from the listing standards [1].

---

### 7. Phase-in / transition rules for newly listed companies (Rule 5615(b))

A company is treated as listing in conjunction with an **IPO** if, immediately prior to listing, it did not have a class of common stock registered under the Act [1]. The phase-in schedules are [1]:

- **Majority-independent board (5605(b)):** comply within **12 months** of the Listing Date (the date securities first trade on Nasdaq).
- **Audit committee (5605(c)(2)):** one member by the Listing Date; a majority within 90 days of the registration statement's effective date; all members within one year of the effective date. Committee size: at least one member by the Listing Date, two within 90 days, three within one year of the Listing Date. *Registered management investment companies cannot use these phase-in periods* [1].
- **Compensation and nominations committees (5605(d)(2), 5605(e)(1)(B)):** one member by the earlier of the IPO closing or five business days from the Listing Date; a majority within 90 days of the Listing Date; all within one year. Compensation committee size: at least one member by the Listing Date and two within one year [1].

Comparable phase-in schedules apply to companies **emerging from bankruptcy**, **transferring from another market**, listing via a **spin-off/carve-out**, and to companies that **cease to qualify** as Smaller Reporting Companies or Foreign Private Issuers — each computed from the relevant trigger date [1]. For example, a company that ceases to be a Smaller Reporting Company phases in the additional 5605(d)(2)(A) compensation-committee eligibility requirements with one member at six months, a majority at nine months, and all members at one year from the Start Date [1]. A company that ceases to be a Controlled Company phases in its majority board and independent nominations/compensation committees on the IPO schedule (computed from when it ceases to be controlled) but must comply with the audit-committee requirements immediately, and executive sessions apply throughout [1].

---

### Summary

NASDAQ's definition of an independent director combines an objective screen with a subjective board judgment: a director qualifies only if (i) they are not an executive officer or employee, (ii) none of the seven bright-line bars in Rule 5605(a)(2)(A)–(G) applies, and (iii) the board affirmatively determines no other relationship would interfere with independent judgment [1]. Audit-committee members face the strictest test (Rule 10A-3's no-fee, no-affiliation bars plus financial conditions); compensation-committee members face a heightened factor analysis that still tolerates certain affiliates [1]. Listed operating companies must maintain a majority-independent board, while controlled companies, foreign private issuers, smaller reporting companies, registered management investment companies, limited partnerships, asset-backed issuers, and cooperatives receive full or partial exemptions — though audit-committee/Rule 10A-3 obligations largely persist — and newly listed companies receive staggered phase-in periods of up to one year [1].
