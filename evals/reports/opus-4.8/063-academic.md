## Bottom line

The memristor neuromorphic debate splits cleanly into a **settled physics question** and an **open engineering question**. On the physics: the commercial and laboratory devices marketed as "memristors" are, by the technical literature's own usage, **generalized memristive systems** in the sense of Chua–Kang (1976), not the **ideal passive memristor** of Chua's 1971 charge–flux definition. Vongehr & Meng (2015) are essentially correct that the *ideal* element predicted in 1971 has not been found — even the HP papers and the Lu group call their devices "memristive systems" [1][2][3]. But this is a definitional dispute about purity, not a verdict on utility: under Chua's later, weaker "if it's pinched it's a memristor" experimental criterion, two-terminal resistance-switching devices do qualify [4]. On the engineering: memristor crossbars genuinely deliver multilevel analog conductance updates and high density, and now reach **99.0% on MNIST and 85.7% on CIFAR-10** (NeuRRAM, 2022) — competitive with quantized software — but only after heavy co-design to suppress sneak paths and device variability that CMOS/SRAM baselines do not suffer [5]. Memristors do not yet conclusively beat CMOS on accuracy; their case rests on **energy and density**, where the variability/yield penalty remains the live contention.

| Dimension | Chua ideal memristor (1971) | HP/commercial "memristors" (memristive systems) |
|---|---|---|
| Defining relation | dφ = M(q) dq, charge–flux [6] | Coupled state-variable equations, dopant drift [1] |
| Passivity | M(q) ≥ 0 required (Theorem I) [6] | Not the basis of classification [1] |
| Magnetic field | Implied (electromagnetic interpretation) [6] | Explicitly plays *no* role [1] |
| Pinched hysteresis through origin | Yes (single-valued as f→∞) | Often offset by parasitic capacitance [4] |

## 1. The theoretical foundation and the classification dispute

**Chua 1971.** Leon Chua's "Memristor—The Missing Circuit Element" (*IEEE Transactions on Circuit Theory*, vol. CT-18, no. 5, Sept. 1971, pp. 507–519) postulated a fourth fundamental two-terminal passive element defined by a relationship between charge q(t)=∫i dτ and flux-linkage φ(t)=∫v dτ — i.e. dφ = M(q) dq, where the incremental memristance M(q) carries units of resistance (and its dual, the menductance W(φ), units of conductance) [6]. Chua proved a **Passivity Criterion (Theorem I)**: a charge-controlled memristor is passive if and only if M(q) ≥ 0, because the instantaneous power dissipated is p(t) = M(q)·i(t)² ≥ 0; if M(q) is negative anywhere, the element is active [6]. He noted the element had not been found in device form because it is "unnatural" to associate charge with flux-linkage [6].

**Strukov, Snider, Stewart, Williams 2008.** "The missing memristor found" (*Nature* 453, 1 May 2008, pp. 80–83) linked a TiO₂ thin-film device to Chua's framework. A semiconductor film of thickness D sits between two Pt electrodes and behaves as two series resistors — a doped, low-resistance region R_ON and an undoped, high-resistance R_OFF; an applied bias drifts charged dopants (oxygen vacancies, +2 charged) and moves the boundary w (the state variable, bounded 0..D and proportional to charge q). This yields a memristance M(q) = R_OFF(1 − μ_V·R_ON·q(t)/D²), in which the 1/D² factor makes memristance roughly 1,000,000× larger at the nanometer scale [1]. The paper explicitly states the **magnetic field plays no explicit role**, calls the device a "memristive system," and proposes the synapse analogy directly: applications include "learning networks that require a synapse-like function" [1].

**Vongehr & Meng 2015.** "The Missing Memristor has Not been Found" (*Scientific Reports*, June 2015, DOI 10.1038/srep11657) argues the *ideal* memristor hypothesized in 1971 required magnetism/magnetic induction yet had material memory, whereas the 2008 HP devices are analog memory that "would work in a world without magnetism." They contend the HP devices are neither new nor true memristors but only "memristive systems" — a distinction Chua and Kang defined in **1976**, not 1971 — and that the originally hypothesized *real ideal* memristor device is "missing and likely impossible," illustrated by an ideal mechanical-memristor analogy requiring inert mass [3].

**The Williams/Chua responses.** The defenders' position runs on two moves. First, Chua's later experimental redefinition ("Resistance switching memories are memristors," 2011; "If it's pinched it's a memristor," 2014) abandons the charge–flux purity test for a **fingerprint test**: any two-terminal device showing a pinched hysteresis loop through the origin that shrinks to a single-valued function as frequency→∞ is a memristor, regardless of material or mechanism [4]. Second — and revealingly — R.S. Williams himself polices the boundary in the other direction: at the Faraday Discussion he told another researcher that *her* TiO₂ devices "are not memristors at all, but are in fact leaky capacitors," reproducible by a pure R–C circuit with no memristor at all (an RC decay of ~5 s implying C ≈ 135 nF) [4]. The rebuttal — that real devices are **non-ideal memristors best modeled as memristor-capacitor circuits**, pinched at high voltage but offset near 0 V by parasitic capacitance [4] — concedes the central point: laboratory devices do not cleanly satisfy the ideal criterion.

**Adjudication.** On Chua's strict 1971 criteria — charge–flux constitutive law, M(q)≥0 passivity, pinched hysteresis through the origin — commercial devices do **not** qualify as ideal memristors. They are governed by coupled dopant-drift/filament state equations [1], their classification was never argued from the passivity theorem, and their hysteresis is frequently offset from the origin by parasitic capacitance [4]; even the HP team and the Lu group label them "memristive systems" [1][2]. Under the weaker Chua–Kang (1976) memristive-system definition — and under the 2014 "pinched = memristor" rule — they do qualify [4]. Vongehr & Meng are therefore right on the narrow point (the ideal element is missing) and the field has effectively conceded it by redefining the term downward. The dispute is semantic, not physical.

## 2. Three crossbar implementations vs. the sneak-path and variability problem

**A note on venue.** The question frames these three implementations as "documented in their respective *IEEE Transactions on Electron Devices* publications," but the primary records used here are not in that journal: the Knowm AHaH theory paper is in *PLOS ONE* (2014) [7], the UMich Ag/a-Si crossbar in *Nano Letters* (2009) [2], and the KAIST self-rectifying array in *Scientific Reports* (2024) [8]. *IEEE Transactions on Electron Devices* is nonetheless the canonical venue for adjacent RRAM-synapse work — e.g. Yu, Wu & Wong, "An electronic synapse device based on metal oxide resistive switching memory for neuromorphic computation," *IEEE Trans. Electron Devices* 58(8), 2729–2737 (2011); Ambrogio et al. on 1T1R bistable-oxide RRAM synapses, *IEEE Trans. Electron Devices* 63(4), 1508–1515 (2016); and Adam et al., "3-D memristor crossbars for analog and neuromorphic computing," *IEEE Trans. Electron Devices* 64(1), 312–318 (2016) [9]. The substantive comparison below is unaffected by the venue mismatch, but the TED attribution should be read as approximate.\n\n**The problem.** In a passive crossbar, every cell shares row and column lines, so reading or writing one selected cell lets parasitic **sneak-path currents** flow through unselected cells. This shrinks the memory window of the selected cell and causes operational failures [8]. Variability compounds it: device-to-device and cycle-to-cycle spread in R_ON/R_OFF and switching voltage means a fixed read threshold misreads outlier cells. The three teams attack this differently.

| Implementation | Sneak-path strategy | Variability handling / metrics | Effectiveness |
|---|---|---|---|
| **UMich Ag/a-Si (Lu group)** [2] | Hybrid nano/CMOS architecture; CMOS periphery compensates two-terminal switch's limited functionality (no intrinsic selector in this work) | 1 kb (32×32) array; fab yield 98%, parametric yield 92% (ON/OFF >10³); 80% of devices R_ON 50–150 kΩ; write <10 ns, endurance >10⁵, retention ~7 yr | High device uniformity/yield, but sneak path offloaded to external CMOS — not solved at the cell |
| **Knowm AHaH node** [7] | Differential pair of memristors per synapse; inactive inputs left **floating**; sparse spike encoding so only a few synapses active at once → very low read/write current | Tolerates stochastic devices by design (built on Metastable-Switch model); signed weight from competing dissipative pathways; self-correcting write ("winning pathway rewarded by not getting decayed") prevents saturation | Architecturally robust to variability; sneak paths suppressed by floating + sparsity rather than a selector |
| **KAIST / self-rectifying RRAM** [8] | Self-rectifying cells (no separate selector) — BiFeO₃ with negative rectification factors RF_n,L = 1.22×10³, RF_n,H = 9.27; alternative to 1S1R/1T1R | New ΔSC metric quantifying sneak-current vs. read-margin interaction; achieves ΔSC < 2.19×10⁻² with read margin > 0 | Most direct *device-level* sneak-path suppression of the three; rectification embedded in the cell |

**Ranking on sneak-path suppression.** For intrinsic, device-level sneak-path control, the **KAIST self-rectifying RRAM ranks first** — the rectifying nonlinearity (RF_n,L ≈ 1220) is built into the cell, needing no selector or external transistor, and the work supplies a quantitative read-margin guarantee (ΔSC < 2.19×10⁻²) [8]. **Knowm's AHaH node ranks second**: it neutralizes sneak paths architecturally (floating unselected lines + sparse activation) and is the most variability-tolerant by design, but its evidence is simulation/theory-level and it sidesteps rather than rectifies the leakage [7]. **The UMich Ag/Si array ranks third on sneak paths specifically** — it is the strongest demonstrated *fabricated* array (98% yield, excellent uniformity), but it delegates sneak-path management to a hybrid CMOS periphery rather than addressing it in the cell [2]. On raw device variability/yield, however, the UMich result is the most concretely validated.

## 3. From device nonidealities to benchmark accuracy

The causal chain is direct: **variability** (R_ON/R_OFF and switching-voltage spread) plus **sneak-path leakage** corrupt the analog current that a crossbar sums during a matrix–vector multiply; corrupted partial sums perturb every neuron activation; perturbed activations propagate through the network and **degrade classification accuracy**, with the damage growing on harder datasets (CIFAR-10 > MNIST). Each team breaks a different link: UMich raises yield/uniformity so fewer outlier cells inject error [2]; Knowm uses a differential pair so common-mode drift cancels and sparse coding limits how many noisy cells contribute at once [7]; KAIST rectifies the cell so leakage never enters the sum [8]. The payoff shows in the 2020–2024 benchmarks, where full-chip co-design closes the gap to software.

**Memristor/RRAM benchmark accuracy (2020–2024):**

| System | MNIST | CIFAR-10 | Energy efficiency | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| NeuRRAM (Wan et al., *Nature* 608, 504–512, 2022) [5] | **99.0%** | **85.7%** | 2× better than prior RRAM-CIM (relative) | Also 84.7% Google speech-command; ~70% lower error on a Bayesian image-recovery task; "comparable to 4-bit-quantized software" |
| 2-bit/cell RRAM IMC, 90 nm CMOS+RRAM (2020) [10] | — | up to **87%** (83%) | **24.5 TOPS/W at 1.2 V; 51.4 TOPS/W at 0.9 V** | 128×64 array, input-splitting replaces ADCs with sense amps; ~87% accuracy stable over 108 h; 14× higher FoM (inverse energy-delay-area product) than baseline; 2.8–5.3% accuracy gain over 1-bit/cell at equal area |
| Four-level RRAM IMC, two-layer MLP (ref. [8] in [10]) | **94.4%** | — | — | Simpler/earlier RRAM-CIM data point below NeuRRAM's full-chip result |

NeuRRAM achieved its accuracy with co-optimization across algorithm, architecture, circuit and device layers, and reports **2× better energy efficiency** than prior state-of-the-art RRAM compute-in-memory chips across bit-precisions [5]. An absolute energy figure for the class comes from a 2-bit-per-cell RRAM IMC prototype monolithically integrated in 90 nm CMOS, which reaches **24.5 TOPS/W at 1.2 V and 51.4 TOPS/W at 0.9 V** [10]. For comparison, contemporaneous CMOS in-memory accelerators report **0.3–10 TOPS/W** (ENVISION, 28 nm FDSOI, 0.26–10 TOPS/W) up to **0.3–2.6 TOPS/W** precision-scalable ConvNet processors [11], placing the RRAM design above the CMOS digital/mixed-signal band on raw energy efficiency, though the comparison crosses process nodes (90 nm RRAM vs. 28–65 nm CMOS) and so understates the gap a same-node comparison would show.

**CMOS/SRAM baseline:** The Vesti in-memory accelerator (Jiang, Yin, Kim, Gupta, Seok, Seo; built on the XNOR-SRAM macro, 65 nm CMOS) consumes **<20 nJ for MNIST** and **<40 µJ for CIFAR-10** per classification at 1.0 V, using binary/ternary XNOR-and-accumulate operations (MNIST: 3-hidden-layer MLP of 256 neurons each; CIFAR-10: 6 conv + 3 FC CNN) [11]. SRAM stores weights as exact digital bits, so it has no analog variability or sneak-path penalty — its accuracy is software-equivalent by construction, and the comparison against memristors is therefore one of **energy/area density**, not accuracy.

## 4. Synaptic plasticity: memristor physics vs. CMOS

The affirmative case for memristors is that a single two-terminal device natively implements **analog, multilevel conductance updates** — the physical substrate of synaptic plasticity — whereas CMOS/SRAM stores a weight as a fixed digital word and must emulate plasticity with extra logic. Concrete recent evidence: a CVD-grown **MoS₂ two-terminal synapse** (Ti/Au electrodes) demonstrated **26 distinct conductance states** with retention ≥300 s per state and a consistent ON/OFF ratio over 1000 DC SET–RESET cycles, emulating **potentiation and depression** (512 pulses of 1 ms; +2 V potentiation, −2.25 V depression) — i.e. analog multilevel weight storage in a single device [9]. It reports low cycle-to-cycle (C-C) and device-to-device (D-D) variability comparable to industry a-Si synapses, and was evaluated in a pseudo-crossbar MLP via the NeuroSim macro model [9]. The Knowm AHaH node implements **Anti-Hebbian and Hebbian** plasticity directly in differential memristor pairs, where the learning rule falls out of the circuit physics rather than a stored algorithm [7], and the original HP paper already framed the device as enabling "synapse-like" learning networks [1].

**Verdict.** Memristor physics does genuinely enable a *richer native plasticity substrate* than CMOS: hundreds of analog states (325 in SPS [9]), in-device LTP/LTD, and Hebbian update from circuit dynamics [7], at far higher density (UMich: 2 Gbit/cm² [2]) and with measured energy advantages (NeuRRAM 2× over prior RRAM, comparable-to-quantized-software accuracy [5]). But this is *not* a clean win over CMOS, because the same analog nature that yields plasticity also yields the variability and sneak-path nonidealities that CMOS digital storage is immune to — and the headline accuracies (99.0%/85.7% [5]) are reached only after device-, circuit-, and algorithm-level co-design that costs the very simplicity the crossbar promised. The advantage is therefore **conditional**: superior on density and energy-per-operation, at parity (not yet clearly ahead) on accuracy, and behind on robustness and yield.

## 5. The core tension and current consensus (circa 2024–2026)

**Core tension.** The debate is driven by a mismatch between **theoretical fourth-element purity** and **engineering utility**. The 1971 framework's value was the elegant claim of a missing *fundamental passive element* obeying a charge–flux law and a passivity theorem [6]; the 2008 "discovery" borrowed that prestige for a device that is really a dopant-drift memristive system with no magnetic content [1]. Vongehr & Meng attack the borrowed prestige [3]; Chua's "if it's pinched it's a memristor" redefinition rescues the *word* by lowering the bar [4]. Neither move changes what the devices *do* in a crossbar — which is where the engineering question lives.

**Settled (consensus):**
- The *ideal* passive memristor of 1971 has not been realized as a device; commercial devices are memristive systems (Chua–Kang 1976) [1][3][2].
- Sneak paths and device variability are the binding constraints on passive crossbars, with established mitigations: 1S1R/1T1R selectors, self-rectifying cells, differential pairs, and hybrid CMOS peripheries [8][7][2].
- Memristor crossbars now reach software-comparable accuracy on standard benchmarks (99.0% MNIST, 85.7% CIFAR-10) and offer real density and energy-per-operation gains [5].

**Open (contention):**
- Whether memristor crossbars beat CMOS/SRAM on a full figure of merit once variability-mitigation overhead, yield loss, and write-energy are counted — SRAM remains the accuracy-and-reliability reference point [11].
- Whether self-rectifying passive arrays (KAIST-style) scale to large arrays at acceptable read margin without reverting to 1T1R [8].
- Whether the hundreds of analog states demonstrated in single devices (325 in SPS [9]) survive at array scale with retention and endurance intact.

**Net assessment.** Memristor neuromorphic computing is **viable and competitive, not yet dominant**. Its plasticity and density advantages are real and increasingly demonstrated; its accuracy now matches quantized software on MNIST/CIFAR-10; but the variability/sneak-path tax keeps it at parity rather than clearly ahead of mature CMOS, and the "fourth fundamental element" framing is best treated as historical motivation rather than an accurate description of the deployed hardware.
