# Did the Buyer Rightfully Reject? A UCC Perfect-Tender and Acceptance Analysis

## Question presented

Vendor delivered 1,000 custom widgets on March 15, 2024 that met all functional specifications (load capacity, durability) but were navy rather than the specified charcoal gray, identified by a color reference number. Buyer installed and operated all 1,000 widgets for eight weeks, painted 600 of them black to match its showroom, and praised their performance in March emails without mentioning color. Buyer first complained of color on May 10, then demanded a 40% refund while keeping the widgets in use; Vendor offered an immediate replacement on a two-week lead time. The contract contained a standard inspection-and-acceptance clause requiring notice of nonconformity within 10 days. The issue is whether Buyer rightfully rejected the goods under the UCC perfect-tender rule or instead accepted them, subject at most to Vendor's right to cure.

**Short answer.** On the facts and the governing UCC provisions, Buyer did *not* rightfully reject. Buyer accepted the goods — through untimely notice and acts inconsistent with Vendor's ownership — and is therefore confined to damages measured by diminished value under § 2-714, not a self-selected 40% refund while retaining and using the goods. Even if rejection rights had survived, Vendor's two-week replacement is a valid cure under § 2-508 that Buyer could not refuse over a minor, trade-tolerated color variance.

---

## 1. The perfect-tender rule and its built-in limits

UCC § 2-601 codifies the perfect-tender rule: where "the goods or the tender of delivery fail in any respect to conform to the contract," the buyer may reject the whole, accept the whole, or accept some units and reject the rest [1]. Read in isolation, that "in any respect" language would let Buyer reject over color alone.

But § 2-601 is expressly subordinated to other Code provisions, and courts and commentators treat the rule as materially eroded by them. The statute itself limits perfect tender by cross-reference to installment-contract rules and to contractual limitations on remedy [1], and the broader doctrinal point is well settled: § 2-601 "essentially codifies the 'perfect tender' rule of pre-Code sales law," but "other provisions in the Code have the effect of restricting the perfect-tender concept" — chiefly the cure right (§ 2-508) and the acceptance/revocation framework (§§ 2-606, 2-608) [2]. The practical question, therefore, is never simply "did the goods conform in every respect," but whether the buyer preserved its rejection right and whether the seller may cure.

---

## 2. Was color a contract term at all?

Color was specified by a reference number, so navy is, on its face, a literal deviation from a charcoal-gray term — enough to trigger § 2-601's "fail in any respect" language [1]. Two doctrines, however, qualify the weight of that term:

- **Usage of trade (§ 1-303 / § 2-208).** A usage of trade is "any practice or method of dealing having such regularity of observance in a place, vocation, or trade as to justify an expectation that it will be observed" [3], and it is admissible to give meaning to, and to "supplement or qualify," the agreement's terms [3]. Industry practice here treats minor color variation as acceptable for *functional* components. Under § 2-208(2), express terms and trade usage are construed as consistent wherever reasonable; only where that construction is unreasonable do express terms control usage [4]. The PO's emphasis on performance criteria (load capacity, durability), with no shade-variance rejection clause, supports reading the color reference as a non-dispositive specification rather than a material condition — so trade usage can fairly qualify it without being overridden.

- **The ceiling on trade usage.** This qualification is not unlimited: where express terms and usage genuinely cannot be reconciled, the express term prevails over usage of trade [4][3]. So if a fact-finder concluded the parties truly bargained for charcoal as a strict condition, navy would be a nonconformity. The point is that color's *materiality* is contestable — and that matters most for cure and for the substantial-impairment threshold below.

The realistic reading: navy is a technical nonconformity, but a minor, trade-tolerated one on a contract that prioritized performance — exactly the kind of defect the Code's cure and acceptance limits are designed to address.

---

## 3. Buyer accepted the goods

Acceptance is the dispositive issue, because acceptance forecloses rejection.

Under § 2-606(1), acceptance occurs when the buyer, after a reasonable opportunity to inspect, signifies the goods conform or that it will keep them despite nonconformity; **or** "fails to make an effective rejection"; **or** "does any act inconsistent with the seller's ownership" [5]. Each independent route is satisfied here:

- **Acts inconsistent with ownership (§ 2-606(1)(c)).** Buyer installed and operated all 1,000 widgets for eight weeks and *painted 600 of them black* — altering 60% of the goods to suit its own showroom. Painting and operating units one does not own is a classic act inconsistent with the seller's ownership.
- **Failure to make effective rejection (§ 2-606(1)(b)).** The color deviation was visible on delivery and discoverable on ordinary inspection (§ 2-513 gives the buyer the right to inspect before acceptance at any reasonable time and manner [6]). Buyer did not reject within a reasonable time (see Part 4).
- **Signifying retention despite nonconformity (§ 2-606(1)(a)).** Buyer's March emails praised performance with no color complaint, consistent with electing to keep the goods.

Acceptance of part of a commercial unit is acceptance of the entire unit [5], reinforcing that operating and modifying the widgets accepted them as delivered.

The consequences of acceptance are decisive. Once goods are accepted, the buyer must pay the contract rate for them, and acceptance "precludes rejection of the goods accepted" [7]. The only post-acceptance escape is revocation under § 2-608 — available solely where the nonconformity "substantially impairs" value and the buyer either reasonably assumed it would be cured or was induced to accept by difficulty of discovery or the seller's assurances, and acts "before any substantial change in condition of the goods which is not caused by their own defects" [8]. Buyer cannot meet this test: a color the architect later flagged as a contrast issue is not plainly a *substantial* impairment of widgets that met every functional spec; the defect was obvious, not hard to discover; and painting 600 units is itself a substantial change in their condition that bars revocation [8].

---

## 4. The notice was neither timely nor seasonable

Rejection "must be within a reasonable time after [the goods'] delivery or tender" and "is ineffective unless the buyer seasonably notifies the seller" [9]. Two timing measures both defeat Buyer:

| Measure | Requirement | Buyer's conduct | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| Contractual inspection clause | Notice within **10 days** (deadline ≈ Mar 25, 2024) | Notice on **May 10, 2024** | ~46 days late |
| § 2-602 reasonable-time test | Reasonable time after delivery for a visible defect | **56 days = 8.0 weeks** of use first | Unreasonable on its face |

For an immediately visible color difference, eight weeks of use before any complaint is not a reasonable time to reject, and the express 10-day clause makes the breach of timing concrete. Even a buyer who *had* accepted retains a § 2-714 damages claim only if it gives notice "within a reasonable time after [it] discovers or should have discovered any breach"; failure to do so bars *any* remedy under § 2-607(3)(a) [7]. The same untimeliness that defeats rejection thus also threatens Buyer's damages claim — though the March emails arguably gave Vendor some general awareness of the relationship, the color-specific notice came only on May 10.

Note too that § 2-602(2) makes any "exercise of ownership by the buyer" *after* a rejection wrongful as against the seller, and obliges a rejecting buyer to hold the goods with reasonable care at the seller's disposition [9]. Buyer's continued use and painting are incompatible with the posture of a buyer who has rejected.

---

## 5. Vendor's right to cure

Even assuming Buyer's rejection were timely, Vendor's two-week replacement offer is a valid cure that Buyer cannot brush aside.

Section 2-508(2) provides that where a buyer rejects a nonconforming tender that the seller "had reasonable grounds to believe would be acceptable," the seller may, on seasonable notice, "have a further reasonable time to substitute a conforming tender" — even beyond the original contract date [10]. The leading application is *T.W. Oil, Inc. v. Consolidated Edison Co.*, 57 N.Y.2d 574 (1982), which is closely analogous. There the seller delivered fuel oil at 0.92% sulfur against a 0.5% specification; the court held that a good-faith seller who had reasonable grounds to believe the tender acceptable — supported by trade customs allowing variation and the buyer's own tolerance for higher sulfur — may cure by substituting conforming goods within a reasonable time after the contract date, on seasonable notice [11]. The court stressed that § 2-508(2) is designed to give sellers a fair chance to cure and to "prevent[] buyers from exploiting minor defects to escape unfavorable contracts," promoting good faith and reasonable commercial standards [11].

Mapping the case here:
- **Reasonable grounds to believe acceptable.** Vendor met every functional specification, and industry practice treats minor color variation as acceptable for functional components — the same kind of trade-custom-plus-tolerance basis the *T.W. Oil* court credited [11].
- **Seasonable notice and reasonable time.** Vendor offered immediate replacement on a two-week lead time, comfortably within the "further reasonable time" § 2-508(2) allows [10].
- **The one counterweight.** The architect's confirmation that navy created showroom contrast issues is the strongest fact for Buyer, but it cuts against cure adequacy only if color was a material, substantially-impairing term — doubtful here given the performance-focused PO, the trade tolerance for color variance, and Buyer's own decision to repaint 60% of the units.

This is precisely the *T.W. Oil* scenario the cure provision exists to police: a buyer should not be able to seize on a minor, trade-tolerated nonconformity to walk away from (or rewrite) the deal when the seller stands ready to make a conforming substitution [11][2].

---

## 6. The remedy: damages, not a 40% refund

Buyer's demand — keep and use the widgets but extract a 40% cash refund — has no basis in the Code's remedial structure.

- **Accepted-goods remedy is value-based.** A buyer who has accepted goods and given § 2-607(3) notice may recover damages for nonconformity, and the warranty measure is "the difference at the time and place of acceptance between the value of the goods accepted and the value they would have had if they had been as warranted," plus any incidental and consequential loss [12]. That is a proven diminished-value figure, not an arbitrary percentage. A 40% reduction untethered to the actual difference in value is not what § 2-714 authorizes [12].
- **Price deduction requires price still due.** Section 2-717 lets a buyer deduct damages from "any part of the price still due under the same contract," and only "on notifying the seller of his intention to do so" [13]. It does not authorize a cash refund on a price already paid, nor self-help untethered to actual damages.
- **Return-and-recover remedies are unavailable.** The buyer's § 2-711 menu — cancel, recover the price paid, and cover or recover market damages — is triggered only where the buyer "rightfully rejects or justifiably revokes acceptance" (or the seller fails to deliver or repudiates) [14]. Having accepted and not validly revoked, Buyer cannot invoke § 2-711.
- **Good faith and unjust enrichment.** Every duty under the Code carries an obligation of good faith in performance and enforcement [15]. Retaining and operating all 1,000 widgets (60% repainted to Buyer's own use), refusing a conforming replacement, and demanding 40% of the price back is in tension with that duty and with the value-based remedial scheme; it would leave Buyer enriched well beyond any actual loss from a color it could have rejected on day one but instead used for eight weeks.

## 7. Weighing the acceptance factors

Applying the factors the question identifies, every material one favors Vendor:

| Factor | Direction | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Conforming goods | Mixed — technical color deviation only; all functional specs met | § 2-601 [1]; trade usage qualifies color term, § 1-303/§ 2-208 [3][4] |
| Substantial performance / materiality of color | Favors Vendor — color likely not a substantial impairment | § 2-608 substantial-impairment test [8] |
| Use after opportunity to inspect | Strongly favors Vendor — 8 weeks' use + painting 600 units = acts inconsistent with ownership | § 2-606(1)(b),(c) [5]; § 2-513 inspection right [6] |
| Timeliness of rejection | Strongly favors Vendor — 56-day delay; ~46 days past the 10-day clause | § 2-602(1) [9]; § 2-607(3) notice bar [7] |
| Cure offered | Favors Vendor — prompt, seasonable two-week substitution | § 2-508(2) [10]; *T.W. Oil* [11] |
| Good faith / unjust enrichment | Favors Vendor — keep-and-discount demand outside the remedial scheme | § 1-304 [15]; §§ 2-711, 2-714, 2-717 [14][12][13] |

## Conclusion

Buyer did not rightfully reject. The perfect-tender rule of § 2-601, though triggered in form by the navy-for-charcoal deviation, is constrained by acceptance, timing, and cure — and all three cut against Buyer [1][2]. By using all 1,000 widgets for eight weeks, painting 600 of them, and praising performance without a color complaint, Buyer accepted the goods under § 2-606 and lost its rejection right; its May 10 notice was both unreasonable under § 2-602 and roughly 46 days past the contractual 10-day window [5][9][7]. Acceptance confines Buyer to § 2-714 diminished-value damages on proper § 2-607(3) notice, not a self-selected 40% refund while retaining the goods [12][7][14]. And even if rejection had been timely, Vendor — having met all functional specs and facing only a trade-tolerated minor color variance — had reasonable grounds to believe its tender acceptable and a § 2-508(2) right to cure that its two-week replacement satisfies, on the authority of *T.W. Oil* [10][11]. Buyer must accept the goods (or the offered conforming replacement), subject at most to value-based damages for any genuine diminution attributable to the color.
