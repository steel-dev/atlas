# UCC analysis: navy widgets delivered instead of charcoal gray

## Bottom line

Buyer likely did **not** make a rightful rejection. Even if the navy/charcoal difference is treated as a technical nonconformity under the UCC perfect-tender rule, Buyer’s conduct—installing and operating all 1,000 widgets for eight weeks, painting 600 of them black, praising performance in March, waiting until May 10 to object despite a 10-day inspection-and-acceptance clause, and then refusing Vendor’s prompt replacement offer while demanding a 40% refund and continuing use—strongly supports **acceptance** under UCC § 2-606 and makes the May 10 rejection ineffective under UCC § 2-602. The more likely result is that Buyer must accept/pay for the widgets, subject at most to Vendor’s cure or to Buyer’s provable damages for any nonconformity under §§ 2-607 and 2-714—not an automatic 40% price reduction.

## Governing UCC rules

The UCC starts with a strict perfect-tender rule. Section 2-601 provides that, subject to other Article 2 provisions and unless otherwise agreed, if the goods or tender “fail in any respect to conform to the contract,” the buyer may reject the whole, accept the whole, or accept commercial units and reject the rest [1]. “Conforming” under Article 2 means goods or conduct that are “in accordance with the obligations under the contract” [2]. Thus, if the purchase order made charcoal gray a contractual obligation, navy widgets are at least a technical nonconformity even though they meet load-capacity and durability requirements.

But perfect tender is not the end of the analysis. A rejection must be timely and communicated: UCC § 2-602 states that rejection “must be within a reasonable time after” delivery or tender and is ineffective unless the buyer seasonably notifies the seller [3]. Section 2-602 also states that after rejection, any exercise of ownership by the buyer over a commercial unit is wrongful against the seller [3]. Section 2-606 defines acceptance broadly: a buyer accepts goods when, after a reasonable opportunity to inspect, it signifies that the goods conform or will be retained despite nonconformity; when it fails to make an effective rejection after a reasonable opportunity to inspect; or when it does an act inconsistent with the seller’s ownership [4].

The UCC also gives sellers a cure opportunity. Under § 2-508, when a nonconforming tender is rejected and the time for performance has not expired, the seller may seasonably notify the buyer of its intent to cure and make a conforming delivery within the contract time; and when the seller had reasonable grounds to believe the tender would be acceptable, with or without a money allowance, the seller may seasonably notify the buyer and have a further reasonable time to substitute a conforming tender [5].

Finally, once goods are accepted, the buyer’s remedies change. Section 2-607 requires the buyer to pay the contract rate for accepted goods, precludes rejection of goods accepted, and requires notice of breach within a reasonable time after discovery or when the buyer should have discovered the breach, or the buyer is barred from any remedy [6]. The buyer bears the burden of establishing breach as to accepted goods [6]. Section 2-714 then measures damages for accepted nonconforming goods by the loss resulting in the ordinary course from the breach, and for breach of warranty generally by the difference at the time and place of acceptance between the value of the goods accepted and the value they would have had as warranted, unless special circumstances show a different proximate amount [7]. These provisions do not support an automatic percentage refund merely because a defect is asserted.

## Timeline and inspection clause

On the stipulated transaction facts, the contract’s standard inspection-and-acceptance clause required Buyer to give notice within 10 days. Delivery occurred on March 15, 2024. A 10-day notice period therefore expired on March 25, 2024, while Buyer first notified Vendor on May 10, 2024—56 days after delivery, i.e., eight weeks, and 46 days after the contractual notice deadline. That contractual deadline matters because § 2-601 applies “unless otherwise agreed,” and § 2-602 independently requires rejection within a reasonable time and seasonable notice [1] [3].

## Was the navy color a nonconformity?

There is a plausible technical nonconformity. On the stipulated transaction facts, the purchase order specified a charcoal gray color reference, and the widgets arrived navy. Under § 2-601, a failure “in any respect” to conform may permit rejection before acceptance, and § 2-106 defines conformity by reference to contractual obligations [1] [2]. Full functional performance—load capacity and durability—does not erase an express color term if that term was part of the bargain.

However, the color issue is weak as a basis for late rejection. On the stipulated facts, the purchase order emphasized performance criteria—load capacity and durability—and contained no rejection clause for shade variance; the widgets met all functional specifications, including load capacity and durability; and the March emails praised widget performance without mentioning color. Industry practice also matters: UCC § 1-303 provides that a usage of trade is a practice or method of dealing with sufficient regularity to justify an expectation that it will be observed in the transaction, and that course of performance, course of dealing, and usage of trade are relevant in ascertaining the meaning of the parties’ agreement and may supplement or qualify its terms [8]. If the asserted industry practice is proven—that minor color variations are acceptable for functional components—it supports Vendor’s position that navy versus charcoal was, at most, a minor or acceptable variance in this performance-focused purchase.

That said, trade usage cannot simply nullify an inconsistent express term if the two cannot reasonably be reconciled. Section 1-303 requires express terms and usage of trade to be construed consistently where reasonable, but if that is unreasonable, express terms prevail over usage of trade [8]. Buyer’s architect’s later confirmation that navy created showroom contrast issues therefore has some relevance: it supports Buyer’s argument that color had aesthetic importance for showroom display. Still, on these facts the color term appears secondary to function, the contract lacked a shade-variance rejection clause, and industry practice treats minor color variations for functional components as acceptable. The better characterization is a **minor disputed nonconformity**, not a failure justifying rejection after prolonged use.

## Cure versus refund

Vendor’s cure offer is important even if the navy widgets are deemed nonconforming. Section 2-508 gives a seller a route to cure nonconforming tenders, including a further reasonable time where the seller had reasonable grounds to believe the tender would be acceptable [5]. Vendor had several such grounds: the widgets met all performance specifications; the PO emphasized load capacity and durability; no shade-variance rejection clause appeared; industry practice treats minor color variations as acceptable for functional components; and Buyer’s March communications praised performance without color objection. A two-week replacement lead time appears facially reasonable in relation to Buyer’s eight-week delay and continued use.

Buyer’s refusal of replacement and demand for a 40% refund while retaining use is not well aligned with the UCC remedial structure. If Buyer had effectively rejected, it could not continue exercising ownership over the goods after rejection [3]. If Buyer accepted, it must pay the contract rate for accepted goods and cannot reject them, although it may preserve other remedies for nonconformity by timely notice and proof [6]. Damages for accepted goods are measured by actual loss or value differential, not by Buyer’s unilateral refund percentage [7].

## Conclusion

Buyer probably must accept the widgets subject to cure and, at most, a claim for proven accepted-goods damages. The navy color may be a technical nonconformity under perfect tender if the charcoal gray reference was a binding specification. But Buyer did not act like a rejecting buyer: it installed and used all goods for eight weeks, altered 600 of them by painting them black, praised their performance, missed the 10-day contractual notice deadline by 46 days, delayed notice until May 10, and rejected a prompt two-week replacement offer while seeking to keep using the widgets and obtain a 40% refund. Under §§ 2-602 and 2-606, that conduct is far more consistent with acceptance than rightful rejection [3] [4]. Under §§ 2-607 and 2-714, the remaining path is payment at the contract rate, cure if appropriate, and any proven damages for a real aesthetic value loss—not rejection or an automatic 40% refund [6] [7].
