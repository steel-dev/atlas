**Bottom line:** Longwood Gardens’ 2008 treehouses were part of **“Nature’s Castles: The Treehouse Reimagined,”** not a documented Metcalfe Architecture & Design commission: the key contemporaneous opening-week source—Brian Bingaman’s **“The sky’s the limit at treehouse exhibit,” The Reporter, April 24, 2008**—attributes **Canopy Cathedral** and **Birdhouse** to **Peter Nelson / TreeHouse Workshop of Seattle**, while identifying **Lookout Loft** with **Forever Young Treehouses of Burlington, Vermont** [1]. Other April 24, 2008 newspaper coverage gives the same overall attribution pattern: Delaware County Daily Times says Lookout Loft was designed by Forever Young, Canopy Cathedral by Peter Nelson and Jake Jacob of TreeHouse Workshop, and Birdhouse by Peter Nelson [2]; LancasterOnline says Canopy Cathedral and Birdhouse were created by Treehouse Workshop of Seattle and Lookout Loft by Forever Young Treehouses [3].

The Metcalfe record points to a different project. Metcalfe Architecture & Design’s own page is for **“Out on a Limb, Morris Arboretum,”** a Philadelphia treetop exhibit with **450 feet** of elevated pathways [4]. ArchDaily likewise identifies **Morris Arboretum’s Tree Adventure exhibit Out on a Limb** as designed by Metcalfe Architecture & Design [5], and Construction Dive describes Morris Arboretum approaching Alan Metcalfe’s Philadelphia-based firm to design a child-friendly walkway **50 feet** up [6]. Together, those sources identify Metcalfe/MAD with Morris Arboretum’s **Out on a Limb**, a separate treetop-walkway project, rather than Longwood’s 2008 **Nature’s Castles** treehouses.

For the Longwood treehouses themselves, the 2008 sources describe three distinct designs. **Canopy Cathedral** was the ornate, two-story treehouse near the Italian Water Garden; The Reporter gives it as **589 square feet**, with a colorful glass facade, balcony, and Norwegian stave-church inspiration by Peter Nelson, while Daily Local names Peter Nelson and Jake Jacob of Treehouse Workshop and notes its carved-creature detailing [1][7]. **Birdhouse** was the tallest/smallest woodland structure: The Reporter describes a **255-square-foot** house near the Peirce-du Pont House, **20 feet** off the ground and also designed by Nelson, while Daily Local describes salvaged dairy-barn wood, cedar construction, benches, and a view into a rotted tree trunk [1][7]. **Lookout Loft** was the rustic Adirondack-style accessible lookout in Forest Walk; The Reporter calls it a **900-square-foot** structure with a wheelchair-accessible ramp overlooking Longwood’s **35-acre** Meadow, and Daily Local says Forever Young’s version used **13,000 pounds** of salvaged steel support and a pin-foundation strategy between tree roots [1][7].

The construction evidence also weighs against the idea of a conventional architecture-firm commission. The Reporter says all three treehouses were supported by sets of **five-foot steel pipes** forming pin foundations rather than being bolted to trees or set on poured concrete, and that each used recycled, reclaimed, salvaged, or sustainably harvested wood [1]. LancasterOnline similarly says none of the three actually sat on a tree; they were nestled around tree groups and held up with pin foundations so the trees would not be harmed, adding that Canopy Cathedral used **37 steel columns, 34 steel beams, and eight steel braces** and salvaged timbers from an Oregon wildfire and a dismantled Toronto toothpaste factory [3].

So the focused correction is: **Longwood Gardens hosted “Nature’s Castles: The Treehouse Reimagined” in 2008; the documented designers/creators were TreeHouse Workshop/Peter Nelson/Jake Jacob for Canopy Cathedral and Birdhouse, and Forever Young Treehouses for Lookout Loft. Metcalfe Architecture & Design is documented for Morris Arboretum’s Out on a Limb, not for Longwood’s treehouses** [1][2][3][4][5][6].
