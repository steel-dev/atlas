# Triage answer: this should be treated as urgent/emergency now

Based on the combination you describe—Crohn’s disease, previously documented intestinal narrowing/possible stricture, severe cramping pain that is making you double over, persistent vomiting with inability to keep down food or liquids since yesterday morning, very dry mouth, and lightheadedness on standing—this is **not a situation to “ride out” at home with clear liquids**. You should **go to the emergency department now** or call emergency services if you feel you may faint, are too weak/lightheaded to travel safely, or the pain is severe. I cannot diagnose a bowel obstruction remotely, but your symptoms fit several published red flags for obstruction and dehydration that require prompt in-person assessment.

## Why this is different from a typical Crohn’s flare you might manage at home

A mild Crohn’s/IBD flare plan may include home steps such as medication adherence, arranging tests, reducing triggers, and contacting the IBD team when symptoms are mild or moderate. In contrast, an IBD flare-management plan classifies **abdominal pain not relieved by usual measures, dark green vomit, dehydration/no oral intake, or fever** as “severe” and says to present to the nearest hospital emergency department for medical assessment [1]. A Crohn’s-specific patient leaflet on obstructive episodes similarly says possible obstruction symptoms include abdominal pain/cramps, bloating/distension, nausea/vomiting, constipation, inability to pass gas, or sometimes bursts of diarrhoea, and states: **“Go to hospital” if you are being sick persistently or have severe abdominal pain** [2].

That is the key triage shift here:

| Feature | More consistent with lower-risk home flare care | Your current description |
|---|---|---|
| Pain | Mild/intermittent or bearable, improving with usual measures | Intense cramping, doubled over |
| Intake | Able to keep fluids down | Vomiting everything since yesterday morning |
| Hydration | No major dehydration symptoms | Very dry mouth + lightheaded standing |
| Obstruction risk | No known narrowing/obstructive symptoms | Known narrowing being watched + cramping/vomiting |
| Recommended setting | Home plan/contact GI if mild-moderate | Emergency assessment now [1], [2] |

## Why the known narrowing raises concern for obstruction

Crohn’s disease can cause **strictures**, which are narrowed areas of intestine caused by inflammation, fibrosis/scarring, or both. The Crohn’s & Colitis Foundation explains that narrowing can become severe enough to cause bowel obstruction, with blockage symptoms including **nausea and vomiting, abdominal pain, bloating, and inability to pass gas or stool**; it also notes that colonoscopy and CT or MRI can be used to diagnose strictures [3]. Crohn’s & Colitis Australia similarly states that strictures may be due to inflammatory swelling, scar-tissue fibrosis, or both, and that poorly treated strictures can cause a partial or full bowel obstruction; obstructive symptoms include abdominal pain, nausea/vomiting, and inability to pass stool or wind, and severe symptoms warrant presentation to a hospital emergency department [4]. Clinically, Crohn’s strictures are assessed with colonoscopy plus imaging; CT enterography and MR enterography are standard cross-sectional tests, and treatment choices depend on whether the stricture is inflammatory, fibrotic, or mixed and on endoscopic/imaging features such as length, location, number, and severity of obstruction [5]. That distinction affects treatment, but it is not something that can be sorted out safely at home during acute severe pain and vomiting.

General bowel-obstruction sources match the symptom pattern. Mayo Clinic lists intestinal-obstruction symptoms as **crampy abdominal pain, vomiting, inability to have a bowel movement or pass gas, and abdominal swelling**, and advises immediate medical care for severe abdominal pain or other obstruction symptoms because serious complications can develop [6]. Cleveland Clinic describes obstruction pain as severe cramping, often with bloating and vomiting, and says severe cramping with bloating and vomiting should prompt immediate care [7]. MSD Manual’s clinical reference describes small-bowel obstruction as causing abdominal cramps and vomiting, complete obstruction as causing obstipation, and partial obstruction as sometimes causing diarrhoea—so having some stool or diarrhoea would not safely rule out a partial obstruction [8].

## Emergency red flags present or important to check now

For a Crohn’s patient with a known narrowing, the following red flags should be treated as urgent/emergency, especially in combination:

- **Severe or worsening abdominal pain/cramping**—Mayo says severe abdominal pain or obstruction symptoms require immediate care [6]; Cleveland Clinic says severe cramping with bloating/vomiting should prompt immediate care [7].
- **Persistent vomiting or inability to keep fluids down**—the Crohn’s obstructive-episode leaflet says to go to hospital if being sick persistently [2]; NHS vomiting guidance says urgent advice is needed when someone keeps being sick and cannot keep fluid down [9].
- **Abdominal bloating/distension**—listed as an obstruction symptom in Crohn’s obstructive episodes and bowel obstruction guidance [2], [7], [6].
- **Inability to pass stool or gas/wind**—listed as a blockage/obstruction symptom in Crohn’s stricture and general obstruction sources [3], [4], [6].
- **Fever**—Cleveland Clinic lists fever among reasons to call a provider in possible bowel obstruction [7].
- **Signs of dehydration or shock**—Cleveland Clinic lists signs of dehydration among reasons to call for obstruction symptoms [7]; NHS dehydration guidance says dizziness on standing that does not go away and reduced urination can be signs of serious dehydration needing urgent treatment, and shock-type signs require A&E/999 [10].

You already report multiple red flags: severe pain, persistent vomiting/no oral intake, dry mouth, and lightheadedness on standing, plus a known narrowing.

## Why clear liquids alone are not safe in this scenario

Oral hydration is appropriate when symptoms are mild and fluids can actually be retained. NHS guidance for diarrhoea/vomiting says home treatment focuses on fluids, using **small sips if you feel sick**, but it also says to seek urgent advice if you keep being sick and cannot keep fluid down [9]. NHS dehydration guidance similarly suggests starting with small sips if nausea/vomiting makes drinking hard, but escalates to urgent help for serious dehydration signs such as dizziness on standing that does not go away [10]. Crohn’s & Colitis UK says mild/moderate dehydration can become severe, severe dehydration needs urgent treatment, and severe signs include a very dry mouth and dizziness on standing; it also identifies repeated vomiting as a dehydration risk [11].

Clear liquids do not address a possible obstruction, electrolyte disturbance, or kidney stress from a day of vomiting. If fluid keeps coming back up, oral rehydration is not functioning as treatment—it is a sign you need medical assessment.

## What the ER can provide that home care cannot

In this scenario, the emergency department can simultaneously evaluate dehydration and possible obstruction/stricture complication. Sources describe the following capabilities:

- **IV fluids and electrolyte replacement.** MSD Manual states that possible intestinal obstruction should be hospitalized and treated with IV fluids, with electrolyte replacement guided by test results; repeated vomiting commonly depletes sodium and potassium [8]. Cleveland Clinic also lists IV fluids and electrolytes as obstruction treatment [7].
- **Blood tests for dehydration, infection, electrolytes, kidney function.** Cleveland Clinic says obstruction evaluation may include complete blood count and electrolyte analysis, and that electrolyte levels can show severe dehydration needing immediate fluids [7]. Mayo Clinic’s dehydration treatment page says blood tests can check electrolytes such as sodium and potassium and how well the kidneys are working [12].
- **Antiemetics and symptom control.** Cleveland Clinic lists anti-emetics to prevent nausea/vomiting and pain relievers as part of obstruction management [7].
- **Abdominal imaging, often CT.** Cleveland Clinic lists imaging procedures for obstruction, including X-ray and CT; Mayo and Merck also describe X-ray/CT use for suspected obstruction [7], [13]. Crohn’s stricture imaging literature notes that acute manifestations of intestinal obstruction in Crohn’s are evaluated with abdominal CT with contrast in the emergency setting [14].
- **Gastroenterology/surgical evaluation.** MSD Manual states that treatment of acute intestinal obstruction proceeds alongside diagnosis and that a surgeon should always be involved [8]. The AGA patient hospitalization resource notes that hospitalized IBD care may involve GI teams and, for Crohn’s intestinal blockage, bowel rest, IV fluids, nasogastric decompression, steroids or surgery depending on the situation [15].

## What can happen if obstruction or dehydration is not treated

The concern is not just discomfort. Untreated intestinal obstruction can become life-threatening. Mayo Clinic says untreated obstruction can cut off blood supply, causing tissue death, perforation, and peritonitis, a life-threatening infection requiring immediate medical and often surgical attention [6]. MSD Manual similarly states that prolonged obstruction can cause bowel ischemia, infarction, and perforation, and that vomiting and third-spacing can cause volume depletion [8].

Severe dehydration also has serious complications. Crohn’s & Colitis UK says severe dehydration needs urgent treatment and, without it, complications can include kidney problems or low blood pressure [11]. AGA’s IBD hydration resource notes that dehydration can lead to electrolyte imbalances and that high or low electrolyte levels can be life-threatening [16]. Mayo Clinic says severe dehydration may require emergency treatment with IV salts and fluids, and that blood tests assess electrolytes and kidney function [12].

## What to do now, practically

1. **Go to the ER now.** If you have severe pain, are continuing to vomit, feel faint, are confused, have a fast heartbeat, cold/clammy skin, shortness of breath, very little urine, fever, a swollen/distended abdomen, or cannot pass gas/stool, call emergency services rather than waiting.
2. **Do not drive yourself.** NHS guidance for dehydration and vomiting/diarrhoea says not to drive to A&E; ask someone to drive you or call an ambulance/999 [10], [9]. This is especially important because you are lightheaded on standing.
3. **Call your GI doctor/on-call IBD clinician if you can do so without delaying ER care.** It is reasonable to notify them, but do **not** wait at home for a callback before seeking emergency evaluation because your current pattern meets hospital/urgent-care red flags [1], [2].
4. **While awaiting transport/care:** do not force food or large amounts of fluid. If you can tolerate it, take only small sips; NHS vomiting/dehydration guidance recommends small sips when nauseated, but persistent inability to keep fluids down requires escalation [10], [9].
5. **Avoid NSAIDs unless your clinician specifically told you otherwise.** The Crohn’s obstructive-episode leaflet advises avoiding NSAIDs such as ibuprofen, naproxen, diclofenac, and aspirin because they can make Crohn’s symptoms worse [2].
6. **Avoid non-prescribed anti-diarrhoeal/constipating medicines such as loperamide unless your IBD team or ER clinician directs it.** Crohn’s & Colitis UK says anti-diarrhoeal medicines should not be taken during a flare, with fever, or blood in stool, and that people with a bowel narrowing/stricture may not be able to take them and should check with the doctor or IBD team first [11]. NHS loperamide guidance also says not to take loperamide if constipated or the stomach looks swollen, or during a flare-up of an inflammatory bowel condition such as ulcerative colitis [17].

## Bottom line

This presentation should be treated as **urgent/emergency now**, not as a usual flare managed at home. The highest-risk explanation to rule out is a partial or complete bowel obstruction related to your known narrowing/stricture, alongside clinically significant dehydration from persistent vomiting. The ER can give IV fluids, check electrolytes and kidney function, control vomiting, image the abdomen, and involve GI/surgery if needed—none of which clear liquids at home can safely replace.
