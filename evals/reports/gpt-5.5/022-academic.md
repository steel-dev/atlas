# Evolution of feminist legal theory across legal traditions

## Executive summary

The sources show four distinct but overlapping trajectories in feminist legal theory. Anglo-American critical feminism moved from formal equal treatment to critiques of male-normed institutions and then to state responsibility for dependency and vulnerability. Islamic feminism, as represented by Ziba Mir-Hosseini and Amina Wadud, works from within Islamic interpretive traditions, distinguishing divine ideals from human juristic rules and rereading scripture around justice, reciprocity, and women’s agency. African feminist legal theory is strongly shaped by legal pluralism: it criticizes both patriarchal invocations of custom and abstract universalism that ignores women’s dependence on kinship and local institutions. Chinese feminist legal thought and activism have developed under state socialism, post-socialist marketization, and authoritarian party-state governance: women’s rights are formally codified through state institutions, but feminist leverage depends heavily on whether activists can work through or around the state [1] [2] [3] [4] [5] [6] [7].

Across the three legal dimensions asked about, the strongest contrast is this:

## 1. Anglo-American critical legal feminism: from formal equality to dominance and vulnerability

The Anglo-American literature in the Stanford Encyclopedia account does not present “feminist legal theory” as a single school. It identifies multiple strands—liberal/neoliberal, pragmatist, relational, cultural/difference, dominance/radical, socialist/Marxist, postcolonial, transnational, postmodern, intersectional, and queer approaches—and places them in a historical sequence of legal reform struggles . In broad outline, nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century feminists sought voting, property, and contract rights; 1960s–1970s liberal feminism targeted formal legal barriers and anti-discrimination rules; 1980s difference feminism focused on work, care, and family structures; 1980s dominance feminism addressed pornography, sexual harassment, rape, and sexual subordination; and 1990s-and-later approaches added intersectional, queer, transnational, and postmodern critiques .

Catharine MacKinnon’s contribution is to shift equality analysis away from whether women are “the same as” or “different from” men. Her dominance theory argues that legal doctrine’s apparently neutral comparison standard is itself male-normed: law asks whether women are similarly situated to men in institutions already structured around male life patterns. The result is that custody, alimony, work, merit, pregnancy, sexuality, and citizenship can be governed by facially neutral rules that reproduce male advantage [8]. On bodily autonomy, this approach treats sexual harassment, rape, pornography, and pregnancy not as isolated private harms but as legal expressions of a hierarchy in which sexuality and social power are organized by gender [8].

Martha Fineman’s vulnerability theory moves in a related but distinct direction. Rather than grounding reform only in identity-based anti-discrimination law, she argues that vulnerability is “universal and constant” in the human condition and that the liberal model of the autonomous legal subject should be replaced by the “vulnerable subject.” The legal implication is a “responsive state” that structures institutions to build resilience and support dependency, care, and embodied need, instead of merely policing formal discrimination after the fact [10].

For women’s rights, the Anglo-American trajectory therefore moves through three layers: first, formal access to property, contract, and vote; second, recognition that formally equal rules may encode male standards; and third, a critique of the liberal legal subject itself. In comparative terms, this tradition is strongest at theorizing how neutral rules conceal structural subordination, but it is less anchored in a single comprehensive women’s-rights code than the African regional or Chinese statutory models discussed below  [10].

## 2. Islamic feminism: reinterpretation of Shari‘ah, fiqh, and Qur’anic equality

Islamic feminism in the sources does not reject Islamic law as such. Its key move is interpretive: it distinguishes Shari‘ah as a divine, ethical path toward justice from fiqh as human, historically situated, and fallible juristic understanding. Mir-Hosseini uses this distinction to argue that patriarchal family-law rules should not be treated as immutable divine commands; they can be criticized and reformed from within Islamic legal reasoning and in dialogue with equality and human-rights frameworks [2] [4].

This produces a different legal map from Anglo-American feminism. In classical fiqh, Mir-Hosseini identifies a paradox: women may retain legal personality, economic autonomy, and control over property within marriage—historically more favorable than English coverture before the Married Women’s Property Act 1882—but may simultaneously lack bodily and marital autonomy because marriage is structured around male sexual access, maintenance in exchange for obedience, unilateral male talaq, and more burdensome divorce routes for women through khul‘ or courts [2]. Property and body are thus separated: economic personality can coexist with sexual and marital subordination.

Amina Wadud’s contribution is more hermeneutic and participatory. She argues from Qur’anic equality in creation, equal moral agency, khilafah, taqwa, tawhid, and reciprocity. Women are not merely beneficiaries of male interpretation; they must be full participants in creating, interpreting, and reforming law and policy. On this view, patriarchy is incompatible with women’s agency and integrity because it denies the equal moral subjecthood that the Qur’an presupposes [3].

Compared with MacKinnon and Fineman, Islamic feminism is less centered on secular constitutional doctrine and more on interpretive authority: who may say what God’s justice requires, and whether patriarchal fiqh can be distinguished from Shari‘ah. Its reform strategy is therefore both internal and transnational—internal because it argues within Islamic concepts, and transnational because Mir-Hosseini’s work also connects Islamic justice claims with human-rights and gender-equality frameworks [2] [4].

## 3. African feminist legal theory: customary law, living law, and regional human rights

African feminist legal theory in these sources is shaped by plural legal orders: statutory law, constitutional or regional human-rights law, and customary or kinship-based systems. Nyamu-Musembi’s legal-pluralist critique rejects both abstract universalism and cultural relativism. Formal statutory rights may be real but practically inaccessible because courts are costly, biased, or socially disruptive; for example, a widow with statutory inheritance rights may still have to preserve relationships with in-laws to secure housing, livelihood, or children’s standing. The recommended strategy is not simply to abandon custom, but to treat kinship and customary forums as sites of rights contestation: document flexibility in “living” custom, enforce obligations already recognized by custom, invoke local ideas of justice and fairness, and expose strategic invocations of “custom” that preserve hierarchy [14].

At the regional level, the Maputo Protocol provides the strongest codified statement among the African sources. It was adopted on 11 July 2003 and entered into force on 25 November 2005. It defines harmful practices as conduct affecting women’s or girls’ life, health, dignity, education, or physical integrity; requires states to eliminate discrimination and harmful practices; recognizes women’s rights as inalienable, interdependent, and indivisible; and allows more favorable national or international protections to prevail [5].

The Protocol directly addresses the three legal dimensions in the research question:

- **Property:** Article 19(c) requires women’s access to and control over productive resources such as land and guarantees property rights. Article 21 gives widows an equitable share in a husband’s property, the right to remain in the matrimonial home, and provides that women and men inherit parents’ property in equitable shares [5].
- **Bodily autonomy:** Article 14 protects control of fertility, decisions on number and spacing of children, contraception, STI/HIV information and protection, family-planning education, and medical abortion in cases of rape, sexual assault, incest, danger to the mother’s mental or physical health or life, or danger to the foetus [5].
- **Political participation:** Article 9 requires positive action or affirmative action, equal participation in elections and electoral processes, equal partnership in state policy and development, and effective representation at all levels of decision-making [5].

The African framework therefore combines a strong human-rights instrument with a feminist caution about implementation. The Maputo Protocol is more explicit than the Anglo-American theory sources on land, reproductive rights, and representation, but Nyamu-Musembi’s critique explains why text alone is not enough in legally plural societies [5] [14].

## 4. Chinese feminist legal scholarship and activism: state socialism, marketization, and managed rights

Chinese feminist legal development follows a different path because feminism has been deeply entangled with the party-state. Wang Zheng identifies three cohorts: socialist state feminists, post-socialist NGO feminists around the Fourth World Conference on Women, and younger feminist activists. The relationship between feminism and the state remains central even after state socialism evolved into state capitalism and marketization [6].

In the early PRC, socialist state feminists used the All-China Women’s Federation’s nationwide institutional reach. The first law adopted by the new socialist state was the 1950 Marriage Law, drafted by a feminist committee led by Deng Yingchao. It attacked arranged and underage marriage, gave women freedom to divorce and remarry, and used state power to establish gender-equality norms. Wang also identifies early achievements in women’s literacy, equal employment and equal pay, political participation, reproductive health, and public facilities reducing working women’s childcare and housework burdens [6].

Marketization changed the terrain. From the 1980s, privatization and market reform made urban women workers “first fired and last hired,” while employers sought to avoid socialist-era reproductive costs such as paid maternity leave and daycare. Decollectivization and the 1979 one-child policy created particular pressures for rural women. After the 1995 Beijing conference, feminists inside and outside the official system used CEDAW, the Beijing Declaration, and the Platform for Action to work on domestic violence, legal aid, rural women’s political participation, sex-ratio imbalances, employment, and gender training [6]. Their most visible legal success was China’s first anti-domestic-violence law, passed on 27 December 2015 after two decades of feminist work [6].

The current statutory framework is the revised Law of the People’s Republic of China on the Protection of Rights and Interests of Women, revised at the 37th Meeting of the Standing Committee of the 13th National People’s Congress on 30 October 2022 and effective 1 January 2023. It frames equality as a “basic national policy,” places women’s-rights protection under Communist Party leadership, and assigns a representational role to the ACWF and local women’s federations [7]. Its structure is comprehensive: political rights; person and personality rights; culture and education; labor and social security; property; marriage and family; remedies; and legal responsibilities [7].

Its rights provisions are detailed:

- **Property:** Article 53 guarantees women equal property rights; Article 54 protects women’s interests in marital community property and family common property; Article 55 gives women equal rights in rural collective economic organization membership, land contracting, collective proceeds, land-expropriation compensation, and house-site use, and requires women to be enumerated in relevant registrations and certificates; Article 56 prohibits village rules from denying rights because women are unmarried, married, divorced, widowed, or without male household members; Article 58 guarantees equal inheritance [7].
- **Bodily autonomy/personhood:** Article 21 declares women’s rights to life, body, and health inviolable; prohibits abuse, abandonment, persecution, trafficking, non-medical fetal sex identification, and sex-selective termination; and requires women’s own consent for fertility operations, special examinations, or special treatments, with the woman’s will respected over family disagreement. Article 23 prohibits sexual harassment and allows complaints, police reports, or civil suits; Article 25 requires employers to establish anti-harassment rules, complaint channels, investigation procedures, privacy protections, and support for victims [7].
- **Political participation:** Articles 12–14 guarantee equal political rights, participation in administration of state, economic, cultural, and social affairs, equal voting and candidacy rights, and an “appropriate number” of women deputies to the National People’s Congress and local people’s congresses, with measures to gradually increase women’s share [7].

Yet the sources also show implementation tensions. In marital property, a 2011 Supreme Court interpretation shifted some premarital housing brought into marriage to a title-based regime; a Journal of Human Resources study reports that before 2011 couples expected equal division even if the home was titled only in the husband’s name, but after the 2011 decision wives’ intrahousehold bargaining power weakened, with reductions in their leisure, increased consumption of male-favored goods, and reduced investment in children [12]. In rural land, a 2024 judicial study found that married and divorced rural women often lose recognition in rural collective economic organizations: birth villages may treat them as married out, while husbands’ villages may not allocate new land during the 30-year contracting period, leaving women without land in either place [13].

Chinese feminist legal strategy is therefore unusually state-dependent. Older feminists often worked behind the scenes through ACWF, party, and personal networks; younger feminists, with fewer policy channels, have turned to public performance, online mobilization, and body politics, while facing repression such as the 2015 detention of the Feminist Five during anti-sexual-harassment activism [6].

## 5. Comparative analysis: how legal systems structure property, body, and voice

### Property rights

The strongest formal property protections in the cited materials are the Maputo Protocol and China’s 2022 Women’s Rights Law. Maputo directly guarantees women’s access and control over land and productive resources and protects widows’ inheritance and residence rights [5]. China’s statute is similarly detailed for equal property, marital property, rural collective membership, land compensation, house-site rights, and inheritance [7].

Islamic feminism presents a more paradoxical structure: classical fiqh may recognize women’s separate property and economic personality, but embeds that autonomy within marital relations that can subordinate women’s bodies and divorce rights [2]. Anglo-American legal feminism historically began with property and contract rights against coverture, but the critical legal theory sources emphasize that removing formal disabilities does not by itself dismantle male-normed economic and family institutions  [8].

### Bodily autonomy

Bodily autonomy is structured most explicitly as a reproductive-rights issue in the Maputo Protocol: Article 14 covers fertility, contraception, spacing of children, STI/HIV information, family planning, and abortion in specified cases [5]. China’s Women’s Rights Law uses a personhood, health, and consent framework: women’s life, body, and health are inviolable, consent is required for fertility operations and special medical procedures, and sexual harassment is prohibited [7].

MacKinnon’s Anglo-American dominance theory is analytically powerful on sexual subordination because it connects rape, harassment, pornography, pregnancy, and sexuality to legal structures of male power rather than treating them as private deviations [8]. Islamic feminism’s bodily-autonomy focus centers on marriage, sexual access, obedience, and divorce: Mir-Hosseini’s critique is that rules of maintenance-for-obedience and unilateral talaq deny women control over their bodies even when their property capacity is recognized [2].

### Political participation

Political participation ranges from formal suffrage and representation to participation in interpretation and institutional design. Anglo-American feminism’s early struggle included voting and citizenship, while later critical theory attacks the male norm of the abstract autonomous citizen  [10]. Wadud’s Islamic feminism defines participation more interpretively: women must be authors and interpreters of law, not merely subjects of male juristic authority [3].

Maputo provides the clearest regional mandate: Article 9 requires affirmative or positive action, equal electoral participation, equal partnership in state policy and development, and effective representation at all decision-making levels [5]. China’s statute guarantees equal political rights and voting/candidacy, but its implementation is mediated through party-state institutions and the ACWF; Wang’s account shows both the power of state channels and the limits imposed by authoritarian monitoring and repression [6] [7].

## Conclusion

The evolution of feminist legal theory across these traditions is not a linear diffusion of one model. Each tradition identifies a different core problem in law. Anglo-American critical feminism exposes the male norm inside formal equality and the liberal subject. Islamic feminism contests patriarchal interpretation by separating divine justice from human fiqh and rereading scripture around reciprocity and agency. African feminism confronts the interaction of customary law, state law, and regional human rights, insisting that women’s rights must be both formally guaranteed and locally usable. Chinese feminist legal scholarship navigates a powerful state that can codify and implement women’s rights nationwide but also constrains independent feminist mobilization, especially under marketization and political tightening.

The comparison shows that women’s legal position depends not simply on whether a system declares equality, but on how it allocates authority: judges and legislatures in Anglo-American systems; jurists and interpreters in Islamic legal traditions; customary, national, and regional bodies in African pluralism; and party-state, ACWF, courts, employers, and village collectives in China. Property, bodily autonomy, and political participation are therefore structured by different institutional logics—but in every tradition, feminist legal theory turns on the same recurring question: whether women are treated as full legal subjects with resources, bodily integrity, and voice in making the norms that govern them.
