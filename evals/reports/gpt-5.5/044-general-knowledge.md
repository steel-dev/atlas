# Women’s labour-force participation, 1970–2025: Saudi Arabia, South Korea, Iceland and India

## Executive comparison

Across the four countries, the 1970–2025 record separates into four distinct trajectories. Iceland moved earliest to high female participation and then plateaued near the top of the group; South Korea rose gradually but still shows a large motherhood penalty and occupational segmentation; Saudi Arabia had the sharpest recent break, more than doubling participation after Vision 2030-era reforms; India is the outlier with a long decline through 2020 followed by a recent recovery that is still heavily concentrated in rural self-employment and unpaid or low-paid work.

**Ranking in 2025:** Iceland remains highest at 70.012%, followed by Korea at 56.782%, Saudi Arabia at 33.573%, and India at 32.425% [1]. The ranking hides very different momentum: Saudi Arabia gained 13.716 percentage points from 2017 to 2025, far more than Korea’s 3.709 p.p., while Iceland declined 2.580 p.p. and India rose 5.847 p.p. from its 2017 low [1].

## Policy and social drivers by country

### Iceland: early mass entry, then consolidation through childcare, individual leave rights and equality law

Iceland’s female participation was already high by 1980: a national-estimate source gives 46.893%, while an Iceland historical source states that by 1980 women’s labour-force participation had reached “about 65 percent,” among the highest in the West [2], [3]. The difference reflects source definitions, but both point to an early takeoff before the 1990 modeled ILO rate of 66.204% [1]. Since 1990, the level has stayed high rather than rising steadily: 66.204% in 1990, 73.471% in 2000, 70.490% in 2010, 70.252% in 2024 and 70.012% in 2025 [1].

The core correlation is between high participation and the build-out of care infrastructure and gender-equality institutions. Before the 1990s, short parental leave and limited childcare were described as blocking women’s path to work, with full-day places historically targeted mainly to single mothers and students [3]. Conditions improved in the 1990s as Reykjavík and other municipalities offered full-day daycare to children of cohabiting parents [3]. In 1980, all working women gained three months of paid maternity leave; in 2000 fathers received an independent right to three months of paid parental leave, mothers received three months, and parents could share an additional three months [3].

The current legal framework is strongly egalitarian. Iceland scores 100/100 in all Women, Business and the Law 2024 dimensions—mobility, workplace, pay, marriage, parenthood, entrepreneurship, assets/inheritance and pension [4]. Act No. 144/2020 states that maternity/paternity and parental leave is intended to ensure a child’s access to both parents and enable parents to coordinate family life and work outside the home [5]. The same Act covers employees and self-employed parents, and also provides maternity/paternity grants for parents not active in the labour market, parents in less than 25% of full-time employment, and full-time students [5]. Icelandic gender-equality reporting also notes that parental leave can be structured flexibly, including as shorter periods or with a reduced full-time/part-time ratio, although unpaid parental leave is separate from the paid maternity/paternity system [6].

Iceland’s plateau after 2000 suggests that policy has not simply raised participation; it has maintained very high attachment while supporting fertility and caregiving. The remaining issue is less access to the labour market than quality and equality within it. Iceland introduced equal pay certification in 2017, requiring companies and institutions to implement a standardized pay-management system based on objective criteria to prevent gender discrimination [3].

### South Korea: sustained rise with a persistent motherhood break and segmented jobs

Korea’s female participation rose from 38.359% in 1970 to 39.463% in 1980 in national-estimate data, then from 47.207% in 1990 to 56.782% in 2025 in the modeled ILO series [2], [1]. Historical data show that this was not a uniform rise across sectors: in 1970, female LFPR was 48.2% in farm households but only 29.8% in non-farm households; by 1985 it was 50.7% in farm households and 37.7% in non-farm households [7]. The non-farm rise is central to Korea’s industrialization story.

A World Bank historical report links Korea’s post-1970 increase to strong economic growth, rural-to-urban migration, rising education, and lower fertility. Women’s average schooling rose from 2.92 years in 1960 to 6.63 years in 1980, while total fertility fell from 4.5 in 1967 to about 2.05 by the mid-1980s under family planning [7]. These shifts helped move women from family farm work into paid non-farm employment.

The modern picture is a high-education but motherhood-constrained labour market. OECD/KDI material reports that women’s employment rate at ages 25–64 increased from 55% in 1996 to 66% in 2023; among women aged 25–34, tertiary attainment rose from 27% in 1997 to 77% in 2023, exceeding men’s 63% in 2023 [8]. Yet tertiary-educated women aged 25–34 are still 7 percentage points less likely to be employed than male peers, and the tertiary gender employment gap among ages 25–64 is 18.3 percentage points [8]. Women remain over-represented in health, welfare, education and clerical work and under-represented in STEM [8].

The policy problem is not only leave duration or education; it is the interaction of care, workplace culture and dual labour markets. A 2024 OECD diagnosis describes Korea as an outlier with total fertility around 0.7/0.72 in 2023, a high gender pay gap, and a large motherhood penalty: KDI cites mothers’ long-term income falling by about 66% as labour-force participation drops after childbirth, while fathers are barely affected [9]. OECD recommendations therefore focus on adjusting childcare to working parents’ needs, expanding parental leave to the whole workforce, strengthening sanctions for workplace discrimination and labour inspection, reducing private education and housing costs, and reducing labour-market dualism [9].

Part-time work has expanded but is not a full solution. A KDI Journal study defines part-time as fewer than 36 weekly hours among employees aged 16–84 excluding self-employed workers, and finds the part-time share doubled from 9.9% in 2010 to 19.8% in 2024 [10]. Workforce ageing, more women and married women in the labour force, and service-sector expansion all coincided with this increase, but compositional change explains only about 30% of the rise, or about 10% after excluding older-worker government-program participants [10]. Korea therefore appears to be adding flexible and short-hour jobs, but the evidence retrieved does not show that these have eliminated the childbirth-related exit.

### Saudi Arabia: the sharpest policy-linked break, led by legal reform, mobility support and Vision 2030 targets

Saudi Arabia’s female LFPR was low and nearly flat for decades: 15.024% in 1990, 16.389% in 2000, 17.628% in 2010 and 19.857% in 2017 [1]. It then jumped to 29.308% in 2020, 33.305% in 2024 and 33.573% in 2025 [1]. A World Bank/IZA study gives an even clearer reform-era measure: Saudi women’s LFPR rose from 17.4% to 35.9% within six years, 2017–2023, more than doubling [11].

The same source attributes this break to Vision 2030 institutional reforms, legal reforms removing employment-discrimination and sexual-harassment barriers, entrepreneurship reforms, active labour-market programs, private-sector demand, sector diversification and norm-change communication [11]. Vision 2030’s original target was to raise women’s LFPR from 22% to 30%; that target was achieved and later raised to 40% [11].

Specific programs addressed mobility, childcare and hiring costs. The Human Resources Development Fund employment subsidy subsidized over 115,491 women in 2019–2021 [11]. Wusool subsidizes transport for Saudi women in the private sector [11]. Qurrah, launched in 2018, subsidizes licensed childcare for children under age 6 for up to four years and up to two children, but by 2021 had reached only 3,090 women; the source links this limited take-up to geographic constraints and shortage of licensed childcare centers [11].

Legal reform remains incomplete. Women, Business and the Law 2024 gives Saudi Arabia 71.3/100 overall: 100 in workplace, pay, entrepreneurship and pension, but only 50 in mobility, 40 in marriage, 40 in parenthood and 40 in assets [12]. The WBL details indicate that Saudi women can travel outside the home and apply for a passport in the same way as men, but cannot choose residence or travel outside the country in the same way; inheritance remains unequal for sons and daughters and for surviving spouses [12]. This matters because the participation surge is correlated with reform, but the remaining legal gaps align with remaining constraints around mobility, family status and assets.

Sectorally, the rise has not fully integrated women into private employment. The Saudi source states that women still represented less than 10% of total private-sector employment despite gains [11]. It also identifies childcare gaps, remaining mobility barriers, limited public transport outside major cities and for lower-income women, preference for public-sector jobs, and skills mismatch as continuing constraints [11]. Education composition has broadened: women with basic and intermediate education more than doubled participation between 2017 and 2021, and Saudi Arabia was the only MENA country in the source with increased participation across all age groups in 2017–2022 [11].

### India: decline, recent recovery, and a large measurement/quality caveat

India’s trajectory is the least linear. World Bank modeled ILO data show female LFPR at 30.257% in 1990, rising to 34.397% in 2000, then falling to 28.425% in 2010 and 25.998% in 2020 before recovering to 32.415% in 2024 and 32.425% in 2025 [1]. A national-estimate World Bank series gives 30.694% in 2000, 28.900% in 2010, 26.168% in 2020 and 34.389% in 2024 [2].

NSS-based research explains the decline more sharply. A World Bank paper estimates that working-age female participation fell from 42.6% in 1993–94 to 31.2% in 2011–12, with the fall concentrated in rural areas and especially in 2004–05 to 2011–12; about 19.2 million women left the labour force between 2004–05 and 2011–12 [13]. ILO research attributes the decline to rising educational attendance, higher household consumption/income, insufficient suitable job opportunities, occupational segregation and measurement limits around unpaid family or domestic production [14]. Among women aged 15–24, FLFP fell from 42% to 22.7% between 1993–94 and 2011–12 while educational participation rose 22.1 percentage points [13].

The 2023–24 official PLFS recovery is substantial but must be interpreted by work status. PLFS reports female LFPR in usual status at 41.7% for ages 15+ in 2023–24, up from 23.3% in 2017–18 [15]. A PLFS summary for all ages reports female LFPR at 35.5% in rural areas and 22.3% in urban areas [16]. Female work is highly rural and self-employed: among rural female workers, 73.5% were self-employed and only 7.8% were regular salaried/wage workers; among urban female workers, 42.3% were self-employed and 49.4% were salaried [16]. Across all workers, 58.4% were self-employed, 21.7% regular wage/salaried and 19.8% casual [16].

The sector distribution also shows why participation and job quality must be separated. Among all workers in PLFS 2023–24, 46.1% were in agriculture, 12.2% in trade/hotel/restaurant, 12.0% in construction and 11.4% in manufacturing [16]. Earnings gaps remain large: in April–June 2024, regular salaried women earned Rs 12,396 per month in rural areas versus Rs 18,200 for men, and Rs 19,879 in urban areas versus Rs 26,105 for men; casual women earned Rs 299/day in rural areas versus Rs 444 for men and Rs 364/day in urban areas versus Rs 537 for men [16].

India’s policy framework has improved maternity protection but remains uneven on shared caregiving. The 2017 Maternity Benefit Amendment increased paid maternity leave from 12 weeks to 26 weeks, extended prenatal leave to eight weeks, provided 12 weeks for adoptive and commissioning mothers, allowed a post-leave work-from-home option, required employers to inform women of benefits, and mandated a crèche for establishments with 50 or more employees; women may visit the crèche four times during the day [17]. WBL/IFC sources note no paid paternity leave and no paid parental leave [18], [17]. IFC warned that employer-liability design can create concerns about discouraging women’s hiring, and its 2018 employer survey found 90% had implemented the 26-week leave but crèche provision lagged, with 22% in place before the Act and 52% either added after the Act or under development [17].

Legal rights are stronger in mobility and inheritance than in pay and parenthood. WBL 2024 gives India 74.4/100 overall, with 100 in mobility, workplace and marriage; 80 in assets; 75 in entrepreneurship and pension; but only 25 in pay and 40 in parenthood [18]. India has equal mobility, and the Hindu Succession Amendment 2005 gives sons and daughters equal inheritance, but the absence of paid paternity/parental leave and weak pay indicators remain relevant constraints [18].

## Cross-country comparisons: what policies correlate most clearly with participation?

### 1. Childcare and parental leave: strongest in Iceland, incomplete in Korea and India, newly targeted in Saudi Arabia

The strongest positive institutional package is Iceland’s combination of full-day childcare expansion, individual father leave rights and comprehensive equality law. The country had already reached very high participation by 1990 and has maintained roughly 70% participation since then [1], [3]. Korea and India both expanded education dramatically, but participation has been held back by care and job-quality constraints. Korea’s problem is not women’s education—young women’s tertiary attainment reached 77% in 2023—but the childbirth-related employment break and 66% long-term income penalty for mothers [8], [9]. India’s 26-week maternity leave is generous for covered formal employees, but no paid paternity/parental leave and employer-financed obligations risk reinforcing women-specific hiring costs [17], [18]. Saudi Arabia’s Qurrah childcare subsidy is directly targeted but had reached only 3,090 women by 2021, too small to explain the full national participation surge [11].

### 2. Mobility and legal reform: decisive for Saudi Arabia, largely settled in Iceland/Korea/India but with gaps in India and Korea pay law

Legal reform appears most tightly linked to a short-period jump in Saudi Arabia, where participation rose from 17.4% to 35.9% in 2017–2023 alongside Vision 2030, anti-discrimination, sexual-harassment, entrepreneurship, transport and norm-change reforms [11]. Yet Saudi Arabia’s WBL score remains the lowest of the four at 71.3/100, with weak mobility, marriage, parenthood and assets indicators [12]. Iceland’s complete WBL score and Korea’s and India’s equal mobility/inheritance scores show that formal legal equality is necessary but not sufficient: Korea still has a weak pay score and strong motherhood penalty, and India still has weak pay/parenthood indicators and low urban female participation [4], [19], [18], [9], [16].

### 3. Education: raises capacity, but effects differ by job structure

Education is most clearly associated with rising participation in Korea and a delayed or ambiguous effect in India. Korea’s female schooling rose from 2.92 years in 1960 to 6.63 in 1980 during the early participation rise, and young women’s tertiary attainment later reached 77% in 2023 [7], [8]. India’s education expansion initially reduced measured participation because young women stayed in school: ages 15–24 FLFP fell from 42% to 22.7% while educational participation rose 22.1 p.p. from 1993–94 to 2011–12 [13]. Saudi Arabia’s recent gains broadened beyond highly educated women, with participation among women with basic and intermediate education more than doubling between 2017 and 2021 [11].

### 4. Sector and full-time/part-time patterns: quality differs more than headline participation

The available sources support different levels of detail by country. Korea has a measured part-time expansion: the part-time share among employees aged 16–84 doubled from 9.9% in 2010 to 19.8% in 2024, but the data do not show that this alone solves the motherhood penalty [10]. India’s PLFS does not frame the main breakdown as part-time/full-time in the retrieved sources; the clearest split is employment status, with rural female work dominated by self-employment at 73.5% and urban female work split between self-employment at 42.3% and salaried work at 49.4% [16]. Saudi Arabia’s retrieved source emphasizes private-sector under-representation—women below 10% of private-sector employment—rather than part-time/full-time shares [11]. Iceland’s retrieved evidence documents flexible parental-leave take-up and shorter-hour collective-agreement developments, but not a quantified female full-time/part-time split for 2025 [6], [3]. Therefore, a strict part-time/full-time comparison can be quantified for Korea from the retrieved evidence and only partly characterized for the other three.

## Demographic projections and future participation

Demography will make female participation more important in all four countries, but through different channels.

* **Korea:** Ultra-low fertility—TFR around 0.7/0.72 in 2023—means the future workforce will shrink unless women’s employment and older-worker employment rise. OECD explicitly frames higher female employment and fertility as a dual necessity, recommending childcare reform, broader parental leave, anti-discrimination enforcement, lower education/housing costs and reduced labour-market dualism [9].
* **India:** India’s age structure still offers a demographic dividend, but projections show that low female participation can prevent it from being realized. Under constant participation by age/sex/education/region, India’s labour-force dependency ratio never falls below 1.65 and reaches 1.86 in 2060; raising female participation to Goa-like best-region levels lowers the 2060 ratio to 1.51, while full gender-equality participation lowers it to 0.79 and raises the worker share of population by 60% relative to constant rates [20].
* **Saudi Arabia:** The retrieved sources do not provide a quantified Saudi long-run dependency projection, but they show a policy target: after surpassing the original 30% women’s LFPR target, the target was raised to 40% [11]. Future gains depend on whether childcare, transport and private-sector integration can scale beyond the early reform surge, especially for women outside major cities and lower-income groups [11].
* **Iceland:** Iceland is already near the upper participation frontier among the four. Future demographic resilience is more likely to depend on maintaining high female attachment, equal pay, usable leave and childcare than on another large participation increase. Its 2025 modeled LFPR, 70.012%, is already more than twice Saudi Arabia’s 33.573% and India’s 32.425% [1].

## Bottom line

The largest 1970–2025 transformation is Saudi Arabia’s post-2017 surge; the most durable high-participation model is Iceland; Korea shows the clearest case where education and growth raised participation but did not remove the motherhood penalty; and India shows that female education and formal maternity protection do not automatically translate into paid, secure employment when job creation, social norms, care burdens and measurement of unpaid work remain binding. Across all four, the evidence points to the same hierarchy: legal access and mobility are foundational; affordable childcare and gender-balanced leave determine whether mothers stay attached; and sectoral job quality determines whether higher participation becomes durable economic empowerment rather than statistical entry into low-paid or insecure work.
