**Bottom line:** for a 1730s British Empire setting, do **not** make the Molasses Act itself a slave-control statute: its 1733 text is a parliamentary trade-and-revenue law about sugar-colony commerce, duties, customs enforcement, and forfeitures, with no classification of enslaved people as property or chattels. The explicit property language belongs instead to colonial slave statutes and compilations: Barbados treated enslaved people as **“Estate real, and not Chattels”** under a 1668 act still printed in early-eighteenth-century colonial statute collections; Jamaica’s older slave-law language treated slaves, for debt and legacy purposes, as **“goods and chattels,”** while a 1736 act regulated marshal sales of enslaved people under writs; South Carolina’s clearest “chattels personal” formula is the post-Stono statute of **10 May 1740**, just outside 1730–1739 but directly responding to events in 1739.[1][2][3][4][5]

## 1. Actual statutory language and where to look

### Molasses Act, 1733: no chattel-slavery clause

The Molasses Act’s formal title and preamble are the key language. It is **6 Geo. II c.13**, titled **“An act for the better securing and encouraging the trade of his Majesty’s sugar colonies in America.”** Its rationale is framed around the **“Welfare and Prosperity”** of the sugar colonies as a matter of **“Trade, Navigation, and Strength”** and around British sugar planters’ inability to compete **“upon an equal Footing with the Foreign Sugar Colonies”** without relief from Great Britain.[1]

The operative duties were imposed on foreign colonial products imported into British American colonies:

| Foreign-American import into British American colonies | Duty in the 1733 act |
|---|---:|
| Rum or spirits | **Nine pence per gallon** |
| Molasses or syrups | **Six pence per gallon** |
| Sugars or paneles | **Five shillings per hundredweight** |

Those duty figures come from the statutory text of the Molasses Act.[1] The text contains no “slave,” “slaves,” or “Negro” classification; it classifies commodities and regulates customs liability, not enslaved persons.[1]

### Barbados: “Estate real, and not Chattels”

For Barbados, the most direct property-language statute is the 1668 act **“declaring the Negro Slaves of this Island to be Real Estate.”** The text states:

> “all Negro Slaves in all Courts of Judicature and other Places within this Island, shall be held, taken, and adjudged to be Estate real, and not Chattels”[2]

It further provides that enslaved people would descend to heirs and widows according to the customs of fee-simple inheritance, while also saying that ordinary sale or alienation of enslaved people did not have to be enrolled like other real-estate transfers; merchants, factors, or agents importing enslaved people were excepted until sale on the island.[2] That combination matters: Barbados used “real estate” language to stabilize plantation inheritance and dower, not to humanize enslaved people.

A separate Barbados act of **22 May 1733**, **“An Act for the better governing of Negroes; and the more effectual preventing the inhabitants of this island, from employing their Negroes or other Slaves, in selling or bartering,”** is useful for 1730s control scenes. It empowered justices, constables, market clerks, and others to seize enslaved people found with listed goods—including sugar-canes, sugar, syrup, rum, molasses, cotton, ginger, grain, metals, and other goods—unless the goods had been sent by the master or mistress with a proper ticket; it also barred enslaved people from planting cotton or ginger for their own or anyone else’s use other than the master’s.[6]

### Jamaica: “goods and chattels” plus 1736 marshal-sale language

Jamaica supplies both older chattel formulae and a 1730s execution-sale provision. In Lunan’s *Abstract of the Laws of Jamaica Relating to Slaves*, the older statute language under **33 Car. II, cap. 21** provides that **“no slave shall be free by becoming Christian”** and that, for **“payment of debts and legacies,”** slaves were to be **“deemed and taken as all other goods and chattels are”** in the hands of executors or administrators; if other goods and chattels were insufficient, necessary slaves could be sold.[3]

The specifically 1730s Jamaica language found here is the **1736** marshal-proceedings act, which states that **“whenever hereafter any slave or slaves, taken on any writ or writs of venditioni, shall be exposed to sale,”** the provost marshal and deputies must **“sell all such slaves singly,”** except that in **“cases of families”** a man, wife, and children could be set up for sale together.[4] This provision does not use the word “chattels,” but it is direct statutory language treating enslaved people as saleable property under writs.

### South Carolina: the best wording is May 1740, not 1730–1739

The clean South Carolina formula is the **10 May 1740** act **“for the better Ordering and Governing Negroes and other Slaves in this Province.”** Its opening declares that enslaved Black, Indian, mulatto, and mestizo people and their offspring **“shall be and they are hereby declared to be and remain for ever hereafter absolute slaves,”** that status follows the mother, and that they **“shall be deemed… taken, reputed and adjudged in law to be chattles personal… to all intents, constructions and purposes whatsoever.”**[5]

For a story confined to 1730–1739, the date matters: this is just outside the decade, but it is the immediate legislative aftermath of the **1739 Stono Rebellion** and remained the major South Carolina slave code.[5][10] Earlier South Carolina slave-law traditions borrowed heavily from Barbados; the South Carolina Encyclopedia notes that the 1690 code borrowed from Barbados, codified chattel slavery, required written passes, and authorized corporal punishments such as branding, nose-slitting, and emasculation, though that 1690 law was disallowed and later replaced by a 1696 code based on Barbadian principles.[10]

## 2. Parliamentary law vs. colonial plantation codes in the 1730s

The legal divide is sharp. British parliamentary law in the Molasses Act worked through **imperial trade regulation**: customs duties, import restrictions, customs officers, public-act status, forfeitures, and the protection of British sugar planters from foreign sugar competition.[1] It did not prescribe plantation discipline, slave trials, whipping, mutilation, capital punishment, passes, or market policing of enslaved people.[1]

Colonial plantation codes in Barbados and Jamaica, by contrast, regulated enslaved people as a laboring and policed population. Barbados’s 1733 act required justices and parish officials to enforce earlier slave-governance statutes, authorized seizure of enslaved people and goods where enslaved people possessed plantation products or market goods without authorization, and prohibited enslaved people from planting cotton or ginger for non-master use.[6] Jamaica’s 1736 act treated enslaved people as property subject to seizure and judicial sale under writs, down to rules about whether individuals or family groups were sold together.[4]

Punishment also differed in institutional logic. Parliament’s Molasses Act punished customs violations through revenue mechanisms; colonial slave codes treated enslaved people as objects of police power and corporal discipline. South Carolina’s later 1740 act shows the same plantation-code pattern in its most explicit form: enslaved people could be tried by local justices and freeholders, punished by whipping or death, and even pressed to inflict corporal punishment on other enslaved people, with lashes authorized against pressed enslaved people who disobeyed constables.[5] Barbados and Jamaica used comparable local magistrate and plantation mechanisms rather than metropolitan parliamentary process.[6][4]

## 3. Recent peer-reviewed scholarship, 2015–2025

The most directly relevant recent peer-reviewed legal-history scholarship in the fetched sources is not primarily in *Journal of British Studies*, *Slavery & Abolition*, or *William and Mary Quarterly*, but in *Law and History Review* and related academic work. Useful items are:

- **Holly Brewer, “Creating a Common Law of Slavery for England and its New World Empire,” *Law and History Review* 39, no. 4 (2021/online 21 February 2022), 765–834, DOI: 10.1017/S0738248021000407.** Brewer argues that English high-court judges created legal mechanisms allowing enslaved people to be “recovered” as assets for debt and helping produce an imperial common law in which people could be deemed absolute property; the article explicitly connects legal personhood-erasure to enforceable property and contract claims.[11]
- **Dana Y. Rabin, “Slavery, Law, and Race in England and its New World Empire,” *Law and History Review* 40, no. 3 (August 2022), 581–590, DOI: 10.1017/S0738248022000347.** Rabin’s forum essay responds to Brewer, accepts the importance of reconstructing precedents that “transformed people into chattel,” and presses the race-formation problem: how law made people into things while Enlightenment legality claimed rule of law and equality.[12]
- **Gautham Rao, “Forum: Holly Brewer’s ‘Creating a Common Law of Slavery for England and its New World Empire’—Introduction,” *Law and History Review* 40, no. 3 (2022), p. 579, DOI: 10.1017/S0738248022000463.** The forum framing is useful because it presents Brewer’s article as a live legal-history debate rather than a settled antiquarian point.[12]
- **Edward B. Rugemer, *Slave Law and the Politics of Resistance in the Early Atlantic World* (Harvard University Press, 2018).** Although a monograph rather than an article, it is directly on point for Barbados, Jamaica, and South Carolina; the publisher summary states that Barbados’s plantation success spawned slave societies in Jamaica and South Carolina, and that in Jamaica and South Carolina “a draconian system of laws and enforcement” used brutal punishments, patrols, imprisonment, and state-sponsored slave catchers to maintain slaveholders’ control.[13]

Read together, these works emphasize that legal dehumanization was not just rhetorical. It made enslaved people recoverable, inheritable, saleable, mortgageable, and punishable under regimes that combined property law with local coercive police power.[11][12][13]

## 4. Was the Molasses Act about trade regulation or slave control?

The stronger reading from the statute itself is **trade regulation**, not direct slave control. The act’s title, preamble, and operative duties all concern the sugar colonies’ trade position, British navigation, and competition with foreign sugar colonies; the duty schedule targets foreign rum, molasses/syrups, and sugar/paneles at fixed rates.[1] The specific Molasses Act scholarship fetched here also frames the controversy commercially: Richard B. Sheridan’s article, “The Molasses Act and the Market Strategy of the British Sugar Planters,” *Journal of Economic History* 17, no. 1 (March 1957), 62–83, DOI: 10.1017/S0022050700059878, says the controversy has been read through Anglo-French commercial rivalry, conflict between British colonial regions, aid to one group of British colonies at another’s expense, and precedents for parliamentary taxation and colonial noncompliance; Sheridan’s own formulation centers the international sugar economy, the British market for sugar and rum, and planters’ effort to adapt the Navigation Acts to market realities.[14] No fetched Molasses Act text classifies enslaved people or creates plantation discipline rules.[1]

That does not make slavery irrelevant. Recent legal and Atlantic scholarship treats sugar-colony law, property law, and plantation power as mutually reinforcing: Barbados, Jamaica, and South Carolina plantation codes converted enslaved people into inheritable or saleable property and subjected them to coercive local discipline, while imperial commercial policy protected the sugar economy in which that coerced labor was embedded.[2][3][4][11][13] The best formulation is therefore: **the Molasses Act was a mercantilist sugar-trade statute whose economic beneficiaries were slave plantation colonies; it was not, in its text, a slave-control code.** For a historical novel, a customs officer or merchant would talk about duties, molasses, rum, seizures, and foreign sugar; a planter, colonial justice, marshal, or overseer would invoke the local slave acts when controlling, punishing, selling, or inheriting enslaved people.[1][6][4]

## 5. Practical source trail

The assumption about source location is broadly right but should be split by jurisdiction. The Molasses Act is a British parliamentary statute, cited as **6 Geo. II c.13** and available in statutory-text repositories and UK legislation databases.[1][15] Barbados material in the fetched database points to **Colonial Office, CO 30/2, The National Archives, Kew**, and to printed compilations such as Richard Hall’s *Acts Passed in the Island of Barbados From 1643–1762*.[2][6] Jamaica’s 1736 item is from Lunan’s *Abstract of the Laws of Jamaica Relating to Slaves*.[4] South Carolina’s 1740 act appears in Grimké’s 1790 *Public Laws of the State of South-Carolina* and in the Laws of Enslavement and Freedom transcript.[5]
