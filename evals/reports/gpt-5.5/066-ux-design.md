# ERP interface design for production supervisors moving from AS/400 to cloud ERP

**Bottom line:** For mid-sized manufacturers moving supervisors aged roughly 45–60 from AS/400-style green screens to SAP S/4HANA, Oracle NetSuite, or Dynamics 365, the evidence favors **simplified role-based views as the default**, not comprehensive dashboards up front. The best pattern is progressive disclosure for daily goods movements, inventory checks, cycle counts, and work-order/production-order execution, combined with **persistent access paths**—global search, favorites, app-to-app links, saved reports/workbooks, supervisor dashboards, and escalation links—for occasional advanced planning, routing, costing, exception, or troubleshooting functions. Public vendor/customer evidence rarely reports true task-completion-rate percentages or training hours for the exact inventory and production-order workflows; the strongest published figures are adjacent: SAP Fiori cases report **0 training** for redesigned tile-based apps at a 10,000+ user university in 2019 and **no training** for Safran Vectronix production/warehouse Fiori apps in 2025, NetSuite/RF-SMART reports Mann Lake inventory accuracy rising from **below 50% to near 90%**, and Microsoft reports Weetabix achieving **94% OTIF**, **8% planned-output improvement**, and acquisition onboarding reduced from **2.5 years to 6–9 months** after Dynamics 365 SCM go-live [1][2][3][4].

## 1. The exact workflows and interface instances being compared

### SAP S/4HANA and SAP Fiori

The SAP comparison should use **SAP S/4HANA Cloud / current SAP S/4HANA with SAP Fiori launchpad** rather than a generic “SAP” interface. In inventory, the relevant official workflow instance is the **Post Goods Movement** app in the S/4HANA Inventory Management and Physical Inventory learning content. It is delivered as an SAP GUI for HTML transaction in business roles **Warehouse Clerk (SAP_BR_WAREHOUSE_CLERK)** and **Inventory Manager (SAP_BR_INVENTORY_MANAGER)**; it supports goods receipts, goods issues, return deliveries, transfer postings, cancellations, display/change/cancel material documents, and reference documents such as purchase orders, orders, reservations, deliveries, and material documents [5].

Post Goods Movement is a useful example of mixed progressive disclosure and persistent navigation. The screen is divided into **Header data, Item overview, Item details, and Overview tree**; users can open/close header and detail areas, show/hide the Overview tree, click an item number to show item details, and run **Check** before posting to see warnings/errors through traffic-light status and a message log. At the same time, it persistently exposes broad list-box choices for goods receipt, goods issue, transfer posting, cancellation, display, and change [5].

For stock inquiry, **Display Stock Overview (MMBE)** shows one material across organizational levels and can be restricted by material, plant, storage location, and batch; users can include special stocks, hide zero-stock lines, choose display levels, and use display versions that control stock types and sequence. Related inventory reports include material-document list, warehouse stock, stocks for posting date, stock in transit, plant stock availability, and product availability monitoring, with SAP List Viewer layouts/display variants supporting persistent detailed list views [7].

For production/work-order equivalents, SAP uses **production orders**. SAP learning content identifies **Create Production Order** and **Manage Production Order** as production-team apps; a production order includes an order header, operations list, and components list, and the apps show operation details, system/user status, planned/actual cost, component and operations overviews. Available functions include release, costing, availability check, capacity planning, reading production master data, technical close, displaying goods movements, cost analysis, and confirmation details [6]. In S/4HANA Cloud Public Edition manufacturing execution, the production-order lifecycle is **creation → release → goods issue → confirmation → goods receipt**, with supporting availability checks, capacity evaluation/leveling, order printing/download, material staging, and background/automatic processing to reduce manual order handling [22].

### Oracle NetSuite

The NetSuite comparison should use current **NetSuite ERP manufacturing and inventory workflows**, not only a dashboard. For execution, **Work Order Completion** is reached through **Transactions > Manufacturing > Enter Work Orders > List**, then viewing a work order and choosing **Enter Completion** or **Enter Completion with Backflush**. Completion records finished assemblies; backflush records completion plus component consumption. The completion form captures reference number, backflush flag, date, posting period, memo, quantity, inventory detail such as bin/serial/lot, department/class/location, component completed quantities, and Save [9].

For structured production, **Manufacturing Routing and Work Orders** define the required assembly steps, work center, cost template, labor resources, and machine resources. On a work order, users select location, subsidiary where applicable, WIP flag, and manufacturing routing; the Items subtab shows issued components and the Operations subtab shows operation tasks [10]. NetSuite’s Work Order Management product page also describes a range from backflushing all materials to tracking materials and labor as WIP costs or routing tickets, with configurable completion logic, barcode/tablet interfaces, shop-floor controls, inventory usage, Supply 360 for component shortages, Available to Build, and Cost Variance Analysis [12].

For persistent supervisory views, NetSuite offers a **Manufacturing Scheduler Dashboard** that shows work-order data by work center in chart/data-grid form, planned schedule task bars, color-coded operation status, daily/weekly/monthly views, work center/order tracking views, task bars for schedule/duration/status, legends, pagination, and operation details [11]. NetSuite **Inventory SuiteAnalytics Workbooks** are predefined analytical workbooks based on inventory records/transactions with pivot tables filtered for transactions and records to monitor warehouse transaction processing and inventory levels; a **Manufacturing Workbook** uses workbook/dataset templates, source data, criteria, pivot tables, and charts [23][24]. For execution on mobile, NetSuite WMS/mobile inventory-count screens process cycle counts with Started status and can use **Tally Scanning** to increment count quantity by barcode scan; **Smart Count mobile** supports directed and spot counts for assembly/BOM and inventory items, complete counts for multiple items, task viewing, entering count quantity, memos, and submit. The **Smart Count Dashboard** uses portlets and a role-accessible Location portlet, with other portlets filtered by selected location [25][26][27].

### Microsoft Dynamics 365 Supply Chain Management

The Microsoft comparison should use **Dynamics 365 Supply Chain Management** and distinguish it from Business Central evidence. Current workflow instances include on-hand inventory inquiry, released products/on-hand inventory, production control, production-order creation/release, the production floor execution interface, and the Warehouse Management mobile app.

For inventory, the **Inventory on-hand mobile workspace** shows reserved and available inventory by product, with search by product number/name and views by site, warehouse, location, batch, inventory status, and quantities for physical inventory, physical reserved, and available physical. The page also notes the finance and operations mobile app/platform is no longer supported in current documentation, so new implementations should prefer current Warehouse Management mobile app/workspace patterns rather than relying on deprecated mobile workspace architecture [15].

For production-order creation, the official task flow is **Production control > Production orders > All production orders > New production order**, then enter **Item number**, **Delivery date**, **Quantity**, and select **Create**. Active BOM and route populate automatically from approved versions but can be changed; validation and updates use the details page, Edit, and the Action Pane for BOM/Route updates [17]. Production release is a lifecycle state that authorizes shop-floor and warehouse execution; it can be done from the order or by batch process, signals operators to start jobs, issues production papers such as route cards/route jobs/job cards, and generates warehouse work for physically reserved materials [16]. After release, related jobs are visible for registration; operators use the **production floor execution interface** to record Start, Stop, and Completion, and registered time/quantity transfer automatically to production journals [16].

The production floor execution interface is configurable for simplification: supervisors/configurers can choose color themes, list versus detail view for scrap reporting, whether to show the material list in a job, which tabs and toolbar buttons appear, worker sign-in method, numpad suppression, license-plate generation/validation, and label printing [18]. The **Warehouse Management mobile app** is a configurable persistent task menu for process execution: it supports creating transfer orders by scanning license plates and selecting Complete order, data inquiry lists, packing by scanning shipment/container, container creation/closing, label printing, receiving-completed confirmation, mixed license plate completion, and tracking-dimension display controls [19].

## 3. Human-factors and cognitive-load implications for 45–60 production supervisors

The user group described—late-career production supervisors with deep plant knowledge and limited software experience beyond AS/400—should be treated as domain experts but interface novices. The sources ground that operationally: Schaeffer replaced a homegrown **AS400 mainframe from 1982** with NetSuite, while Weetabix explicitly framed its Dynamics 365 SCM migration around avoiding disruption and avoiding overwhelming a “largely manufacturing-based workforce,” because most teams had never done an ERP project [13][4]. The human-factors risk is therefore not lack of manufacturing knowledge; it is interference between old procedural memory and new navigation, terminology, and screen structure. Named adoption models such as Venkatesh et al.’s UTAUT/UTAUT2 and Morris and Venkatesh’s age/adoption work support focusing on perceived usefulness, effort, facilitating conditions, and support for older workers, but no primary academic UTAUT source was in the fetched source set; the fetched implementation evidence supports the same design implication through tailored training, phased rollout, early end-user engagement, and simplified workflows [28][4][31].

Cognitive-load theory maps cleanly onto ERP transitions. **Intrinsic load** comes from the manufacturing task itself: identifying the right item, batch/serial, location, work center, BOM/route, quantity, order status, and posting consequence. **Extraneous load** comes from unnecessary UI complexity: unfamiliar icons, hidden menus, long dashboards, ambiguous terminology, switching between apps, or having to remember whether the task starts in Inventory Management, Production Control, Warehouse Management, or a role center. **Germane load** is the useful schema-building that helps a supervisor understand the new cloud ERP model: how a goods issue changes stock, why release generates warehouse work, how backflush consumes components, or how a production completion updates journals. Named cognitive-load researchers such as Sweller, Chandler, Paas, and van Merriënboer describe limited working memory, intrinsic/extraneous/germane load, and split attention; no primary cognitive-load paper was in the fetched source set, so the report applies those established terms as an analytical lens and grounds the ERP-specific load in the actual screens: SAP’s Post Goods Movement has header/item/detail/overview areas and many movement/reference choices, NetSuite completion requires backflush, inventory detail, location/class, and component quantities, and Dynamics release/warehouse flows connect production, warehouse work, scanning, and journals [5][9][16][19].

The AS/400-to-cloud transition also creates post-implementation learning pressure. Named change frameworks such as Markus and Tanis’s ERP lifecycle, Kotter’s change model, and Prosci ADKAR are useful shorthand for shakedown, reinforcement, and ability-building, but no primary framework source was in the fetched source set. The fetched ERP implementation evidence still supports the operational practices those models would predict: Weetabix used a phased rollout, business-process experts paired with technical specialists, and tailored training/communication for manufacturing teams [4]; Naturipe’s SAP Fiori rollout emphasized early end-user engagement, learning actual processes, obtaining buy-in, and using the upgrade to change processes [28]; Jim Lawrence/Business Central described a difficult transition in which most effort went into process redesign and staff training rather than technical configuration [31]. For supervisors, this translates into super-user networks, floor-walking support during go-live, short practice tasks using real plant examples, and phased rollout by line/site/work center rather than a single dashboard-heavy cutover.

## 4. Progressive disclosure versus persistent navigation for the named workflows

### Primary-task efficiency

Progressive disclosure is better for high-frequency tasks when the task path is stable: receiving goods, issuing material, checking stock, confirming completion, or entering a count. SAP’s Post Goods Movement demonstrates this in a hybrid way: header/detail areas, item details, Overview tree, Help, and Check messages can be opened when needed, while the basic posting flow remains focused [5]. Dynamics 365 production floor execution similarly allows the implementation to show only relevant tabs/buttons, suppress unnecessary numeric keyboard behavior, and choose list/detail views [18]. NetSuite mobile counts and Smart Count are also progressive: users view count tasks, enter quantity, add memo if needed, and submit, while system rules determine process variation [25][26].

Comprehensive dashboards can help supervisors understand a shift, but they slow daily postings if they become the entry point for every transaction. NetSuite’s Manufacturing Scheduler Dashboard is valuable for work-center visibility and color-coded status, but a supervisor who simply needs to complete/backflush a work order still needs the transaction form path and fields [11][9]. SAP’s stock reports and SAP List Viewer variants are powerful for investigation, but they are not the lowest-load path for routine receipt/issue [7].

### Discoverability of advanced functions

Persistent navigation is better for occasional advanced functions because users cannot build muscle memory for tasks they do rarely. Examples include SAP availability check/capacity planning/technical close/cost analysis, NetSuite manufacturing routing/cost variance/Supply 360, or Dynamics BOM/route validation, license-plate configuration, and warehouse inquiries [6][12][17][19]. If these functions are hidden too deeply, supervisors lose confidence and escalate too often. The right design is not to place all advanced functions on the primary screen, but to keep **persistent access paths**: a visible “More actions,” role-based favorites, search, saved workbooks/reports, exception dashboards, and My Inbox/escalation links.

### Memory burden

For AS/400 users, persistent left navigation or full dashboards can appear reassuring because everything is visible, but visibility is not the same as learnability. A dense dashboard forces users to remember which tile, tab, portlet, FastTab, or action pane relates to the task. Progressive disclosure lowers memory burden by letting the user start from the production role: “Receive material,” “Issue to order,” “Check stock,” “Start/complete job,” “Count location,” or “Release order.” SAP’s tile-based Fiori cases explicitly replaced menu-heavy navigation with intuitive tile apps requiring no training in a 10,000+ user implementation and no training in Safran Vectronix production/warehouse apps [1][2].

### Error recovery

Persistent dashboards and logs matter after something goes wrong. SAP Post Goods Movement’s Check function, traffic-light status, and message log are a strong pattern because they provide error recovery before posting [5]. Dynamics 365/Weetabix’s clearer error messages let operators fix issues quickly and block damaged/noncompliant stock from handhelds, reducing mistakes and administrative fixes [4]. NetSuite/Phoenix’s custom records and SuiteScripts made work-order issues, completions, closes, and inventory-status changes auditable and reversible where needed [30]. Therefore, the design should progressively disclose fields but persistently expose validation, message history, reversal/correction paths, and escalation.

### Confidence

Confidence comes from completing daily transactions without help, seeing immediate system feedback, and knowing there is a safe path to advanced functions. The SAP, NetSuite, and Microsoft evidence all points in that direction: simplified Fiori apps reduced or eliminated training in adjacent cases, NetSuite WMS improved accuracy by replacing paper with scan-driven transactions, and Dynamics handhelds gave operators immediate inventory/production/quality visibility [1][2][3][4].

## 5. Platform-by-platform design tendencies

### SAP: role-based launchpad plus deep transactional capability

SAP Fiori tends to favor role-based launchpad tiles and app-to-app navigation, but many manufacturing/inventory tasks still expose deep SAP transaction logic. Post Goods Movement is not a minimalist consumer-style screen; it contains header/item/detail/overview areas, many business-transaction and reference-document options, and validation messages [5]. The implementation lever is therefore **role curation**: give supervisors tiles for the few daily apps—Post Goods Movement, stock overview, production-order list, confirmations, My Inbox/release approvals—and reserve deeper reports, cost analysis, capacity planning, and technical close for search/favorites or advanced groups. Naturipe’s Fiori Launchpad experience reinforces this: moving processes into Fiori improved access, but adoption was initially difficult when users wanted simpler one-button flows, so end-user engagement and process redesign were necessary [28].

### NetSuite: dashboards/role centers plus transaction forms

NetSuite tends to combine a role/dashboard model with transaction forms. The Manufacturing Scheduler Dashboard and Smart Count Dashboard provide persistent visibility, while Work Order Completion, Routing, and Inventory Detail fields remain form-based and subtab-based [11][27][9][10]. This is powerful for supervisors who need context—shortages, work center status, counts, pending completions—but can overwhelm if every portlet is visible by default. The implementation lever is **role-center configuration**: supervisor home pages should show only exceptions and next actions, with SuiteAnalytics Workbooks, saved searches, Manufacturing Scheduler, and Inventory Workbooks available as persistent drill-downs [23][24].

### Dynamics 365 SCM: workspaces and mobile/task execution

Dynamics 365 SCM tends to favor role/workspace navigation and configurable process execution through production floor and warehouse mobile interfaces. Production-order creation and release remain structured module flows, while the production floor execution interface and Warehouse Management mobile app are designed for operational execution with configurable tabs, buttons, menu items, scanning, license plates, labels, and inquiry lists [16][17][18][19]. The implementation lever is **workspace/mobile menu design**: create a production-supervisor workspace for exceptions and shift control, and task-specific mobile/workspace entries for receiving, issuing, on-hand lookup, start/stop/complete, and count.

## 6. Recommendation: default role-based simplification, with persistent advanced paths

For production supervisors performing daily inventory and work-order tasks but only occasional advanced planning/configuration, the recommended design is:

1. **Default to simplified role-based views.** Provide a production supervisor home with daily tasks: goods receipt/issue, stock lookup, cycle count, work-order/production-order release, start/stop/complete, completion/backflush, and exception review. This aligns with SAP’s Fiori training reductions, NetSuite scan/mobile accuracy improvements, and Dynamics handheld/operator benefits [1][2][3][4].
2. **Use progressive disclosure inside the transaction.** Show required fields first; reveal batch/serial/bin, cost, routing, component, quality, and reversal details only when needed. SAP Post Goods Movement’s open/close areas and item-detail drilldown, NetSuite completion/backflush inventory detail, and Dynamics production floor configuration all support this pattern [5][9][18].
3. **Keep persistent access paths for advanced functions.** Add global search, favorites, saved reports/workbooks, Manufacturing Scheduler/Smart Count dashboards, SAP list variants, Dynamics inquiry lists, and escalation/My Inbox links so occasional functions remain discoverable [7][11][27][19].
4. **Expose comprehensive dashboards only as the fallback/default for exception-heavy roles.** If supervisors frequently troubleshoot cross-functional shortages, quality holds, routing capacity, or multi-site stock issues, provide a dashboard-first mode with limited widgets: late orders, component shortages, blocked stock, failed postings, open counts, and help/escalation queues. Do not make a comprehensive dashboard the default for users whose primary goal is posting/confirming transactions.

## 7. Recommended measurement design for a mid-sized AS/400 migration

Because public case studies do not provide exact supervisor-level task-completion/training metrics for the named workflows, the manufacturer should instrument them directly. The measurement design should define proficiency, errors, and time-to-competency before go-live and track them at **2 weeks, 30 days, 60 days, and 90 days**.

### Baseline before migration

Capture AS/400 baseline by role, age/experience cohort, plant/line, and task frequency:

- Median and 90th percentile task time for: goods receipt, goods issue to work order, transfer posting, stock lookup by item/location/batch, cycle-count entry/adjustment, work-order creation, release, completion/backflush, reversal/correction.
- Error rates: wrong item, wrong location/bin, wrong quantity, wrong batch/serial, wrong movement type, reversal/adjustment rate, failed transaction count, production-order rework, unplanned component issue, inventory-record accuracy, scan compliance where scanning exists.
- Assistance: number of supervisor/IT assists per user per shift, help-desk tickets per user, repeated questions by category.
- Training: hours completed, practice transactions completed, assessment score, and pass/fail for each critical transaction.

### Usability and training tests before go-live

Run moderated tests with representative supervisors, including late-career AS/400-heavy users, on the actual configured system:

| Task | Proficiency metric | Error metric | Target checkpoint |
|---|---|---|---|
| Goods receipt | % independently complete receipt without trainer; median time vs AS/400 baseline | wrong PO/item/location/batch; reversal rate | 2-week simulation; 30-day live |
| Goods issue to order | % independently issue correct component/quantity/location | wrong-item/wrong-location issue; failed posting | 2-week simulation; 30/60-day live |
| Stock lookup | % find available quantity by site/warehouse/location/batch | incorrect interpretation of reserved vs available | 2-week simulation |
| Cycle count | % complete count/adjustment without assistance | count variance entry error; recount rate | 30/60-day live |
| Work-order/production-order creation or release | % create/release correct order with BOM/route/date/quantity | wrong BOM/route/date; release without availability | 30/60-day live |
| Completion/backflush/confirmation | % complete order and consume components correctly | over/under-consumption; missed serial/batch; correction count | 30/60/90-day live |

### Post-go-live telemetry and support

At **2 weeks**, measure stabilization: assisted transactions, top help-desk categories, failed postings, repeated navigation errors, and users not yet completing critical tasks independently. At **30 days**, measure independent completion and median task time against baseline. At **60 days**, measure error reduction, reversal/adjustment rates, cycle-count accuracy, and help-desk reduction. At **90 days**, measure time-to-competency: percentage of supervisors who pass the proficiency checklist, complete all critical transactions independently, and require no more than an agreed threshold of assists per week.

Successful migrations should report proficiency gains as operational metrics rather than satisfaction alone: **percentage of users independently completing goods issue/receipt, cycle-count adjustment, production-order creation/release, or work-order completion; assessment/pass rates; number of assisted transactions; and 30/60/90-day milestone attainment**. They should report error reduction using concrete transaction metrics: **inventory adjustment/reversal rates, wrong-item/wrong-location postings, production-order rework, failed transactions, help-desk tickets per user, correction counts, scan compliance, and inventory-record accuracy before and after go-live**. These measures match the outcomes seen in the public cases—inventory accuracy, order-processing time, click/manual-step reduction, onboarding time, and error-message/scan-driven correction—but make them specific enough to evaluate the interface design for the target supervisors [3][8][13][4][20][21].

## Final verdict

Choose **simplified, role-based progressive disclosure** as the default for production supervisors. SAP, NetSuite, and Dynamics 365 all can be configured that way, although they start from different defaults: SAP through curated Fiori launchpad tiles and app-to-app navigation, NetSuite through role centers/dashboards plus transaction forms, and Dynamics 365 through workspaces, production floor execution, and Warehouse Management mobile menus. Use comprehensive dashboards as an exception-management layer, not the front door for routine work. The measurement program should prove the design by tracking task time, independent completion, training hours, assisted transactions, help-desk/error categories, and inventory/production correction rates at 2-week, 30-day, 60-day, and 90-day checkpoints.
