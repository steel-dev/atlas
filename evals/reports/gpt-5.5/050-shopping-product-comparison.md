**Bottom line:** For a 2,400 sq ft Phoenix renovation where the equipment must be a whole-home heat pump and must carry capacity through 115°F+ afternoons, **Carrier Infinity 26 / 25VNA4 is the best-supported choice and the lowest modeled 15-year cost per effective cooling ton among the actual heat-pump options**. Using the cited installed-cost and operating assumptions, a 5-ton Carrier Infinity-class heat pump comes out at about **$32,297 15-year TCO** and **$6,938 per effective 115°F cooling ton**; the Mitsubishi ducted Hyper-Heat H2i comparison is about **$29,597 TCO** but only **3.5 nominal/effective tons**, or **$8,456 per ton**. ## Efficiency and AHRI-rating comparison

The Carrier and Mitsubishi options are actual heat pumps; Lennox SL28XCV is not. Carrier’s 25VNA4 product data describes tested combinations as AHRI-rated net values and gives AHRI cooling conditions at **95°F outdoor / 80°F dry bulb / 67°F wet bulb indoor**; example AHRI-style pairings in the product data include **25VNA448 + FE4ANB006L + UI = 46,000 Btuh, 13.5 EER, 22.0 SEER, 11 HSPF** and **25VNA460 = 58,500 Btuh, 13.0 EER, 21.0 SEER, 11 HSPF**, while the later Carrier offerings sheet lists the 25VNA4 line at **up to 24.0 SEER2, 15 EER, 13 HSPF** [1][2][3].

Mitsubishi’s ducted PVA-A42AA7 + PUZ-HA42NKA1 H2i combination is materially less cooling-efficient in the cited submittals: **15.4 SEER2** and **9.8 EER2**, with **42,000 Btuh rated and maximum cooling capacity** at the AHRI 95°F outdoor cooling point [12][5]. Its heating rating needs the regional label kept with the number: the 2023 sheet reports **HSPF2 Region IV = 9.3**, while the 2024 submittal reports **HSPF2 Region V = 7.9**; both list HSPF = 10.0 [5][12].

LennoxPros identifies SL28XCV-048 as a **4-ton, 208–230 V, 1-phase, variable-capacity air conditioner**, not a heat pump, with **up to 25.80 SEER2 / 28.00 SEER** and “Top AHRI Matches,” but no HSPF2 because there is no heat-pump heating function in this model [4]. If the project requires one system to cool and heat the whole home electrically, the comparable Lennox product family would need to be a Lennox heat pump, not SL28XCV.

## Noise and the property-line screen

All three units meet the user’s requested **<65 dB** screen using manufacturer sound ratings at the unit rating basis: Carrier 25VNA4 is listed “as low as **51 dB**,” Lennox SL28XCV-048 “as low as **56 dBA**,” and Mitsubishi PVA/PUZ H2i outdoor unit **49 dB(A) cooling / 51 dB(A) heating** [1][4][12]. Phoenix code sources cited here do not state a general numeric HVAC property-line limit of 65 dB; the Phoenix nuisance/noise provisions regulate loud and excessive noise categories, while residential zoning language uses boundary/compatibility concepts such as not emitting noise, odor, or vibration beyond or above the general level at the lot boundary for specified uses [14][15]. In practical siting terms, the manufacturer ratings are comfortably below 65 dB before distance attenuation, but final property-line compliance should be checked from the actual pad location, reflective walls, and setback.

SRP’s own savings table estimates that replacing an 11 SEER system with a variable-capacity **21 SEER / 20.0 SEER2** unit saves **$370/year for 3 tons, $490/year for 4 tons, and $610/year for 5 tons**, assuming **$0.11/kWh** and **2,141 cooling hours** [7].

## Reliability and failure-rate weighting

The reliability evidence available for this comparison is a **Consumer Reports owner survey**, not a technician failure-rate survey. It covers **18,172 central air conditioners** from the 2020 and 2022 Summer Surveys, installed between **2007 and 2022**, and its predicted reliability model estimates problem rates by the end of the **8th year** for systems not covered by an extended warranty or service contract [16]. On that basis, Mitsubishi scores **5/5 predicted reliability and 5/5 owner satisfaction**, while Carrier and Lennox each score **4/5 predicted reliability and 5/5 owner satisfaction** [16].

That reliability result helps Mitsubishi qualitatively, but it does not overturn the desert-cooling cost result: the Mitsubishi ducted H2i option is quieter and rated more reliable in the owner-survey data, yet its fetched ducted combination has much lower **15.4 SEER2** and only **3.5 tons** of rated cooling, so its modeled 15-year cost per effective cooling ton remains higher than the Carrier 5-ton Infinity-class heat-pump scenario [12][16][3].

## Recommendation

Choose **Carrier Infinity 26 / 25VNA4**, sized by Manual J and verified against the actual duct static and panel load calculation, if the goal is **lowest cost per cooling ton in extreme desert conditions** among these named whole-home heat-pump options. It is the only actual heat pump in the comparison with fetched 115°F and 125°F capacity data; the 5-ton scenario retains **55.86 kBtuh at 115°F**, yielding the lowest modeled heat-pump cost of **about $6,938 per effective 115°F ton**. Mitsubishi Hyper-Heat H2i is the quieter and stronger reliability-survey choice, but in this ducted whole-home configuration it is less cooling-efficient and more expensive per ton. Lennox SL28XCV is a strong high-SEER AC, but it is not a whole-home heat pump and should be removed from the heat-pump shortlist [1][3][12][4].
