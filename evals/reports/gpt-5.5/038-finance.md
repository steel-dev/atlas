# Industrial automation for manufacturing in Saudi Arabia, 2015–2025

**Bottom line.** Saudi Arabia is a policy-driven automation opportunity, but the public data are asymmetric: the latest accessible market endpoint is **USD 2.3 billion in 2025** for Saudi Arabia’s **industrial automation components** market, while the exact **2015 Saudi revenue value** is in paid R&M/6W historical datasets but is not exposed in the public pages reviewed, so a defensible 2015–2025 market CAGR cannot be computed from public values. The demand signal is nevertheless strong: Vision 2030, NIDLP and the National Industrial Strategy explicitly push localization, 4IR, smart factories, high-tech exports and factory growth, while IFR’s public robot-density data show Saudi Arabia is not among the disclosed benchmark economies, implying sales teams should treat robot-density benchmarking as a qualification tool rather than the core proof of demand.

### Robot penetration and IFR benchmark

IFR defines robot density as operational industrial robots relative to employees, usually **robots per 10,000 manufacturing employees**; it is calculated as operational robot stock divided by manufacturing employment on a uniform base [6]. The public denominator trail can be built from World Bank labor indicators, but the numerator is missing from the public IFR extracts. Using the already-fetched World Bank series as an industrial-employment denominator proxy—`labor force × (1 − unemployment rate) × industry employment share`—Saudi Arabia had about **3,323,511 industrial employees in 2015** (`13,349,016 × (1 − 5.6%) × 26.373991%`) and about **3,994,910 in 2025**, the latest available World Bank year in the fetched series (`17,858,342 × (1 − 3.038%) × 23.070887%`) [7], [8], [9]. The intended robot-penetration formula is therefore `IFR Saudi operational industrial-robot stock ÷ manufacturing employees × 10,000`; however, in the public IFR/OWID extracts reviewed, **Saudi Arabia and UAE/GCC robot-density values and Saudi IFR operational stock/installations are not disclosed**, although IFR’s data-availability chart lists Saudi Arabia and the UAE as countries covered in the paid industrial-robot installations database [10], [6]. Therefore, the Saudi 2015 and latest robots-per-10,000-manufacturing-workers values should be sourced from the paid IFR country table before quoting a penetration rate.

## 2) How Vision 2030 drives demand for robotics and automation

Saudi Vision 2030 creates automation demand through three linked mechanisms: larger non-oil production, localization of industrial supply chains, and digital/4IR capability building.

### Vision 2030 core targets relevant to factory automation

The 2016 official Vision 2030 document sets economy-wide targets that directly raise the need for modern factories, controls, robotics, data systems and local production capacity:

| Vision 2030 target | Exact target | Why it drives automation demand |
|---|---:|---|
| Private-sector contribution | From **40% to 65% of GDP by 2030** | Expands private industrial investment and supplier competition [11] |
| Non-oil exports | From **16% to 50% of non-oil GDP by 2030** | Requires export-grade productivity, quality control, traceability and scalable production [11] |
| Non-oil government revenue | From **SAR 163 billion to SAR 1 trillion by 2030** | Reinforces diversification away from oil rents and toward productive non-oil sectors [11] |
| PIF assets | From **SAR 600 billion to more than SAR 7 trillion by 2030** | Provides capital backing for industrial platforms, giga-project supply chains and new sectors [11] |
| Oil and gas localization | From **40% to 75% by 2030** | Pulls local suppliers into higher-specification manufacturing, inspection, process control and automation [11] |
| FDI | From **3.8% to 5.7% of GDP by 2030** | Supports foreign automation vendors, integrators, JVs and local manufacturing partnerships [11] |

The same Vision 2030 document identifies manufacturing mechanisms: localization of renewable energy and industrial equipment, investment in the digital economy, localization of oil and gas, and using the Kingdom’s oil and petrochemical expertise to develop adjacent and supporting sectors [11]. These are not robotics targets by themselves, but they create the customer base for robotics: petrochemicals, energy equipment, industrial equipment, renewables, logistics, mining and advanced manufacturing.

### NIDLP: the official 4IR and industrial demand bridge

The National Industrial Development and Logistics Program (NIDLP), a Vision 2030 Realization Program, is the most direct policy bridge between diversification and automation. Its official Vision 2030 page describes NIDLP as **“paving the way for Saudi Arabia’s Fourth Industrial Revolution”** and links it to Made in Saudi, local content in oil and non-oil sectors, localization of promising manufacturing industries, military-industry localization, mining growth, energy localization, logistics hubs and non-oil sector localization.

NIDLP’s 2025 targets include:

| NIDLP target / measure | Exact value / year | Automation relevance |
|---|---:|---|
| Oil and gas sector localization | **70% by 2025** | Increases demand for local suppliers with automated QA, machining, process controls and traceability |
| Pharmaceutical localization | **30% by 2025** | Drives regulated automation, batch control, packaging, serialization and inspection |
| Defense/military industry localization | **20% by 2025** | Drives high-precision manufacturing and secure local supply chains |
| Special Economic Zones for NIDLP sectors | **8 SEZs by 2025** | Lowers investment friction for industrial and logistics projects |
| Fourth Industrial Revolution centers | **5 centers by 2025** | Institutionalizes 4IR adoption support, pilots and capability development |
| Mining-license issuance | **60 days by 2025** | Speeds mining and mineral-processing investment, a process-automation target segment |
| Geological survey completion | **50% by 2025** | Supports mining project pipeline and downstream mineral-processing automation |
| Logistics Performance Index | **3.38/5 by 2025** | Improves supply-chain competitiveness for automated plants |
| Port utilization | **70% by 2025** | Supports export manufacturing and industrial logistics |
| Aquaculture | **300,000 tons/year by 2025** | Creates controlled-process, cold-chain and packaging automation demand |

NIDLP Delivery Plan 2021–2025 defines the program around four sectors—**energy, mining, industry and logistics**—plus local content and Fourth Industrial Revolution focus areas. It explicitly includes AI, IoT, 3D printing, robots and smart factories/mines/grids as 4IR technologies to increase production and competitiveness; it also identifies legal frameworks, digital infrastructure, human capabilities, domestic technology companies, foreign 4IR specialists and R&D centers as adoption enablers [12].

The Delivery Plan makes 4IR adoption operational through smart-model-factory maturity assessments and use cases, with stated outcomes including **20% productivity increase in 100 factories**, **2,000 trainees**, **50 use cases**, support for **50 local factories with 4IR use cases**, 4IR capability centers and a digital-enablers platform. It also cites the National Productivity Program for **22 factories**, the Tanafusiya program to fund **100 factories** adopting 4IR technologies, and optical fiber in more than **1,500 model factories** [12].

NIDLP’s financing and industrial-base figures reinforce the size of the target account universe: the Delivery Plan aims to enable **SAR 1.4 trillion** in private-sector investment by 2030 across energy, mining, industry and logistics; by end-2020, delivered NIDLP-sector projects represented more than **SAR 300 billion** investment and more than **50,000 jobs**. It cites a **SAR 105 billion** Saudi Industrial Development Fund available fund, **SAR 30 billion** Saudi EXIM Bank capital, **SAR 9 billion** industrial-infrastructure investment, **9,800** existing/under-construction industrial factories, **114** ready factories completed, and a 2020 industry snapshot of **10,029 industrial facilities**, **129** ready-made factories and **SAR 1.133 trillion** total investment [12].

### National Industrial Strategy: the 2022 scale-up plan

The National Industrial Strategy, launched on **18 October 2022**, is the strongest industrial-scale signal. Reported from SPA and summarized in public sources, it targets by 2030:

| National Industrial Strategy item | Exact value / target | Automation demand implication |
|---|---:|---|
| Industrial GDP | Roughly **tripling by 2030** | Requires productivity, throughput and quality gains [13] |
| Industrial exports | **SAR 557 billion** by 2030, about **USD 148–149 billion** | Export manufacturing needs automation, testing, packaging and compliance systems [14], [13], [15] |
| Investment opportunities | **800+ opportunities worth SAR 1 trillion**, about **USD 266 billion** | Creates a large project pipeline for automation vendors and integrators [13], [16] |
| Additional industrial investment | **SAR 1.3 trillion**, about **USD 346 billion** | Expands capex pool for controls, drives, robots, MES/SCADA and industrial software [14], [13], [16] |
| Advanced-technology product exports | Increase by about **6x** | Supports high-tech, electronics, EV, aerospace, medical/pharma and precision manufacturing automation [14], [13] |
| Jobs | Tens of thousands of quality, high-value jobs | Raises need for training, cobotics, safety systems and maintenance capability [14], [13] |
| Industrial focus | **12 subsectors** and **118 selected product groups** | Provides segmentation for account targeting [13] |
| Implementation architecture | **15 enablers** and **136+ initiatives** | Creates procurement hooks for localization, incentives and capability-building [13] |
| Factory count | From **10,640** at launch to **36,000 by 2035** | Expands installed base for brownfield retrofits and greenfield automation [14], [15] |

## 5) Reconciling market evidence with giga-project evidence

The best reading is that Saudi automation demand has two layers:

1. **Addressable manufacturing automation market.** This is the USD **2.3 billion 2025** industrial automation components market—sensors, PLC/DCS, drives, HMIs, networks and related components—and the manufacturing-adjacent automation demand created by factory expansion, industrial GDP targets, localization and 4IR programs [2], [12], [13]. This is where factory robotics, PLC upgrades, motion control, MES/SCADA, quality inspection and industrial IoT should be sold.

2. **Giga-project smart infrastructure market.** Red Sea Global and NEOM are relevant because they buy smart energy, grid, building, e-mobility and digital infrastructure systems. ABB’s Red Sea awards show that global automation/electrification vendors can win at PIF-backed mega-developments, but the named public ABB awards are energy/building/e-mobility packages, not industrial robot installations on factory floors. Siemens’ public NEOM/RSG award record reviewed here does not show a named disclosed-value award since 2021, while Siemens’ Ceer automation work is a stronger manufacturing signal even though it is outside NEOM/RSG.

Therefore, giga-project awards should be used as credibility evidence for complex Saudi delivery, localization, power/digital-infrastructure integration and sustainability—not as a proxy for Saudi robot density or manufacturing robot demand.

## 6) Three practical implications for a sales rep targeting Saudi manufacturing

### 1. Prioritize verticals where policy targets and operating pain overlap

Rank highest-fit accounts in this order:

1. **Automotive / EV and advanced manufacturing.** Ceer’s 2023 Siemens automation announcement shows concrete demand for advanced automation systems in a Saudi EV plant, while the National Industrial Strategy targets advanced-technology exports up about **6x** and **SAR 1.3 trillion** additional industrial investment [14], [13].
2. **Petrochemicals, refining, chemicals and oilfield/localized equipment suppliers.** Vision 2030 targets oil-and-gas localization from **40% to 75%**, and manufacturing IPI in 2024 rose in coke/refined petroleum products **2.8%** and chemicals **2.9%** [11], [18]. Sell process automation, rotating-equipment controls, inspection, asset monitoring and local service.
3. **Food, pharma and regulated packaging.** NIDLP targets pharmaceutical localization at **30% by 2025**, while food manufacturing IPI rose **6.2% in 2024**, [18]. Sell batch control, serialization, packaging automation, cobots, vision inspection and hygienic automation.
4. **Mining and mineral processing.** NIDLP targets a **60-day** mining-license process, **50%** geological survey completion by 2025 and mining growth; smart mines are explicitly part of 4IR, [12]. Sell process controls, drives, condition monitoring and remote operations.

### 2. Tie every proposal to localization, 4IR and measurable productivity—not just “robots”

A Saudi manufacturing pitch should explicitly map to Vision 2030 and NIDLP language: non-oil exports from **16% to 50% of non-oil GDP**, private sector from **40% to 65% of GDP**, oil-and-gas localization from **40% to 75%**, NIDLP’s **20% productivity increase in 100 factories**, **50 use cases**, **2,000 trainees**, Tanafusiya funding for **100 factories**, and the National Industrial Strategy’s **SAR 557 billion** industrial-export target [11], [12], [13].

In practical terms, proposals should include: local-content/service plan, Saudi workforce training plan, ROI and productivity model, integration with PLC/DCS/MES/SCADA, cybersecurity and data architecture, and evidence that the project can support export quality, traceability or localization KPIs.

### 3. Use a fallback evidence strategy when robot-density or giga-project data are incomplete

Do not wait for public IFR Saudi robot density or disclosed NEOM/RSG award values to qualify accounts. Use three substitute evidence layers:

- **Market layer:** cite the **USD 2.3 billion 2025** automation-components endpoint and the USD **4.7 billion 2034** IMARC forecast as the automation-component TAM anchor [2].
- **Policy/funding layer:** cite NIDLP’s **SAR 1.4 trillion** private-investment enablement target, **SAR 105 billion** SIDF fund, **SAR 30 billion** EXIM capital and 4IR factory programs [12].
- **Account layer:** qualify each plant by capex cycle, installed PLC/DCS base, line speed, reject/scrap rates, labor constraints, export/localization obligations and available incentives; treat ABB’s Red Sea awards as infrastructure credibility and Siemens/Ceer as the more relevant manufacturing-automation reference.

This fallback is especially important because the public IFR benchmark table gives exact 2024 robot-density comparators—South Korea **1,220**, Germany **449**, Japan **446**, United States **307**, China **166**, world **132**, Asia **131**—but not Saudi Arabia or UAE/GCC public density values. For Saudi manufacturing sales, the better proof of urgency is policy-backed factory modernization and industrial investment, not a publicly disclosed Saudi robots-per-worker statistic.
