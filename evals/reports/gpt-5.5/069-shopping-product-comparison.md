## Evidence base and AS/NZS 2442.1 test basis

The current Australian regulatory basis is the **Greenhouse and Energy Minimum Standards (Rotary Clothes Dryers) Determination 2024**, made **10 September 2024** and in force as **F2024L01261**. It covers electric household rotary clothes dryers as one product class, including condenser dryers and heat-pump condenser rotary clothes dryers; “condenser dryer” means the dryer separates water vapour inside the dryer, converts it to liquid and drains or stores it rather than exhausting moist air outside [1]. The Energy Rating industry page states that the 2024 determination commenced **4 October 2025**, suppliers may continue to sell existing registered models, and the referenced standards include **AS/NZS 2442.1:2021 Performance of household electrical appliances — Rotary clothes dryers, Part 1: Methods for measuring performance, energy and water consumption** and **AS/NZS 2442.2:2021 Part 2: Energy efficiency labelling requirements**, including amendments existing when the determination was made [2].

The public Standards Australia page for **AS/NZS 2442.1:2021** says it is current, was published **19 February 2021**, has **43 pages**, and specifies test procedures and minimum performance criteria for household electric rotary clothes dryers; its table of contents identifies measured/test items including **moisture removal, energy consumption and water consumption, maximum fabric temperature, preparation of standard test loads, test conditions, program settings, method of loading, drying clothes in a single operation, and recording of results** [3]. The Energy Rating page further states that the 2024 dryer determination changes testing by expanding heating methods to include heat pumps, incorporating the cool-down period up to programme termination, allowing loads up to **20 kg**, and specifying **230 V** test voltage; labels must show **Program Time**, the time the product requires to complete the specified programme used to measure energy performance [2].

For the textile question, the important limitation is substantive rather than clerical: **AS/NZS 2442.1 and GEMS registration data establish the standard damp-load, energy, moisture-removal and maximum-temperature test framework, not a public per-model wool/cotton/synthetics performance table**. Manufacturer manuals identify which cycles exist and, for LG, the cotton test-program setting, while CHOICE public pages list the data fields it tests—cycle time, measured kWh/load, final moisture content, noise and bedding—but keep exact results and recommendations behind member access for these two public pages [4] [5].

## Installation, heritage overlay and shared-wall noise

All three are heat-pump/condenser dryers and therefore **do not need an external wall vent**: Bosch uses a condensation container by default but can connect the supplied water drain hose [16]; LG is explicitly a **ventless Heat Pump Condenser Dryer** and includes a drain hose kit [8]; Samsung’s EU fiche identifies an electric condenser tumble dryer [12]. This is the central heritage advantage: no new external vent grille is required if the condensate tank or an existing internal drain is used.

The address is still required before giving a definitive heritage-permit verdict. In Victoria, the relevant planning control is typically **Victorian Planning Provisions / local planning scheme Clause 43.01 Heritage Overlay**, which turns on the specific property schedule and local council practice. For a City of Melbourne terrace or another inner-Melbourne municipality, external services, penetrations, visible condenser drains, new grilles and changes to significant fabric can trigger a heritage permit; placing a ventless dryer wholly inside the laundry with a removable condensate tank generally avoids external works, but connecting a permanent drain through heritage fabric should be checked with the responsible council once the exact address is known.

For a shared-wall terrace, the operational mitigation is more important than a 2 dB label difference: level the feet, avoid timber-floor bounce, use anti-vibration pads if the dryer sits on suspended boards, keep the unit off party-wall cabinetry where possible, and use delay start to avoid night-time cycles. LG’s manual specifically warns that timber or suspended flooring may contribute to excessive vibration and imbalance and may require reinforcement or bracing, and instructs levelling feet adjustment until the appliance no longer wobbles [10].
