# Bottom line

As of 2024, edge computing is changing industrial IoT from a “send telemetry to a cloud platform, infer there, then command back” pattern into a split architecture: fast, safety- or bandwidth-sensitive inference and coordination run on industrial PCs, AMR compute modules, and feeder/substation gateways, while cloud platforms remain the system of record for historical storage, digital twins, fleet optimization, model training, reporting, and cross-site orchestration. The three use cases differ in the binding constraint: AMRs are the most latency-sensitive, energy distribution is the most reliability- and safety-governed, and manufacturing predictive maintenance is the most bandwidth/storage-driven.

## 1. The baseline being displaced: cloud-centric industrial IoT

The displaced pattern is common across the three sectors: sensors, PLCs, robots, or intelligent electronic devices feed a gateway or private/public cellular network; the gateway forwards time-series, event, image, or point-cloud data to a cloud IoT platform or data lake; cloud analytics or model inference computes a result; and a command, work order, route, or switching recommendation returns to the asset. Representative platforms still matter in 2024, but their role changes: AWS IoT SiteWise remains the industrial time-series and asset-model service, with SiteWise Edge processing raw equipment data locally and sending only aggregated data to AWS Cloud to optimize bandwidth and storage [16]; AWS/SiteWise plus Siemens Industrial Edge is described as an “edge-first” shop-floor pattern in which local Industrial Edge hosts connectors, HMIs, AI inference, model monitoring, local KPIs, preprocessing, aggregation, and buffering, while AWS IoT SiteWise, TwinMaker, S3, Athena, QuickSight, and SageMaker remain the cloud layer for digital twins, long-term storage, visualization, cross-site analytics, scalable model training, retention, and reporting [17]. Siemens Industrial Edge Management and Industrial Edge Hub provide app orchestration, lifecycle management, deployments, observability, and model updates for the distributed shop-floor layer [18][4][17].

The cross-use-case edge stack therefore has two halves. In **manufacturing**, the edge layer is industrial gateways/IPCs and container runtimes: Siemens Schuler Pressen deploys Industrial Edge apps as **Docker containers** on a SIMATIC IPC227E, and Siemens describes Kubernetes-based management for Industrial Edge; OT interoperability is tied to **OPC UA**, vendor connectors, **MQTT Sparkplug B** where publish/subscribe stateful industrial telemetry is used, and secure MQTT/HTTPS links to SiteWise or Siemens cloud services [18][16][19]. In **AMRs**, the stack is robot compute plus ROS/Isaac workloads, onboard sensor buses, OTA update tooling, and **3GPP private 5G/MEC** or public-5G/MEC connectivity for fleet orchestration rather than cloud perception; Makino’s implementation used public 5G plus AWS Wavelength, VPN tunnels, and device certificates [7][6][8]. In **energy**, the stack is feeder/substation gateways and IEDs using **DNP3** backhaul, **IEC 61850** and **CIM** concepts in **OpenFMB** message profiles, **Protobuf** serialization, **NATS or MQTT** pub-sub, and, for DER coordination where applicable, **IEEE 2030.5**/DERMS-style interfaces [13][14]. Cybersecurity moves from perimeter-only thinking to device identity, secure boot, signed updates, network segmentation, and industrial-control standards such as **ISA/IEC 62443** for industrial automation—Siemens cites **IEC 62443-4-2** compliance, RBAC/MFA, encrypted updates, and container scanning for Industrial Edge—and NERC CIP controls where distribution devices meet Bulk Electric System cyber-system definitions [19][20].

## 2. Manufacturing predictive maintenance

### Before versus after architecture

**Before: cloud-centric condition monitoring.** The conventional predictive-maintenance architecture streams PLC, SCADA, historian, drive, and sensor data—especially vibration, temperature, current, and status variables—from machines through a gateway to a cloud platform or data lake. Feature extraction, anomaly detection, model inference, dashboards, and maintenance recommendations are computed centrally, then alerts or work orders are sent back to the plant. This works for non-real-time analytics, but it is expensive when high-rate vibration streams are uploaded continuously and brittle when WAN latency or connectivity delays alerting.

**After: Siemens Industrial Edge / MindSphere-style edge-distributed monitoring.** The concrete 2024 vendor pattern is Siemens Industrial Edge at the machine/cell level, with an Industrial Edge Device such as a SIMATIC IPC, Industrial Edge Management/Hub for app lifecycle, and cloud synchronization to Siemens cloud services or AWS IoT SiteWise. Siemens Drivetrain Analyzer Edge runs on-premises Industrial Edge analytics for SINAMICS drives, uses SIPLUS CMS1200 vibration/status variables, calculates KPIs such as mean and standard deviation, and performs anomaly/mechanical-anomaly detection locally [3]. Siemens Schuler Pressen’s implementation runs Industrial Edge on a SIMATIC IPC227E; edge apps collect press data and forward results to Schuler Cloud/Siemens network, with Docker-container apps onboarded through Industrial Edge Hub and centrally managed by Industrial Edge Management [18]. In the AWS/Siemens Frontmatec case, AWS IoT SiteWise Edge runs on Siemens Industrial Edge for predictive maintenance and global performance management; ML models can be trained in AWS and deployed at the edge, and central management automates app deployment [4].

The computation that moves is feature extraction, local asset-model KPI calculation, vibration/temperature/current anomaly scoring, and first-level alerting. What remains centralized is historical model training, cross-site benchmarking, long-term retention, dashboards, digital twins, and reporting. The removed dependency is cloud round-trip inference for every raw sensor segment. The new dependency is reliable synchronization of edge apps, model versions, feature-extraction parameters, buffered data, and event schemas through Industrial Edge Management, SiteWise Edge, or Siemens/AWS cloud services [4][16][17].

### Quantified impacts

| Manufacturing metric | 2024 value / range | Evidence type/date and interpretation |
|---|---:|---|
| Edge inference/control-loop target | **~135 ms** for edge MobileNet vibration fault diagnosis | A 2024 Sensors 24(16):5156 rotating-machinery edge benchmark completed computation within 135 ms on mechanical vibration WSN data [1]. |
| Cloud round trip displaced | **~1,000 ms** raw-data transmission to monitoring center for four vibration sensor nodes | Same benchmark compared local computation with raw transmission to a monitoring center [1]. |
| Data-volume reduction, lower bound | **98.21%** | A cloud-edge vibration feature pipeline reduced transmitted data volume by 98.21% and bandwidth by 98.25% versus pure cloud upload [2]. |
| Data-volume reduction, upper bound | **99.9%** | The 2024 MobileNet vibration WSN benchmark transmitted only 0.1% of original data after edge processing [1]. |
| Availability / uptime metric | **45% downtime reduction** | Siemens Drivetrain Analyzer Onsite states AI analytics can prevent failures and reduce downtime by 45% [3]. |
| Deployment-maintainability metric | From “several hours” to **15 minutes** | Frontmatec’s AWS IoT SiteWise Edge on Siemens Industrial Edge deployment time improved from several hours to 15 minutes [4]. |

### Real-world example and trade-offs

The strongest 2024 vendor case is **Frontmatec**, described in an AWS/Siemens 2024 blog as a meat-industry machine manufacturer operating in **15 countries** with about **2,000 employees**; its equipment processes **88% of pork in North America**, and about **30% of 255 installations** are robotic [4]. The case names the monitored class only broadly as Frontmatec’s machines/installed equipment rather than disclosing a specific bearing, motor, conveyor, or robot axis; it does disclose mixed OT/operational data integration through **EtherNet/IP for PLCs**, **Modbus TCP/IP for power-supply monitoring**, and **HTTP for other devices**, plus analysis of production data patterns for bottlenecks, inefficiencies, and machine-parameter adjustment [4]. Frontmatec used **AWS IoT SiteWise Edge on Siemens Industrial Edge** for predictive maintenance and global performance management, with ML models trainable in AWS and deployable at the edge; the reported business result was deployment time cut from several hours to **15 minutes** [4]. The more specific sensor/asset technical example is Siemens Drivetrain Analyzer Edge: **SINAMICS drives**, **SIPLUS CMS1200 vibration/status variables**, KPI calculations such as mean and standard deviation, and local anomaly/mechanical-anomaly detection, paired with Siemens’ Drivetrain Analyzer Onsite product claim that AI analytics can reduce downtime by **45%** [3]. Siemens’ Schuler Pressen case separately grounds the Industrial Edge device pattern for press data: Industrial Edge runs on **SIMATIC IPC227E**, collects press data, and forwards results to Schuler Cloud/Siemens systems, with Docker app packaging and Industrial Edge Management [18].

The economic trade-off is favorable when vibration/current/temperature data are high-rate and long-retention: edge feature extraction avoids cloud ingest, storage, and compute for raw streams, and AWS SiteWise Edge explicitly supports processing raw data locally and sending only aggregated data to AWS Cloud to optimize bandwidth and cloud storage, with up to **30 days** local edge retention depending on disk [16]. The cost shifts to Industrial Edge Devices/IPCs, edge app licenses, connectors, plant-floor storage, local backup power, and lifecycle management. Maintainability improves for standardized deployments—Frontmatec’s deployment time fell to 15 minutes—but the plant now owns distributed model drift, app rollback, observability, and patch scheduling across many cells [4]. Security obligations also move outward: for AWS SiteWise Edge on Siemens Industrial Edge, AWS states that the customer physically secures the device/network, the partner keeps the solution secure with patches, updates, and confidentiality, and AWS isolates the partner from AWS cloud resources [21][16].

## 3. Autonomous mobile robots

### Before versus after architecture

**Before: fleet-manager/cloud-centered AMRs.** Earlier AMR architectures often centered route assignment, map management, analytics, and sometimes heavier perception or coordination in a fleet manager or cloud/edge server, while robots had limited onboard autonomy and depended on Wi-Fi or a factory server for command updates, traffic coordination, and monitoring.

**After: onboard NVIDIA Isaac AMR / Isaac ROS edge autonomy.** The concrete 2024 architecture is NVIDIA Isaac Perceptor / Isaac ROS on **Nova Orin** or **Jetson AGX Orin**. NVIDIA’s reference architecture is a vision-based 3D perception stack for mobile robots with visual input from three Hawk stereo cameras on Nova Orin, visual odometry, semantic segmentation, voxel-grid mapping, planning/navigation with Nav2 using Nvblox 2D/3D costmaps, and visualization; it is tested on the Nova Orin Developer Kit and Nova Carter, both with Jetson AGX Orin [7]. NVIDIA states the perception tasks in the reference architecture run entirely on a single Jetson AGX Orin, while navigation can be split to another Jetson AGX Orin or x86 system when required [7].

The computation that moves to the robot is perception, visual odometry/SLAM, semantic segmentation, obstacle and freespace processing, local costmaps, safety-zone handling, and local planning/control. What remains centralized is fleet-level task allocation, global maps, logs, simulation, model updates, analytics, and orchestration. The removed dependency is cloud round-trip perception/control. The new dependency is synchronization of maps, software images, calibration, safety policies, model versions, and fleet state across many mobile compute endpoints.

### Quantified impacts

| AMR metric | 2024 value / range | Evidence and interpretation |
|---|---:|---|
| Onboard perception/control latency target | **1.7–80 ms** component range on Jetson AGX Orin | Isaac ROS 3.2 benchmarks: freespace segmentation 576p **1.7 ms @30 Hz**, light stereo disparity 288p **5.9 ms @30 Hz**, AprilTag 720p **6.3 ms @30 Hz**, DNN stereo disparity 576p **17 ms @30 Hz**, depth segmentation 576p **80 ms @30 Hz** [5]. |

| Bandwidth reduction | **~96%–99% indicative**, from raw uncompressed 1200p stereo-camera backhaul to robot telemetry/events/maps; vendor field cases do not state a measured stream-reduction KPI | NVIDIA benchmarks three Hawk cameras at **1200p** in Isaac Perceptor/ESS graphs and keeps perception onboard; using Makino’s measured **140 Mbps uplink** as the telemetry/control-path bound, replacing three uncompressed stereo RGB camera streams at 30 Hz yields roughly **98.6%** reduction if 1200p is treated as 1920×1200 RGB, or roughly **95.8%** if treated as single-channel grayscale. This is an engineering proxy, not a Makino/NVIDIA published field reduction [5][7][6]. |

### Real-world example and trade-offs

The cited AMR edge case is **Makino Milling Machine Co. in Japan**, published **11 September 2023**. Makino launched an AMR robot-control system over **public 5G plus AWS Wavelength** in **less than five months**, moving the AMR control server from factory premises to the Wavelength Zone. The AMR transports and replaces heavy tools for machining centers, uses **LiDAR** for obstacle detection and auto mapping, and communicates with a Wavelength Zone control server over public 5G through VPN tunnels; device authentication is performed with device certificates during VPN tunnel creation [6]. Makino measured **10–15 ms** latency from the Wavelength Zone instance to the AMR and **~40 ms** overall application-to-AMR latency over HTTPS over a VPN tunnel; it also measured **1.1 Gbps** average downlink and **140 Mbps** average uplink, and reported no disconnections while moving across multiple 5G base-station areas [6]. The business result was network stability/performance, lower initial cost than building private 5G, better maintainability, remote monitoring, and elimination of factory servers [6].

The cost trade-off is sharper than in predictive maintenance. A robot equipped with Jetson AGX Orin/Nova Orin-class compute, cameras, LiDAR, and 5G/Wi-Fi becomes more expensive than a thin client, but it avoids sending high-rate perception streams to a remote processor and keeps safety behavior deterministic. Makino’s case shows an alternative cost path: public 5G plus AWS Wavelength avoided private-5G equipment investment, spectrum/license work, and local expert maintenance, while still achieving ~40 ms application-to-AMR latency [6]. Maintainability shifts to fleet software updates, map/version control, calibration, and observability for mobile endpoints. Security must be designed into the robot: Jetson Linux for Orin supports Secure Boot, preventing execution of unauthorized boot code, UEFI secure-boot keys, capsule updates, image-based OTA updates, and A/B bootloader redundancy that keeps a workable bootloader partition available during update [22][23][24].

## 4. Energy distribution

### Before versus after architecture

**Before: centralized SCADA/ADMS polling and head-end analytics.** Traditional distribution automation uses a centralized DMS/ADMS or SCADA master in a hub-and-spoke structure. Field devices such as reclosers, switches, relays, RTUs, and IEDs report status through DNP3 or related protocols; operators or centralized FLISR applications compute switching actions and send commands back. DNP3 can be event-driven, but polling intervals still anchor response time: one Geo SCADA DNP3 example polls Class 1 events every **5 s**, Class 2 every **30 s**, Class 3 every **5 min**, and integrity daily [11]; another DNP3 guide gives typical event polling of Class 1 every **1 s**, Class 2 every **5 s**, Class 3 every **30 s**, and integrity every **300 s** [9]. Schneider Geo SCADA recommends consistent poll divisions such as **5- or 10-second intervals** [10].

**After: OpenFMB / substation-feeder edge.** The concrete 2024 architecture is OpenFMB plus feeder/substation edge gateways or Schneider EcoStruxure ADMS/DERMS at the distribution edge. OpenFMB enables distributed intelligence at the grid edge with platform-independent message profiles that use IEC 61850 and CIM concepts serialized with Protobuf; its pub-sub topics identify the device module, message profile, and unique device, and it can work with or adapt DNP3, Modbus, DDS, MQTT, and other protocols [13]. NAESB RMQ.26 codifies OpenFMB model business practices around pub-sub transports such as NATS or MQTT to authenticate and exchange information among field equipment [14]. In the OpenFMB FLISR proof of concept, an **SEL-3360** industrial computer hosted the OpenFMB harness and FLISR control locally, five **SEL-651R** relays formed the feeder network, an **SEL-3530 RTAC** provided DNP3 backhaul to the control center, and a **Typhoon HIL 604** emulated the feeder [13].

The computation that moves to the edge is FLISR rules, local switching recommendations or actions, DER/microgrid coordination, protection-adjacent analytics, and local event filtering/pub-sub. What remains centralized is supervisory control, outage management, regulatory reporting, planning studies, long-term historian data, and operator override. The removed dependency is waiting for all field events to traverse a central DMS/ADMS loop before local restoration logic can run. The new dependency is rigorous synchronization of feeder topology, settings groups, protection limits, DER state, operator permissions, and cybersecurity policy across field gateways.

### Quantified impacts

| Energy metric | Value / range | Evidence and interpretation |
|---|---:|---|
| Before-state SCADA/ADMS polling interval | **1–5 s** for critical Class 1 events in examples; **5–10 s** recommended consistent poll intervals; slower classes at **30 s–5 min** | DNP3/Geo SCADA examples and guidance [9][10][11]. |
| Edge FLISR/protection-adjacent response target | **Sub-minute**, with a field case reporting **<1 minute** average and later **<20 s** restoration | Schneider/Tata self-healing grid case [12]. OpenFMB proof-of-concept demonstrates direct low-latency exchange but did not publish exact ms delay reduction [13]. |
| Bandwidth reduction | Exact OpenFMB percentage not stated; DNP3 already uses event reporting, and OpenFMB adds local pub-sub exchange so summaries/supervisory data, not every local coordination message, need backhaul | OpenFMB pub-sub and protocol-adapter design [13][14]. |
| Reliability metric, Schneider/Tata | Restoration from hours to **<1 minute**, later summarized as hours to **<20 s** | Tata Power Mumbai self-healing grid [12]. |
| Reliability metric, Central Georgia EMC | SAIFI down **32%** in first year; SAIDI down from **130 min** to **65.8 min** in two years, a **49.4%** reduction; crews spent **~20%** less time restoring outages | Central Georgia EMC FLISR case [15]. |

### Real-world utility examples and trade-offs

The OpenFMB example is the DOE/EPRI/PNNL-style FLISR testbed: OpenFMB on an **SEL-3360** industrial computer, five **SEL-651R** relays, **SEL-3530 RTAC** DNP3 backhaul, and **Typhoon HIL 604**, using DNP3 and NATS/OpenFMB pub-sub to validate direct low-latency information exchange and protocol interoperability [13]. The utility field example with measured restoration impact is **Tata Power Mumbai** with **Schneider Electric EcoStruxure ADMS**, published **February 2018**: the stated service scope was more than **350,000 households** in Mumbai, and the deployed package included **EcoStruxure ADMS**, smart digital substations, smart RMUs, smart packaged systems, and smart circuit breakers rather than a disclosed count of feeders, substations, or RMUs [12]. Tata Power adopted a **decentralized** self-healing grid in which smart RMUs/substations communicate with each other to execute fault isolation and restoration while the existing centralized **SCADA/DMS** can monitor the system; the case says local deployment is by installing **FRTU, motors, and auxiliary switches**, but it does not name DNP3, IEC 61850, OpenFMB, or a specific Schneider ADMS interface standard for this project [12]. The case reports restoration reduced from hours to **under one minute** on average and later summarizes the result as **hours to <20 seconds**, with improved SAIDI/FI, reduced ENS, fewer manual errors, and a 24/7 supply goal [12]. A second, undated Survalent **SurvalentONE ADMS/SCADA/FLISR** example, **Central Georgia EMC**, grounds the reliability metric at cooperative scale: the Jackson, Georgia utility serves parts of **14 counties** and more than **55,000 customers**; it upgraded from SCADA to fully integrated **SurvalentONE ADMS** with model-based centralized FLISR using real-time network data to diagnose faults, reconfigure the network, isolate damage, and restore upstream/downstream customers, reducing SAIFI by **32%** in the first year, SAIDI from **130 min** to **65.8 min** two years later, and field-crew restoration time by **~20%** [15].

Energy’s trade-off is governed less by cloud cost than by safety, testing, and compliance. Edge FLISR can restore faster and tolerate backhaul loss, but a wrong local action can energize a faulted section, violate protection coordination, or endanger crews. Maintainability therefore includes feeder model accuracy, settings-group governance, hardware-in-loop testing, rollback plans, operator training, and auditability. Cybersecurity obligations are also heavier: NERC CIP-005-7 defines Cyber Security — Electronic Security Perimeter(s) and manages electronic access to BES Cyber Systems through controlled Electronic Security Perimeters; its applicability depends on whether the assets are categorized as high-, medium-, or low-impact BES Cyber Systems, so not every distribution feeder gateway is automatically in scope, but BES-connected distribution-edge systems require CIP scoping and segmentation analysis [20]. OpenFMB’s NAESB RMQ.26 pub-sub model explicitly includes authenticated exchange among field equipment, but it also expands the number of cyber-physical endpoints that must be inventoried, patched, monitored, and tested [14].

## 5. Cross-use-case comparison and ranking

**Latency ranking.** AMRs are most latency-sensitive: perception and control must react to people, forklifts, pallets, and dynamic obstacles, so NVIDIA’s onboard components operate in the **1.7–80 ms** range and Makino’s MEC control path targets **~40 ms** application-to-AMR [5][6]. Manufacturing predictive maintenance is next: 135 ms local vibration inference is useful for near-real-time anomaly scoring, but most maintenance actions are alerts or work orders, not immediate safety stops [1]. Energy distribution’s FLISR decisions can be sub-minute rather than millisecond robot-control loops, but they are safety-governed and protection-adjacent; the value is restoration from hours to **<1 minute / <20 s**, not perception-frame latency [12].

**Bandwidth ranking.** Predictive maintenance is most bandwidth/storage-driven because raw vibration streams are large and continuous; the grounded reduction range is **98.21%–99.9%** when raw vibration is converted into local features/events [2][1]. AMRs also avoid enormous camera/LiDAR backhaul, but the cited vendor cases report latency, throughput, and reliability rather than a percentage reduction [7][6]. Energy distribution is event-heavy rather than raw-video-heavy; DNP3 already supports event reporting, and OpenFMB’s gain is local coordination and reduced backhaul dependence rather than a published percent-reduction figure [13][14].

**Availability/reliability ranking.** Energy distribution is most reliability- and safety-governed: FLISR directly affects SAIDI/SAIFI and customer interruptions, with Central Georgia EMC reporting **32%** SAIFI reduction and **49.4%** SAIDI reduction, and Schneider/Tata reporting restoration from hours to **<1 minute / <20 s** [15][12]. AMRs rank next because lost connectivity can stop fleets or create unsafe behavior; Makino reported no disconnections across 5G base-station areas and kept robot sensing onboard [6]. Manufacturing improves uptime too—Siemens reports **45%** downtime reduction for AI drivetrain analytics—but most predictive-maintenance systems can degrade to delayed alerts rather than immediately unsafe states [3].

## 6. Cost, security, and maintainability pattern

Across all three sectors, edge computing improves autonomy, determinism, bandwidth cost, and outage tolerance, but shifts cost and risk into distributed hardware refreshes, cybersecurity patching, model and map drift, observability, rollback, and operational maintainability.

- **Manufacturing:** adds Industrial Edge Devices/IPCs, connectors, local storage, Industrial Edge app/runtime licensing, and plant-floor lifecycle operations; Siemens describes centralized Industrial Edge deployment, security patches, firmware updates, zero-touch provisioning, Docker-standard containers, and Kubernetes-based management, while SiteWise Edge avoids raw-data cloud ingest/storage/compute by sending aggregated data and supports up to **30 days** local retention [19][16]. Security is shared among customer physical/network protection, partner patch/update responsibility, and AWS cloud isolation responsibilities [21].
- **AMRs:** adds Jetson AGX Orin/Nova Orin-class compute, cameras, LiDAR, batteries sized for compute, robot OTA, calibration, and fleet observability; avoids cloud perception and may avoid private-5G capex if a public-5G/MEC path like Makino’s is acceptable [6]. Security controls include Jetson Secure Boot, UEFI secure-boot keys, capsule updates, image-based OTA, and A/B bootloader redundancy [22][23][24].
- **Energy distribution:** adds substation/feeder gateways, RTAC/industrial computers, local comms, HIL testing, topology/settings management, and compliance documentation; the benefit is faster restoration and resilience when the central DMS link is delayed or lost [13][12]. Security obligations are tied to IEC 61850/OpenFMB/DNP3 endpoint hardening and NERC CIP scoping for BES Cyber Systems, including CIP-005-7 electronic security perimeters where applicable [13][20].

Cloud-centric architecture remains preferable as the fallback and system of record for non-real-time analytics, historical training, fleet-level optimization, long-term storage, regulatory reporting, cross-site benchmarking, and operator-supervised decisions. The durable architecture is therefore not “edge instead of cloud,” but “edge for deterministic local action and data reduction; cloud for learning, coordination, governance, and history.”
