# Explaining the Cambrian diversification: trigger, toolkit, feedback, and the changing evidentiary frame

## Executive assessment

The best-supported current interpretation is not a single-cause “oxygen trigger,” “Hox-gene trigger,” or “predation trigger,” but a staged diversification in which environmental permissiveness, pre-existing developmental capacity, and ecological feedbacks acted at different levels. Oxygenation is strongest as a **permissive ecological constraint**: geochemical work associated with Lyons, Reinhard, Planavsky and colleagues documents low earlier oxygen and Cambrian expansion of oxygenated marine habitats, and oxygen plausibly enabled larger, more active, metabolically expensive animals and oxygen-demanding tissues [1][2][3]. The Davidson–Erwin genetic-toolkit view is strongest as an explanation of **body-plan possibility and constraint**: Hox/ParaHox genes, hierarchical gene regulatory networks (GRNs), conserved developmental kernels, and cis-regulatory architecture made bilaterian morphogenesis evolvable, but much of the toolkit appears to predate the visible Cambrian radiation [4][5][6][7]. The Marshall–Valentine ecological-feedback model is strongest as an explanation of **why latent capacity was expressed rapidly in ecology and morphology**: predation, burrowing, eyes, skeletons, defensive structures, repaired injuries, and changing seafloors supplied escalating selection pressures [8][7][9][10][11].

Recent Chengjiang and Burgess Shale discoveries, plus phylogenetic reinterpretation of “weird wonders” as stem lineages, have shifted debate away from a literal instantaneous origin of phyla and toward a compressed but structured interval of stem- and crown-lineage assembly. Molecular clocks using Bayesian relaxed-clock approaches generally deepen some divergences into the Ediacaran, but recent recalibrations have narrowed the gap with fossils relative to older Precambrian estimates, making the Cambrian record look incomplete but not wholly misleading [12][13]. Taphonomic and stratigraphic work complicates the word “explosion”: exceptional Burgess Shale-type preservation reveals soft-bodied diversity unavailable in ordinary shelly records, but the same studies imply a real early Cambrian ecological and anatomical signal rather than a pure preservation artifact [14][15].

## Timing: what was rapid, and how rapid?

The current chronologic framework places the formal Ediacaran–Cambrian boundary at the base of the Fortunian; the current absolute age is commonly given as ~538.8 Ma, while older Cambrian-explosion literature commonly used 542–543 Ma [12][7]. The formal boundary marker is ichnological: the Fortunian proposal ties the base of the Cambrian to the FAD of *Trichophycus*/*Treptichnus pedum* and the base of the *T. pedum* Zone at Fortune Head, Newfoundland [16]. That marker is debated because an ichnotaxon can be affected by ichnotaxonomy, behaviour, facies control and stratigraphic occurrence; Marshall also notes that *T. pedum* occurs a short distance below the formally defined base in Newfoundland, so the boundary is not a clean biological starting gun [7][17].

A useful way to summarize the timing is to separate formal stratigraphy, first appearances, and Lagerstätten:

| Event or record | Best-supported timing in gathered sources | Significance for “explosion” debate |
|---|---:|---|
| Ediacaran–Cambrian boundary | ~538.8 Ma in current chronostratigraphy; older sources use 542–543 Ma | Formal marker is the *T. pedum* ichnofossil zone at Fortune Head, not the first animal or first bilaterian [7][16] |
| Terminal Ediacaran trace fossils | Horizontal traces regular by ~555 Ma | Shows bilaterian-like activity began before the boundary [7] |
| First lightly skeletonized Ediacaran forms | Last ~5 Myr of Ediacaran, including *Cloudina* and *Namacalathus* | Skeletonization begins before Cambrian, weakening a strict boundary-origin model [7] |
| Main early Cambrian rise of skeletonized and recognizable bilaterian body fossils | Roughly first ~20 Myr from heavily skeletonized animals to first trilobite body fossils | The core “explosive” signal: fast relative to Phanerozoic time but not instantaneous [7] |
| Chengjiang/Maotianshan biota | Early Cambrian Epoch 2, Age 3; U–Pb work constrains the biota to not earlier than 518.03 ±0.69/0.71 Ma; commonly summarized as ~518 Ma | First major Cambrian exceptional-preservation window; already high disparity and complex ecosystems [18][19][9] |
| Burgess Shale biota | Middle Cambrian/Miaolingian, Wuliuan; about 505–510 Ma, commonly ~508 Ma | Later than the main early Cambrian rise; records established ecosystems and many stem lineages [20][21] |

The central timing implication is therefore mixed. The paleontological record supports a **geologically rapid** radiation, especially for preservable macroscopic bilaterians, but not an instantaneous appearance of animals. Marshall’s review explicitly warns that “explosion” should be used in quotation marks: the radiation was fast compared with the time from the Cambrian to the present, yet it extended over ~20 Myr or longer if Ediacaran prehistory and the full Cambrian are included [7]. Recent trilobite-rate work reinforces the same point: Chengjiang and younger Burgess Shale-type deposits are better treated as early post-explosion records of modern-style marine ecosystems, while many stem- and crown-group members of bilaterian phyla appear to have diversified in about 20 Myr [22].

## Environmental-trigger hypothesis: oxygen as threshold and habitat expansion

Lyons and Reinhard’s environmental hypothesis rests on the idea that animal evolution was constrained by the oxygenation state of the ocean–atmosphere system. The proxy basis is multi-stranded rather than a single oxygen meter. Lyons, Reinhard and Planavsky review the rise of oxygen using palaeosols, chromium isotopes, sulfur and sulfate records, redox-sensitive trace elements, and evidence for anoxic/euxinic marine states [1]. Planavsky, Reinhard, Lyons and coauthors specifically used sedimentary chromium isotope data from Proterozoic successions in China, Australia and North America, with petrography and LA-ICP-MS trace-element mapping to guard against secondary alteration, to infer inhibited oxidative Cr cycling and very low mid-Proterozoic atmospheric oxygen, probably below early animal requirements [2]. Later work in the same geochemical tradition used molybdenum isotopes, uranium concentrations, and iron speciation to argue that oxygenated bottom waters expanded with early Cambrian bioradiation and that modern-like ocean oxygenation was reached near ~521–520 Ma [23].

The biology tied to oxygen is explicit in Reinhard et al.’s synthesis. Oxygen supplies the high-energy terminal electron acceptor for aerobic metabolism; mitochondrial use of O₂ can greatly increase energy flux relative to genome size; oxygen is required in enzymatic pathways for regulatory membrane lipids and structural proteins such as collagen; and it permits large body size and active lifestyles [3]. Crucially, their argument is not just “animals need oxygen.” They distinguish oxygen thresholds for different milestones: origin of the metazoan last common ancestor, diversification of basal and bilaterian clades, and later emergence of larger bodies and complex interactions such as predation and burrowing [3]. Their models also stress spatial habitat: at low atmospheric pO₂, surface waters and benthic habitats can be decoupled, and benthic anoxia can exclude animals from large areas of seafloor; oxygenation therefore expands habitable benthic ecospace [3].

This makes oxygen a plausible **enabler** of large, active, collagen-bearing, burrowing and predatory animals, and of the expansion of habitable seafloor. It is less convincing as a sole **trigger** of the exact timing of body-plan disparity, because molecular and developmental evidence indicates that some animal lineages and toolkits existed before modern-like Cambrian oxygenation, and because oxygen estimates are spatially heterogeneous and method-dependent [12][3].

## Genetic-toolkit hypothesis: body-plan possibility before fossil abundance

Davidson and Erwin’s genetic-toolkit hypothesis differs from the oxygen hypothesis in explanatory level. Oxygen explains physiological and ecological feasibility; the toolkit hypothesis explains how complex body plans could be generated and stabilized. Their key mechanism is not simply “more genes,” but the evolution of hierarchical GRNs. In their framework, cis-regulatory changes have different evolutionary consequences depending on where they occur in a GRN hierarchy: high-level developmental kernels are deeply conserved and canalized, while peripheral regulatory modules and differentiation gene batteries are more labile [4][5]. Davidson–Erwin kernels are small sets of regulatory genes connected by recursive positive feedback, deployed upstream in developmental specification; disruption of such circuits causes developmental catastrophe, which helps explain body-plan stability after early establishment [4][5].

Hox and ParaHox genes are part of this broader regulatory architecture. Marshall’s review summarizes the developmental inference: the origin of the bilaterian developmental system, including Hox genes, was critical to the Cambrian radiation; gene duplication was important, including the relationship between Hox and ParaHox clusters; and the inferred ur-bilaterian possessed regulatory capacities for anterior–posterior patterning, dorsal–ventral patterning, regionalized gut and nervous system, segmentation-related patterning, photoreceptor organs, circulatory pumps, and body-wall outgrowths [7]. Erwin and Davidson also emphasize that many conserved developmental control genes are shared with cnidarians, including Hox/ParaHox-class genes and other transcription-factor and signaling systems, indicating that parts of the eumetazoan toolkit predate the Cambrian body-fossil radiation [6].

This timing matters. If core Hox/ParaHox and metazoan regulatory genes predate the Cambrian, then the toolkit cannot be a simple Cambrian on-switch. Instead, it is best interpreted as a **precondition** whose ecological and environmental expression became visible later. This is why the genetic-toolkit hypothesis complements, rather than replaces, the oxygen and ecological hypotheses: it explains the construction kit, not necessarily why the kit was deployed at that moment.

## Ecological-feedback model: arms races as the accelerator

Marshall and Valentine’s ecological-feedback model asks why complex morphologies and diversity expanded when they did if both environmental conditions and developmental capacities may have been partly in place earlier. Marshall’s answer is ecological “roughening”: once macroscopic animals began interacting through predation, avoidance, competition, and ecosystem engineering, the number of adaptive demands increased sharply, causing rapid exploration of developmental possibility [8][7]. In this view, predation is especially important because every way of pursuing prey induces multiple ways of avoiding predators, which in turn selects for new predatory strategies [8]. Valentine’s niche-space argument adds that early open ecospace made major innovations easier to establish, whereas later ecospace saturation limited the subsequent origin of new phyla [7].

The fossil indicators are broad and mutually reinforcing:

- **Predatory anatomy:** Chengjiang and Burgess Shale preserve anomalocaridids/radiodonts and other arthropod predators; IUGS identifies *Anomalocaris canadensis* from the Burgess Shale as a top Cambrian predator [9][21].
- **Eyes and sensory escalation:** Marshall discusses macroscopic eyes as important to Cambrian biotic interaction, while cautioning that eyesight was probably not the only route to ecological radiation [7].
- **Trace fossils and burrowing:** horizontal traces are regular by ~555 Ma and increase in size and complexity through the Cambrian; vertical and three-dimensional burrowing mark the Cambrian substrate revolution [7][10].
- **Skeletons, spines and armor:** lightly skeletonized Ediacaran forms precede the Cambrian, small shelly fossils become abundant in the early Cambrian, and many small shelly fossils represent armor elements of larger animals [7].
- **Repaired injuries and durophagy:** Chengjiang includes trilobites with healed bite marks, and abnormal Cambrian trilobites preserve repaired injuries used to infer predation and other traumatic interactions [9][11].
- **Coprolites and gut contents:** Chengjiang clusters interpreted as coprolites and later evidence of shell-crushing and shelly gut contents support trophic complexity [9][11].

This model is powerful because it explains acceleration and feedback. Its limitation is that it presupposes organisms already capable of active movement, sensing, feeding, and defense—capacities that depend on oxygen and developmental architecture. Thus, ecological feedback is best ranked as the **proximate accelerator**, not the sole ultimate cause.

## Chengjiang and Burgess Shale: fossils that changed the debate

Chengjiang shifted the Cambrian debate because it brought Burgess Shale-quality soft-bodied preservation much closer to the early Cambrian diversification. The IUGS summary describes Chengjiang as an early Cambrian Lagerstätte with more than 300 reported species and more than 20 phyla, preserving diverse metazoan body plans and vertebrates relevant to the transition between amphioxus-like ancestors and craniates [18]. The GSA Today review lists a broad fauna shared in part with Burgess Shale—sponges, cnidarians, ctenophores, brachiopods, hyoliths, priapulid and annelid worms, lobopods, arthropods, hemichordates and chordates—while noting that mollusks and echinoderms are rare or absent in Chengjiang [9]. Chengjiang therefore demonstrated that many major body-plan experiments, ecological roles, and stem-lineage morphologies were already present at ~518 Ma—constrained by U–Pb dating to not earlier than 518.03 ±0.69/0.71 Ma—rather than appearing only in the middle Cambrian Burgess Shale [18][19][9].

Burgess Shale remains equally important, but its interpretive role has changed. The Burgess Shale is Wuliuan/Miaolingian, about 505–510 Ma, and records a mature soft-bodied Cambrian ecosystem rather than the first moment of animal body-plan invention [20][21]. Its classic taxa—*Marrella*, *Yohoia*, *Opabinia*, *Hallucigenia*, *Anomalocaris*, *Nectocaris*, *Sanctacaris*, *Metaspriggina*, *Oesia*, *Branchiocaris* and *Naraoia*—made early disparity vivid [24][25][20][21]. But reinterpretation has reduced the idea that Burgess animals represent a swarm of wholly separate extinct phyla. Briggs’s historical analysis describes three phases: Walcott’s assignment of fossils to modern groups, Gould and Whittington’s “weird wonders,” and the newer cladistic/stem-group phase in which many Burgess forms are placed along stems of living clades [24]. The result is subtle: Burgess Shale still supports high early anatomical disparity, especially among stem arthropods and related ecdysozoans, but it no longer requires a claim that early Cambrian seas contained dozens of unrelated phylum-level body plans that later vanished.

## Molecular clocks and Bayesian relaxed-clock recalibration

Molecular clocks have moved the debate from “fossils versus molecules” toward calibration-sensitive reconciliation. Older clocks often implied very deep Precambrian animal origins; recent Bayesian relaxed-clock analyses use fossil calibrations and models allowing rate variation among lineages to estimate a shorter hidden history. The 2024 Science Advances study revised fossil calibrations, analyzed fixed alternative topologies in a Bayesian node-calibrated relaxed-clock framework, and used MCMCtree to model rate variation and calibration uncertainty [12][13]. Its averaged estimates place crown Metazoa at 613.2–593.4 Ma, crown Eumetazoa at 590.7–578.2 Ma, Bilateria at 581.8–569 Ma, Deuterostomia at 574.4–558.4 Ma and Protostomia at 571.3–558.5 Ma; under a conventional topology, crown Metazoa is 598.6–590.2 Ma, Eumetazoa 587.4–579.7 Ma and Bilateria 577.6–567.9 Ma [12][13]. The study argues for a short prehistory before fossil representation rather than a Tonian or Cryogenian origin: crown Metazoa and crown Eumetazoa are Ediacaran, Bilateria is upper Ediacaran, many crown phyla originate across the Ediacaran–Cambrian interval or within the Cambrian, and the fossil gap for Metazoa is on the order of ~19 Myr while Bilateria, Protostomia and Deuterostomia require ~20–40 Myr of prehistory before their known fossil appearances [13]. It further concludes that metazoans began radiating in the middle Ediacaran, with all major phyla originating by the late Cambrian, over somewhat less than ~100 Myr when maximum and minimum estimates are considered [12]. Some phyla are estimated to have Cambrian crown origins, including Euarthropoda, Echinodermata, Ctenophora, Hemichordata, Rotifera and Chaetognatha, while Chordata, Cnidaria and Mollusca are inferred to have late Ediacaran origins [12].

These recalibrations shift the debate in two ways. First, they undercut a strictly literal fossil reading in which most major lineages began only at their first mineralized or exceptionally preserved fossils. Second, they also undercut extreme molecular-clock claims of a vast, cryptic Precambrian animal fauna. The emerging compromise is a modest Ediacaran stem history followed by Cambrian ecological success, fossil visibility and crown/stem sorting [22][12][13].

## Preservation bias, Butterfield, and stratigraphic boundaries

Butterfield’s taphonomic work is central because Burgess Shale-type preservation is both revelatory and biased. Burgess Shale-type deposits preserve whole assemblages of soft-bodied animals as primary carbonaceous remains, often with extraordinary anatomical detail, and are therefore essential for seeing non-biomineralized taxa and soft anatomy—lobopods, arthropod appendages, guts, eyes and related tissues—that ordinary shelly records miss [14][15][21]. But Butterfield’s abstract stresses that exceptional non-biomineralizing fossils help resolve details of the Cambrian explosion “but little” of its overall pattern, and that terminal Proterozoic–Cambrian exceptional-preservation types are restricted in space and time [14]. Later Burgess Shale-type syntheses place the main window primarily in early and middle Cambrian strata, Series 2–3, and emphasize that more than 50 deposits are controlled by both global seawater chemistry and local depositional filters [15]. Butterfield also warns that some anatomical identifications have been overread: he questions, for example, interpretations of some putative Chengjiang deuterostomes and Sirius Passet lobopods, reinterpreting some as arthropods [14]. Thus, taphonomy has two opposite effects: it reveals that ordinary records undercount early soft-bodied diversity, but it also disciplines claims of extraordinary disparity by forcing careful anatomical reinterpretation.

The stratigraphic boundary issue points in the same direction. The formal Cambrian base is tied to a trace fossil, not to the first appearance of animals, skeletons, bilaterians, or phyla [7]. Trace fossils, lightly skeletonized forms and possible bilaterian indicators occur before the boundary, while many crown-group representatives appear after the early Cambrian [7]. The boundary is therefore useful for chronostratigraphy but dangerous as a biological discontinuity. Stratigraphic analyses that challenge traditional Cambrian boundaries do not erase the diversification; rather, they spread its onset into the terminal Ediacaran and show that the apparent abruptness partly reflects how geologists draw boundaries, how trace fossils are correlated, and where preservation windows open [7][19][10].

Overall, the paleontological evidence supports a real rapid diversification of macroscopic, ecologically complex bilaterians and skeletonized animals, but not a literal instantaneous appearance of all animal phyla. Preservation bias amplifies the contrast between Ediacaran and Cambrian records, yet Chengjiang, Burgess, small shelly fossils, trace fossils, trilobite rates, and taphonomic scrutiny together indicate that the early Cambrian signal is not merely an artifact [22][7][9][14][15].

## Phylogenomics: constraints and complications

Phylogenomics has sharpened, but not simplified, the problem. At the deepest metazoan node, the sponge-first versus ctenophore-first debate affects how many times nervous systems, muscles, and complex cell types must have originated or been lost. The 2023 synteny study argues that ancient gene linkages support ctenophores as sister to all other animals [26]. Under ctenophore-first, neurons in ctenophores and parahoxozoans either evolved convergently or were present in the metazoan ancestor and then lost in sponges; either scenario complicates a simple toolkit narrative [26]. The same study discusses Parahoxozoa—bilaterians, cnidarians and placozoans—defined partly by Hox/ParaHox-class genes, with sponges and ctenophores outside that clade [26]. This supports the idea that major regulatory architectures predate the Cambrian, but it complicates inference about what the earliest animals looked like.

For Bilateria, phylogenomics and comparative development reinforce the importance of Protostomia, Deuterostomia, Ecdysozoa and Spiralia/Lophotrochozoa as major branches: one all-phyla phylogenomic study reports strong support for Parahoxozoa, Planulozoa, Bilateria, Nephrozoa, Deuterostomia, Protostomia, Ecdysozoa and Spiralia, although the metazoan root remains less secure [27]. Fossils such as Chengjiang and Burgess often fall on stem lineages rather than neatly inside living crowns [28][24][25]. This means fossil disparity may represent stem-grade experimentation rather than the sudden origin of living phyla as modern crowns. It also means ecological interpretations must be phylogenetically cautious: a predatory appendage, eye or armor element may illuminate a stem lineage’s ecological role without proving that a modern crown group had already originated.

Xenacoelomorpha placement further complicates reconstruction of the bilaterian ancestor. The 2024 clock study treated Xenacoelomorpha as sister either to Nephrozoa or to Ambulacraria, and also tested monophyletic versus paraphyletic Deuterostomia; its standard tree used Porifera-first, monophyletic Deuterostomia and Xenacoelomorpha sister to Nephrozoa, but it noted that not all proposed relationships fit a single resolved tree [12][13]. Other phylogenomic work finds that Xenacoelomorpha may be sister to Nephrozoa or sister to Ambulacraria, and that these alternatives imply very different ancestral bilaterians: respectively a simpler ancestral bilaterian or a more complex ancestor followed by secondary simplification in Xenacoelomorpha [29][30].

## Ranking the explanations

A balanced ranking by explanatory role is:

1. **Necessary permissive condition: oxygenation.** Oxygen best explains why large, active, collagen-bearing, burrowing and predatory animals became physiologically and ecologically feasible, and why habitable seafloor expanded [23][3]. It is weaker as a precise, sole trigger because some animal and toolkit origins predate the main Cambrian oxygenation signal [12][6].
2. **Necessary constructive condition: developmental GRNs.** Hox/ParaHox genes, conserved kernels and hierarchical regulatory complexity explain the evolvability and stability of bilaterian body plans [4][5][6]. They are weaker as a Cambrian trigger because much of the toolkit appears older than the explosion [6][7].
3. **Most plausible proximate accelerator: ecological feedback.** Predator–prey escalation, burrowing, skeletonization, sensory systems and ecosystem engineering explain why diversity and disparity could rise rapidly once oxygen and developmental capacity were in place [8][7][9][10][11]. It is weaker as an ultimate cause because ecological escalation itself requires active animals, oxygen and regulatory architecture.

Thus, the Cambrian Explosion is best understood as a rapid ecological and anatomical radiation of visible bilaterian life, not as the instantaneous origin of animals or of all developmental genes. Fossils still support a real explosion in rates and ecological restructuring, but molecular clocks, taphonomy, stratigraphy and phylogenomics have transformed it from a single-boundary miracle into a staged Ediacaran–Cambrian transition: developmental potential assembled first, environmental opportunity widened, and ecological feedbacks made the latent possibilities visible in the fossil record.
