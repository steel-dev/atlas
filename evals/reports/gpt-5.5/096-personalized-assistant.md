# Bottom line (planning baseline: 2026-06-23)

For a 42-year-old Toronto, Ontario resident freelance/self-employed graphic designer earning CAD 95,000 with irregular monthly income, two children about age 11 and 8 (born approximately 2015 and 2018), and the eldest expected to start post-secondary in 2032, the highest-confidence strategy is: **protect liquidity first, fund each child’s RESP at CAD 5,000 per year from 2026 through 2032 to capture the maximum available Basic CESG under the annual catch-up cap, then use RRSP or spousal RRSP room at the same up-front deduction value, and put tax refunds/overflow into TFSA**. Spouse/common-law partner status and spouse taxable/retirement income are not provided, so the spousal RRSP recommendation must be conditional: use a **spousal RRSP only if a spouse/common-law partner exists and is expected to have materially lower retirement taxable income**; otherwise use the individual RRSP.

Assumptions for the projection, because actual essential expenses, existing RESP/CESG history, RRSP/TFSA room, spouse income, and portfolio risk level were not provided: no prior RESP contributions or CESG; sufficient RRSP and TFSA room; emergency fund already at target; CAD 95,000 remains earned income; annual contributions made at calendar year-end from 2026 through 2032; RRSP refund reinvested in TFSA; 5.0% nominal after-fee annual return; 2.5% inflation; RESP education withdrawals taxed in the students’ hands at an assumed low/zero effective rate; RRSP shown at both pre-tax value and after 25% assumed retirement tax.

## Tax baseline at CAD 95,000 taxable income in Ontario for 2026

The 2026 federal brackets are: CAD 0–58,523 at 14%; CAD 58,523.01–117,045 at 20.5%; CAD 117,045.01–181,440 at 26%; CAD 181,440.01–258,482 at 29%; and over CAD 258,482 at 33% [8]. The 2026 Ontario brackets are: CAD 0–53,891 at 5.05%; CAD 53,891.01–107,785 at 9.15%; CAD 107,785.01–150,000 at 11.16%; CAD 150,000.01–220,000 at 12.16%; and over CAD 220,000 at 13.16% [8]. Ontario surtax matters at this income: the 2026 surtax is 20% of Ontario tax over CAD 5,818 and an additional 36% over CAD 7,446; for a taxpayer with only the basic personal amount, the 20% surtax starts at taxable income of CAD 94,907 and the additional 36% surtax starts at CAD 111,814 [5].

At CAD 95,000 of Ontario taxable income, the combined federal/Ontario marginal rate on ordinary income is **31.48%** for the narrow band over CAD 94,907 to CAD 107,785; below CAD 94,907 and down to CAD 58,523, the combined marginal rate is **29.65%** [5]. Therefore, a CAD 1 RRSP deduction at CAD 95,000 saves about **CAD 0.3148** for the first roughly CAD 93 of deduction and about **CAD 0.2965** for most additional deductions until taxable income falls below CAD 58,523.

Indicative 2026 self-employed cash-flow baseline, before family spending and before any RRSP deduction: let **E = deductible business expenses** and **T = CAD 95,000 − E**. At E = CAD 0 for illustration, the bracket math gives federal tax before non-refundable credits of about **CAD 15,671**, Ontario basic tax of about **CAD 6,483**, Ontario surtax of about **CAD 133**, and maximum self-employed CPP/CPP2 of **CAD 9,292.90**, leaving about **CAD 63,421** before personal credits and any other deductions/benefits; actual spendable cash is therefore approximately **CAD 95,000 − E − income tax(T) − CPP(T) − HST/remittance obligations if applicable**, not CAD 95,000. This is why the CAD 10,000 RESP + CAD 17,100 RRSP + CAD 7,000 TFSA annual targets should be treated as a waterfall against actual surplus, not as automatically affordable from gross billings [8] [5] [9] [10].

## RRSP room, individual RRSP vs spousal RRSP

Under Income Tax Act section 146, RRSP deduction room is essentially unused room carried forward plus the lesser of the RRSP dollar limit and 18% of the previous year’s earned income, adjusted for pension adjustments, PSPAs, and PARs [6]. The 2026 RRSP dollar limit is **CAD 33,810** [11]. CRA’s T4040 definition of earned income includes self-employment earnings and net income from a business carried on alone or as an active partner, so freelance graphic-design income counts for RRSP room purposes [12]. At CAD 95,000 of earned income, new RRSP room from the 18% formula is **CAD 17,100**, before any pension adjustment or carry-forward.

The up-front deduction is the same whether the contribution goes to the client’s own RRSP or a spousal/common-law partner RRSP: ITA s.146(5.1) allows the contributor to deduct premiums paid to a spouse/common-law partner RRSP within the contributor’s own RRSP deduction limit, so the deduction at CAD 95,000 has the same 31.48%/29.65% marginal value either way [6]. The difference is ownership and later taxation: CRA says the annuitant spouse/common-law partner owns the spousal RRSP/RRIF and withdrawals normally report to the annuitant unless attribution applies [12]. The attribution rule in ITA s.146(8.3) generally taxes the contributor on the lesser of the annuitant’s withdrawal and contributions made in the withdrawal year or either of the two preceding calendar years, with exceptions such as relationship breakdown separation, non-residency, certain transfers/annuities, or contributor death [6] [12].

**Use a spousal RRSP** if there is a spouse/common-law partner and that partner is expected to have lower taxable retirement income, because the same up-front deduction can shift future withdrawals to the lower-income spouse after the three-calendar-year attribution window. **Use the individual RRSP** if there is no spouse/common-law partner, the spouse is expected to have similar or higher retirement income, the relationship status is uncertain, or near-term withdrawals are possible within the attribution window. In the projection above, a spousal RRSP and an individual RRSP have identical 2026–2032 balances and refunds; the spousal version only improves after-tax wealth if retirement withdrawals are taxed at a lower family rate than the contributor’s own future withdrawals.

## RESP path for each child, 2026–2032

The federal Basic Canada Education Savings Grant pays 20% of annual personal RESP contributions for a qualifying beneficiary, up to CAD 500 per beneficiary per year, or CAD 1,000 if unused grant room from prior years is available, with a CAD 7,200 lifetime CESG limit per beneficiary [3]. Unused CESG room accumulates until the end of the calendar year the child turns 17, and with carry-forward a subscriber can contribute more than CAD 2,500 and receive CESG on up to CAD 5,000 per year, i.e. CAD 1,000 maximum annual CESG [4]. RESP personal contributions have a CAD 50,000 lifetime limit per beneficiary and no annual contribution limit after 2007; CESG, CLB, and provincial benefits do not count toward that CAD 50,000 limit [13].

Assuming **no prior CESG has been received** and each child has unused CESG room, the maximum Basic CESG that can be captured during the seven calendar years 2026–2032 is CAD 7,000 per child, not the full CAD 7,200 lifetime maximum, because the annual catch-up cap is CAD 1,000 per beneficiary per calendar year. For the eldest, this depends on the exact birthdate because CESG is available only until the end of the calendar year the beneficiary turns 17 [3]:

For the younger child, the same 2026–2032 path captures CAD 7,000 by the eldest’s start date; if no prior CESG exists, an additional CAD 1,000 contribution in 2033 would capture the final CAD 200 of Basic CESG, subject to the lifetime limit and eligibility continuing through the year the child turns 17 [4] [3].

Age-16/17 CESG rules should not block this plan if contributions start in 2026: RESPs for beneficiaries age 16 or 17 can receive CESG if, before the end of the year the beneficiary turned 15, either at least CAD 2,000 was contributed and not withdrawn or at least CAD 100 was contributed in any four prior years [3]. The eldest turns 15 around 2030, so the CAD 5,000 annual contributions from 2026 onward satisfy the CAD 2,000-before-age-15-year-end test.

### Additional CESG and Canada Learning Bond eligibility at CAD 95,000

For 2026, Additional CESG pays an extra 20% on the first CAD 500 contribution if adjusted family net income is CAD 0–58,523, an extra 10% if it is greater than CAD 58,523 but not more than CAD 117,045, and nothing above CAD 117,045; Basic CESG remains 20% regardless of income [14]. Adjusted family income for CESG/CLB is the primary caregiver’s line 23600 pre-tax income plus spouse/common-law partner line 23600 income if applicable, minus UCCB/RDSP income received and plus UCCB/RDSP repayments [4]. On the client’s CAD 95,000 income alone and two children, the family appears eligible for the **10% Additional CESG** band in 2026, worth up to **CAD 50 per child per year** on the first CAD 500 contribution, but this cannot be finalized until any spouse/common-law partner income is added [14] [4].

For the Canada Learning Bond, the July 1, 2025–June 30, 2026 threshold for a family with 1 to 3 children is adjusted family income of **CAD 57,375 or less**; at CAD 95,000 with two children, the family is not CLB-eligible, and spouse income would only increase adjusted family income [4].

## RESP front-loading vs even annual contributions

A large front-loaded RESP contribution can compound tax-sheltered sooner, but it does **not** accelerate Basic CESG beyond the annual cap: with unused room, only up to CAD 5,000 of annual contribution per beneficiary earns the 20% Basic CESG, producing at most CAD 1,000 per child per calendar year [4]. Therefore, the recommended RESP funding is **front-load only up to CAD 5,000 per child per year**, not a lump sum far above that amount unless education funding is already secure and RRSP/TFSA priorities are satisfied.

Break-even logic: an unmatched extra RESP contribution made early must earn enough extra compounded return to beat the value of preserving cash for later CESG-eligible contributions and/or RRSP/TFSA use. For example, putting an extra CAD 10,000 into the RESP in 2026 rather than reserving it for CAD 5,000 CESG-eligible contributions in 2027 and 2028 gains one to two years of earlier compounding on the principal, but it does not create additional CESG in 2026; at a 5% nominal after-fee return, one year of early compounding on CAD 5,000 is about CAD 250, while a later CAD 5,000 contribution still earns a CAD 1,000 CESG if unused room exists. The CESG cap dominates under moderate return assumptions unless the family has excess cash after fully funding the annual CAD 5,000-per-child RESP targets, RRSP, TFSA, tax reserve, and emergency fund.

## TFSA role: liquidity and overflow

The 2026 TFSA annual dollar limit is **CAD 7,000** [11]. TFSA room accumulates for Canadian residents from age 18; available room equals the current-year limit plus unused prior room plus prior-year withdrawals minus current-year contributions, withdrawals are tax-free, and withdrawn amounts are added back to room on January 1 of the next calendar year [7]. If the client was a Canadian resident and age 18+ for all TFSA years and made no TFSA contributions, cumulative room through 2026 would be **CAD 109,000**, but actual room must be confirmed in CRA My Account because prior contributions and withdrawals change it [7].

The TFSA is therefore the best registered account for **emergency-fund overflow**: unlike an RRSP, withdrawals do not create taxable income; unlike an RESP, withdrawals are not tied to education-beneficiary rules; and room returns the following calendar year. The first six months of essential expenses should still be in cash or cash-equivalent investments, not volatile long-term holdings.

## Self-employed cash-flow reserve: tax, CPP, EI

Because the client is self-employed with irregular income, the first “investment” account is a tax/CPP reserve. CRA generally requires individual tax instalments when net tax owing for the current year will exceed CAD 3,000 and net tax owing exceeded CAD 3,000 in either of the two prior years; instalments are due March 15, June 15, September 15, and December 15 [15].

CPP is also material. Every person over 18 who works in Canada outside Quebec and earns more than CAD 3,500 must contribute to CPP; employees split contributions with employers, but self-employed people pay the whole contribution [16]. For 2026, YMPE is CAD 74,600, the year’s basic exemption is CAD 3,500, the self-employed base plus first additional CPP rate is 11.90% with a maximum of CAD 8,460.90, and CPP2 applies between YMPE and YAMPE of CAD 85,000 at 8.00% self-employed with a maximum of CAD 832.00; at CAD 95,000 of earnings the combined maximum self-employed CPP/CPP2 is **CAD 9,292.90** before tax deductions/credits [9] [10].

Optional EI is relevant only as special-benefits insurance, not unemployment protection. Canada.ca says self-employed people can voluntarily participate for EI special benefits such as maternity, parental, sickness, family caregiver, and compassionate care; the 2026 benefit rate can be up to 55% of earnings to a maximum of CAD 729 per week, but this is not regular EI for lack of freelance work [17].

## Automation dates and irregular-income waterfall

RESP/CESG is calendar-year based: beneficiaries qualify on contributions made up to the end of the calendar year they turn 17, so the practical deadline is a posted RESP contribution by **December 31** each year [3]. RRSP contributions made in the year or within 60 days after year-end can be deducted for that tax year; for the 2026 tax year, the 60-day deadline is **Monday, March 1, 2027** [6] [12].

Use this automation schedule; if actual after-tax surplus is lower than these targets, the **waterfall order**, not the full-dollar target, governs:

| Monthly/receipt layer | Target amount | Trigger and deadline |
|---|---:|---|
| 1. Tax/CPP reserve | On every client payment, set aside the amount needed for CRA instalments and expected self-employed CPP before investing | CRA instalments are due Mar. 15, Jun. 15, Sep. 15, and Dec. 15 when the net-tax-owing test is met [1] |
| 2. Emergency-fund shortfall | Fill to **6 × actual monthly essential expenses** before long-term investing beyond current-year CESG capture | FCAC says emergency savings should be accessible quickly, withdrawable without penalty, and ideally 3–6 months of regular expenses [2] |
| 3. RESP | **CAD 833.34/month total**, split CAD 416.67 per child, or lump-sum top-ups to CAD 5,000 per child by Dec. 31 | Captures up to CAD 1,000 Basic CESG per child per year where unused room exists [4] |
| 4. RRSP/spousal RRSP | Target equivalent to **CAD 17,100/year** = CAD 1,425/month, if room exists | Use monthly automation only after layers 1–3 are on track; otherwise make a February/top-up contribution before the 60-day RRSP deadline [6] [12] |
| 5. TFSA | **CAD 7,000/year** = CAD 583.33/month, plus RRSP refunds and overflow if room exists | Use for flexible, tax-free surplus after the above layers; TFSA withdrawals create room again the next January [7] |

If cash flow is below target in a given month, use this fallback order: (1) tax/CPP reserve and essential bills; (2) emergency fund until it reaches 6 × monthly essential expenses; (3) RESP contributions required to capture the current year’s CESG, preferably by December 31; (4) RRSP/spousal RRSP contributions before the 60-day deadline; (5) TFSA overflow. If a large receivable is paid late, use annual lump-sum top-ups: RESP before December 31, then RRSP before the following 60-day deadline.

## Fallback strategies

- **No spouse or no lower-income spouse:** use the individual RRSP; the up-front deduction is identical, and the spousal RRSP only adds value if later withdrawals can be taxed at a lower spouse rate after the attribution window.
- **Spouse has similar/higher future retirement income or relationship uncertainty:** prefer individual RRSP and TFSA; avoid creating spousal-plan attribution and ownership complexity.
- **Near-term RRSP withdrawal risk:** avoid spousal RRSP contributions because withdrawals in the contribution year or two preceding calendar years can attribute back to the contributor under ITA s.146(8.3) [6].
- **RRSP room is insufficient:** after funding RESP CESG targets, use TFSA for flexible retirement/education overflow.
- **Emergency fund is underfunded:** pause RRSP and TFSA investing except the minimum RESP funding needed to preserve CESG matching; rebuild cash first.
- **Irregular income causes shortfalls:** do not abandon the plan; use December RESP top-ups and February RRSP top-ups to convert variable freelance cash flow into annual targets.

Under the stated assumptions, the strategy that maximizes 2032 after-tax wealth is not pure RRSP or pure RESP front-loading. It is the ordered combination: cash reserve and emergency liquidity first, **CAD 5,000 per child per year to RESP**, RRSP/spousal RRSP contributions up to the room and tax-rate level justified by expected retirement income, and TFSA for refunds and flexible overflow.
