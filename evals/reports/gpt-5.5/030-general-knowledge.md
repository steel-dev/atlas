# Vertical integration in cocoa processing, 2018–2024: Barry Callebaut, Cargill Cocoa & Chocolate and ofi/Olam

## Executive findings

The evidence supports a clear hierarchy in **disclosed vertical-integration depth**, but not a complete year-by-year public ledger of “direct-sourced versus broker-purchased” cocoa for all three processors. All three were described during 2018–2024 as among the largest global cocoa/chocolate processors. A 2022 Cocoa Barometer table of “Traders and Processors: Used Cocoa 2021” put ofi at **1.019 million tonnes**, Barry Callebaut at **988,000 tonnes** and Cargill at **875,000 tonnes** of cocoa-bean equivalents, the three largest figures in that table [1]. Company disclosures are consistent with that top-tier status: Cargill called itself the “second-largest producer of cocoa and chocolate globally” in its 2024 cocoa impact report, and Olam Group’s 2024 annual report described ofi as a “top three” global cocoa processor [2][3].

Across 2018–2024, their integration strategies diverged as follows:

1. **Barry Callebaut** combined the broadest downstream chocolate-manufacturing outsourcing model with deep origin engagement through Forever Chocolate, Cocoa Horizons, Farm Services, direct cooperative contracting and farm mapping. Its disclosure is strong on mapped farms, certified/verified product share, farmer-support activities and customer programs, but it does **not** publish a full annual percentage split of total cocoa sourced directly versus through third-party/broker channels. It disclosed that its cocoa farmer database covered **80% of its direct cocoa supply chain**, and that in 2023/24 it had mapped **669,174 cocoa farm plots**, enabling farm-level traceability for cocoa from mapped farms [4].
2. **Cargill Cocoa & Chocolate** disclosed the clearest direct/indirect split: its externally assured Cargill Cocoa Promise scope is “part of our direct supply chain” and accounts for **about one third of the cocoa we source**, while it directly sources in six origins and indirectly in 19 additional origins [2]. That implies roughly two-thirds of sourced cocoa sits outside the direct Cocoa Promise scope, but Cargill does not publish a precise broker-purchased tonnage by year. Its competitive strength is operational scale plus a highly quantified EUDR-relevant traceability stack across the Promise supply chain.
3. **ofi/Olam** disclosed the strongest proportional direct-sourcing position in the sources retrieved: Olam Cocoa reported **100% traceability across its direct global supply chain** and described direct sourcing as **approximately two-thirds of global cocoa supply**, with indirect/conventional sourcing making up the balance [5][6]. In 2024, ofi was still described as a top-three global cocoa processor and was explicitly “well-placed” for EUDR obligations through traceable chain-of-custody information, technologies and field assessments [3].

For premium chocolate customers, these strategies create **different advantages rather than one uniform winner**. Barry Callebaut has the clearest advantage in large-scale chocolate manufacturing outsourcing and custom sustainable-chocolate solutions, including documented ties to Mondelēz and named customer-linked living-income and labor-group programs [4]. ofi has a comparative advantage where customers want traceable cocoa ingredients and origin-to-ingredient transparency, including a documented Mondelēz collaboration and the AtSource/Cocoa Compass data architecture [7][8]. Cargill is at least at parity for large confectionery customers that require scale, certified/verified cocoa and EUDR-ready due diligence, but its lower disclosed direct-sourcing share compared with ofi means more reliance on indirect-channel assurance and supplier data quality controls [2][9]. Lindt is a partial exception: it emphasizes its own Farming Program and in-house cocoa-mass production, so the processors’ premium opportunity is more likely in cocoa butter, powder, specialty ingredients or overflow/ingredient supply than in replacing Lindt’s core bean-to-mass model [10][11].

## Direct sourcing versus broker/indirect sourcing

Public disclosure is not standardized. The companies use different scopes: Barry Callebaut reports “direct cocoa supply chain” metrics but not total direct share; Cargill reports that Cocoa Promise is about one-third of sourced cocoa; ofi/Olam reports direct supply chain traceability and, in the Cocoa Compass evidence, a direct supply chain equal to about two-thirds of global supply.

Barry Callebaut may still have very deep direct cooperative engagement in absolute scale, but that is not the same as a disclosed percentage of total sourced cocoa.

## Competitive implications for premium chocolate and confectionery customers

Premium chocolate and confectionery customers increasingly need four things from cocoa processors: (1) assured supply in a volatile cocoa market; (2) certified/verified or customer-program cocoa; (3) traceability compatible with EUDR and corporate no-deforestation claims; and (4) credible farmer-livelihood programming to support brand commitments. The processors’ vertical-integration choices affect each of those dimensions differently.

### Barry Callebaut: advantage in outsourced chocolate manufacturing and sustainable chocolate solutions

Barry Callebaut’s strongest competitive position is not simply bean sourcing; it is the combination of sourcing, processing and **contract chocolate manufacturing**. The retrieved sources show it positioning Forever Chocolate as a customer-facing sustainable-solutions platform and explicitly linking its labor-group expansion to Mondelēz International, Ben & Jerry’s and Cocoa Horizons customers [4]. It also discloses digital premium payments to more than 60,000 West African farmers and three-year contracts with 90% of directly sourced Côte d’Ivoire cooperatives serving customer-specific commitments [4].

This is strategically valuable for premium and branded chocolate manufacturers because it allows Barry Callebaut to offer not only cocoa liquor, butter or powder, but finished or semi-finished chocolate ingredients tied to verified/certified cocoa and customer sustainability programs. Its 55.5% share of products containing 100% verified or certified cocoa/chocolate is broadly comparable to Cargill’s 54% sustainable cocoa volumes sold [4][2]. The disadvantage is disclosure: because Barry Callebaut does not publish a total direct-versus-broker sourcing split, customers requiring fully segregated, farm-level EUDR documentation may need contract-specific data rather than rely on group-level disclosure.

### Cargill: scale and compliance parity, but lower disclosed direct-sourcing share

Cargill’s position is strongest where a customer needs large global supply, multiple origins, processing capacity and rigorous compliance systems. It directly sources from six origins but also has 19 indirect origins, so its network is broader than the Cocoa Promise direct-supply scope [2]. Cargill’s 2024 data are highly relevant to EUDR buyers: 99.9% of Promise farms polygon mapped in Côte d’Ivoire, 96.0% in Ghana, 100% direct-supply traceability to first purchase point and satellite/GIS/Meridia assurance controls [2][9]. Its disclosed origin investment—US$113 million for Côte d’Ivoire/Ghana expansions and US$13 million at Tema—also supports reliable origin processing and proximity to bean supply [15][16].

The relative disadvantage is that Cargill’s assured Cocoa Promise direct supply is only about one-third of cocoa sourced [2]. For premium, segregated, low-deforestation contracts, Cargill can compete strongly within its Promise and EUDR-ready supply; for broader commodity volumes, customers may face more indirect-supply assurance complexity.

### ofi/Olam: advantage in traceable cocoa ingredients and origin-to-customer transparency

ofi/Olam’s clearest competitive advantage is in traceable cocoa ingredients supported by Cocoa Compass and AtSource-type transparency. Its direct supply chain was reported as fully traceable and monitored for deforestation by 2020, and the direct-sourcing share was approximately two-thirds of global supply in the Cocoa Compass evidence [5][6]. Its 2024 messaging explicitly ties transparency, traceability, deforestation mapping, child-labor monitoring, AI-powered carbon tools and customer data needs to upcoming European regulation [8][3].

This positions ofi well for customers that want cocoa ingredients rather than outsourced chocolate manufacturing. Mondelēz and ofi announced a 2021 collaboration in Indonesia to create a 2,000-hectare sustainable commercial cocoa farm, with Mondelēz described as a leading chocolate maker and ofi as a leading supplier of cocoa beans and cocoa ingredients [7]. Although that project is outside West Africa, it demonstrates ofi’s customer-facing model: joint supply-chain transformation with major confectionery companies. In West Africa, ofi’s advantage is a high direct-sourcing proportion and a living-income analytics claim, not a retrieved in-period Ghana/Côte d’Ivoire capex figure.

### Lindt, Ferrero and Mondelēz: what the sources support

- **Mondelēz:** The strongest retrieved evidence links Mondelēz to Barry Callebaut and ofi. Barry Callebaut names Mondelēz International as a key partner in labor-group activities in West Africa [4]. Mondelēz and ofi publicly announced a 2021 sustainable cocoa collaboration, describing ofi as a leading supplier of cocoa beans and ingredients [7]. A traceability case study explains that Mondelēz’s tier-1 suppliers are couverture producers or bean exporters and tier-2 suppliers are cooperatives, underscoring why processors with digital traceability are strategically important [22].
- **Ferrero:** Ferrero’s Cocoa Charter reporting emphasizes responsible sourcing, supplier-list transparency and progress against livelihoods, human rights, environmental protection and supplier-transparency pillars [23]. The retrieved source establishes Ferrero’s demand profile but does not identify a specific 2018–2024 Barry Callebaut/Cargill/ofi contract. Therefore, the competitive implication is inferential: all three processors’ certification and traceability systems are relevant to Ferrero-type procurement, but the retrieved evidence does not prove contract wins or losses.
- **Lindt & Sprüngli:** Lindt’s 2024 sustainability report says it is among the few chocolate manufacturers that produce their own cocoa mass from the beans it sources, allowing it to oversee the process closely [10]. Its responsible-sourcing report prioritizes strategic cocoa suppliers, supplier codes and responsible sourcing across priority materials [11]. This means processor advantage with Lindt is likely narrower than with Mondelēz: compliance-ready cocoa ingredients may matter, but Lindt’s core premium positioning rests heavily on its own bean sourcing and in-house mass production.

## Overall assessment

The three processors converged on the same destination—traceable, certified/verified, deforestation-controlled cocoa—but from different starting points:

- **Best disclosed direct-sourcing depth:** ofi/Olam, because the retrieved evidence gives a direct-supply share of about two-thirds and 100% direct-supply traceability/deforestation monitoring [5][6].
- **Best quantified 2024 EUDR mapping disclosure:** Cargill, because it publishes country-level polygon-mapping percentages for Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana and explicit exclusion criteria for unmapped/deforested farms, even though it does not disclose excluded volumes [2][9].
- **Best downstream premium chocolate-manufacturing position:** Barry Callebaut, because its model integrates origin sustainability, cocoa processing and large-scale chocolate solutions/customer programs, with named links to Mondelēz, Ben & Jerry’s and Cocoa Horizons customers [4].
- **Best origin-country capex evidence retrieved:** Cargill, because it disclosed US$113 million in Côte d’Ivoire/Ghana site expansions and a US$13 million Tema expansion [15][16].
- **Best living-income outcome disclosure:** ofi/Olam, because it disclosed 30,168 farmers earning a living income, although not all farmers and not a Ghana/Côte d’Ivoire-only baseline/endline series [8].

The major caveat is disclosure asymmetry. The sources establish strong programmatic evidence, but they do not support a fully comparable annual 2018–2024 table of direct versus broker-purchased cocoa, capex by facility for all three companies, or farmer-income deltas for all Côte d’Ivoire and Ghana programs. Where the data are missing, the absence is itself analytically important: buyers may receive more detailed contract-level data than is public, but public investors and civil-society reviewers cannot fully compare the three processors on direct-sourcing share, farmer-income impact or non-compliant volume exclusions from public reports alone.
