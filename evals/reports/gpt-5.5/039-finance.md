**Bottom line:** Premium Resources’ December 2024 equity deterioration was not just a non-cash accounting swing: shareholders’ equity fell by **CAD $13,192,746**, or **135.37%**, from **CAD $9,745,686** at December 31, 2023 to a **CAD $3,447,060 deficiency** at December 31, 2024, while the company disclosed recurring losses, no operating cash flow and going-concern uncertainty [1]. A March 2025 financing restored working capital by June 30, 2025 and March 31, 2026, but management still stated that additional financing would be needed for Botswana acquisition commitments and later advancement work; therefore the risk is **near-term refinancing urgency tied to asset payments and development timetable**, not merely reported losses [2] [3]. The warrant overhang is **severe** for a junior miner: **23,464,096 warrants** versus **35,512,606 common shares** equals **66.07% of current shares**, or **39.79% of the post-exercise share base** [4] [3].

## Equity erosion and refinancing urgency

Premium Resources’ audited equity deteriorated from **CAD $9,745,686** at December 31, 2023 to a **CAD $(3,447,060)** shareholders’ deficiency at December 31, 2024, a **CAD -$13,192,746** change, or **-135.37%** [1]. That deterioration coincided with the 2024 Form 10-K going-concern disclosure: the company reported recurring net losses, **no operating cash flows**, a **CAD $42,420,283** net loss for 2024, uncertainty over the success of future financings, and material uncertainties that cast substantial doubt on its ability to continue as a going concern; the same annual filing stated that development of material projects would require additional equity, debt or project-level debt financing [1].

The financing urgency was partly relieved, but not eliminated, by the March 2025 financing. At June 30, 2025, Premium Resources had **CAD $26,463,957** cash and **CAD $23,875,340** working capital, compared with **CAD $6,105,933** cash and **CAD $3,410,490** working capital at December 31, 2024, because the company had closed a March 2025 private placement; however, the June 2025 filing still described the properties as pre-revenue, stated that the company depended on external financing, had no source of operating cash flows and no significant credit lines, and said the proceeds were expected to fund planned activities and administrative costs only into **Q1 2026** [2]. Management specifically stated that the **CAD $34,107,500 / US$25,000,000** second Selebi APA instalment due **January 31, 2026** could require additional funding because the company did not then have sufficient funds to meet that obligation, and that material uncertainties continued to cast substantial doubt on going-concern status [2].

By March 31, 2026, cash remained **CAD $26,220,756** and working capital **CAD $26,519,278**, but the accumulated deficit had increased to **CAD $(216,696,861)** and Q1 2026 net loss was **CAD $10,623,437** [3]. The March 2026 liquidity update said the working-capital position was sufficient to advance planned activities, including completion of a PEA for Selebi, and cover administration into **Q4 2026**, while also stating that Premium Resources intended to pursue financing alternatives before year-end 2026 to support continued advancement of Selebi and Selkirk toward development; going-concern material uncertainties remained [3]. The correct conclusion is therefore: the December 2024 equity erosion signaled a real refinancing problem, but the timing shifted after the 2025 financing from immediate balance-sheet survival to funding the acquisition milestone and the 2026 technical/development program.

## Warrant dilution and capital-structure risk

Using the latest issuer share-structure date located, Premium Resources/NexMetals reported **35,512,606** common shares issued and outstanding as of **June 17, 2026** [4]. The warrant overhang totals **23,464,096** warrants, with exercise prices from **CAD $8.00** to **CAD $28.75**, expiries from **June 28, 2026** to **June 21, 2029**, and a **CAD $9.84** weighted-average exercise price in the March 31, 2026 Form 10-Q warrant table [3]. The major warrant lines were **301,200** at **CAD $28.75** expiring June 28, 2026; **1,012,981** at **CAD $22.00** expiring June 14, 2029; **801,089** at **CAD $22.00** expiring June 21, 2029; **3,833,334** at **CAD $11.00** expiring March 18, 2028; **3,480,392** at **CAD $8.00** expiring March 18, 2028; and **14,035,100** tradeable warrants at **CAD $8.00** expiring November 17, 2027 [4] [3].

The dilution math is direct:

| Dilution convention | Calculation | Result |
|---|---:|---:|
| Warrants / current shares | 23,464,096 / 35,512,606 | **66.07%** |
| Warrants / shares plus warrants | 23,464,096 / (35,512,606 + 23,464,096) | **39.79%** |

For a junior mining issuer, this warrant-only calculation is **severe**, not modest, and it is the requested metric: full warrant exercise would increase the common share count by roughly two-thirds. The broader capital-structure overhang is larger when non-warrant equity-linked instruments are included: the June 17, 2026 issuer share-structure disclosure also listed **920,871 options**, **487,640 RSUs**, **90,887 DSUs**, **657 preferred shares**, and an issuer-stated fully diluted total of **60,612,315** securities [4]. The effect depends on moneyness. In-the-money **CAD $8.00** and **CAD $11.00** warrants could provide substantial cash if exercised, making them a potential financing source, but they also cap upside because new shares would be issued at fixed prices below the market price that triggered exercise. Out-of-the-money **CAD $22.00–$28.75** warrants provide no near-term cash unless the share price rises materially; until then they remain an overhang that can pressure valuation without solving the company’s funding needs [3].

## Botswana assets, obligations and contingent payments

Premium Resources’ Botswana project scope consists of the **Selebi Mines** and the **Selkirk Mine**. The Selebi Mines include **Selebi / #2 Shaft** and **Selebi North / #4 Shaft**, with associated infrastructure, under a single **11,504-hectare mining licence 2022/1L** expiring **May 26, 2032**; Premium Resources acquired the Selebi Mines and current Selebi mining lease from the **BCL Limited liquidator** on **January 31, 2022** through indirect wholly owned **Premium Nickel Resources Proprietary Ltd.** [2] [1]. Premium Resources acquired the **Selkirk Mine** in **August 2022** through indirect wholly owned **Premium Nickel Group Proprietary Ltd.** out of liquidation, in the Tati Nickel/BCL liquidation context [2] [1].

As of June 30, 2025, the near-term cash obligations disclosed in the commitments and contractual-obligations notes were:

| Obligation category, June 30, 2025 | Due within 12 months | Components / treatment |
|---|---:|---|
| Committed capital expenditures | **CAD $7,180** | Capital-expenditure commitments over the next 12 months [2]. |
| Contractual obligations table | **CAD $4,993,101** | **CAD $4,812,152** trade payables and accrued liabilities plus **CAD $180,949** vehicle financing [2]. |
| Total contractual obligations, all maturities | **CAD $5,447,045** | Same table across less than 1 year, 1–2 years and 2–5 years [2]. |
| DSU liability | **CAD $1,120,153 excluded** | Excluded because management considered allocation into maturity groupings impractical [2]. |
| Selebi second instalment | **CAD $34,107,500 / US$25,000,000 due Jan. 31, 2026**, excluded from the contractual-obligations table because unrecognized | Due on the earlier of Botswana MMRGTES approval of Section 42/43 applications and January 31, 2026; management said it might require additional funding because the company did not currently have sufficient funds [2]. |

Combining the disclosed near-term obligation stack as of June 30, 2025, **CAD $7,180** of committed capital expenditures plus **CAD $4,993,101** of contractual obligations due within one year equaled **CAD $5,000,281**; adding the excluded **CAD $34,107,500 / US$25,000,000** Selebi second instalment gives **CAD $39,107,781** of indicative near-term cash requirements. That was above June 30, 2025 cash of **CAD $26,463,957** by about **CAD $12.64 million**, and above working capital of **CAD $23,875,340** by about **CAD $15.23 million**, before ongoing exploration, development and administrative burn [2].

The BCL-linked fixed acquisition milestones are not **US$20.0 million**. The Selebi APA aggregate purchase price is **US$56.75 million** in three instalments: **US$1.75 million** payable on closing, already paid; **US$25.0 million** due on the earlier of MMRGTES approval of the company’s Section 42 and Section 43 applications and **January 31, 2026**; and **US$30.0 million** due on completion of mine construction and production start-up by **January 31, 2030**, but no later than four years after Section 42/43 approval [2]. The remaining fixed milestone/installment payments are therefore **US$55.0 million**, not US$20.0 million, and were not accrued at June 30, 2025 because the triggering conditions had not been met and the company had an option to cancel the second and third payments and return Selebi to the liquidator if it determined the mines were uneconomic [2].

The other BCL-linked contingent consideration is variable rather than a fixed aggregate cash amount. For Selebi, it consists of a sliding-scale payment of **US$0.50/t to US$1.40/t of ore** for discovery of new mineable deposits greater than **25 million tonnes** from a **15.9 million tonne** base case at a minimum **2.5% nickel-equivalent** grade, accrued at the decision to mine, plus **15%** of post-tax net earnings directly attributable to a commodity-price increase of **25% or more**, calculated quarterly for seven years from first shipment of concentrate or other materials [2]. Selkirk has similar contingent consideration, while the assets also carry royalties: a **2% Selebi NSR** with a 50% buyback right and a **1% Selkirk NSR** with a full buyback right [2]. The **US$20.0 million** figure in the filings is the optional price to repurchase **50% of the Selebi 2% NSR**, not a fixed BCL milestone payment; the corresponding Selkirk NSR buyback right is **US$2.0 million**, and Cymbria paid **CAD $2.75 million** for an option to participate in those NSR repurchases [2] [1].

## Development timetable and funding implications

As of the June 2025 interim MD&A, the company’s near-term Botswana work program was still cash-consuming. It disclosed key milestones and estimated costs through **September 30, 2025**, including Selebi and Selkirk drill programs of **CAD $3.3 million–$3.7 million**, Selebi underground development of **CAD $0.6 million–$0.9 million**, and ongoing capital expenditures; it had incurred **CAD $14,614,458** on Selebi exploration and evaluation in the six months ended June 30, 2025 and **CAD $1,705,673** on Selkirk over the same period [2]. The company also reported project-to-date exploration and evaluation expenditures, after acquisition costs, of **CAD $83,911,034** for Selebi and **CAD $4,764,175** for Selkirk at June 30, 2025 [2].

The latest development/work-program timeline located after June 2025 is the January 15, 2026 strategy update. For **Selebi**, the 2026 program includes borehole electromagnetic surveying and approximately **30,000 metres** of Selebi Main surface drilling for resource expansion, Selebi North underground development to improve access for future infill drilling needed for economic studies, continuing metallurgical flowsheet optimization, an updated Selebi mineral resource estimate planned for **H2 2026**, and a Selebi preliminary economic assessment planned for **H2 2026** [5] [6]. For **Selkirk**, the 2026 program includes continued metallurgical testing to support a resource update and future economic studies, a Selkirk mineral resource estimate update planned for **H1 2026**, and Selkirk PEA timing contingent on ongoing technical results [5] [6]. A September 2025 Selebi metallurgy release also stated that separate saleable copper and nickel-cobalt concentrates could reduce capital intensity by removing the potential need for an on-site smelter or hydrometallurgical facility, but that statement supports a lower-capital restart concept rather than eliminating the need for development financing [7].

Taken together, the financial pressures point to a constrained funding path:

- **Liquidity covers ordinary near-term work, not all obligations.** June 30, 2025 working capital of **CAD $23.875 million** was less than the excluded **CAD $34.108 million / US$25.0 million** Selebi instalment due January 31, 2026, even before the **CAD $4.993 million** of contractual obligations due within one year and the ongoing exploration/development burn [2].
- **The 2025 financing bought time, but did not remove refinancing risk.** By March 31, 2026, working capital of **CAD $26.519 million** was expected to fund planned activities, including a Selebi PEA, into **Q4 2026**, but management still planned to pursue financing alternatives before year-end 2026 and continued to report going-concern material uncertainty [3].
- **The BCL payment stack is lumpy and project-timing dependent.** Remaining fixed Selebi payments total **US$55.0 million**, with **US$25.0 million** tied to licence approval or January 31, 2026 and **US$30.0 million** tied to construction completion/production start-up by January 31, 2030; if Selkirk were developed first, the same second and third Selebi instalments could be triggered by Selkirk licence approval and commissioning [2].
- **Warrants can help only at the right share price.** The **CAD $8.00** and **CAD $11.00** warrants could supply cash if exercised, but full exercise would dilute existing holders by **39.79%** of the post-exercise base; higher-strike warrants do not fund the business unless the equity trades materially above their exercise prices [4] [3].

**Conclusion:** Premium Resources can fund a defined portion of operations and technical work with the post-financing cash base, but it cannot prudently be viewed as able to meet all Botswana development and acquisition-timeline requirements without additional capital. The December 2024 equity deficiency, the June 2025 disclosure that current funds were insufficient for the US$25.0 million Selebi instalment, the severe warrant overhang, and the H1/H2 2026 MRE/PEA timetable together indicate that the company will likely need new equity, warrant-exercise proceeds, debt, project-level financing, offtake/prepay funding, an asset-level transaction, or a deferral/renegotiation of timelines to keep Selebi and Selkirk on schedule [2] [1] [3] [5].
