# Maternal-health indicators and delivery models: Cox’s Bazar Rohingya camps vs. Mae La camp, 2017–2023

## Bottom line

The evidence supports a clear qualitative comparison but not the fully specified 2017–2023 annual indicator panel requested. Cox’s Bazar has response-wide maternal-death surveillance from 2020 onward and Health Sector 4W/HMIS indicators for 2022–2023; Mae La has a usable UNHCR iRHIS site profile for 2018 and broader Thailand-country/camp-literature data, but not a public 2017–2023 Mae La annual series for pregnancies, live births, maternal deaths, postpartum hemorrhage, or ANC4/SBA. On the comparable figures that are available, Mae La’s 2018 maternal-death count was 0 with 95% skilled birth attendance and 6 neonatal deaths per 1,000 live births, whereas a Cox’s Bazar site profile for Kutupalong New in 2018 showed 2 maternal deaths, 83% skilled birth attendance, and 22 neonatal deaths per 1,000 live births; these are not like-for-like because Kutupalong New is only one site inside the Rohingya response [1] [2].

## 2. ANC4+, skilled birth attendance, postpartum hemorrhage, and neonatal mortality

### Antenatal-care completion

Cox’s Bazar has a clearly grounded 2023 ANC4 figure: the Jan 2024 Health Sector bulletin defines the indicator as “Antenatal care coverage — at least four visits (%) — Rohingya,” measured as a percentage “against women with live births,” with a target ≥80%; it reports 75% for 2023 and 82% for Jan 2024 [8]. The Dec 2023 Health Sector bulletin provides the 2023 numerator for mothers with ANC4 or above visits: 26,008 total, with monthly counts from Jan–Dec 2023 of 2,048, 1,881, 2,193, 1,778, 2,432, 2,161, 2,494, 2,353, 2,015, 2,199, 2,268, and 2,186; the same table labels this as “75% ANC4 for Rohingya” [10].

Mae La’s iRHIS 2018 site profile reports ANC coverage of 91% against a UNHCR standard >90%, but the extracted profile does not print the numerator, denominator, or whether it is exactly ANC4 rather than the profile’s broader “ANC coverage” label [1]. The best contextual Karen-camp series comes from Mae La Oon and Mae Ra Ma Luang, where 900–1,600 pregnant women per year were recorded and 93.3%–98.8% attended at least four ANC visits across 2000–2018; those camps are not Mae La, so this is model-context evidence rather than Mae La site-year evidence [11].

### Skilled birth attendance

Cox’s Bazar reports 85% of births assisted by a skilled attendant in 2023, with the Jan 2024 bulletin defining the indicator as “Percentage of births assisted by a skilled attendant (Facility-based delivery),” percentage of births, target ≥80% [8]. The Health Sector Strategic Plan baseline defines skilled birth attendance as births attended by “skilled personnel (doctors, nurses, midwives)” and records a 70% baseline with a target >80% [12]. The Dec 2023 Health Sector bulletin lists 1,277 average skilled birth attendants in 2023, composed of 37% medical doctors, 27% nurses, and 36% midwives [10].

Mae La iRHIS reports 95% skilled birth attendance in 2018, defined as “of all births,” and the site profile’s staffing table lists 3 doctors, 72 qualified nurses, and 64 community health workers [1]. The 2017 Thailand country iRHIS report gives a cross-site skilled-birth-attendance range of 75%–100%, not a Mae La-specific annual value [7]. The Thai-border camp study reports that skilled and unskilled birth attendants both continued to conduct births; deliveries by unskilled attendants at home in 2018 were 20.3% in Mae Ra Ma Luang and 14.7% in Mae La Oon, while caesarean sections averaged 3.6%–3.7% of total deliveries [11].

### Postpartum hemorrhage

The requested annual PPH incidence — cases per 100 deliveries with numerator and denominator — is not published in the cited Cox’s Bazar or Mae La public sources. Cox’s Bazar does ground hemorrhage as a major mortality cause: in 2023, obstetric hemorrhage accounted for 20 of 57 maternal deaths, 35%, and hypertensive disorders accounted for 19 of 57, 33% [6]. This is a mortality-cause distribution, not incidence among deliveries. The 2025 WHO Cox’s Bazar feature also describes training in postpartum-hemorrhage management at the WHO-supported Multidisciplinary Skills Lab at Cox’s Bazar Medical College, but does not state PPH incidence [9].

### Neonatal mortality within 28 days

For Cox’s Bazar, the response-wide 2017–2023 annual neonatal mortality series is not published in the cited public reports. The Kutupalong New 2018 UNHCR iRHIS site profile reports neonatal mortality of 22 deaths per 1,000 live births and infant mortality of 48.9 per 1,000 live births [2]. The Cox’s Bazar MPMSR 2023 report states that perinatal-death surveillance began in August 2023; therefore its 304 perinatal deaths are only for Aug–Dec 2023, and 38% of verbal-autopsy-classified perinatal deaths were early neonatal deaths [6]. A Sept 2023 Health Sector bulletin states that stillbirths and neonatal deaths accounted for about 20% of deaths recorded through mortality surveillance Jan–Sept 2023, and that infant mortality Jan–Aug 2023 was 25.2 per 1,000 live births and under-5 mortality 28.4 per 1,000 live births; it does not give a neonatal numerator/denominator [20]. WHO’s later feature reports perinatal mortality, not neonatal mortality: 2022=16.55, 2023=10.69, 2024=17.90 per 1,000 live births [9].

For Mae La, the 2018 iRHIS site profile reports neonatal mortality of 6 per 1,000 live births and infant mortality of 6.2 per 1,000 live births, without printing neonatal-death numerator or live-birth denominator in the extracted text [1]. The Thai-border camp study reports that in Mae La Oon and Mae Ra Ma Luang, neonatal mortality declined from 23.5 per 1,000 in 2000 to 7.7 per 1,000 in 2018, infant mortality from 40 to 10.1 per 1,000, and perinatal mortality from 32.5 to 16.9 per 1,000 [11].

## 3. Delivery models and emergency obstetric access

### Cox’s Bazar model: NGO/UN humanitarian field network plus government referral system

Cox’s Bazar operated as a Health Sector-coordinated humanitarian service network rather than a single-provider system. In Q1 2022, the Health Sector reported 98 partners, 44 primary health centres, 90 health posts, and 32 BEmONC facilities; it also recorded 6,076 facility-based deliveries, 5,943 facility live births, 222 facility stillbirths, and 17 maternal deaths during the quarter [13]. In Q2 2022, it reported 98 partners, 46 PHCs, 93 health posts, 32 BEmONC facilities, 5,997 facility births, and 12 maternal deaths [14]. By Aug 2022, the bulletin listed 93 health posts, 46 PHCs, 32 BEmONC facilities, 3 CEmONC centres, and 5 secondary-care facilities, with 1,745 facility-based births in that month and 75% health-facility births [15]. In Dec 2023, the Health Sector listed 77 partners, 67 health posts, 47 PHCs, 1 facility with CEmONC services, 8 secondary-care facilities, 507 doctors, 345 nurses, and 466 midwives [10].

The Cox’s Bazar model included referral pathways and government capacity: the 2021 bulletin states that the emergency obstetric and neonatal care referral pathway was revised after the 2020 MPMSR review, and that only 24 of 42 PHCs — 57% — provided all BEmONC signal functions, despite the Minimum Essential Service Package expectation that all PHCs provide BEmONC [19]. The 2022 bulletins record Health Sector referral SOP approval and repeated tracking of BEmONC/CEmONC facility counts [15]. The strategic plan describes the system in terms of health posts, PHCs, beds, skilled birth attendants, 4W/DHIS2 reporting, and SRH Working Group maternal-death reporting [12].

Named facility/provider evidence in the cited sources is partial but concrete for several actors. The Q1 2022 bulletin says the existing camp network included “more than 90 Health Posts, 44 PHCs, and 4 secondary healthcare facilities,” naming MSF, Friendship, Turkish, and Hope Field Hospitals [13]. The Q2 2022 bulletin states that Health Posts, PHCs, and Field Hospitals providing critical lifesaving services were most affected by funding gaps [14]. The Aug 2022 bulletin adds that MSF opened two dedicated scabies clinics at PHC Jamtoli and Hakimpara in March 2022, showing MSF’s named PHC footprint in the camps, though this example is not obstetric-specific [15]. The Dec 2023 bulletin names a UNICEF-supported Partners in Health and Development PHC in Camp 16 and reports UNHCR handover of two PHCs to BRAC [10]. The cited passages do not fully enumerate the Cox’s Bazar District Hospital/Ukhiya-Teknaf obstetric referral hospital chain, so those referral hospitals should not be treated here as a complete grounded inventory.

### Mae La model: camp-based providers with Thai referral links, not full Thai MOPH integration

The premise that Mae La was simply integrated into a Thai Ministry of Public Health model is too strong. The available evidence describes camp-based health services delivered by humanitarian/camp providers, with referral access to Thai hospitals and reporting links to Thai public-health authorities. A Mae La health-services study states that camp OPD and inpatient departments were supported and equipped by different actors, free of charge to camp and non-camp patients, with referrals to district hospitals for surgical operations, radiology, specialist consultations, and tests not available in the camp laboratory [16]. It also reports that Mae La camp health workers could speak Karen and Burmese and that the camp functioned as a basic health-care system with versatile health workers, complementary to Thai state facilities for populations with limited host-system access [16].

For the Thai-border Karen camps studied by the 2024 MCAH analysis, reports were submitted to local health authorities and the Bureau of Epidemiology at the Thai Ministry of Public Health, which cooperated with WHO and Myanmar health authorities during outbreaks [11]. Maternal care followed the UNFPA Minimum Initial Service Package, but not the full BEmONC/CEmONC package because vacuum extraction was not performed in camps [11]. The nearest Thai hospital for Mae La Oon/Mae Ra Ma Luang was Mae Sariang, 80 km away, with travel time of 3.5–7 hours depending on season; that is outside a 2-hour comprehensive emergency obstetric-care radius for those two camps, and it demonstrates why referral time, not just nominal hospital linkage, matters [11]. Mae La itself, by contrast, had referral to Thai district/Mae Sot hospital systems in older Mae La evidence, but the cited sources do not publish a 2017–2023 Mae La-specific 2-hour EmOC map [16].

## 4. Midwife and skilled-birth-attendant density

Cox’s Bazar has a usable 2023 skilled-birth-attendant denominator. The Dec 2023 bulletin reports an average of 1,277 skilled birth attendants in 2023 — 37% doctors, 27% nurses, and 36% midwives — serving a Dec 2023 camp population of 969,719 [10]. That equals 13.2 skilled birth attendants per 10,000 population. If only the 36% midwife component is counted, 1,277×36%=460 midwives, or 4.7 midwives per 10,000 population. Using the inferred 2023 live-birth denominator from the revised WHO figure, 57 deaths at 200.39/100,000 implies about 28,444 live births; the 1,277 skilled birth attendants equal 44.9 per 1,000 live births, and the estimated 460 midwives equal 16.2 per 1,000 live births [9] [10].

Cox’s Bazar also has a 2023 strategic-plan baseline of 1,055 skilled birth attendants — 392 medical officers, 369 nurses, and 294 midwives — described as equivalent to 23 SBA per 19,699 people, compared with a Sphere target of 23 per 10,000 [12]. Using the source’s own numerator and the 969,719 Dec 2023 population, 1,055 equals 10.9 SBAs per 10,000 population, and 294 midwives equals 3.0 midwives per 10,000 [12] [10]. These two Cox’s Bazar counts differ because the strategic plan baseline and Dec 2023 4W table use different reporting moments and frames.

Mae La has a 2018 health-workforce count but not a midwife-specific count: 3 doctors, 72 qualified nurses, and 64 community health workers for 39,061 refugees [1]. Doctors plus qualified nurses equal 75 clinically skilled staff, or 19.2 per 10,000 population; including CHWs gives 139 staff, or 35.6 per 10,000. The source does not identify which nurses were midwives or skilled birth attendants, so a midwife-to-population ratio cannot be computed without over-classifying the workforce [1]. The Thai-border camp study reports that up to 26 midwives, 23 MCH assistants, and 4 mother-support-group facilitators provided services in Mae La Oon/Mae Ra Ma Luang, but those are not Mae La site counts [11].

## 5. Cultural mediation and traditional birth attendants

Cox’s Bazar’s cultural mediation problem is documented in the 2021 and 2022 Health Sector materials. Home delivery fell from 88% in 2018 to 59% in 2019, 42% in 2020, and approximately 30%–35% in 2021, showing a major shift toward facility delivery but also persistent home birth [19]. The Q2 2022 bulletin describes qualitative research on Rohingya and host-community knowledge, attitudes, and perceptions on ANC, safe delivery, and danger signs among first-time pregnant women, new mothers, and traditional birth attendants [14]. The Dec 2023 bulletin records misinformation linking scabies drugs with family planning and states that targeted communication and community engagement were being used to address refusals, illustrating the same trust-and-mediation dynamics that affect SRH uptake [10]. The cited sources do not state that Rohingya TBAs were trained to conduct deliveries; the Health Sector emphasis was on shifting deliveries toward skilled/facility care and strengthening referral/quality, while engaging community actors and TBAs around knowledge, danger signs, and service use [19] [14].

For Mae La, the cultural-mediation model was more embedded in camp staffing. The Mae La health-services study states that camp health workers could speak Karen and Burmese, and that free care was provided regardless of camp-resident status [16]. The Thai-border camp study states that primary health care was provided in close cooperation with trained camp residents to ensure access to basic services, while skilled and unskilled birth attendants continued to attend births over time [11]. It also reports mother-support groups, up to 26 midwives, 23 MCH assistants, and 4 facilitators in the two studied Karen camps, and notes locally appropriate neonatal guidelines and intensive coaching in delivery skills [11]. That supports a model of culturally mediated, locally staffed maternal care with referral escalation, not a program that simply replaced local birth attendants overnight.

## 6. Funding shock: UNFPA in Cox’s Bazar vs. Mae La

The cited Cox’s Bazar Health Sector documents ground major funding stress in 2022–2023 but do not provide an exact 2021 UNFPA-only percentage cut or dollar reduction for maternal-health services. Q1 2022 reported a Health Sector appeal of USD 110 million with only USD 2,112,676 received by end-Q1, or about 1.9% funded [13]. Q2 2022 reported the same USD 110 million appeal with USD 48 million received/committed, and stated that Health Posts, PHCs, and Field Hospitals providing critical lifesaving services were most affected, 51% unfunded [14]. Dec 2023 reported a USD 97.3 million Health Sector funding requirement, 3.4% reported in the UN OCHA Financial Tracking System, while a June 2023 sector funding analysis suggested about USD 47.3 million, or 49%, may have been received/committed [10]. These are Health Sector funding figures, not UNFPA-only reductions.

UNFPA’s operational role in Cox’s Bazar is grounded through MPMSR and SRH programming: MPMSR was supported by UNFPA, the 2021 report links the system to UNFPA-supported implementation, and the 2023 MPMSR report says SRH partners responded to audit findings by initiating a unique Maternal and Child Health card for all SRH partners to improve documentation and continuity [4] [6]. However, the cited sources do not quantify a UNFPA 2021 funding cut affecting specific midwife posts, women-friendly spaces, dignity kits, clinics, or referrals. The most defensible conclusion is that Cox’s Bazar maternal-health continuity faced a documented sector-wide funding shock in 2022–2023 and UNFPA-supported SRH surveillance/coordination remained central, but the exact 2021 UNFPA-only cut is not stated in the grounded sources [13] [14] [10].

For Mae La, the cited evidence does not support direct UNFPA funding exposure in 2021. The Thai-border camp study says maternal care followed the UNFPA Minimum Initial Service Package, but that is a technical framework, not evidence of direct UNFPA financing [11]. The Mae La and Thailand iRHIS documents identify UNHCR health reporting and camp-site service indicators [1] [7]. The appropriate funding frame is therefore UNHCR/CCSDPT/camp-provider/donor support and Thai referral links, not UNFPA cuts. The Thai-border camp study highlights that coordinated donor contributions and secured funding were part of the PHC model and that exchange rates, medicine prices, referrals, and salaries affected budgets, with budget estimates for 2016–2018 not on a yearly base [11]. No cited source quantifies a 2021 Mae La continuity-of-care disruption attributable to UNFPA.

## 7. Source comparability and limitations that affect interpretation

The data systems are not directly interchangeable. UNHCR iRHIS site profiles are standardized camp/site snapshots; Mae La 2018 reports a site population of 39,061 and indicators such as maternal deaths, ANC coverage, SBA, C-section rate, and NMR, but the extracted profile does not print live-birth denominators or annual pregnancy counts [1]. Cox’s Bazar’s UNHCR iRHIS Kutupalong New 2018 site profile is a single-site profile inside a much larger emergency response and therefore cannot be used as a response-wide Rohingya denominator [2].

Cox’s Bazar Health Sector bulletins are operational 4W/HMIS reports. They track facility-based births, live-birth percentage, health-facility birth percentage, ANC4, SBA, workforce, BEmONC/CEmONC availability, and funding, but their figures can be monthly, quarterly, or annualized depending on the bulletin [13] [14] [15] [10] [8]. MPMSR is a death-surveillance and review system: it provides stronger cause-of-death and maternal-death review data, including place of death and verbal autopsy, but it is not a complete annual live-birth or PPH-incidence registry in the cited reports [6] [4] [5] [3]. The 2023 MPMSR perinatal component began in August 2023, so its perinatal counts are not full-year neonatal mortality [6].

Thai camp evidence also has scope limits. The 2017 Thailand iRHIS country report reports ranges across multiple camp sites and a country total of 104,532 refugees, so it cannot be disaggregated to Mae La without the missing site profile [7]. The peer-reviewed Thai-border study is strong for model and long-run outcomes but covers Mae La Oon and Mae Ra Ma Luang, not Mae La; it also pools camp data because there were no significant socio-demographic differences between the camp populations [11]. The older Mae La health-services study is directly about Mae La but covers 2006–2007, so it is model evidence for camp-based services and Thai referral links, not an annual 2017–2023 indicator source [16].

## 8. Comparative synthesis: what the outcomes track with

The strongest observed correlation is with continuity and quality of maternal/newborn care — high ANC uptake, higher skilled attendance, locally staffed culturally mediated services, and functional referral pathways — rather than with a simple “NGO field hospital versus Thai MOPH integration” dichotomy. Mae La’s 2018 profile combines 91% ANC coverage, 95% skilled birth attendance, 13% C-section rate, 0 maternal deaths, and 6/1,000 neonatal mortality [1]. Cox’s Bazar’s 2023 system achieved 75% ANC4 and 85% skilled attendance, but MPMSR still recorded 57 maternal deaths, including 20 hemorrhage deaths and 19 hypertensive-disorder deaths, and 75% of maternal deaths occurred in facilities or during referral [8] [6]. That pattern points less to lack of nominal facility contact alone and more to timeliness, quality of intrapartum/emergency care, referral functioning, and management of hemorrhage and hypertension.

Midwife/SBA density plausibly contributes, but the data do not prove causation. Cox’s Bazar had large absolute workforce numbers in 2023 — average 1,277 SBAs, including about 460 midwives by composition — yet the strategic plan still judged the SBA baseline below the Sphere comparison and identified quality and distribution gaps [10] [12]. Mae La’s 2018 clinical staff density, counting doctors and qualified nurses, was 19.2 per 10,000 population, with 95% SBA, but the profile does not identify midwives separately [1]. Thus, density correlates with coverage but cannot explain outcomes without facility capability, referral delay, and case-severity data.

Emergency obstetric access is a more persuasive explanatory factor for maternal deaths than ANC alone. Cox’s Bazar had many BEmONC facilities — 32 in 2022 — but only 57% of PHCs provided all BEmONC signal functions in the 2021 bulletin, and CEmONC capacity was limited to 3 centres in Aug 2022 and 1 facility with CEmONC services in Dec 2023 [19] [15] [10]. Mae La/Thai-border camp evidence shows that referral links to Thai hospitals existed, but some Karen camps faced 3.5–7 hour travel to the nearest emergency obstetric/neonatal hospital, beyond a 2-hour CEmONC radius [11]. Therefore, neither setting should be described as having uniformly adequate 2-hour EmOC access; the difference is that Mae La’s 2018 profile shows better outcome indicators despite the broader border-region referral constraints, while Cox’s Bazar shows high mortality even with dense humanitarian facilities.

Cultural mediation likely affected uptake in both settings. Cox’s Bazar moved home delivery down from 88% in 2018 to about 30%–35% in 2021, a large behavioural and service-access shift, but persistent home birth and misinformation around reproductive-health-related issues remained documented [19] [10]. Mae La’s model relied on Karen/Burmese-speaking camp health workers and trained camp residents, which likely reduced language and trust barriers [16] [11]. This supports correlation between culturally embedded staffing and higher coverage, but not a clean causal estimate.

The 2021 UNFPA-funding premise is only partly supported. Cox’s Bazar had severe Health Sector funding shortfalls in 2022–2023 and UNFPA-supported SRH/MPMSR functions were central, but the cited sources do not quantify a 2021 UNFPA-only cut or prove a direct causal link from that cut to the 2022–2023 maternal-death pattern [13] [14] [10] [4]. Mae La does not show direct UNFPA service financing in the cited sources; its relevant continuity risks were camp-provider/UNHCR/CCSDPT/donor and referral-cost risks rather than a UNFPA funding shock [11] [1].

Overall, the available evidence ranks the plausible drivers as follows: (1) quality and timeliness of EmOC/referral and management of hemorrhage/hypertension; (2) sustained ANC4 and skilled-attendance coverage; (3) culturally mediated community staffing and TBA referral practices; (4) workforce density and distribution; and (5) funding shock as an enabling constraint rather than a directly demonstrated cause. Mae La’s premise should be corrected: it was not simply Thai MOPH-integrated, and the 2021 UNFPA funding-reduction hypothesis does not apply to Mae La on the cited evidence [16] [11].
