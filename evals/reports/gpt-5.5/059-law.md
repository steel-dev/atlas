# Respondeat superior analysis for the June 14, 2024 Route 7 collision

## Bottom line

On the record described, the company is **not entitled to summary judgment on a “personal detour/frolic” defense**. If the governing road is **Virginia Route 7**, Virginia law supplies the controlling respondeat superior rules; VDOT confirms a Virginia Route 7/Leesburg Pike in Loudoun County and the Town of Leesburg, but the public-source search did **not** independently establish that this Rocha collision occurred there, because no fetched source corroborated the Rocha accident facts or the “team support errands” policy [1]. Subject to that jurisdictional caveat, Virginia law strongly supports treating Rocha as an employee acting within the scope of employment when returning from the lunch stop: the trip was supervisor-directed, expressly covered by a policy effective before the collision, a few blocks off route, undertaken for coworkers and company “team support,” reimbursed by the company, and followed by an immediate planned return to deliveries.

The better summary-judgment ruling is therefore: **vicarious-liability/scope should be resolved against the company, or at minimum sent to the jury rather than dismissed.** Negligence and damages remain separate predicates. The rear-end collision, two-second phone glance, functioning brake lights, and Virginia following-distance/distracted-driving rules create a triable negligence case against Rocha notwithstanding the sedan’s sudden braking for fallen lumber; Virginia’s sudden-emergency doctrine does not automatically excuse a following driver who may have contributed to the emergency by distraction or inadequate following distance. If Rocha’s negligence and causation are found, the company is vicariously liable under respondeat superior.

## Governing jurisdiction and legal framework

The scenario states only that the crash occurred “on Route 7.” Public searches did not produce an independent source tying Daniel Rocha, the June 14, 2024 collision, or the company policy to any jurisdiction. VDOT confirms that Virginia Route 7/Leesburg Pike runs in Loudoun County and the Town of Leesburg, but no source or stipulated fact in this record establishes that the Rocha accident occurred on that Virginia roadway [1]. Because the accident state/locality could not be established, the legal analysis below applies **only if the collision occurred on Virginia Route 7**. Under Virginia law, the operative doctrine is respondeat superior: an employer is liable when its employee was performing the employer’s business and acting within the scope of employment. The Virginia Supreme Court’s formulation in **Kensington Associates v. West** is the controlling scope rule: an act is within scope if it was expressly or impliedly directed by the employer or naturally incident to the business, and was performed—however mistakenly or ill-advisedly—with intent to further the employer’s interest, rather than wholly from an independent personal motive [2].

Virginia’s civil model instructions track that same structure. They define employer/employee by the power or right to control the means and methods of work; make the employer liable for damages proximately caused by an employee’s negligence within scope; and instruct that, after the employee relationship is shown, scope may be presumed unless the employer produces evidence that the employee was outside scope [3]. The same instructions are especially important here because they recognize mixed motives: an employer may be responsible when the employee was trying “to some extent” to serve the employer’s business even if the employee also acted for himself or a third person [3].

## Summary-judgment standard and who decides scope

Virginia Rule 3:20 provides the baseline summary-judgment standard: the court grants summary judgment only when the record shows the moving party is entitled to judgment, and summary judgment “shall not be entered if any material fact is genuinely in dispute” [4]. In respondeat superior cases, Virginia adds a specific allocation: once an employer-employee relationship is established, the employer bears the burden to prove the employee was outside the scope of employment, and if the evidence leaves the question in doubt, it is for the jury [2].

Virginia also uses a frolic/detour spectrum. If the undisputed evidence shows a deviation that is slight and not unusual, or one that is great and unusual, the court may decide scope as a matter of law; if the deviation falls between those extremes, the jury decides [2]. **Plummer v. Center Psychiatrists** reinforces that scope is not defeated merely because the employee violated rules or acted from a wrongful or mixed motive; the question is whether the service in which the tort occurred was within the ordinary course of the employer’s business, and doubts go to the jury [5].

## Employee status: Rocha is a company employee, not an independent contractor

Virginia’s employee-status test asks whether a master-servant relationship exists by considering: (1) selection and engagement, (2) payment of compensation, (3) power of dismissal, and (4) power of control. The fourth factor—the right to control—is determinative [6]. The Virginia model instructions likewise distinguish an employee from an independent contractor by the employer’s right to control the means and methods of the work [3].

The facts supplied in the prompt satisfy that test. Rocha is a company delivery driver working an assigned delivery route and schedule. The company regulated route deviations through a policy prohibiting personal detours, later recognized an exception for “team support errands,” and reimbursed mileage for the detour. Rocha also received supervisor direction to pick up the lunches. Those facts show company control over route, schedule, use of the vehicle for errands, and job duties. No contrary facts—such as Rocha owning an independent delivery business, choosing routes without company control, bearing entrepreneurial risk, or being paid by contract result alone—were found or supplied. On this record, employee status is established, or at least no reasonable independent-contractor inference defeats summary judgment on employee status.

The company’s retroactive reimbursement under that policy is strong evidence that the errand was not merely a personal detour but fell within a category the company recognized as work-related.

## Scope of employment: supervisor-directed, short, mixed-purpose detour

The scope-of-employment factors favor vicarious liability.

First, the errand was **expressly directed or approved**. Kensington asks whether the act was expressly or impliedly directed by the employer or naturally incident to the business [2]. Rocha received a supervisor text directing him to pick up lunch for two warehouse coworkers; the deleted approval email’s metadata confirms that an approval email existed. The Virginia model instructions similarly treat express or implied direction and intent to further employer interests as core scope factors [3]. Supervisor authorization therefore materially distinguishes this case from a purely private lunch run.

Second, the trip served at least a mixed employer purpose. The sandwiches were for two warehouse coworkers who requested them that morning, and the company later reimbursed mileage citing the May 1 “team support errands” policy. Under the Virginia multiple-motives instruction, the employer may be responsible if the employee was trying to serve the employer’s business “to some extent,” even if the primary motive also benefited the employee or a third person [3]. The company’s own reimbursement rationale makes it difficult to characterize the trip as “wholly” external to the employer’s business, which is the standard that would defeat scope under Kensington [2].

Third, the four-block deviation is the kind of short detour Virginia treats differently from a frolic. In **Master Auto Service Corp. v. Bowden**, the Virginia Supreme Court held that an employee who went outside the directed route/zone for a purely personal purpose and contrary to instructions was outside scope, and that simply heading back toward work does not by itself prove reentry; the true test is whether the employee was then engaged in the master’s business [7]. But Bowden distinguished **Railway Express v. Lewis**, where permission, a deviation of only a few blocks, remaining deliveries, no intent to abandon business, and a personal mission coupled with the master’s business supported scope even if the accident occurred before the personal mission was complete [7]. Rocha’s facts align more closely with the slight-deviation side of that distinction: four blocks, supervisor direction, remaining deliveries, on schedule, immediate intent to resume deliveries, and a company-recognized team-support purpose.

Fourth, the company’s contrary policy does not defeat scope as a matter of law. The old rule against personal detours matters, but the effective May 1 policy allowed “team support errands,” the supervisor directed the task, and the company reimbursed mileage after the collision. Plummer teaches that even rule-violating or mixed-motive conduct can remain within scope if the service in which the tort occurred was within the ordinary course of the employer’s business [5]. Here the service was a supervisor-approved, company-reimbursed delivery-driver errand on the way back to deliveries.

## Negligence trigger: Rocha’s driving conduct remains actionable despite sudden braking

Respondeat superior attaches only if Rocha committed a tort causing injury. The negligence record is sufficient to proceed.

The key facts are: dashcam video shows Rocha glanced at his phone **two seconds before impact**; the sedan braked suddenly for fallen lumber; and the sedan’s brake lights functioned properly. Virginia law gives the sedan driver several negligence theories:

| Rule | Source | Application |
|---|---|---|
| Handheld device rule | Va. Code § 46.2-818.2(A) makes it unlawful, while driving a moving vehicle on Virginia highways, to hold a handheld personal communications device, absent exceptions [8]. | If Rocha’s phone glance involved holding or using the device while moving, it supports negligence per statutory duty; even if not a statutory violation, it supports ordinary distraction. |
| Following distance | Va. Code § 46.2-816 prohibits following more closely than reasonable and prudent considering speed, traffic, and highway conditions [9]. | A rear-end impact after the lead car braked for an obstacle supports an inference that Rocha failed to maintain reasonable distance. |
| Brake lights | Va. Code § 46.2-1014 requires brake lights visible 500 feet to the rear when brakes are applied [10]. | Proper brake lights weaken any defense that the sedan gave no warning. |
| Rear-end prima facie case | Garnot holds that striking a stopped/stopping lead car from the rear makes a prima facie negligence case, and the following driver must keep lookout for sudden stops, use that lookout, and keep a reasonable distance [11]. | The rear-end collision itself shifts the practical burden to Rocha to explain why he was not negligent. |
| Sudden emergency | VMI Civil No. 7.000 excuses a driver only if, without negligence on his part, he faced a sudden emergency and acted reasonably [3]. Garnot states that a sudden stop in traffic is not unexpected enough by itself to warrant a sudden-emergency instruction [11]. | Fallen lumber may explain why the sedan braked, but it does not automatically excuse Rocha if distraction or following too closely contributed. |
| Contributory negligence | Virginia does not compare ordinary negligence; any plaintiff negligence that proximately caused the accident bars recovery [3]. | The company may argue the sedan driver reacted negligently to fallen lumber, but functioning brake lights and the emergency obstacle make that a factual defense rather than a clean summary-judgment bar. |

Because the brake lights worked and the phone glance occurred two seconds before impact, a reasonable jury could find Rocha failed to keep a proper lookout, failed to maintain reasonable following distance, or was distracted. The sudden stop for fallen lumber is a causation and possible contributory-negligence issue, not a complete legal defense on this record.

## Injury and causation

The sedan driver alleges neck strain, and medical imaging shows soft-tissue injury. Under Virginia’s tort instructions, proximate cause is a cause that in natural and continuous sequence produces the injury and without which the injury would not have occurred; there may be multiple proximate causes [3]. Virginia permits recovery for bodily injuries, pain and mental anguish, inconvenience, medical expenses, lost earnings, and property damage caused by the defendant’s negligence, and the plaintiff must prove each claimed damage item and causation by the greater weight of the evidence, though not the exact amount if the evidence permits a reasonable estimate [3].

A rear-end impact followed by claimed neck strain and imaging-confirmed soft-tissue injury is enough to create a triable damages and causation issue. It does not, without more, defeat summary judgment for lack of injury.

## Summary-judgment disposition

The factors rank as follows:

1. **Employee status — strongly established.** Rocha was a delivery driver subject to company route, schedule, detour, and supervisor controls. Virginia’s right-to-control test points to employee status [6], [3].
2. **Scope of employment — strongly favors liability, and at least defeats the company’s motion.** The lunch run was supervisor-directed, supported by a deleted approval email’s metadata, within a policy effective May 1, reimbursed by the company, only four blocks off route, undertaken while on schedule, and immediately connected to resuming deliveries. Under Kensington, the errand was expressly or impliedly directed and at least partly intended to further the employer’s interests [2]. Under Bowden’s frolic/detour distinction, this is much closer to the permitted few-block, mixed-purpose detour than a purely personal abandonment of the employer’s business [7].
3. **Employer benefit — materially supports scope.** The coworkers’ lunch request alone might look personal, but supervisor direction, the “team support errands” policy, and mileage reimbursement convert the trip into a company-recognized team-support task. Virginia’s multiple-motives rule does not require the employer benefit to be exclusive [3].
4. **Negligence — triable and likely sufficient to attach vicarious liability if found.** The phone glance two seconds before impact, rear-end impact, functioning brake lights, and following-distance law create a prima facie negligence case; sudden braking for fallen lumber does not automatically excuse Rocha [8], [9], [11], [10], [3].
5. **Damages/causation — triable.** Neck strain with imaging-confirmed soft-tissue injury is sufficient to present personal-injury damages and causation to the factfinder under Virginia proximate-cause and damages rules [3].

Accordingly, assuming Virginia is the controlling jurisdiction, the company should be held **vicariously liable for Rocha’s negligence if negligence and causation are established**. On a defense motion for summary judgment, judgment should be **denied** because the undisputed facts do not show a frolic as a matter of law. On a plaintiff’s motion limited to employee status and scope, the record is strong enough to support summary judgment that Rocha was acting within the scope of employment; at the very least, any remaining dispute about the purpose or authorization of the lunch trip is for the jury, not a basis to dismiss vicarious liability.
