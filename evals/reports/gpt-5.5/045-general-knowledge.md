# Evolution of lithium extraction methods and environmental footprints, 2015–2024

**Bottom line.** From 2015 to 2024 the three regions diverged rather than converged: Chile’s Salar de Atacama remained a high-volume evaporation-brine system with improving recoveries but the strongest hydrological and indigenous-rights scrutiny; Western Australia scaled hard-rock spodumene quickly, with the environmental footprint concentrated in mining water, pit/processing disturbance, tailings and downstream conversion rather than brine drawdown; China’s Qinghai/Tibet salt-lake pathway moved fastest toward adsorption/membrane/DLE-style processing because high-Mg brines made conventional evaporation alone less competitive, but the fetched record is much thinner on independently documented hydrological impacts and local disputes.

## 1. Chile’s Salar de Atacama: evaporation route, rising recovery, and constrained brine/water rights

### Extraction route and volumes

SQM Salar S.A. and Albemarle used the same basic route through 2015–2024: brine extraction from Salar de Atacama wells, concentration in solar evaporation ponds, and chemical conversion/refining to battery or technical lithium carbonate and lithium hydroxide at downstream plants. SQM’s 2024 SEC technical report states that nominal capacity rose from **160 kt/y lithium carbonate and 21.5 kt/y lithium hydroxide in 2021** to **195 kt/y lithium carbonate and 26 kt/y lithium hydroxide in 2023** [1]. SQM’s environmental extraction limit under RCA 226/2006 is **1,600 L/s net brine plus 240 L/s freshwater**; its 2018 arbitration/CCHEN-CORFO terms set an accumulated sales limit of **349,553 t lithium metal, equal to 1,860,670 t LCE**, plus about **64,816 t lithium metal, equal to 345,015 t LCE**, from the original authorization [1][5]. The SEC reserve model for 2024–2030 extracts about **210–211 Mm3 brine**, with flow declining from about **1,159–1,166 L/s in 2024** to **822 L/s in 2030**, and forecasts recovered LCE of **240 kt in 2024, 230 kt in 2025, 230 kt in 2026, 200 kt in 2027, 180 kt in 2028, 190 kt in 2029 and 170 kt in 2030** [1]. SQM’s reported recovery improved from a historical Salar yield of about **43%** to an average process recovery of about **52%** in the 2023 reserve model [1].

Albemarle’s Salar de Atacama/La Negra operation is governed by a CORFO quota that was amended in 2016: the amendment added **262,132 t lithium metal**, and with **69,083 t lithium-metal equivalent** remaining from the original quota as of 31 August 2022 the total remaining quota was **331,215 t Li metal, or about 1.81 Mt LCE**, expiring in **2044** [2]. Albemarle’s RCA 21/2016 authorizes **442 L/s brine** and **23.5 L/s freshwater**—Tilopozo 8.5 L/s, Tucucaro 10 L/s and Peine 5 L/s—while the 2023 SEC model assumes **414 L/s brine pumping** to meet drawdown constraints and reports current freshwater consumption of **16.9 L/s** [2]. The La Negra 1+2 complex has about **45,000 t/y LCE** capacity; La Negra 3 targets about **84,000 t/y lithium carbonate by 2027**, while the RCA text allowed **88,000 t/y lithium carbonate plus 4,500 t/y LiCl**, about **94,000 t/y LCE-equivalent** [2]. Albemarle’s recovery pathway was explicitly an efficiency project: salar evaporation recovery was about **40% historically/currently**, with the Salar Yield Improvement Program targeting **65% by 2025**, followed by about **80% recovery** in the La Negra carbonate plant [2].

### Water footprint: numerator definitions matter

The most important comparison is that “water” in brine mining can mean freshwater, brackish/fresh industrial water, brine pumped, brine evaporated, or full life-cycle process water. These are not interchangeable.

### Pond land intensity and DLE contrast

Evaporation ponds are the land-intensive part of the Chilean route. SQM’s Salar de Atacama evaporation pond area was about **3,227 ha in 2021**; against 2023 nominal lithium capacity of about **222 kt/y LCE**—195 kt/y Li2CO3 plus 26 kt/y LiOH counted approximately as LCE—this is **~14.5 ha per kt/y LCE capacity**, or **0.0145 ha per t/y**, with the caveat that the ponds also support potash and other salt circuits [1]. Albemarle’s RCA 21/2016 expanded evaporation surfaces from **326 ha** by **510 ha** to **836 ha**; this equals **18.5 ha per kt/y** against the current 45.3 kt/y carbonate capacity, or **9.5 ha per kt/y** against the 88 kt/y Phase 3 carbonate approval [2].

The fetched DLE benchmark is Eramet/Éramine’s Centenario-Ratones adsorption project: **24,000 t/y LCE**, **+90% lithium recovery**, **5-day to 1-week processing time**, **20 brine wells** drilled about **400 m** deep with **25 cm** diameter holes, and a dedicated brine reinjection network [14][15][16]. This is not an Atacama project, but it provides a named commercial adsorption benchmark for how DLE substitutes compact wells/process units and reinjection for large evaporation pond systems. Through 2024, the fetched notes did not establish a commercial SQM, Albemarle or Codelco/SQM DLE unit in the Salar de Atacama; the grounded 2023–2024 change is instead the Chilean National Lithium Strategy and Codelco-SQM partnership negotiation that pushed lower-impact technology and state-partnership models [1].

### Hydrological impacts and disputes

The strongest documented physical impact in the fetched record is brine-level drawdown. Albemarle’s technical report states that SQM extracts brine at about **1,700 L/s** and Albemarle at an authorized **442 L/s**, and that exploitation lowers brine levels, with drawdown greatest near extraction wells and reaching **“several meters”** in some cases before declining with distance [2]. The same record identifies the environmentally sensitive margin systems—**Soncor, Peine, Aguas de Quelana, La Punta-La Brava, Quelana and Tilopozo**—as lagoon/wetland systems dependent mainly on aquifers recharged by upper-basin rainfall [2]. For Albemarle, monitoring obligations include **125 monthly brine/freshwater phreatic-level points**, **13 quarterly saline-interface profiles**, **74 monthly brine-flow measurements**, **3 monthly industrial-water flow measurements**, **40 quarterly water-quality points**, **20 monthly lagoon limnimetric levels**, and **4 biannual lagoon-area measurements**, with quarterly and annual reporting to the SMA [2].

The indigenous-water-rights dispute is inseparable from this hydrogeology. The Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños represents communities including **Toconao, Camar, Socaire and Peine**; those communities challenged and participated in permitting over insufficient scientific information on salar hydrology and waterfowl habitat and sought revocation or stricter review of operating permits [5][1]. SQM’s 2023 Extraction Reduction Plan triggered an Indigenous Consultation Process under ILO Convention 169 for **Camar, Talabre, Socaire, Peine and Toconao** after the environmental authority found potential effects on the environmental value of ancestral territory from transport and road works [1]. SQM’s compliance program also includes participatory environmental monitoring with **Toconao, Talabre, Socaire, Peine, CPA and Camar** [1].

## 2. Western Australia: fast hard-rock scale-up, water/tailings controls rather than DLE

### Production evolution and conversion to LCE

Western Australia’s 2015–2024 evolution was rapid scaling of spodumene concentrate and downstream hydroxide conversion. Greenbushes/Talison remained the anchor: in FY24 it produced **1.383 Mt spodumene concentrate**, down from **1.491 Mt in FY23**, at **A$330/t production cash cost**, up from **A$244/t in FY23**; CGP3 under construction would add about **0.5 Mt/y concentrate capacity** [6]. The associated Kwinana Train 1 produced **3,508 t lithium hydroxide monohydrate in FY24**, up **86%** from **1,884 t**, and **90%** met battery-grade standards [6][18].

Pilgangoora/Pilbara Minerals processed **3.957 Mt ore** in FY24 and produced **725,329 dmt spodumene concentrate**, up from **620,147 dmt in FY23**, at **5.2% Li2O**, shipping **707,133 dmt** and achieving **67.7% lithia recovery**, slightly below FY23’s **68.2%** and within a quarterly range of **65.3–72.2%** [7]. Its realized SC5.3 CIF China price collapsed to **US$1,176/t in FY24** from **US$4,447/t in FY23**, showing the sensitivity of hard-rock margins to lithium prices [7]. Its WA licence/works approval route uses crushing/concentration, concentrate pressure filtration to **<7% moisture**, tailings thickening and disposal to a tailings management facility, with excess and decant water recycled to the process water pond [9].

Wodgina and Mt Marion add the Albemarle–Mineral Resources and Ganfeng–Mineral Resources positions. MinRes reported record FY24 lithium shipments of **201,000 dmt SC6 from Wodgina** and **218,000 dmt SC6 from Mt Marion**, combined **419,000 dmt SC6**, at a realized **US$1,279/t SC6**, down **76%** [8]. Wodgina has **three processing trains** with nominal capacity of about **820,000 t/y** spodumene concentrate at **5.5% Li2O**, using crushing, ball milling, deslime cyclones, magnetic separation, flotation, concentrate/tailings dewatering, wet TSF fine-tailings disposal and waste-rock blending for coarse tailings, with product trucked to Port Hedland [19].

The LCE conversion is mechanical: Li2O is **46.45% Li**; **1 t SC6** contains **27.87 kg Li** and therefore **0.1484 t LCE** using **5.3228 t LCE per t Li**, before conversion losses. **1 t SC5.2** contains **24.15 kg Li**, or **0.1285 t LCE**. Thus FY24 output implies **~205.2 kt LCE contained** at Greenbushes, **~93.2 kt LCE contained** at Pilgangoora, and **~62.2 kt LCE contained** in Wodgina + Mt Marion shipments [3][6][7][8].

### Hydrological footprint and regulation

The hard-rock footprint differs categorically from Atacama: it is not salar brine drawdown. The fetched Pilgangoora approval record grounds impacts and controls as processing-water abstraction/recycling, tailings thickening, TMF disposal, concentrate filtration and transport rather than brine depletion [8]. For Greenbushes and Pilgangoora, the fetched notes did not establish a quantitative groundwater-level drawdown series comparable to Atacama’s “several meters” brine-level drawdown; therefore the grounded comparison is by impact type, not like-for-like water-level magnitude. Pit dewatering, processing-water abstraction, TSF seepage controls and transport dust/moisture management are the relevant mechanisms under WA approvals, not brine evaporation or lagoon-margin drawdown.

WA’s legal framework pushes incremental efficiency and closure assurance. The named regime includes the **Mining Act 1978**, **Mining Regulations 1981**, **Environmental Protection Act 1986** Part IV and Part V approvals, **Rights in Water and Irrigation Act 1914** water licences, **Aboriginal Heritage Act 1972/2021 transition issues**, the **Mining Rehabilitation Fund Act 2012**, and **2020 Mine Closure Plan Guidance**. The fetched notes ground the regulatory conclusion—tailings, water recycling, closure plans and rehabilitation obligations—more strongly than the exact bond/fund amounts for each named mine; therefore project-specific rehabilitation-payment values are not stated here. The causal result is clear: unlike Chile and China, WA regulation does not push DLE, because there is no brine lithium stream; it pushes better recoveries, water recycling, TSF management, dust/concentrate handling and closure planning.

Native title and Aboriginal heritage processes affect Greenbushes, Pilgangoora and Wodgina through WA heritage/native-title approval pathways, but the fetched notes did not establish named disputes equivalent to the Consejo de Pueblos Atacameños conflict. The grounded distinction is therefore that WA has formal Aboriginal heritage/native-title processes under the cited state regime, while the record fetched here does not show a single 2015–2024 water-rights dispute of the same type as the Salar de Atacama case.

## 3. China’s Qinghai/Tibet salt lakes: high-Mg brines and industrial policy accelerate adsorption/membrane extraction

China’s salt-lake evolution is best understood as a technology response to brine chemistry. Qinghai brines, especially high-Mg/Li brines, are harder to process by simple solar evaporation than Atacama or Zabuye-type low-Mg brines. Fetched technical literature specifically addresses high Mg/Li brine using a membrane process for lithium carbonate preparation [17]. Qinghai Salt Lake Industry’s 2024 annual-report summary states that China’s lithium resources are mostly salt-lake brines, concentrated in **Qinghai and Tibet**, and that the company had formed an integrated industrial demonstration process coupling **adsorption lithium extraction and membrane separation/concentration** to extract lithium salts from **ultra-high Mg/Li, low-lithium brine** [12]. It states existing carbonate capacity of **40,000 t/y**, planned **43,000 t** carbonate production in 2025, and a new **40,000 t/y** integrated basic lithium-salt project under construction, with contract amount **RMB 4.243 billion**, total project progress **55%**, civil works **78%** and installation **32%** at the reporting date [12].

For Zabuye/Tibet Mineral Development, the fetched annual report grounds the processing route rather than a full 2015–2024 production series: Zabuye brine uses a **solar-gradient salt-field/solar-pond crystallization method**, relying on solar energy and cold sources in pre-evaporation and evaporation ponds, and the brine is characterized as high-lithium, low-magnesium and carbonate-rich, close to or at lithium-carbonate saturation [13]. For East Taijinar, West Taijinar and Ganfeng-linked Qinghai assets, the fetched notes did not establish complete annual LCE output, Mg/Li ratios or pond ha/t-y values; those specific data remain unestablished in the fetched record and should not be inferred from general Chinese salt-lake commentary.

The policy effect is nevertheless grounded. China’s 2015–2024 framework combines the **Mineral Resources Law**, **Water Law**, **Environmental Impact Assessment Law**, Five-Year Plan resource-security and new-energy priorities, Qinghai’s world-class salt-lake industrial-base policy, provincial resource planning, and Tibet mining/environmental approvals. In the fetched company record, this appears as consolidation and resource-security language: Qinghai’s government and China Minmetals signed a cooperation agreement to form China Salt Lake Group, intended to integrate and optimize salt-lake resources and improve comprehensive resource utilization [12]. The causal effect is different from Chile’s: state industrial policy accelerated domestic adsorption/membrane technology because it raised the strategic value of exploiting difficult high-Mg brines, while resource consolidation gave firms a reason to invest in 40 kt/y-scale process upgrades.

Hydrological/ecological impacts in China are the least transparent in the fetched set. The fetched notes did not establish specific lake/brine-level changes, freshwater inflow reductions, salinity-shift measurements, wetland impacts, or named herder/ethnic-minority water-use disputes for Qarhan, Taijinar or Zabuye comparable to Atacama’s monitoring network. Therefore the report distinguishes verified technology and policy adoption from unverified impact claims.

## 4. Battery-grade purity, processing cost and margin implications

The fetched record directly grounds realized quality for Australian hydroxide: Kwinana Train 1 produced **3,508 t LiOH·H2O in FY24**, and **90% met battery-grade standards** [18]. It also grounds method-based quality positioning. Atacama brines have long produced technical and battery-grade carbonate through La Negra and SQM refining [2][1]. Qinghai high-Mg brines require adsorption/membrane separation to remove Mg and concentrate lithium before carbonate precipitation [17][12]. Zabuye’s lower-Mg, carbonate-rich brine has a simpler solar crystallization advantage [13].

The fetched notes do not include the exact Chinese GB/T impurity thresholds for battery-grade lithium carbonate or lithium hydroxide monohydrate, so exact Na, K, Ca, Mg, Fe, sulfate/chloride thresholds are not stated. The grounded operational conclusion is narrower: by 2024 Atacama brine and Australian spodumene conversion routes were proven battery-grade supply routes, while Qinghai high-Mg brine required membrane/adsorption upgrading to approach the same customer-quality reliability.

Cost and margin differences are clearer:

- **Atacama brine** has the lowest fetched freshwater intensity—**15.5–32.8 m3/t Li2CO3** cradle-to-gate—and historically low operating cost because solar evaporation supplies much of the concentration energy, but it carries large pond land use and hydrological/social-regulatory risk [3][1][2].
- **Greenbushes/Talison** remained a low-cost hard-rock leader at **A$330/t concentrate production cash cost in FY24**, although that was up from **A$244/t in FY23** [18].
- **Pilbara Minerals/Pilgangoora** shows the price-cycle risk: FY24 realized **US$1,176/t SC5.3 CIF China**, down from **US$4,447/t in FY23**, while lithia recovery was **67.7%** [6].
- **Wodgina/Mt Marion** similarly realized **US$1,279/t SC6**, down **76%**, showing that hard-rock projects can lose margin quickly when lithium prices fall [20].
- **China/Qinghai Salt Lake Industry** stresses salt-lake cost advantage in the 2024 low-price market and says adsorption + membrane technology kept extraction cost on the left side of the industry cost curve, but the fetched notes do not give a RMB/t LCE cash-cost figure [12].
- **DLE capex benchmark**: Qinghai Salt Lake Industry’s new **40 kt/y** integrated lithium-salt project had signed contracts of **RMB 4.243 billion** and was **55%** complete; Eramet’s commercial adsorption benchmark is **24 kt/y LCE**, **+90% recovery**, compact wellfield and reinjection [12][14][15][16].

By 2024, lithium prices impaired high-cost transitions more than they supported them. Qinghai Salt Lake Industry reports battery-grade lithium carbonate averaged about **RMB 103,700/t** in the first half of 2024, down about **69% year on year**, with a high around **RMB 113,000/t** in mid-March and a low of **RMB 88,900/t** at end-June [12]. Pilbara’s concentrate price decline from **US$4,447/t to US$1,176/t** and MinRes’s **76%** price decline confirm the same margin compression in hard rock [6][20]. That price environment favored low-cost incremental efficiency and delayed marginal high-capex conversion unless policy or resource-security goals justified it.

## 5. Regulation as a causal driver of technology choice

**Chile:** Law **19,300/1994**, as modified by Law **20,417/2010**, and Supreme Decree **40/2012** require mining projects under article **10(i)** and projects in or near protected areas under article **10(p)** to enter the SEIA before development [1]. The EIA/DIA choice turns on impacts such as health risk, adverse effects on renewable natural resources, resettlement/living-environment effects and protected-area impacts [5]. DGA grants freshwater rights, while brine extraction has been regulated primarily through SEIA/RCA approvals as mineralized groundwater or brine, a water/brine divide contested by indigenous groups [5]. SMA enforces RCA commitments, and CORFO/CCHEN contracts set lithium quotas and lease economics [1][2]. This framework made recovery improvements valuable: SQM’s recovery rose toward **52%**, Albemarle’s SYIP targets **65%** salar recovery, and the 2023 National Lithium Strategy pushed state partnership, extraction-reduction and lower-impact technology discussion [1][2].

**Australia:** WA’s framework—the Mining Act/Regulations, Environmental Protection Act Part IV/Part V, RIWI Act water licences, Aboriginal heritage laws, MRF Act and mine-closure guidance—does not create a DLE pathway because the resource is ore, not brine. It channels innovation into ore sorting/recovery, water recycling, tailings thickening, TSF controls, concentrate moisture control and closure planning. Pilgangoora’s approval conditions illustrate the pattern: tailings are thickened to TMF, excess/decant water is recycled, and concentrate is filtered to **<7% moisture** [8].

**China:** China’s resource-management framework and Five-Year Plan priorities make salt-lake lithium a strategic domestic resource. Qinghai Salt Lake Industry’s record links policy-backed consolidation, resource-security research and China Salt Lake Group formation with adsorption/membrane process upgrading [12]. This is the clearest causal technology push among the three: high-Mg brines made technology necessary, and industrial policy made it investable.

## 6. Producer positioning and fallback branches

**SQM** is best positioned on scale and cost but most exposed to Chilean social-hydrological regulation. Its 2023 capacity of **195 kt/y Li2CO3 + 26 kt/y LiOH**, 2024 recovered LCE forecast of **240 kt**, **1,600 L/s** brine limit, **240 L/s** freshwater right, and recovery improvement to **~52%** make it a high-volume low-cost supplier [1][5]. Its risk is that water scrutiny, indigenous consultation and the Codelco partnership constrain expansion. If DLE is not technically or economically viable, SQM’s fallback is continued evaporation with lower pumping, better recovery, participatory monitoring and brine-management reductions.

**Albemarle** has a lower-volume but strategically important Atacama position: **442 L/s** authorized brine, **414 L/s** modelled pumping, **23.5 L/s** freshwater rights, **45 kt/y** current La Negra capacity moving toward **84–94 kt/y**, and SYIP recovery moving from **~40%** toward **65%** at salar stage [2]. Its customer value is proven carbonate quality from brine and optional hydroxide/carbonate conversion, but its regulatory risk is similar to SQM’s. Its fallback is continued evaporation with yield improvement rather than near-term commercial DLE in the fetched 2024 record.

**Ganfeng Lithium** is positioned across methods: Mt Marion hard-rock feed gives battery-supply reliability through SC6 concentrate, while Ganfeng-linked Qinghai salt-lake assets, not fully quantified in the fetched record, offer domestic brine optionality. The method trade-off is clear: hard-rock is quality-reliable but cost/price sensitive; salt-lake brine can be lower cost but requires impurity-control technology and more transparent water governance.

**Pilbara Minerals** is leveraged to hard-rock scale and market prices. Pilgangoora’s **725,329 dmt SC5.2** FY24 output, **67.7%** lithia recovery and **~93.2 kt LCE contained** position it as a major upstream supplier, but the price fall to **US$1,176/t** from **US$4,447/t** compressed margins [6]. Its lower-impact pathway is not DLE; it is higher concentrator recovery, water recycling, tailings controls, selective downstream conversion and disciplined production when prices fall.

**Overall ranking on environmental footprint and supply reliability:** Chilean brine has the lowest comparable freshwater intensity but the highest local hydrological/social conflict; Australian hard rock has the highest comparable freshwater and energy burden in conversion but the most transparent mining-closure/tailings control regime and reliable battery-grade hydroxide feed; Chinese salt lakes have the strongest policy push toward lower-evaporation adsorption/membrane technology but the weakest fetched evidence on local hydrological impacts and community disputes. The practical transition path is therefore not one universal DLE solution: it is DLE/adsorption where brine chemistry and regulation justify it, improved evaporation management where it does not, and continued hard-rock mining with better water recycling, tailings management and downstream conversion efficiency in Western Australia.
