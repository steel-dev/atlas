# New Zealand construction metalworks / structural steel fabrication market, 2018–2024

## Scope, classification boundary, and evidence limits

This report limits “construction metalworks” to **structural steel fabrication for buildings, bridges and other structures**, not general metal manufacturing, reinforcing-bar production, steel distribution, or on-site erection alone. The closest narrow official industry boundary is **ANZSIC C2221 — Structural Steel Fabricating**, defined as units mainly engaged in fabricating structural steel components of buildings or other structures. That boundary is narrower than **C222 Structural Metal Product Manufacturing** and excludes on-site structural steel erection and complete prefabricated metal building manufacturing [1][2].

The evidence base is uneven:

* **Employment** is available directly for C2221 from Stats NZ via Figure.NZ, as employee counts at February [1].
* **Revenue** could not be extracted directly at C2221 from the fetched Stats NZ / Figure.NZ tables. The closest directly fetched official financial series is Stats NZ Annual Enterprise Survey / Figure.NZ **C22 Fabricated Metal Product Manufacturing total income**, which is broader than construction structural steel [3]. To isolate structural steel more closely, this report also provides a **derived C2221 revenue proxy**, allocating C22 income by C2221’s share of C222 employment. That proxy should be read as an allocation, not a published Stats NZ C2221 revenue series.
* **Tonnage** is not published as an official Stats NZ / MBIE fabricated-structural-steel production series in the fetched sources. The best available construction-specific proxy is Steel Construction New Zealand’s (SCNZ) industry workload data and repeated market statement that the sector turns about 100,000 tonnes of plate, hot-rolled and hollow sections into buildings and bridges through distributors, fabricators and erectors [4][5][6][7][8][9][10][11][12].
* **Top-fabricator rankings** by 2023 revenue or annual capacity could not be fully established from public Companies Office filings or annual reports in the fetched evidence. Most large NZ fabricators are privately held and do not publish detailed revenue or tonnage. The report therefore ranks only where a comparable public metric is available and flags the unestablished items rather than inventing revenue/capacity values.

## 3. Import tariffs on raw and structural steel inputs

### 3.1 Normal and preferential rates by HS heading

NZ Customs’ Working Tariff, Section XV / Chapter 72, is the relevant tariff schedule for iron and steel inputs [20][21]. For headings relevant to structural fabricators, the fetched schedule shows many lines as either **Free/Free** or **Normal 5% with Preferential Free**. Customs also states that, unless otherwise indicated, preferential rates for listed trade-agreement parties are Free, subject to origin rules [22][21].

| HS heading | Input type | Normal tariff pattern in NZ Working Tariff | Preferential tariff pattern | Relevance to structural fabricators |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 7207 | Semi-finished iron/non-alloy steel | Free | Free | Upstream semi-finished input; limited direct use by fabricators [21]. |
| 7208 | Hot-rolled flat products | Generally 5%, with some Free lines | Generally Free | Plate/flat inputs for built-up members and welded fabrications [21]. |
| 7209 | Cold-rolled flat products | Generally 5%, with some Free lines | Generally Free | Less central than plate/sections but relevant to components [21]. |
| 7210 | Coated/plated flat products | Mixed Free or 5% | Generally Free | Cladding/component inputs; not core heavy sections [21]. |
| 7211 | Narrow flat-rolled products | Mixed Free or 5% | Generally Free | Component/input category [21]. |
| 7212 | Coated narrow flat products | Mixed Free or 5% | Generally Free | Component/input category [21]. |
| 7213 | Hot-rolled bars and rods | Mixed Free or 5% | Generally Free | Bar/rod inputs; structural use depends on product [21]. |
| 7214 | Other bars and rods | Mixed Free or 5% | Generally Free | Bar inputs; some construction relevance [21]. |
| 7215 | Other bars and rods | Mixed Free or 5% | Generally Free | Component/input category [21]. |
| 7216 | Angles, shapes and sections | Many common structural sections Free; some U/equal-angle/cold-formed lines 5% | Generally Free | Core imported structural sections. Common hot-rolled I sections, H sections and other hot-rolled sections are Free; specified U, equal-angle and cold-formed lines can be 5% normal [21]. |

### 3.2 Preferential treatment and source-country relevance

Preferential tariffs materially reduce the applied duty for many imported steel inputs because NZ’s major structural-section suppliers are largely countries or territories with preferential access. WITS/Comtrade shows 2023 NZ imports of HS 7216 angles, shapes and sections at **US$55.2 million / 46,251 tonnes**, led by Australia, Taiwan, Thailand, Korea, China and Japan [23]. NZ Customs’ tariff header lists Free preferential rates for a broad set of agreement parties including Australia, China, CPTPP, Korea, Malaysia, RCEP, Singapore, Thailand, Taiwan and the UK, subject to rules of origin [22][21].

| Taiwan / Other Asia n.e.s. | US$13.8m | 11,779 t | Preferential treatment listed for TW in tariff header where conditions met [21][23]. |

### 3.3 Effective tariff impact on fabricator margins

The direct tariff burden on NZ structural fabricators is generally **low**, but not zero:

1. **Core raw sections are often duty-free.** Many common HS 7216 structural sections — including I sections, H sections and other hot-rolled sections — are Free at normal tariff, so no tariff cost arises even before preference [21].
2. **Where normal duty is 5%, preference often removes it.** For many plate/flat products and specified section lines, normal duty is 5%, but preferential rates are Free for qualifying origins [22][21]. Because major HS 7216 suppliers include Australia, Thailand, Korea, China and Taiwan, the applied duty on qualifying imports is often materially below the 5% normal rate [21][23].
3. **Margin sensitivity is still meaningful for non-preferential or non-qualifying supply.** If raw steel is assumed to account for 35–50% of a fabricator’s sales value, a 5% unrecovered duty on that input would equal **1.75–2.50% of sales**. For a contractor earning a 5% operating margin, that would absorb roughly **35–50% of operating profit** if it could not be passed through. If only half the duty is passed through, the margin hit is about **0.9–1.25% of sales**. This is an illustrative sensitivity based on the tariff schedule and input-cost-share assumption, not a disclosed sector margin [15][21].
4. **Import competition matters more than raw-input duty.** WITS shows 2023 imports into NZ of fabricated structures and parts under HS 730890 from China alone at **US$81.7 million / 50,922 tonnes**, exceeding total HS 7216 raw structural-section import tonnage of 46,251 tonnes [24][23]. That means local fabricators face a larger competitive exposure from imported fabricated steelwork than from tariffs on raw structural sections. Tariffs that are Free or preference-free reduce input costs, but they also lower landed costs for some imported competing materials and do not shield local fabrication margins from offshore fabricated-structure competition [24][23].

## 5. Strategic risks and opportunities for fabricators

### Top three risks

1. **Pipeline cyclicality and timing gaps.** The SCNZ workload proxy recovered from about 70,700 t in 2019 to about 88,500 t in 2023, but was almost flat in 2024 and below the repeated “about 100,000 t” sector statement [14][4][5][6][10]. Large projects such as CRL, Te Kaha and Penlink are material but lumpy; delays or completion cliffs can leave capacity underutilised.

2. **Import competition from fabricated structures.** Imports of HS 730890 fabricated structures and parts from China were about **50,922 t** in 2023, compared with **46,251 t** of all HS 7216 raw structural sections imported by NZ [24][23]. This indicates that offshore fabrication is not a marginal exposure; it is of comparable or greater physical scale than key raw-section imports, and it can pressure local pricing when domestic workload softens.

3. **Input-price and contract-margin exposure.** The tariff schedule itself is generally favourable, but many non-core lines carry a 5% normal tariff if preference is unavailable [21]. More importantly, steel input prices and fabricated-metal input costs can move faster than fixed-price project contracts allow, especially on long-duration infrastructure packages [15]. A 5% duty or equivalent cost shock on steel inputs representing 35–50% of sales can reduce margins by 1.75–2.50% of sales before pass-through, which is material for a low-margin contractor.

### Top three opportunities

1. **Infrastructure and complex-building packages favour certified local capability.** Category 4 SFC fabricators such as Culham, D&H and Jensen are positioned for higher-complexity buildings and infrastructure where traceability, welding quality and compliance matter [16]. Te Kaha, CRL station works and Penlink-type bridge packages all require coordination, quality systems and local installation support; the general local-fabricator capability and certification evidence is supported by D&H’s fabrication disclosure and SFC listings [17][16].

2. **Tariff-free or preference-free raw inputs can protect competitiveness.** Many common HS 7216 structural sections are Free at normal tariff, and many 5% lines become Free under preference for major source countries [22][21][23]. That gives local fabricators access to internationally sourced steel without a large tariff penalty, provided documentation and origin requirements are managed.

3. **Local-content, resilience and logistics advantages.** The sector’s construction-specific role is turning plate, hot-rolled and hollow sections into buildings and bridges through a local network of distributors, fabricators and erectors [4][5][12]. For projects where programme risk, site coordination, late design changes, seismic detailing and quality assurance are important, local fabrication can compete on responsiveness and compliance rather than on raw steel cost alone [17][16].

## Conclusion

By 2024, the New Zealand structural steel fabrication market was **stable in physical tonnes but higher in nominal value than in 2018**. The best narrow revenue proxy for C2221 rose from about **NZ$2.1 billion in 2018 to NZ$2.7 billion in 2024**, peaking near **NZ$2.8 billion in 2023**, while direct C2221 employment moved only from **4,250 to 4,450 employees** over the same period [13][3][1]. SCNZ workload data points to annual committed fabrication demand of roughly **88,000 tonnes** in 2023/24, against the industry’s broader statement of about **100,000 tonnes** processed into buildings and bridges [14][4][5][10].

Tariffs are not the main drag on local fabricator margins: common structural sections are often duty-free, and many otherwise dutiable inputs become duty-free under preference. The larger strategic issues are pipeline timing, imported fabricated steelwork, input-price pass-through and the ability of local fabricators to differentiate on certification, delivery certainty and complex project execution [16][21][24][23].
