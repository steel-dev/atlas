# Technical comparison of digital identity standards and government digital ID systems

**Bottom line.** Use **FIDO/WebAuthn** for phishing-resistant account authentication; use **W3C Verifiable Credentials/Decentralized Identifiers (VC/DID)** or the **EU European Digital Identity Wallet (EUDI Wallet)** for attribute presentation; use **Aadhaar** and **Estonia eID/e-Residency** mainly where their jurisdictional legal rails or government services require them. FIDO/WebAuthn is the strongest fit for login because its public-key credentials are scoped to a relying-party origin/RP ID and are not discoverable by other relying parties [1]. VC/DID and EUDI Wallet are better for showing facts such as age, license, address, professional status, or eligibility because they are designed around signed attestations, selective disclosure, and status mechanisms [2] [3] [4]. Aadhaar and Estonia eID provide high-adoption state identity rails, but their privacy and recovery properties are bounded by centralized state registries and national-law architecture rather than by a portable open-web trust layer [5] [6].

## Headline ranking matrix

Rank: **1 = strongest / best fit** for that dimension among the five systems; **5 = weakest / narrowest fit**.

| System | Privacy | Interoperability | Surveillance resistance | Recovery | Assurance level | Deployability | Best primary role |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| **FIDO/WebAuthn** | **1** | **1** | **1** | **2** | **2** | **1** | Phishing-resistant authentication and step-up login |
| **EU EUDI Wallet / eIDAS** | **2** | **2** | **2** | **3** | **1** | **3** | Regulated attribute presentation and cross-border EU public/private services |
| **W3C DID/VC** | **3** | **4** | **3** | **4** | **4** | **4** | Portable signed credentials where a trust registry/governance framework exists |
| **Estonia eID/e-Residency** | **4** | **3** | **4** | **1** | **1** | **2** | Estonian/EU legal transactions, company administration, signatures |
| **India Aadhaar** | **5** | **5** | **5** | **3** | **2** | **2** | Indian resident identity proofing, subsidy/service delivery, domestic KYC rails |

The ordering reflects the technical model, not a universal moral ranking: FIDO is best for authentication but does not carry rich identity attributes; VC/DID can be privacy-preserving but only when issuers, holders, wallets, status lists, and verifiers are governed correctly; and state systems deliver high assurance and legal acceptance inside their jurisdictions at the cost of centralized dependency.

Its best use is Estonian/EU legal transactions rather than universal identity portability. |

## Privacy and surveillance resistance

### W3C DID/VC

DID/VC privacy is strongest when identifiers are **subject controlled**, **pairwise**, and used only for a specific relationship or presentation. DID Core standardizes decentralized identifiers and DID documents, but it deliberately leaves method operations—creation, update, key rotation, deactivation, ledger anchoring, and service endpoints—to DID methods. That design enables pairwise DIDs, but the same flexibility creates method fragmentation: the DID ecosystem had **103 experimental DID method specs** at DID Core publication.

VC Data Model v2.0 supports privacy by separating issuer-signed claims from verifier presentation and by allowing a holder to present only relevant data when the chosen proof format supports it [2]. Bitstring Status List v1.0 reduces revocation-check correlation by placing many credentials into one compressed status list and requiring a **131,072-entry** default herd-privacy floor [3]. BBS-style unlinkable selective disclosure is promising, but as of the fetched W3C page **Data Integrity BBS Cryptosuites v1.0** was still a **Candidate Recommendation Draft dated 7 April 2026**, so production ecosystems must treat it as a profile choice rather than a settled Recommendation [9].

The main DID/VC surveillance risks are correlation and issuer dependency. Correlation occurs if the same DID, credential identifier, status URL, schema, or unique attribute set is reused across verifiers. Issuer dependency remains because a verifier generally trusts an issuer or trust registry, not the holder alone; even with holder-controlled wallets, a powerful issuer can define the credential semantics, revocation policy, and reissuance path [2] [3].

### FIDO/WebAuthn

FIDO/WebAuthn has the strongest built-in anti-phishing and anti-cross-site-correlation model among the five. WebAuthn Level 3 §1 defines strong, attested, scoped public-key credentials created at the relying party’s request with user consent; after creation, a credential can be accessed only by origins belonging to that relying party, and other relying parties cannot detect properties or even the existence of credentials scoped elsewhere [1]. Its security discussion says the authenticator/client include and sign the full requester origin in the attestation object and assertions, while credentials are also scoped to a relying-party identifier (RP ID) so the authenticator uses only credentials created for the same RP ID; registration verification checks the expected `rpIdHash` [1].

WebAuthn also has privacy-sensitive attestation controls. The attestation conveyance preference values are **`none`**, **`indirect`**, **`direct`**, and **`enterprise`**; **`none`** is the default and replaces non-self attestation with a none statement to avoid relaying identifying authenticator information to the relying party [1]. WebAuthn Level 3 also distinguishes discoverable/resident credentials, supports passkey platform authenticators, and defines backup eligibility/state flags: backup-eligible credentials are **multi-device credentials**, while non-backup-eligible credentials are **single-device credentials** [1].

The privacy trade-off is that synced passkeys shift some recovery and availability trust to a passkey provider. FIDO states passkeys are FIDO credentials using WebAuthn and CTAP, may be synced by a passkey provider or device-bound, and become available on a new device after the user signs into the same provider account [21]. Apple states iCloud Keychain passkeys sync end-to-end encrypted, require Apple Account two-factor authentication, and can be recovered even if all devices are lost via iCloud Keychain escrow/recovery [22].

### EU EUDI Wallet

The EUDI Wallet has the most explicit statutory privacy model. Article **5a(4)** requires wallet users to request, obtain, select, combine, store, delete, share, and present person-identification data and electronic attestations **under the sole control of the user**, online and where appropriate offline, while ensuring **selective disclosure** [4]. Article **5a(4)(b)** requires wallets to generate pseudonyms and store them encrypted and locally [4]. Recital **59** makes selective disclosure a basic design feature [4].

Relying-party controls are also legal obligations, not merely wallet UX. Article **5b** requires relying-party registration, identification, and authentication; it forbids relying parties from refusing pseudonyms where identification is not required by Union or national law; and it treats intermediaries as relying parties that must not store transaction content [4]. Article **45h** requires electronic-attestation-of-attributes providers not to combine attribute-service personal data with personal data from other services or commercial partners and to keep the data logically separate [4]. Implementing Regulation **(EU) 2024/2982**, Article **3(10)** requires privacy-preserving techniques ensuring unlinkability across wallet-relying parties when the attestation or person-identification data does not require identifying the wallet user [12].

The surveillance risk is therefore not absence of privacy rules, but implementation and governance: Member State wallets, access certificates, attestation providers, authentic sources, and relying-party registrars must all work correctly and be audited.

### Aadhaar

Aadhaar is the least surveillance-resistant design in this comparison because it is a centralized resident identity and authentication system built around a unique number, central repository, biometrics, and authentication logs [5]. The Supreme Court’s **Justice K.S. Puttaswamy (Retd.) v Union of India** Aadhaar judgment is dated **26 September 2018** [14]. It limited the system by striking or reading down uses including private-sector Section **57** use that lacked adequate legal basis, and it held that authentication records should not be kept beyond **six months**, invalidating five-year archiving for UIDAI authentication transaction data in the earlier form [14].

The fetched 2021 regulations reflect a mixed retention picture: requesting entities and Authentication Service Agencies maintain authentication logs for **2 years**, then archive for **5 years** or longer if law, court order, or pending dispute requires; they may not retain PID information [15]. UIDAI retains authentication transaction data for **6 months**, must not store the purpose of authentication or non-process metadata, and then deletes it unless a High-Court-judge-level order or pending dispute requires retention [15]. Offline options—QR Code verification and Aadhaar Paperless Offline e-KYC—reduce online-call dependency for some use cases, but they do not change the centralized enrolment and biometric architecture [23] [15].

Known exclusion risks are real in the system design: UIDAI itself recognizes authentication can fail because of poor fingerprint quality, network availability, and other technological/biometric limits, and advises alternate mechanisms such as face, iris, OTP, or other service-provider methods [23].

### Estonia eID / e-Residency

Estonia’s model is privacy-stronger than Aadhaar for transactional transparency but still built on mandatory national digital identity architecture. X-Road is the integration layer for public-sector and private-sector data exchange, and Estonian eID is a national PKI credential used for authentication and signatures [20]. Estonia also has citizen-facing transparency over data access through official e-services, and X-Road-style auditing provides institutional traceability for data exchanges [20].

The main surveillance-resistance limitation is structural: Estonian residents are in a mandatory national-ID ecosystem, while e-residents receive a limited digital ID for remote Estonian services. e-Residency is expressly not citizenship, tax residency, physical residency, or immigration status [19]. A mature national PKI and audit trail can deter misuse, but it does not remove the centrality of the state registry and certificate infrastructure.

## Recovery mechanisms and loss handling

| System | Recovery / revocation model | Concrete limitation |
|---|---|---|
| **DID/VC** | DID recovery is **method-specific**. DID Core allows DID documents to express verification methods and service endpoints, but update, rotation, recovery, and deactivation are defined by the DID method, not by DID Core itself. VC recovery is generally wallet/issuer based: a lost holder wallet needs backup, reissuance by the issuer, or social/cloud recovery implemented by the wallet provider; revoked or suspended VCs are reflected through mechanisms such as Bitstring Status List [3]. | `did:key` is simple and portable but has no external update/recovery channel once the private key is lost; `did:web` can rotate by updating a web-hosted DID document but depends on DNS/TLS/web-host control; ledger/network methods such as `did:ion` or peer methods can support richer rotation but add network, anchor, or relationship-state dependencies. |
| **FIDO/WebAuthn** | WebAuthn defines backup eligibility/state and single-device vs multi-device credentials, but account recovery is not solved by WebAuthn itself [1]. FIDO passkeys may be synced by a passkey provider or device-bound; a new device receives synced passkeys after sign-in to the same provider account, and hardware security keys can act as recovery credentials [21]. Apple’s iCloud Keychain implementation uses end-to-end encryption, Apple Account 2FA, sponsorship from an existing device or keychain recovery, and escrow recovery if all devices are lost [22]. | Recovery strength depends on Apple/Google/Microsoft/third-party passkey-provider account recovery, enterprise policy, and whether the credential is synced or device-bound; direct/enterprise attestation can raise assurance but reduces privacy relative to `none` attestation [1]. |
| **EU EUDI Wallet** | eIDAS requires wallets to support requesting, storing, deleting, sharing, and presenting PID and electronic attestations under user control; Member State provisioning, certification, relying-party registration, and lifecycle procedures are defined through eIDAS and implementing acts [4] [13]. | Wallet revocation/re-issuance details are national wallet-provider procedures within the eIDAS framework; the central legal obligation is to provide wallets and certified ecosystem components, not a single EU-wide user-support flow [4]. |
| **Aadhaar** | Users can update demographic and biometric information through UIDAI processes; authentication can fall back to alternate modalities such as face, iris, OTP, or other service-provider verification where fingerprints fail [23]. Offline QR/Paperless Offline e-KYC can be used where online authentication is unnecessary [23] [15]. | The Aadhaar number and biometric enrolment are persistent; recovery is not a private-key recovery problem but a correction/update/exception-handling problem in a centralized system. |
| **Estonia eID/e-Residency** | Estonian ID-card credentials are PKI certificates; compromised or lost cards can be suspended/revoked and replaced through official ID-card/e-Residency procedures [16] [24]. | Recovery is strong because the state can revoke and reissue, but it is jurisdictional and certificate-authority dependent; a non-resident e-resident’s credential does not substitute for residence or citizenship status [19]. |

## Interoperability and deployability anchors

**FIDO/WebAuthn ranks first for deployability** because WebAuthn is built into major browsers/platforms and passkeys are described by FIDO as supported in all major operating systems, internet browsers, and by third-party passkey providers [21]. CTAP covers authenticator-to-client transports such as USB, NFC, and BLE in the FIDO2 stack [11]. The FIDO Metadata Service is the trust anchor for authenticator metadata and certification decisions in higher-assurance deployments, while the WebAuthn API lets ordinary web services deploy without becoming identity issuers.

**EUDI Wallet ranks second for legal interoperability inside the EU** because eIDAS creates cross-border recognition and wallet-acceptance obligations. Article **5a(1)** sets Member State wallet provision, Article **5b** sets relying-party registration and authentication, and Article **5f(2)** sets a **36-month** acceptance obligation for specified strong-auth private relying parties [4]. Its adoption is still rollout-dependent because key obligations run from implementing acts and relying-party registration under Implementing Regulation **(EU) 2025/848** applies from **24 December 2026** [13].

**Estonia ranks high inside its legal ecosystem** because its eID/e-Residency credential, eIDAS-notified scheme, and X-Road integrations support real public and private transactions, and the e-Residency program had **140,000+** e-residents, **41,800+** e-resident-established companies, and **100+** Marketplace service providers as of **9 June 2026** [16] [18] [20]. It is less portable outside Estonian/EU legal contexts.

**DID/VC interoperability is conditional.** DID Core’s **103 experimental DID method specifications** at Recommendation publication show both innovation and fragmentation. VC Data Model v2.0 standardizes the credential model and recognizes JOSE/COSE and Data Integrity securing mechanisms, but issuer-verifier trust registries, schemas, proof profiles, status-list policies, and wallet conformance remain ecosystem choices [2] [3].

**Aadhaar interoperability is domestic.** Aadhaar APIs and offline verification options are designed for Indian services and Indian resident identity proofing, not cross-border identity federation [5] [15]. Aadhaar can be extremely deployable inside India, but it is not a general-purpose international standard.

## Known failure and abuse cases

| System | Failure / abuse pattern | Evidence and scale where grounded |
|---|---|---|
| **Aadhaar** | Exclusion from biometric or network failure; surveillance concern from centralized authentication logs. | UIDAI acknowledges poor fingerprint quality, network availability, and other biometric/technological limits and advises alternate authentication processes [23]. The Supreme Court judgment dated **26 September 2018** held six-month UIDAI authentication-record retention sufficient and invalidated five-year archiving in the earlier form [14]. |
| **Estonia eID** | Cryptographic vulnerability in national ID-card certificates. | Estonia’s 2017 ID-card ROCA incident involved suspension/revocation/replacement at national scale; this illustrates that centralized PKI gives strong reissuance powers but also creates systemic cryptographic monoculture risk [20]. |
| **FIDO/WebAuthn** | Phishing-resistance depends on correct origin/RP-ID validation and secure relying-party origins. | WebAuthn signs origin-bound client data and requires RP origin validation, which blocks ordinary credential replay to a phishing origin; relying parties are warned against unsafe subdomain/origin acceptance [1]. |
| **DID/VC** | Correlation and issuer-dependency risks. | Reuse of identifiers, credential IDs, status URLs, or overly specific attribute bundles can correlate presentations; issuer trust and revocation/status policy remain external governance dependencies [2] [3]. |
| **EUDI Wallet** | Relying-party overcollection or tracking if legal/technical controls are weakly implemented. | eIDAS addresses this directly with selective disclosure, pseudonyms, relying-party registration, non-storage duties for intermediaries, logical separation duties for attribute providers, and unlinkability requirements in Implementing Regulation **2024/2982 Article 3(10)** [12] [4]. |

## Use-case decision analysis

### Banking and financial services

**Preferred pattern:** FIDO/WebAuthn for login and transaction step-up; EUDI Wallet or VC for KYC attributes; Aadhaar or Estonia eID only where law or bank policy requires those rails.

FATF-style digital identity guidance treats reliable digital ID as relevant to customer due diligence, but banks still need risk-based customer identification, verification, sanctions/AML screening, recordkeeping, and ongoing monitoring rather than mere possession of a credential [25]. FIDO/WebAuthn should be the authentication layer because it is origin-bound and phishing-resistant [1]. For EU banking, EUDI Wallet has a direct statutory path because Article **5f(2)** includes banking and financial services among sectors that must accept wallets within the **36-month** deadline when the user voluntarily requests it [4]. For India, Aadhaar e-KYC/Yes-No authentication can be appropriate where Indian law and UIDAI rules permit; fallback should include non-Aadhaar KYC documents and exception handling where authentication fails [23] [15]. For Estonian companies and EU-qualified signatures, Estonia eID/e-Residency is appropriate where Estonian corporate administration or eIDAS-recognized signatures are required [19] [18].

### Healthcare

**Preferred pattern:** FIDO/WebAuthn for clinician/patient portal authentication; EUDI Wallet or VC for minimum necessary attributes; avoid persistent universal identifiers unless required by law.

Healthcare identity systems should minimize attributes because HIPAA’s minimum-necessary rule requires covered entities to make reasonable efforts to limit protected health information to the minimum necessary, and GDPR treats health and biometric data as special-category data subject to stricter processing conditions. FIDO/WebAuthn protects account access without disclosing identity attributes to the authenticator vendor or other relying parties [1]. EUDI Wallet’s selective disclosure and pseudonym support are well aligned with proving insurance eligibility, professional license, age, or residency without oversharing [4]. VC/DID can do the same in non-EU or cross-institution contexts if the healthcare network defines issuer trust, schemas, revocation/status, and emergency-access governance [2] [3]. Aadhaar-style centralized identifiers are the least privacy-favorable default for healthcare because medical history is expressly excluded from Aadhaar demographic information, yet authentication still depends on a central ID rail [5].

### Humanitarian aid, refugees, and stateless populations

**Preferred pattern:** privacy-preserving VC credentials with offline verification and plural recovery paths; avoid making aid conditional on one biometric or one state-issued credential.

For stateless or refugee populations, the central requirement is often proving eligibility, family linkage, vaccination, training, or aid entitlement without exposing people to state surveillance, hostile actors, or exclusion if a biometric fails. DID/VC can be the best technical fit when credentials are issued by trusted humanitarian organizations, stored in user-controlled or custodian-assisted wallets, and verified offline with minimal disclosure and status-list privacy [2] [3]. EUDI Wallet may help EU residents and recognized refugees inside EU legal systems, but it presupposes Member State wallet access and legal identity issuance [4]. Aadhaar and Estonia eID are poor humanitarian defaults outside their jurisdictions: Aadhaar is for Indian residents and depends on UIDAI rails [5], while e-Residency is not residence, citizenship, or travel status [19]. Fallbacks should include paper credentials, offline QR or signed PDFs, community attestations, assisted custody, and non-biometric exception processes, mirroring UIDAI’s own recognition that biometric/network authentication can fail and must have alternate processes [23].

## Final recommendations

1. **For authentication, choose FIDO/WebAuthn.** It has the best combination of privacy, phishing resistance, interoperability, and deployability: per-RP public-key credentials, origin/RP-ID binding, default `none` attestation, and passkey support across major platforms [1] [21]. Use synced passkeys for consumer recovery and device-bound/security-key credentials for high-assurance or regulated roles; require at least two enrolled authenticators or a hardware security key recovery path for administrators and high-risk users [21] [22].

2. **For attribute presentation, choose EUDI Wallet in the EU and VC/DID elsewhere when governance exists.** EUDI Wallet has the clearest legal obligations—Articles **5a**, **5b**, **5f**, and **45h**, plus Implementing Regulation **2024/2982 Article 3(10)**—for selective disclosure, relying-party registration, pseudonym support, non-tracking, and regulated-sector acceptance [12] [4]. VC/DID is more globally flexible but needs a trust registry, credential schemas, proof-format profile, status-list policy, and recovery policy to avoid fragmentation [2] [3].

3. **Use Aadhaar and Estonia eID only where their legal rails are the point.** Aadhaar is appropriate for Indian resident identity proofing, UIDAI-regulated authentication, domestic benefits, and legally permitted e-KYC, with offline XML/QR and exception handling where possible [23] [15]. Estonia eID/e-Residency is appropriate for Estonian company administration, Estonian public services, and eIDAS-recognized EU transactions, but e-Residency does not create citizenship, residence, or immigration status [19].

4. **Fallback paths by sector:**
 - **Banking:** if EUDI Wallet/VC attributes are unavailable, use jurisdictionally accepted documentary KYC plus FIDO/WebAuthn for account security; in India, use Aadhaar only where permitted and provide non-Aadhaar alternatives and exception handling [23] [15] [25].
 - **Healthcare:** if wallet credentials are unavailable, use institutional identity proofing and FIDO/WebAuthn login while collecting only the HIPAA/GDPR-minimum attribute set needed for care or payment.
 - **Humanitarian aid:** if DID/VC wallets are unavailable, use paper/offline signed credentials, assisted custody, community attestations, and non-biometric exceptions; do not make food, shelter, or healthcare contingent on one online biometric match [23].

Overall, **FIDO/WebAuthn should be the default authentication layer; EUDI Wallet or well-governed VC/DID should be the attribute layer; Aadhaar and Estonia eID should be treated as powerful jurisdiction-specific identity rails, not general replacements for open, privacy-preserving credential presentation.**
