# Memristor neuromorphic-computing debate: adjudication and implementation comparison

**Bottom line.** Chua’s 1971 *IEEE Transactions on Circuit Theory* memristor is a mathematically defined two-terminal fourth element whose constitutive relation is a charge–flux law, **dφ = M(q)dq**, giving **v = M(q)i** [1]. HP Labs’ 2008 TiO₂ device was an important nanoscale **memristive/RRAM** demonstration and plausibly useful for dense nonvolatile conductance storage, but the strongest reading of the later debate is that commercial RRAM/memristor products should be treated as **generalized memristive systems or resistive switches**, not as verified ideal Chua fourth elements. For deployment, use memristor/RRAM crossbars where dense, low-precision in-memory multiply–accumulate and local/online adaptation dominate; use CMOS or hybrid CMOS–RRAM when high-accuracy, calibrated, reliable training and production inference dominate.

## 1. What Chua predicted and what HP claimed in 2008

Chua’s article is **Leon O. Chua, “Memristor—The Missing Circuit Element,” IEEE Transactions on Circuit Theory, vol. 18, no. 5, pp. 507–519, September 1971**. It defines charge and flux linkage as **q(t)=∫i(t)dt** and **φ(t)=∫v(t)dt**, and introduces the missing two-terminal relation **dφ = M(q)dq**, hence **v = M(q)i**, as the fourth basic circuit element relation complementing resistor, capacitor and inductor relations [1].

HP Labs’ paper is **Dmitri B. Strukov, Gregory S. Snider, Duncan R. Stewart and R. Stanley Williams, “The missing memristor found,” Nature 453:80–83, 1 May 2008, DOI 10.1038/nature06932** [2]. The device was a Pt/oxide/Pt cross-point with a thin TiO₂/TiO₂−x film: one region was insulating TiO₂ and the oxygen-deficient TiO₂−x region contained mobile oxygen vacancies acting as positively charged dopants, shifting the internal boundary under bias [2]. HP’s paper stated that “until now there has not been a material realization of a memristor,” and presented this TiO₂ device as that realization, while also naming “ultradense, semi-non-volatile memories and learning networks that require a synapse-like function” as applications [2].

The 2008 HP state model is the core of the technical claim. With normalized state **w/D**, the device memristance is:

| Quantity | HP 2008 expression |
|---|---|
| State | **w/D**, where *w* is the doped-region width and *D* the device thickness |
| Memristance | **M(w)=R_ON(w/D)+R_OFF(1−w/D)** |
| Terminal equation | **v(t)=i(t)** |
| Linear drift | **dw/dt=μᵥR_ON i(t)/D** |
| Charge relation | **w(t)=μᵥR_ONq(t)/D**, and for **R_ON≪R_OFF**, **M(q)=R_OFF** |

That model is why the neuromorphic analogy was attractive: a nonvolatile conductance state can encode a synaptic weight, and a crossbar of such devices can compute weighted sums locally by Ohm’s and Kirchhoff’s laws while updating weights near the synapse. HP’s own 2008 application language was narrower than later popular accounts—it pointed to synapse-like learning networks rather than proving a full brain-like computer [2].

## 2. The Vongehr–Meng critique and the Strukov/Williams line of response

The exact 2015 critique is **Sascha Vongehr and Xiangkang Meng, “The Missing Memristor has Not been Found,” Scientific Reports 5, Article 11657, published 25 June 2015, DOI 10.1038/srep11657**; the wording “has Not Not been Found” is not the article title [3]. Its thesis is not that TiO₂, RRAM or ReRAM devices cannot switch resistance. It is that the HP/RRAM class had not demonstrated Chua’s ideal fourth element as originally hypothesized.

The critique’s main technical objections are threefold:

- **Pinched hysteresis is not sufficient.** A zero-crossing pinched I–V loop can occur in a wide class of nonlinear dynamical resistive systems, so it is not by itself proof of Chua’s ideal memristor [3].
- **The charge–flux relation was not independently demonstrated.** Chua’s defining relation is between **q** and **φ**, whereas many RRAM demonstrations report voltage-current hysteresis and resistance states rather than an independently verified single-valued **φ(q)** law [3].
- **RRAM is better classified as a memristive system/resistive switch.** Vongehr & Meng distinguish a perfect/ideal memristor **M(Q)** from generalized memristive systems **M(Q,I)** and argue that HP-type devices fall into the latter family or into pre-existing resistive-switching technology [3].

There is no formal Strukov/Williams reply article cited here with a distinct title, date and venue responding to Vongehr & Meng. The HP-side position grounded in Strukov/Williams’ own 2008 paper is instead a definitional distinction: the paper first states the ideal current-controlled memristor equations **v=R(w)i**, **dw/dt=i**, then explicitly invokes Chua and Kang’s broader 1976 **memristive systems** equations **v=R(w,i)i**, **dw/dt=f(w,i)** before modeling practical TiO₂ switching [2][12]. In that framing, a TiO₂ RRAM cell is useful because its resistance depends on an electrically changed internal state; it need not behave as a mathematically perfect **M(q)** element in every operating regime to serve as a nonvolatile analog device [2][4].

## 3. Commercial status: products are RRAM/memristive devices, not ideal-Chua claims

As of the stated date, the cited commercial examples are **Knowm SDC/MSS-style memristor products** and **Crossbar/ReRAM-type resistive-memory products or IP**. Knowm’s own product material describes **Self Directed Channel (SDC) memristors** as metal-ion conducting electrochemical-metallization-type devices relying on Ag⁺ motion/agglomeration in channels, and Knowm announced commercial products in 2015 including discrete memristor chips, a BEOL CMOS+memristor service, SENSE/Application Servers, the Knowm API and Thermodynamic RAM/kT-RAM development support [13][14]. That is a practical device/product claim, not an independently demonstrated ideal-Chua-fourth-element claim; the ReRAM literature likewise treats commercial ReRAM as resistive memory or computing-in-memory technology, not as a verified ideal **M(q)** element [4][15].

## 6. Adjudication: what the field can and cannot claim

The strongest synthesis is a two-level verdict.

First, **ideal-Chua-element validity** remains unproven for commercial memristors/RRAM. Chua’s element is defined by a constitutive **φ(q)** relation, **dφ=M(q)dq** and **v=M(q)i** [1]. HP’s 2008 model maps a TiO₂ oxygen-vacancy state to charge-controlled resistance and was historically important [2]. But Vongehr & Meng’s critique is persuasive against the overstatement that pinched I–V hysteresis or useful resistive switching alone proves the 1971 ideal element [3]. The practical devices should therefore be called **memristive systems**, **RRAM/ReRAM**, or **resistive switches** unless an experiment independently establishes the relevant ideal charge–flux law.

Second, **generalized-memristive-device usefulness** is real but conditional. Memristor/RRAM physics naturally gives nonvolatile analog conductance, dense two-terminal cells and local electrical update mechanisms, which are valuable for low-precision in-memory MAC and some local-learning schemes [2][5]. Yet the same physics brings stochastic filament formation, nonlinear conductance updates, limited write precision, stuck-at faults, endurance/retention trade-offs, sneak paths, line resistance and peripheral-converter overhead [7][4][10]. The best 2020–2024 systems win by co-design—selectors or rectifying devices for sneak paths, differential mapping for signed weights, calibration/training-aware compensation for variation, and CMOS periphery for control—not by relying on an ideal memristor law alone [7][5][19].

**Choose memristor crossbars** when the target is dense, low-precision in-memory inference or local adaptation where approximate analog weights are acceptable and the workload can tolerate calibration, quantization and retraining. **Choose CMOS or hybrid CMOS–RRAM** when the target is high-accuracy reliable training/deployment, robust software tooling, precise updates, long product qualification, or workloads such as high-accuracy CIFAR/ImageNet-class CNNs where peripheral overhead and error correction are decisive. In short: memristor physics enables useful synaptic devices, but it has not displaced CMOS as the general-purpose substrate for reliable neuromorphic computing.
