# UCC acceptance, rejection, and cure analysis

## Bottom line

On the assumed record, Vendor’s shipment was nonconforming because the October 15 purchase order specified letter-size paper, while the November 8 delivery consisted of 500 legal-size reams. Article 2’s perfect-tender rule, not common-law substantial performance, therefore initially gave Buyer a right to reject a nonconforming tender. But Buyer did not exercise that right in the UCC manner. After a reasonable opportunity to inspect an obvious size variance, Buyer stored the shipment, consumed 180 reams, and custom-printed the remaining 320 reams with its letterhead/logo before demanding a refund 22 days after delivery. Those acts are inconsistent with Vendor’s ownership and are acceptance under UCC § 2-606; they also make Buyer’s November 30 attempted rejection ineffective under UCC § 2-602. Vendor should prevail on summary judgment on Buyer’s claimed right to reject and recover the price, while Buyer’s only remaining path is an accepted-goods damages claim if it gave adequate breach notice and can prove nonconformity damages under §§ 2-607 and 2-714.

Vendor’s five-business-day exchange offer strengthens, but is not the primary reason for, this result. Section 2-508 cure is triggered by a buyer’s rejection of nonconforming goods; here the stronger conclusion is that Buyer had already accepted all 500 reams before the purported rejection. If a court nevertheless treated November 30 as a timely rejection of some stock, Vendor’s prompt offer to substitute letter-size paper within five business days would be a reasonable cure proposal on this record, and Buyer’s refusal would further undercut rescission.

## Controlling legal framework

Article 2 starts with the perfect-tender rule. UCC § 2-601 provides that, unless Article 2’s installment-contract and contract-remedy exceptions apply, “if the goods or the tender of delivery fail in any respect to conform to the contract,” the buyer may reject the whole, accept the whole, or accept any commercial unit and reject the rest . “Conforming” goods are those “in accordance with the obligations under the contract,” so conformity is measured by the contract’s actual specifications, not merely by market equivalence . This is materially different from common-law substantial performance: Nolo describes common law as often treating substantial, nonperfect performance as nonbreaching, whereas UCC sales law demands “perfect tender” of goods that meet the contract terms [1]. The Washburn Law Journal similarly states that substantial performance does not apply to contracts for the sale of goods and that § 2-601 is the UCC’s perfect-tender rule [2].

Here, the operative contract evidence points to letter-size paper. The October 15 purchase order specified letter-size copy paper, while the accompanying wording also used the broader phrase “standard office paper” without defining dimensions. Under ordinary interpretation principles, the document’s language controls, ambiguity exists only if a reasonable reader could interpret the wording more than one way, and general words following or accompanying specifics are commonly construed consistently with the specific class or item identified [3] [4] [5]. Letter and legal are distinct U.S. office-paper sizes: letter is 8.5 × 11 inches, while legal is 8.5 × 14 inches [6] [7]. Thus, even if legal-size paper has comparable market value, a legal-size shipment does not match a letter-size purchase-order specification.

That nonconformity, however, did not leave Buyer free to reject indefinitely. UCC § 2-513 gives a buyer a right, before payment or acceptance, to inspect tendered or delivered goods at a reasonable place, time, and manner [8]. UCC § 2-602 then requires rejection within a reasonable time after delivery or tender and makes rejection ineffective unless the buyer seasonably notifies the seller; after rejection, any buyer exercise of ownership over a commercial unit is wrongful against the seller, and a buyer in possession must hold the goods with reasonable care at the seller’s disposition [9].

Acceptance is defined separately in § 2-606. A buyer accepts goods when, after a reasonable opportunity to inspect, it signifies that goods are conforming or that it will keep them despite nonconformity; fails to make an effective rejection after reasonable opportunity to inspect; or does any act inconsistent with the seller’s ownership [10]. Acceptance of part of a commercial unit is acceptance of the whole unit [10]. Quarles’ UCC summary gives the same three routes to acceptance and identifies “using the goods after rejecting them” as an example of conduct inconsistent with the seller’s ownership [11]. Courts likewise treat continued business use as acceptance or re-acceptance: in W.M. Hobbs, continued use of a copier as an integral business tool, totaling about 60,000 copies by the summary-judgment stage, made a prima facie showing of re-acceptance and the buyer’s business-efficiency explanation did not create a genuine issue; in Hooper Handling, complaints about nonconforming shelving/mezzanine were treated as cure requests rather than clear rejection, and continued use constituted acceptance [12] [13].

Once acceptance occurs, the remedy structure changes. Under § 2-607, the buyer must pay the contract rate for accepted goods; acceptance precludes rejection, although it does not by itself waive other Article 2 remedies for nonconformity. The buyer must notify the seller of breach within a reasonable time after discovering or when it should have discovered the breach, and bears the burden of establishing breach as to accepted goods [14]. Under § 2-714, a buyer that accepted goods and gave § 2-607 notice may recover damages for nonconformity measured by ordinary-course loss determined reasonably; warranty damages are usually the difference between the value as warranted and as accepted at the time and place of acceptance, plus incidental and consequential damages where appropriate [15]. Section 2-715 defines incidental damages to include reasonable inspection, receipt, transportation, care/custody, cover, and similar breach-related expenses, and consequential damages to include losses from needs the seller had reason to know at contracting and that could not reasonably be prevented by cover or otherwise [16].

Finally, Article 2’s cure rule softens perfect tender. If nonconforming goods are rejected and the contract time has not expired, § 2-508(1) lets the seller seasonably notify the buyer of intent to cure and make conforming delivery within the contract time. If the seller had reasonable grounds to believe the tender would be acceptable, § 2-508(2) allows seasonable notice and a further reasonable time to substitute a conforming tender [17]. T.W. Oil explains that § 2-508(2) can permit a good-faith seller, objectively reasonably expecting acceptance, to cure within a reasonable period beyond the performance time after proper rejection; the inquiry incorporates good faith and commercial fair dealing [18].

## Application to the paper delivery

The key facts are undisputed for purposes of this analysis:

| Event / fact | Legal significance |
|---|---|
| October 15 purchase order specified letter-size copy paper; contract also said “standard office paper” without dimensions. | The specific letter-size term defines conformity; the undefined general phrase does not erase the specific size requirement. |
| November 8, 2024: Vendor delivered 500 reams to Buyer’s office, all legal size rather than letter size. | Nonconforming tender under § 2-601 because the goods failed to conform in at least one respect. |
| Office manager accepted delivery and stored the reams in the supply closet. | Receipt/storage alone could be consistent with inspection or temporary holding, but it started Buyer’s opportunity to inspect. |
| Over about three weeks, staff used 180 reams for internal drafts and overflow projects. | Consumption of 36% of the shipment is ordinary ownership use, not inspection. |
| Buyer printed company letterhead/logo on the other 320 reams. | Custom alteration of the remaining 64% made those reams Buyer-specific and inconsistent with Vendor retaining ownership. |
| November 30: Buyer demanded return of payment and refused Vendor’s offer to exchange remaining stock for letter-size paper within five business days. | Attempted rejection came 22 calendar days after delivery and after 100% of the shipment had been consumed or materially altered. |

The initial nonconformity is straightforward. Letter and legal paper differ by three inches in length, and the purchase order’s letter-size specification was not satisfied by legal-size paper [6] [7]. Vendor’s “equivalent value” argument does not defeat nonconformity under § 2-601 because perfect tender asks whether the goods conform to the contract, not whether the substitute goods are substantially equivalent in market value  [1].

Buyer’s problem is acceptance. A reasonable inspection of copy paper size requires little more than checking the packaging or a sample sheet; § 2-513 protects that inspection right, but it does not authorize weeks of operational consumption and customization as if Buyer owned the goods [8]. The office manager’s initial receipt and storage might have been neutral if promptly followed by inspection and notice. But the later conduct was not neutral:

- **180 reams used:** Buyer consumed 36% of the delivery in its own internal drafts and overflow projects. That is a productive use of the goods for Buyer’s business, not sampling, testing, preservation, or holding for Vendor.
- **320 reams printed with letterhead/logo:** Buyer altered the remaining 64% by adding its standard letterhead and logo. Although no on-point case was located specifically about printing a logo on paper, § 2-606(1)(c) does not require resale or destruction; it asks whether the act is inconsistent with the seller’s ownership [10]. Custom-printing all remaining reams made them Buyer-branded stock and materially less returnable as Vendor’s inventory.
- **All 500 affected before notice:** By November 30, 180 + 320 reams—100% of the shipment—had either been consumed or altered. That conduct is stronger evidence of acceptance than merely storing goods pending inspection.

Those facts fit both § 2-606(1)(b) and § 2-606(1)(c). Buyer failed to make an effective rejection after a reasonable opportunity to inspect, and it performed acts inconsistent with Vendor’s ownership [10]. They also conflict with § 2-602’s post-rejection obligations, because a buyer that rejects must seasonably notify the seller and hold goods with reasonable care at the seller’s disposition rather than exercise ownership over them [9]. Quarles’ example—using goods after rejecting them—is directly analogous as to the 180 consumed reams, and W.M. Hobbs and Hooper Handling support treating continued business use as acceptance notwithstanding complaints or asserted operational need [11] [12] [13].

The employee complaints about reformatting documents do not change the acceptance analysis. They may show Buyer noticed inconvenience or nonconformity, but notice to internal staff is not seasonable notice of rejection to Vendor under § 2-602 [9]. Nor does workflow inconvenience make Buyer’s use merely preservational; using paper for drafts and overflow projects depleted the goods, and branding the rest converted generic paper into Buyer-specific inventory.

## Cure and the refused exchange offer

Vendor’s offer was to exchange the remaining stock for letter-size paper within five business days after Buyer’s November 30 demand. If the offer was made on November 30, five business days would run to Friday, December 6, 2024. A five-business-day substitution of fungible office paper is facially prompt.

Strictly speaking, however, § 2-508 cure presupposes a rejection of nonconforming goods [17]. Because Buyer had accepted the shipment before November 30, Vendor did not need § 2-508 to defeat rejection. Buyer’s acceptance already precluded rejection under § 2-607 [14]. The cure offer is therefore best treated as a mitigation or accommodation that Buyer could not convert into a renewed right to rescind.

If the court were to assume, contrary to the stronger view, that Buyer still had a live rejection right on November 30 as to some stock, Vendor’s offer would be important. Section 2-508 allows a seller with reasonable grounds to believe the tender acceptable to seasonably offer a conforming substitute within a further reasonable time [17]. T.W. Oil emphasizes that this cure right is tested objectively and in light of good faith/commercial fair dealing [18]. The facts that the contract used the general phrase “standard office paper,” that legal paper is a common U.S. office-paper format, and that Vendor offered a quick exchange support reasonable grounds and seasonable cure. Buyer’s outright refusal of that cure would make rescission even less appropriate.

## Summary-judgment assessment

Rule 56 permits summary judgment only when there is no genuine dispute of material fact and the movant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law; a factual dispute is material only if it could affect the outcome, and the nonmovant must point to sufficient evidence rather than speculation [19] [20]. Applying that standard, the material facts for rejection/acceptance are the contract specification, delivered size, inspection opportunity, timing of notice, and Buyer’s use or alteration of the goods.

Those facts are not meaningfully disputed on the stated record. Vendor’s incomplete delivery records and missing packing slips for four other accounts are potential credibility or recordkeeping concerns, but they do not create a material dispute about this delivery if Buyer admits it received 500 legal-size reams on November 8 and then used or altered all 500 before November 30. Missing slips for other accounts would matter if identity, quantity, date, or contents of this shipment were genuinely disputed; on the stated facts, they do not alter the § 2-606 acceptance analysis.

Buyer’s workflow-disruption claim is also insufficient to defeat summary judgment on rejection. It may be relevant to accepted-goods damages, but Buyer cannot quantify hours lost. Sections 2-714 and 2-715 allow recovery for reasonably determined ordinary-course loss and proper incidental/consequential damages after acceptance, but the buyer bears the burden of establishing breach and damages for accepted goods [14] [15] [16]. An unquantified assertion of wasted staff time does not create a triable issue on whether Buyer timely rejected or accepted the paper.

Accordingly, the strongest summary-judgment ruling is:

1. **Nonconformity:** Legal-size paper did not conform to the letter-size purchase-order term under the perfect-tender rule.
2. **No rightful rejection:** Buyer’s November 30 refund demand was too late and ineffective after 22 days of possession and after all 500 reams had been consumed or custom-altered.
3. **Acceptance:** Buyer accepted the shipment under § 2-606 by failing to reject after a reasonable opportunity to inspect and by acts inconsistent with Vendor’s ownership.
4. **Payment and residual remedies:** Buyer must pay the contract rate for accepted goods under § 2-607, but may pursue any properly noticed and provable nonconformity damages under §§ 2-607, 2-714, and 2-715.
5. **Cure:** Vendor’s five-business-day exchange offer is not necessary to establish acceptance, but if a rejection right survived, the offer would likely qualify as a seasonable, reasonable cure proposal that Buyer could not simply refuse while demanding a full refund.

Thus, Buyer did not rightfully reject. On summary judgment, Buyer must accept the goods subject, at most, to accepted-goods damages and to Vendor’s proposed exchange/cure for any unconsumed stock; Buyer is not entitled to rescission or return of the full payment on these facts.
