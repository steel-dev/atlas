### Tandoor ovens

Tandoor ovens should also be standardized by specification. Trutec Singapore’s tandoori oven provides a useful benchmark: **304 stainless steel** body and cover, insulated ceramic chamber, reinforcement bands, 10 stainless skewers, optional gas or charcoal heat, gas burner up to **20 kW / 68,243 Btu/h**, optional downdraft exhaust, and round/square chamber formats [11]. MFK Indonesia’s Round Tandoor Oven TO600 is another benchmark: **565 × 810 mm**, **21 kW**, **75 MJ burner**, LPG and natural gas, and **150 kg** weight [12]. The chain should specify:

- stainless external surfaces and insulated ceramic chamber for heat retention and operator safety;
- gas as the default where local approvals permit, with charcoal only where the concept requires it and ventilation/fire controls are designed for it;
- direct hood or downdraft capture interface, especially for open kitchens;
- removable skewers, accessible ash/grease areas, and cleanable floor junctions around the unit.

### Grills and charbroilers

For gas charbroilers, Daneng provides concrete capacity benchmarks: **3-burner 45,000 Btu/h** and **5-burner 75,000 Btu/h** LPG/city-gas models in countertop or freestanding versions [9]. Electrolux Professional’s modular platform can cover more standardized grills, griddles, fryers, and hot-line equipment through its e-XP 700/900 and thermaline ranges [27][21].

The best approach is split: use Electrolux modular cooking where repeatability and service commonality matter most, and use local grill/charbroiler suppliers where the menu depends on live-fire character, charcoal, or regional gas formats.

## 3. Ventilation for tropical, high-heat kitchens

Ventilation is the most important cross-city engineering standard because tropical kitchens pay twice for poor capture: heat and grease enter the workspace, and conditioned replacement air is exhausted.

Halton is the strongest cited specialist for hotel-scale kitchen ventilation in the target region. Its Singapore references include Shangri-La The Line in 2009 and Parkroyal Collection Marina Bay’s Peppermint in 2021; its Jakarta reference includes Grand Hyatt Jakarta’s Sumire in 2018 [5][13][6]. Halton states that Capture Jet technology can reduce exhaust airflow rates by as much as **50%**, while KSA Cyclonic Grease Filter Technology is positioned to reduce cleaning cost and energy consumption and improve hygiene/fire safety; UV Capture Ray neutralises grease released by cooking appliances [5][6]. GIF ActiveVent is a strong alternative for ventilated ceilings, covering production/hot kitchen, dishwashing, servery, prep/cold kitchen, and large-scale kitchens such as inflight catering operations up to **120,000 meals/day** with a reversible modular structure for hygiene [7].

## 7. Final recommendation and implementation standard

The chain should choose **Singapore as the pilot engineering template**, then adapt utility connections and vendor service locally. Singapore has the clearest cited requirements for fume treatment, outdoor discharge, annual specialist exhaust degreasing, and inspection records [14][18]. Build the standard around those stricter practices, then deploy the same SOP in Bangkok, Manila, and Jakarta.

**Recommended standard package:**

1. **Combi ovens:** choose either RATIONAL iCombi or Electrolux SkyLine as the chain standard. RATIONAL has strong Indonesia service through Lotus and strong cleaning-water savings; Electrolux has stronger quantified energy/cleaning feature claims in the cited Singapore product source [1][17][2]. If one must be selected from the cited evidence for energy management, choose **Electrolux SkyLine** because the source states multiple quantified savings—up to 33% water, 43% detergent, 25% energy, 50% cleaning running-cost reduction, 20% energy-cost saving through Plan-n-Save, and 10% idle convection energy reduction from triple glass [2].
2. **Warewashing:** choose **HOBART** as the regional standard because its cited service evidence spans Singapore, Indonesia, Malaysia, the Philippines, and Thailand/Indochina through the Bangkok spare-parts center [3][19]. Use Winterhalter where YEK Singapore support, PSG/EEG grant eligibility, or site layout makes it preferable [4][23].
3. **Wok stations:** specify 25 kW-per-chamber class output for high-volume stir-fry, premix/high-efficiency burners where available, flame-failure protection, stainless construction, and local parts support. Daneng Singapore and Foodmach Philippines provide the strongest fetched benchmarks [8][10].
4. **Tandoors:** specify insulated ceramic chamber, stainless exterior, gas/LPG/NG compatibility, optional charcoal only where permitted, and direct exhaust capture. Use Trutec’s 20 kW stainless/ceramic benchmark and MFK’s 21 kW LPG/NG benchmark [11][12].
5. **Grills:** standardize modular electric/gas griddles and grills through Electrolux where repeatability matters; use local charbroilers where flame profile matters, with Daneng’s 45,000 and 75,000 Btu/h models as gas-output benchmarks [27][9].
6. **Ventilation:** specify Halton/GIF-class source capture, grease filtration, and cleanable duct/ceiling systems. Target airflow reduction through capture technology rather than simply increasing exhaust volume, because Bangkok’s ventilation rates are high and Halton claims up to 50% exhaust-airflow reduction [15][5][7].
7. **Cleaning SOP:** adopt Singapore’s annual specialist exhaust degreasing with records as the minimum chain standard; use automatic combi cleaning and high-standard warewashing to reduce labour variability and inspection risk [18][14][17][2][3].

This gives the chain standard operating consistency without forcing unsuitable single-brand procurement in markets where the strongest evidence shows that hot-line cooking equipment and service are still local.

- Cleaning/inspection implications by city: Singapore is the strictest fetched standard for exhaust records (annual specialist duct/hood degreasing and inspectable records) and smoke/fume treatment; Bangkok has high kitchen ventilation rates and grease-trap cleanability; Manila emphasizes impervious/easily cleaned floors/walls, 3-inch coved floor-wall junctions, cleanable equipment areas, nuisance-free exhaust discharge, and grease traps. Fetched sources did not provide a verified Indonesian/Jakarta health-inspection regulation; only supplier sources showed stainless steel/installation/service capabilities, so Jakarta regulatory compliance remains a gap to verify with current Indonesian/DF Jakarta rules.
