# Carbon pricing outcomes in California, the EU ETS, and China’s national carbon market, 2020–2024

**Bottom line:** From 2020–2024, the EU ETS delivered the clearest combination of a high carbon price, automatic surplus withdrawal, large auction revenue, near-universal compliance, and measured covered-sector emissions decline: installation emissions fell from 1,356 MtCO2e in 2020 to 1,096 MtCO2e in 2023, and were about 47.6% below 2005 [1]. California’s program was steadier and lower-priced, with auction prices rising from a 2020 average of $17.04/tCO2e to $35.23/tCO2e in 2024 and FY2020-21–FY2023-24 proceeds of $25.45 billion, but statewide emissions outcomes are best read as the combined effect of cap-and-trade plus complementary rules [2] [3] [4]. China’s national market scaled quickly to roughly 5.1 BtCO2e/year of covered power-sector emissions, with prices rising to a 2024 year-end close of 97.49 yuan/t, but it remained primarily an intensity-based, freely allocated compliance market through 2024 rather than a revenue-raising cap-and-auction system [5] [6].

## 1. Implementation and market design

California entered 2020 with a mature linked cap-and-trade market, linked to Québec since 2014, using quarterly auctions, a rising auction floor, banking, offsets within regulatory limits, and industrial free allocation to address leakage [10] [11]. Its price-containment architecture became more explicit after AB 398: the auction floor rises by 5% plus inflation annually, the 2024 floor was $24.04/tCO2e, the 2024 hard price ceiling was $88.22/tCO2e, and the 2024 Allowance Price Containment Reserve tiers were $56.20 and $72.21/tCO2e [14]. No reserve sale had been completed by December 2024; a September 2024 reserve sale was cancelled because there were no qualified applicants or bidders [14].

The EU ETS was in the transition from Phase 3 to Phase 4 during the period. It retained an absolute cap, increasingly relied on auctioning, and used the Market Stability Reserve (MSR) as the central mechanism for surplus management [1] [8]. The 2023 revision tightened the 2030 ETS target from a 43% to a 62% reduction below 2005 for covered sectors, expanded the system to maritime transport from 2024, strengthened the MSR, and paired the ETS with the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) [15] [1].

China’s national ETS was much newer. Trading launched in July 2021 after the December 2020 allowance plan and entity list and February 2021 trading rules [13]. The first compliance cycle covered 2019–2020 power-sector emissions from 2,162 entities; the second covered 2021–2022 power-sector emissions from 2,257 entities, including captive power plants; the third covered 2023 power-sector emissions from 2,096 entities; and the fourth cycle expanded 2024 coverage to power, iron and steel, cement, and aluminium smelting [5] [13]. The threshold was annual covered-sector emissions above 26,000 tCO2e [13]. Unlike California and the EU ETS, China’s national market through 2024 used intensity-based benchmark allocation without setting an absolute enterprise emissions cap [6].

## 2. Allowance price trajectories

California’s price path was the lowest and smoothest of the three mature trading venues, held down by its auction floor and cost-containment design. Current-vintage auction prices averaged $17.04/tCO2e in 2020, $22.04 in 2021, $28.45 in 2022, $33.03 in 2023, and $35.23 in 2024 [2]. The COVID shock was visible in 2020: only 81.3% of current allowances offered were sold that year, and the May and August auctions cleared at the $16.68 floor [2]. By contrast, from 2021 through 2024 current auctions were fully subscribed, and prices rose above $30/tCO2e in 2023 before reaching nearly $42/tCO2e in February 2024 and ending 2024 at $31.91/tCO2e in November [2] [14].

The EU ETS price trajectory was sharper and more volatile. Auction/primary-market averages rose from €25/tCO2 in 2020 to €54.15 in 2021, €80.18 in 2022, and €83.60 in 2023, then fell to €65/tCO2 in 2024, an 18% decline from 2023 [7] [8] [1]. Within 2023, the highest auction price was €96.33 on 28 February and the lowest was €66.49 on 18 December; in the first half of 2024, auction prices ranged from €49.50 on 23 February to €75.35 on 3 June [1]. The EU therefore had the strongest price signal, but also the largest post-energy-crisis correction.

China’s price rose from a low base. CEAs traded in a 40–60 yuan/t range in the first compliance cycle, 50–82 yuan/t in the second, closed 2023 at 79.42 yuan/t, exceeded 100 yuan/t for the first time on 24 April 2024, peaked at 105.65 yuan/t on 13 November 2024, and closed 2024 at 97.49 yuan/t [5] [6]. That 2024 close was 22.75% above the end-2023 close and 103.10% above the opening price on the first trading day in 2021 [6]. China’s 2024 price increase was partly associated with formal implementation of new regulations and expectations of sectoral expansion, while the fourth-quarter allowance banking policy helped keep the composite closing price within 97–106 yuan/t rather than producing an end-cycle price spike [6].

## 3. Emissions reductions and benchmarks

The EU ETS has the most direct covered-sector emissions accounting. Verified stationary-installation emissions declined from 1,356 MtCO2e in 2020 to 1,337 Mt in 2021, 1,313 Mt in 2022, and 1,096 Mt in 2023 [1]. The 2023 decline was especially large: installation emissions fell 16.5% year on year, with electricity and heat down 23.9% to 552 MtCO2e and industrial production down 7.5% to 544 MtCO2e [1]. Relative to the program baseline, 2023 installation emissions were about 47.6% below 2005, leaving the system on track for the 2030 target of 62% below 2005 [1].

California’s reported outcome is best interpreted at the statewide portfolio level because cap-and-trade operates alongside the Low Carbon Fuel Standard, Renewables Portfolio Standard, Advanced Clean Cars, methane rules, and investment programs. Statewide emissions were 371.1 MMTCO2e in 2022, down 9.3 MMTCO2e, or 2.4%, from 380.4 MMTCO2e in 2021 [4]. California had already fallen below the AB 32 2020 limit of 431 MMTCO2e in 2014, at 428.1 MMTCO2e, and remained below it through 2022; relative to that statutory benchmark, 2022 emissions were 59.9 MMTCO2e lower [4]. Industrial emissions were 72.7 MMTCO2e in 2022, the lowest in the reported series and 21.7% below 2000 [4].

China’s national ETS uses an intensity benchmark rather than an absolute cap, so the best official outcome metrics are intensity and allowance-balance measures rather than absolute reductions against a cap. In the second compliance cycle, China issued 5.096 Bt of CEAs for 2021 against 5.094 Bt of verified emissions obligated for surrender, a 1.47 Mt surplus equal to 0.03% of allocated allowances; for 2022 it issued 5.104 Bt against 5.091 Bt, a 12.98 Mt surplus equal to 0.25% [5]. For 2023, obligated emissions were 5.24 Bt and the total deficit was 29 Mt, or 0.55% of obligations [6]. On performance, national thermal-power CO2 intensity in 2023 was 2.38% below 2018 and total power-generation CO2 intensity was 8.78% below 2018; by 2024, national power-sector carbon intensity was 10.8% below 2018 [5] [6].

## 4. Revenue generation and allocation to climate programs

California generated substantial public revenue because a large share of allowances is auctioned. From FY2020-21 through FY2023-24, cap-and-trade proceeds totaled $25.45 billion: $16.27 billion for the Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund (GGRF) and $9.18 billion for utility ratepayer protection [3]. Annual proceeds rose from $1.496 billion utility / $2.624 billion GGRF in FY2020-21 to $3.044 billion utility / $5.133 billion GGRF in FY2023-24 [3]. Under current law, about 65% of annual GGRF auction revenue is continuously appropriated to High-Speed Rail (25%), Affordable Housing and Sustainable Communities (20%), Transit and Intercity Rail Capital (10%), Low Carbon Transit Operations (5%), and Safe and Affordable Drinking Water (5%, capped at $130 million), plus $200 million off the top for forest health and wildfire prevention [16]. Discretionary 2013–2023 examples include $3.2 billion for Low Carbon Transportation, $1.4 billion for AB 617 Community Air Protection, $1.5 billion for Forest Health, $461 million for Agriculture, and $269 million for organics/waste [16].

The EU ETS generated the most revenue in absolute terms. In 2023, total auction revenue was €43.6 billion, of which €33 billion went directly to Member States; Member States had disbursed €22.2 billion and committed a further €0.8 billion to Article 10(3) climate and energy purposes [1]. In 2024, total auction revenues were €38.8 billion: €24.4 billion to Member States, €2.4 billion to the Innovation Fund, €6.3 billion to the Modernisation Fund, €5.6 billion to the Recovery and Resilience Facility, and €0.25 billion to EFTA countries and Northern Ireland [7]. Member State direct revenue fell 27% from 2023 to 2024, reflecting an 18% fall in average allowance prices and a larger share routed to EU-level funds [7]. Before June 2023, Member States were encouraged to spend at least 50% of ETS revenue on climate and energy; from mid-2023, all relevant revenues, or equivalent financial value, must support climate action, energy transformation, and green-transition social impacts, except for indirect carbon-cost compensation [7] [17].

China’s national ETS through 2024 was not a government auction-revenue mechanism because allowances were allocated for free under an intensity-based method [6]. Its monetary scale was therefore market turnover rather than public revenue: by the end of 2023, cumulative CEA trading volume was 442 Mt with transaction value of 24.92 billion yuan; in the second compliance cycle alone, volume was 263 Mt and value 17.26 billion yuan [5]. In 2024, the market operated for 242 trading days, with 189 Mt of annual trading volume and 18.11 billion yuan of transaction value, a record annual value since launch [6]. China also relaunched the national voluntary market; as an example of climate-program-like revenue to project owners rather than the state, an offshore wind project in Dongtai, Jiangsu, with 857,000 tCO2e of approved CCERs was expected to generate nearly 70 million yuan at 80 yuan/t [6].

## 5. Compliance rates and enforcement

Compliance was high in all three systems, but China’s official percentages are the most granular for 2021–2023. California reported that all covered businesses met their 2018–2020 compliance obligations, a 100% compliance rate, and CARB’s FAQ describes near-100% compliance at each compliance event through November 2020 [9] [10]. The California program uses multi-year compliance periods, and noncompliance triggers a four-to-one additional surrender requirement for missing compliance instruments [10].

The EU ETS uses an annual compliance cycle: operators submit verified emissions reports by 31 March and surrender allowances by 30 September for the prior year [1]. The penalty is €100 per tCO2 not surrendered, adjusted for inflation, in addition to the obligation to surrender the missing allowances [1] [15]. In the 2023 compliance cycle, compliance remained very high; the Commission reported that in most years operators responsible for over 99% of installation and aviation emissions met obligations on time [1]. Registry data for 2023 show installations with 1,096,788,327 tCO2e of verified emissions and 1,099,802,112 surrendered units, and aviation with 54,128,536 tCO2e of verified emissions and 54,186,768 surrendered units, with small differences reflecting timing and linked Swiss-system units [1].

China reported compliance rates of 99.61% for 2021, 99.88% for 2022, and 99.98% for 2023 [5] [6]. In the second cycle, 80 key emitting entities failed to comply fully and on time, 98 fewer than in the first compliance cycle [5]. Enforcement tightened from the third compliance cycle: non-surrender can trigger a fine of five to ten times the market value of missing allowances, based on the average price in the month before the compliance deadline, plus a future allowance deduction equivalent to the missing amount if correction is refused [13].

## 6. COVID-19 disruption and recovery handling

California handled COVID through its existing price floor and unsold-allowance rules rather than a special cap adjustment. Demand fell sharply in 2020: current-vintage auction sales were only 81.3% of offered allowances, and May and August 2020 cleared at the $16.68 floor [2]. The program’s built-in rule withholds unsold allowances; if demand does not exceed supply for 24 months, unsold allowances are permanently removed from regular auctions and can be sold only through higher-price reserve sales [10]. Recovery appeared in auction demand rather than rule changes: all current-vintage auctions from 2021 through 2024 sold 100% of offered allowances [2].

The EU ETS also relied on an automatic mechanism rather than an emergency redesign. The total number of allowances in circulation rose from 1.39 billion in 2019 to 1.58 billion in 2020 because COVID reduced allowance demand and the UK auctioned two years of supply in 2020 [8]. The MSR then absorbed surplus: 348 million allowances entered the MSR in 2021 and 368.5 million in 2022 [8]. With TNAC at 1.14 billion at the end of 2022, the MSR absorbed 24%, or 272 million allowances, reducing auction volumes from September 2023 to August 2024 [8]. From 2023, MSR holdings above the previous year’s auction volume were invalidated; from 2024, the MSR validity limit is 400 million allowances, and the first deletion in January 2023 was about 2.5 billion allowances [8].

China used explicit compliance flexibility in response to pandemic-related pressures. For the second compliance cycle, authorities allowed key emitting entities with high CEA deficits to borrow CEAs and introduced tailored relief mechanisms for entities undertaking major public welfare tasks; these flexible mechanisms helped 202 key emitting entities fulfill their compliance obligations [5]. China later moved from two-year to annual compliance beginning with the 2023 cycle, and introduced restrictions on banking of surplus allowances from the third compliance cycle: maximum bankable allowances equal 10,000 tonnes plus 1.5 times the sum of net sales of allowances labelled 2019–2020, 2021, 2022, 2023, and 2024 [13].

## 7. Interaction with complementary policies

California’s carbon price works as one part of a broader regulatory portfolio. CARB identifies other AB 32 programs that work in tandem with cap-and-trade, including the Low Carbon Fuel Standard, Renewables Portfolio Standard, and Advanced Clean Cars program [9]. Auction proceeds also reinforce complementary measures through transportation, housing, transit, drinking-water, forest-health, wildfire-prevention, community air, agriculture, and waste programs [16]. The implication is that California’s emissions reductions cannot be attributed to the carbon price alone; the price raises revenue and creates a covered-sector incentive while complementary standards mandate technology and fuel shifts.

The EU ETS showed the strongest interaction between carbon pricing and power-sector policy. The 2023 emissions drop was driven mainly by renewable electricity growth, especially wind and solar, and by resumed gas-for-coal switching supported by average carbon prices above €80/tCO2 [1]. Renewables supplied 44.7% of EU electricity in 2023, while fossil electricity fell 19.7% from 2022 to 32.5% of total electricity [1]. The EU added 56 GW of solar capacity in 2023 after 40 GW in 2022, and wind reached 221 GW of installed capacity, with 16 GW added in 2023 and wind surpassing natural gas as the EU’s second-largest electricity-generation technology behind nuclear [1]. In the first half of 2024, renewables supplied about 50% of electricity and wind plus solar generated more electricity than fossil fuels combined [1]. Coal phase-out policy also interacts directly with the ETS through Article 12(4): Member States may cancel allowances when national measures close electricity-generation capacity, capped by the closed plants’ verified emissions over the preceding five years [1]. Germany notified in December 2023 that it intended to cancel allowances associated with two power plants closed in 2022 under its coal phase-out, with cancellations planned for 2025–2030 and the exact quantity to account for replacement generation and MSR operation [1].

China’s ETS was built around the power sector partly because power generation accounts for over 40% of national CO2 emissions and has relatively reliable plant-level data [13]. Its intensity-based design fits China’s broader policy context of maintaining electricity supply and regulated power pricing while shifting from energy-intensity and energy-consumption control toward carbon-intensity and total-carbon control [13]. The market also complements renewable and clean-energy policy indirectly: gas-fired power plants receive additional allowances if the benchmark formula falls short of actual emissions, so gas plants do not need to buy extra allowances, a design justified by gas’s lower carbon intensity and flexibility for grids with higher renewable shares [13]. The voluntary CCER market further links carbon pricing to complementary mitigation categories, with methodologies including afforestation carbon sinks, grid-connected solar thermal power, grid-connected offshore wind power, mangrove vegetation creation, coalbed methane use, and tunnel-lighting energy savings [13].

## 8. Leakage concerns and border or allocation responses

California’s leakage strategy is mainly output-based free allocation for industry plus a border-like rule for electricity imports. CARB allocates allowances annually to industrial facilities to minimize emissions leakage while preserving incentives for efficient in-state production [11]. More than 98% of industrial allocation is product-based, calculated from verified facility output, an assistance factor, benchmark, and cap adjustment factor [11]. An IEMAC draft quantified the scale of this leakage protection: in 2020, more than 58 MMT of allowances were allocated free to industrial producers; at an allowance price of about $17/t, this represented roughly $1 billion of potential GGRF revenue diverted to leakage mitigation [12]. For electricity, California requires importers to surrender allowances for imported-power emissions, functioning as a state-level border carbon adjustment for that sector [12].

The EU ETS uses the most developed leakage architecture. For 2021–2030, its carbon leakage list includes 63 sectors and subsectors covering about 94% of industrial emissions in the EU ETS [1]. CBAM was adopted in 2023 to address leakage as EU climate ambition rises; it covers cement, aluminium, fertilisers, hydrogen, iron and steel, and electricity, and those sectors represent about 54% of total 2021–2025 free allocation [1]. Transitional CBAM reporting began in October 2023, with no payments until the end of 2025; from 2026, importers of CBAM goods must buy and surrender certificates for embedded emissions while free allocation is phased down [1]. The free-allocation/CBAM factor is 97.5% in 2026, 95% in 2027, 90% in 2028, 77.5% in 2029, 51.5% in 2030, 39% in 2031, 26.5% in 2032, 14% in 2033, and 0% in 2034 [8]. For export leakage, the Commission’s 2024 assessment compared export intensity and emissions intensity at the 8-digit CN product level; hydrogen and cement had the highest emissions intensity, while fertiliser, aluminium, and steel goods were more frequently exported, and hydrogen peroxide was cited with a high combined indicator value of 2.109 [1].

China’s national ETS addressed competitiveness and compliance pressure through free, intensity-based allocation. The system does not set an absolute cap on enterprise emissions; instead, allowance allocation is based on benchmarks and actual output, which limits direct output-cost pressure relative to an absolute cap-and-auction design [6]. The 2023–2024 design also includes gas-plant allowance support and banking limits, both of which affect allowance scarcity and compliance pressure [13]. As China expands from power into steel, cement, and aluminium smelting, the relevant leakage issue is less cross-border adjustment within the ETS and more the calibration of benchmarks, free allocation, and future movement toward an absolute cap [13] [6].

## Comparative assessment

**Price signal:** EU ETS ranked highest, with prices averaging €80.18/tCO2 in 2022 and €83.60/tCO2 in 2023 before easing to €65/tCO2 in 2024 [1] [7] [8]. California ranked second in stability but not stringency, rising to $35.23/tCO2e on average in 2024 while remaining far below its 2024 reserve tiers and price ceiling [2] [14]. China ranked third on absolute price level but first in percentage increase from launch, closing 2024 103.10% above its 2021 opening price [6].

**Emissions outcome:** EU ETS produced the clearest covered-sector decline, with 2023 installation emissions 47.6% below 2005 [1]. California’s 2022 statewide emissions were well below the AB 32 2020 benchmark, but that outcome reflects a policy portfolio rather than cap-and-trade alone [4] [9]. China’s ETS demonstrated intensity improvement and high coverage, but its intensity-based design means emissions can grow with output; the official 2024 power-sector result was a 10.8% carbon-intensity reduction relative to 2018 [6].

**Revenue model:** EU ETS generated the largest public revenue, €38.8 billion in 2024, followed by California’s multi-billion-dollar annual auction proceeds and dedicated climate-investment structure [7] [3]. China generated large market turnover, 18.11 billion yuan in 2024, but not comparable public auction revenue because allocation remained free [6].

**COVID resilience:** The EU ETS had the most automatic macro-stabilizer through the MSR; California relied on the auction floor and unsold-allowance rules; China used explicit compliance relief, borrowing, and later banking limits [8] [10] [5] [13].

**Leakage management:** The EU ETS moved furthest toward a border mechanism through CBAM, California applied sector-specific output-based allocation and an electricity-import compliance obligation, and China relied on free intensity-based allocation during the period [1] [12] [6].
