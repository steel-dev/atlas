**Bottom line:** Rubber Leaf’s headline gross-margin deterioration in Q1 2024 was driven primarily by the factory-relocation loss, not by the recurring economics of the indirect supply model alone. Reported gross margin fell from **+2.8616%** in Q1 2023 to **-6.6549%** in Q1 2024, a **-9.5165 percentage-point** year-over-year change; excluding the **$190,703** factory-relocation loss included in cost of sales, Q1 2024 gross margin would have been **-0.2004%**, a **+6.4544 percentage-point** uplift versus reported margin and only **-3.0621 percentage points** below Q1 2023. Thus, about **67.82%** of the reported YoY margin decline is mechanically attributable to relocation cost, while the remaining pressure reflects underlying sales-mix and cost economics after direct-supply revenue dropped to zero and indirect-supply revenue became all of Q1 2024 sales [1][2].

Rubber Leaf describes two revenue models operated through **Rubber Leaf Sealing Products (Zhejiang) Co., Ltd. (RLSP)**, the wholly owned China subsidiary included in its consolidated financial statements [1]. Under **Model A (Direct Supply Model)**, after auto OEM on-site inspections, RLSP is listed as a first-tier supplier; eGT directly signs purchase or supply agreements with RLSP; RLSP procures raw materials, manufactures final products, directly delivers finished goods to OEM warehouses, and recognizes revenue when control transfers, with payment made directly by the OEM [1]. Under **Model B (Indirect Supply Model)**, RLSP receives purchase orders from related parties **Shanghai Xinsen** and **Xinsen Sealing Products (Hangzhou) Co., Ltd.** (together, “Xinsen Group”); Xinsen Group is a certified second-tier supplier for branded auto manufacturers; RLSP procures rubber materials and outsources production to third-party manufacturers for work-in-process or finished products; RLSP fulfills its obligation when products reach Xinsen Group’s customers and pass quality inspection; and the invoice date marks transfer of ownership and control from RLSP to Xinsen Group and indirectly to upstream first-tier suppliers [1].

The sales mix shows the transition clearly: in Q1 2023, Rubber Leaf had **$897,432** of direct-supply sales and **$1,448,109** of indirect-supply related-party sales; in Q1 2024, direct-supply sales were **$0** and indirect-supply related-party sales were **$2,954,607** [1][2]. Management attributed Q1 2024 revenue growth to rising demand from the indirect supply model, while explaining that RLSP began relocating to a newly constructed facility in **early August 2023**, obtained the property title certificate in **December 2023**, and had not resumed production as of **March 31, 2024** because relocation required **QS16949** recertification, typically **two to three months**, followed by direct-supply client on-site evaluations before formal production; management anticipated production resumption in **June 2024** [1]. The Q1 2024 statement of operations separately reports **“Loss on factory relocation” of $190,703** inside total cost of sales, and MD&A states that RLSP’s production suspension since **July 2023** led to that **$190,703** relocation loss for the three months ended March 31, 2024 [1].

The margin bridge separates the two effects. Reported Q1 2024 gross margin of **-6.6549%** minus Q1 2023 reported gross margin of **2.8616%** equals a **-9.5165 percentage-point** reported YoY decline. Removing the factory-relocation loss raises Q1 2024 gross margin to **-0.2004%**, so the relocation-loss impact is **+6.4544 percentage points**; because **6.4544 ÷ 9.5165 = 67.82%**, the relocation item explains roughly two-thirds of the reported deterioration [1][2]. But the adjusted Q1 2024 margin remains below the Q1 2023 margin by **-3.0621 percentage points**, so the indirect-heavy operating base was still weaker than Q1 2023 even before relocation loss [1][2].

On that evidence, the right classification is: **Q1 2024 margin pressure was primarily relocation-driven, with a secondary underlying margin weakening from the shift away from higher-margin direct supply.** The one-time relocation cost turned a near-breakeven adjusted gross margin of **-0.2004%** into a reported gross margin of **-6.6549%**; however, adjusted margin did not recover to Q1 2023’s **2.8616%**, and management itself attributed the overall margin decrease to generating less sales from the higher-margin direct supply model [1][2]. One source inconsistency matters for interpretation: the Q1 2024 MD&A margin row extracted as “Total 7%” for 2024 and “3%” for 2023 conflicts with the statement-of-operations arithmetic and with the same MD&A’s statement that gross profit/loss was **$(196,625)** and **$67,121**; the financially consistent margins are therefore **-6.6549%** for Q1 2024 and **2.8616%** for Q1 2023, calculated from the revenue and cost-of-sales lines in the financial statements [1][2].
