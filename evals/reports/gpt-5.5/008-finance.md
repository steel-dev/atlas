# Columbus McKinnon: capital allocation and financial-risk profile

Columbus McKinnon’s recent disclosures point to a capital-allocation posture that is strategic but risk-conscious: the company has used acquisitions to reposition the business toward precision conveyance and “intelligent motion,” while also taking visible steps to simplify pension obligations and reduce debt after acquisition-funded leverage increased. The main financial risks that remain are not concentrated in one area. Pension risk is being actively reduced; leverage has been managed down between acquisition waves; equity-incentive effectiveness has deteriorated sharply because nearly all outstanding stock options are out of the money; and acquired intangibles have long amortization lives, suggesting durable strategic value but also a balance sheet increasingly dependent on the long-run realization of customer-relationship and technology assets.

## Pension management: risk transfer rather than simply funding improvement

The pension funded status improved from a $68.433 million deficit at March 31, 2024 to a $64.389 million deficit at March 31, 2025. That is a **$4.044 million improvement**, or a roughly **5.9% reduction in the deficit**. The improvement was not the result of growth in the pension asset pool: the benefit obligation fell from $268.025 million to $158.629 million, while plan assets fell from $199.592 million to $94.240 million. Both sides of the pension balance sheet were reduced by a $95.080 million settlement item in fiscal 2025 [1].

The settlement activity is therefore more important strategically than the funded-status change alone. Columbus McKinnon terminated both Canadian pension plans in fiscal 2024 and one U.S. pension plan in fiscal 2025; the Canadian terminations and related lump-sum activity produced a $4.984 million settlement charge in fiscal 2024, and the U.S. termination culminated in a September 30, 2024 annuity purchase to settle remaining liabilities, producing a **$23.634 million non-cash settlement charge** in fiscal 2025 [1]. At termination, the U.S. plan had a $6.974 million surplus, which the company is using to fund certain U.S. defined contribution obligations; $6.342 million of that surplus remained at March 31, 2025 [1].

This pattern indicates an explicit pension-risk transfer and de-risking strategy rather than a passive approach to pension funding. The company also discloses a glide-path investment policy: as funded status improves, it increases liability-hedging investments; its 2026 target allocation is 0% equity and 100% fixed income, compared with actual allocations of 11% equity/89% fixed income in 2025 and 8% equity/92% fixed income in 2024 [1]. Management also states that it expects the U.S. plan to be fully funded in fiscal 2026 and will further de-risk the portfolio, with funding at least at ERISA minimums and possible additional contributions to minimize PBGC premiums [1].

The capital-allocation implication is that Columbus McKinnon is willing to absorb non-cash settlement charges to remove legacy volatility and administrative/risk burdens. That reduces long-term pension uncertainty and may free management attention and capital flexibility, but it also shows that reported earnings can be affected by discrete pension-risk-management actions, as seen in the fiscal 2025 other-expense increase tied to the settlement charge [1].

## Debt reduction: deleveraging between acquisition waves

From March 31, 2022 to March 31, 2023, Columbus McKinnon reduced both gross debt and net debt. Total debt declined from $497.135 million to $458.052 million, while cash and cash equivalents increased from $115.390 million to $133.176 million [2]. On a total-debt-less-cash basis, net debt fell as follows:

| Fiscal year-end | Total debt | Cash and cash equivalents | Net debt |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| March 31, 2022 | $497.135m | $115.390m | $381.745m |
| March 31, 2023 | $458.052m | $133.176m | $324.876m |
| Change | $(39.083)m | +$17.786m | **$(56.869)m** |

The $56.869 million net-debt reduction reflects both scheduled/voluntary debt paydown and cash preservation. The cash-flow statement and liquidity discussion show fiscal 2023 financing cash outflows included **$40.550 million of debt repayment** and $8.008 million of dividends [2]. The Term Loan B principal balance was $462.560 million at March 31, 2023, down from $502.560 million at March 31, 2022, with $40.000 million of Term Loan B principal payments during fiscal 2023; the company also stated it planned to pay down approximately $40.000 million of principal over the following 12 months [2].

The context matters because Columbus McKinnon had materially increased leverage to fund acquisitions. In connection with Dorner, the company incurred $450 million of Term Loan B debt after a $207 million equity offering, and Garvey added another $75 million of Term Loan B indebtedness through an accordion feature [2]. The 2023 filing also noted expected additional indebtedness for the montratec acquisition, and described plans to finance that transaction with an expanded revolving credit facility and additional debt through receivables securitization [2].

The strategic reading is that deleveraging was not a retreat from acquisitions; it was a balance-sheet reset between acquisition periods. The company used fiscal 2023 cash generation to lower net debt after Dorner/Garvey and ahead of montratec. That indicates a capital-allocation priority hierarchy in which acquisitions are central to portfolio transformation, but debt reduction is used to preserve flexibility and reduce financial risk before or during the next acquisition phase.

## Employee retention and incentive risk: options overwhelmingly underwater

At March 31, 2025, Columbus McKinnon had **1,253,626 stock options outstanding** at a weighted-average exercise price of $36.38. The company states that only **51,957 options** were in the money, and that its closing stock price at March 31, 2025 was **$16.70** [1]. Therefore:

- Underwater options = 1,253,626 − 51,957 = **1,201,669**.
- Percentage underwater = 1,201,669 ÷ 1,253,626 = **95.9%**.
- Percentage in the money = 51,957 ÷ 1,253,626 = **4.1%**.

The concentration of underwater options is severe. It is not just that higher-strike tranches are out of the money; only the $10.01–$20.00 exercise-price band, with 51,957 options at a $15.16 weighted-average exercise price, was in the money at the $16.70 stock price. The remaining option bands—$20.01–$30.00, $30.01–$40.00, $40.01–$50.00, and $50.01–$60.00—represented the overwhelming majority of options outstanding and were above the market price [1].

Recent stock performance explains the deterioration in retention value. At March 31, 2024, the closing stock price was $44.63 and 1.048 million options were in the money; by March 31, 2025, the closing price was $16.70 and only 51,957 options were in the money [3][1]. That is a **$27.93 decline**, or about **62.6%**. Fiscal 2025 also saw 115,655 option cancellations at a $41.26 weighted-average exercise price, while only 12,648 options were exercised at a $29.44 weighted-average exercise price [1].

This creates a retention and incentive-design risk. Options generally vest over three years and are granted at not less than fair market value on the grant date [1]. When nearly all outstanding options are underwater, they provide limited current retention value and may be less effective at aligning employees with shareholder value creation unless the stock recovers materially before expiration. Columbus McKinnon still has other equity instruments, including restricted stock units and performance shares, but the option pool itself is highly impaired as a motivational tool at March 31, 2025 [1].

## Intangible amortization: long-lived acquired assets support the strategic thesis but raise execution risk

Columbus McKinnon’s acquisition strategy is visible in the composition of its intangible assets. At March 31, 2025, finite-lived identifiable intangible assets consisted of $22.770 million of trademarks, $355.845 million of customer relationships, $112.507 million of acquired technology, and $3.868 million of other intangibles, for total finite-lived gross intangibles of **$494.990 million**. The company also had $46.294 million of indefinite-lived trademarks that are not amortized [1].

Fiscal 2025 amortization expense was **$29.946 million** [1]. Using the gross finite-lived intangible base, the actual amortization rate is:

- $29.946 million ÷ $494.990 million = **6.05%**.
- Implied gross-life equivalent = $494.990 million ÷ $29.946 million = **16.5 years**.

A category-weighted calculation using the disclosed weighted-average amortization periods—13 years for trademarks, 17 years for customer relationships, 15 years for acquired technology, 6 years for other, and 16 years in total—produces a similar implied annual amortization of about $30.829 million and a weighted-average amortization rate of about **6.23%**, implying a **16.1-year** weighted life [1]. The small difference from the actual fiscal 2025 expense rate is consistent with using current-period expense versus category-life approximations.

| Finite-lived intangible class | Gross carrying amount | Disclosed amortization life | Approx. annual amortization from class life | Share of finite-lived gross intangibles |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Trademark | $22.770m | 13 years | $1.752m | 4.6% |
| Customer relationships | $355.845m | 17 years | $20.932m | 71.9% |
| Acquired technology | $112.507m | 15 years | $7.500m | 22.7% |
| Other | $3.868m | 6 years | $0.645m | 0.8% |
| **Total finite-lived** | **$494.990m** | **~16 years total** | **$30.829m** | **100.0%** |

The strategic implication is that acquired value is concentrated in assets expected to generate benefits over many years, not in short-lived backlog or transitory items. Customer relationships and acquired technology together totaled **$468.352 million gross**, or **94.6%** of finite-lived gross intangibles, and **$296.306 million net**, or **95.5%** of finite-lived net intangibles [1]. Recent transactions fit that pattern: montratec added customer relationships valued at $33.470 million, technology valued at $16.196 million, and a trade name valued at $2.915 million, with a 14-year weighted-average life for acquired identifiable intangibles [1]. Dorner and Garvey were described as expanding precision conveyance offerings and accelerating the shift to intelligent motion; the company said these acquisitions added a platform for higher-technology automation solutions and complementary adjacencies [2].

This is the most strategically durable part of the acquisition case, but it is also where execution risk is concentrated. Long amortization lives imply that management expects acquired customer relationships and technology to support cash flows over roughly 15–17 years. If those customer relationships prove sticky and the acquired technologies remain relevant, the acquisition strategy has durable value. If integration, demand, or technology relevance falls short, the long-lived intangible base could become a future impairment risk. The disclosures support the conclusion that Columbus McKinnon’s acquisition strategy is oriented toward long-duration market access and technology capability rather than short-term revenue alone.

## Overall assessment

Across the four areas, Columbus McKinnon’s strategic priorities appear ordered as follows:

1. **Portfolio transformation through acquisitions** is the central strategic use of capital. Dorner, Garvey, and montratec are tied to precision conveyance, automation, and intelligent-motion expansion [2][1].
2. **Balance-sheet risk control** is the counterweight to acquisition spending. The March 2022–March 2023 net-debt reduction shows active deleveraging after acquisition-related borrowings and before additional acquisition debt [2].
3. **Legacy liability de-risking** is being pursued through pension plan terminations, annuity transfer, settlement charges, and a fixed-income-heavy glide path [1].
4. **Human-capital incentive risk** has worsened because almost all outstanding options were underwater at March 31, 2025, reducing the retention and motivational effect of option awards after the sharp stock-price decline [1][3].

The financial-risk profile is therefore mixed but identifiable. Pension risk is declining through deliberate settlement and asset-allocation actions. Debt risk has been actively managed down when acquisition activity allows, although the company remains acquisition-oriented and has used debt to fund strategic expansion. The weakest area is equity-based retention: the option program’s incentive value was substantially impaired by March 31, 2025. The intangible-asset profile supports the strategic rationale for acquisitions because customer relationships and technology dominate the acquired asset base and carry long useful lives, but that also makes future value creation dependent on sustained execution over a long horizon.
