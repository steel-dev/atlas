# Grounded theory after the Glaser–Strauss split: emergence, procedure, construction

**Bottom line.** The original 1967 Glaser–Strauss formulation defined grounded theory as systematic theory generation from data through constant comparison, theoretical sampling, and saturation, explicitly resisting a sociology organized mainly around testing preconceived theory [1]. Glaser’s 1978 line sharpened that program into **classic grounded theory**: open/substantive coding stays open to concepts generated from data, theoretical coding relates those substantive codes, and analysts should avoid received or preconceived frameworks that force the analysis rather than earn their way into it [2] [3] [4]. Strauss and Corbin’s 1990 line made grounded theory more teachable and procedural: open, axial, and selective coding, a coding paradigm of conditions/actions-interactions/consequences, a conditional matrix, and validation/verification procedures [5] [6]. Charmaz’s 2006 constructivist reformulation is the dominant intellectual compromise in much contemporary qualitative-methods teaching: it keeps comparison, theoretical sampling, memo-writing, and flexible coding, but recasts data and analysis as co-constructed rather than simply “discovered” [7] [8] [9].

The most defensible contemporary conclusion from the fetched evidence is **not** that citation counts prove philosophical superiority or training-program dominance. They are, at best, a proxy. In the normalized Crossref article-level subset that could be counted from the fetched 2020–2024 records, Charmazian citations led: 31 article-level presences, or 72.1% of the 43 school-classified presences, compared with 12 classic/Glaserian presences, or 27.9%, and 0 Straussian/Corbinian presences in that counted subset [10] [11] [12] [13] [14]. That subset was built from Charmaz-grounded-theory reference queries and therefore supports a qualified visibility finding, not a complete census of all grounded-theory citation behavior.

## 1. Shared 1967 baseline: theory generation before theory testing

Glaser and Strauss’s *The Discovery of Grounded Theory: Strategies for Qualitative Research* (Aldine, 1967) was framed as a strategy for “discovering theory as a process,” with comparative analysis as the route to grounded theory, both substantive and formal [1]. Its central methodological claims were:

- **Generate theory from data, rather than merely test inherited theory.** The book criticized the hypothetico-deductive overemphasis on verification and on developing precise theories or hypotheses before data collection, arguing for the “initial, systematic discovery of the theory from the data” so that theory would “fit and work” [3].
- **Use constant comparison.** The constant comparative method combines explicit coding with theory development; its purpose is “to generate theory more systematically” through joint coding and analysis, not to reproduce quantitative-style provisional testing [15] [1].
- **Use theoretical sampling.** The 1967 strategy links comparative analysis with theoretical sampling—the collection of data for comparative analysis guided by the developing theory [1].
- **Stop at theoretical saturation.** The method relies on saturation of data, not exhaustive consideration of all available cases; because saturation and theoretical sampling serve generation, the data are coded enough to generate and suggest theory rather than to prove it [15].
- **Reject merely testing preconceived theory.** The 1967 text contrasts theory generation with “provisional testing,” leaving proof or later testing to subsequent, more rigorous studies when needed [15].

That baseline already contained a productive tension: it encouraged disciplined procedures, but it also privileged emergence, fit, and discovery over prior theoretical imposition and formal verification.

## 3. Prior theory: sensitivity versus framework

The sharpest substantive disagreement concerns what counts as legitimate “theoretical sensitivity.” In Glaser’s 1978 formulation, substantive codes are developed during open coding from the empirical material, while theoretical codes conceptualize relationships among those substantive codes; examples include relational concepts such as causes, contexts, conditions, and consequences [3]. Kelle’s detailed reconstruction of *Theoretical Sensitivity* states the distinction more precisely: substantive coding and substantive codes are developed ad hoc during open coding for the empirical substance, while theoretical coding and theoretical codes “conceptualize how the substantive codes may relate to each other as hypotheses to be integrated into a theory” (p. 72); Glaser’s theoretical “coding families” are loose relational resources rather than fixed substantive frameworks [3]. This is not an endorsement of importing a substantive theory and applying it to data. Glaser’s later defense of the classic position is even stricter: the researcher should approach the field with “abstract wonderment,” should not need a prior substantive literature review, and should avoid anything that would “contaminate,” “constrain,” “inhibit,” or “stifle” category generation [3].

Strauss and Corbin move in the opposite direction. Their 1990 *Basics* is explicitly a procedures-and-techniques manual, with chapters on open coding, axial coding, selective coding, process, the conditional matrix, theoretical sampling, memos and diagrams, and criteria for judging grounded theory studies [5]. The coding paradigm—causal conditions, context, intervening conditions, action/interaction strategies, and consequences—functions as a skeleton for developing grounded theories and as a way “to think systematically about data and to relate them in very complex ways” [3]. In other words, where Glaser fears a pre-existing frame will become a mold, Strauss and Corbin treat analytic devices as aids for relational discovery.

## 4. “Forcing” data: the polemic and the methodological issue behind it

Glaser’s 1992 *Basics of Grounded Theory Analysis: Emergence vs Forcing* turned the disagreement into a public split. He accused Strauss and Corbin’s axial coding, coding paradigm, and conditional matrix of forcing categories onto the data rather than allowing categories to emerge [3]. The polemical formula—“If you torture the data long enough, it will give up!”—captures his charge that in Strauss and Corbin’s method data are not allowed to speak for themselves and that “Forcing by preconception constantly derails it from relevance” (p. 123) [9] [2]. Urquhart’s reconstruction identifies two concrete targets: Strauss and Corbin’s four prescriptive coding steps and their ready-made conceptualization tools, especially the coding paradigm and conditional matrix [9].

Strauss and Corbin’s counter-position is not simply “impose categories.” Their claim is that systematic procedures help the analyst discover and specify relationships that otherwise remain underdeveloped. Axial coding asks whether open-coded categories relate to phenomena, causal conditions, contexts, intervening conditions, action/interaction strategies, and consequences; the goal is to model action and interaction in context [3]. The methodological tension is therefore not just a coding preference. It is a conflict between **emergence versus procedure**: Glaser sees a single coding paradigm as a threat to emergence; Strauss and Corbin see procedure as a safeguard against thin description and underdeveloped theory.

## 5. Verification: fit/workability versus validation/checking

The verification dispute follows from the same divide. The 1967 baseline already rejected making grounded theory primarily a method of provisional testing; the constant comparative method generated and suggested theory, while later testing could be done by other methods [15]. Glaser’s later quality vocabulary continues that trajectory. In classic grounded theory, concepts should be grounded in data so that they show fit, relevance, and workability; this is presented as an alternative to conjectured concepts with forced fit and low relevance [4].

Strauss and Corbin accept a stronger role for validation within qualitative analysis. Their 1990 article on “Grounded Theory Research: Procedures, Canons and Evaluative Criteria” states that in grounded theory hypotheses are “constantly revised during the course of the research until they hold true for the phenomena under study,” using repeated interviews, observations, or documents; embedded in those verification procedures is the search for negative or qualifying evidence [6]. Heath and Cowley likewise characterize Strauss and Corbin’s 1990 model as one in which deduction and verification have a prominent place, later softened in the 1998 edition toward validation and elaboration through further data comparisons [16].

Thus, Glaser’s criteria ask whether the theory fits, works, is relevant, and remains modifiable as more data appear; Strauss and Corbin ask the analyst to verify, validate, and revise developing hypotheses procedurally against data. The dispute turns on whether verification protects groundedness or reintroduces the very hypothetico-deductive logic grounded theory was meant to resist.

## 6. Charmaz 2006: resolution and rupture

Charmaz’s constructivist grounded theory is best read as both a mediation and a break. It mediates the Glaser–Strauss/Corbin dispute by keeping the practical core of grounded theory—coding, constant comparison, theoretical sampling, memo-writing, category development, and theory construction—while loosening procedural rigidity [7] [8] [9]. It also rejects the strict Glaserian assumption that theory simply emerges from data if the analyst avoids preconceptions. In Charmaz’s account, data do not speak for themselves; researchers and participants co-construct data, meanings, and analytic categories [7] [8].

That move resolves one old tension by making explicit what the earlier dispute often left implicit: all observation is theoretically and socially situated. But it introduces a new epistemological rupture. The 1967 language of discovery and Glaser’s later language of emergence retain a realist/objectivist tendency: theory is found in data through disciplined openness [3] [1]. Charmaz shifts grounded theory into an interpretivist/constructivist register: theory is constructed through interaction, standpoint, reflexive analysis, and interpretive practice [7] [8]. Her reformulation therefore softens Strauss and Corbin’s proceduralism without returning to Glaser’s objectivist discovery claim.

## 7. Paradigm wars context: why this became more than a coding dispute

The Glaser–Strauss/Corbin split unfolded within the broader “paradigm wars” over positivist, postpositivist, critical, constructivist, and interpretivist inquiry. Guba and Lincoln’s 1994 “Competing Paradigms in Qualitative Research” located methodological disputes in competing ontological, epistemological, and methodological assumptions rather than mere technique [17]. The Denzin and Lincoln *Handbook of Qualitative Research* tradition—the 1994 first edition and later editions in 2000 and 2005—became a central site for this framing of qualitative inquiry as a field shaped by paradigm debates over positivist/postpositivist versus constructivist/interpretivist assumptions [7] [18].

Within that setting, grounded theory’s internal dispute maps neatly onto larger qualitative-methods controversies:

- **Discovery versus construction.** Glaser’s language of emergence preserves discovery; Charmaz relocates grounded theory in construction [7] [3].
- **Objectivist realism versus interpretivism.** Glaserian GT treats data as capable of yielding theory if preconceptions are bracketed; constructivist GT treats data and categories as situated products [7] [8].
- **Procedure versus reflexivity.** Strauss and Corbin’s procedures answer the training and transparency problem; Charmaz retains method while requiring reflexivity about the researcher’s role [5] [7].

This is why the debate persisted: it was not only about whether to use axial coding, but about what qualitative research can claim to know and how those claims should be warranted.

## 9. Final assessment

The divergence from the 1967 baseline can be summarized as follows. **Glaser 1978** preserves the strongest version of emergence: theory should arise from constant comparison, theoretical sampling, memoing, substantive coding, and theoretical coding without being forced through prior frameworks [3]. **Strauss and Corbin 1990** translate grounded theory into a teachable procedural apparatus: open, axial, and selective coding; a conditions/actions-interactions/consequences paradigm; the conditional matrix; and validation of hypotheses against data [5] [3] [6]. **Charmaz 2006** resolves part of the conflict by keeping grounded theory’s analytic craft while rejecting both rigid proceduralism and objectivist discovery: grounded theory becomes a reflexive, constructivist practice of building interpretive theory from co-constructed data [7] [8].

The contemporary field is therefore not governed by a single pure lineage. Constructivist grounded theory appears most influential in broad qualitative-methods discourse and training, Straussian coding remains common in applied health and social-science research because it is teachable and auditable, and Glaserian classic grounded theory remains active in specialist communities. Mixed and hybrid usage—such as studies citing Charmaz while using selected axial-coding tools, or invoking grounded theory without declaring a school—means that citation presence is an imperfect proxy for methodological allegiance.
