## Evaluation basis and hard requirements

The CDC Vaccine Storage and Handling Toolkit recommends purpose-built or pharmaceutical-grade units designed to refrigerate or freeze vaccines; it describes those units as having microprocessor-based controls, digital temperature sensors, fan-forced air circulation, uniform temperature, and fast temperature recovery [11]. For outage planning, CDC states that an on-site generator can avoid emergency vaccine transport during a power outage, should have fuel to run **at least 72 hours**, and should be tested quarterly and serviced annually; a backup battery power source may be used in lieu of a generator but likewise should be tested quarterly and serviced annually [11]. During an outage, CDC instructs sites with external temperature monitoring displays to record room temperature, unit temperature, and min/max temperatures without opening the unit door [11].

For a Montana/Wyoming clinic network with recurring 6–12 hour outages, the key practical distinction is that **device controller/alarm battery backup is not the same as refrigerated holdover under power loss**. Helmer’s 20-hour figure is explicitly for the i.C3 display/alarm backup, while Panasonic’s 9-hour figure is for optional CO₂ chamber backup under specified ULT conditions [1], [5]. The CDC-level solution is therefore facility backup power, not reliance on refrigerator controller batteries [11].

## Product-by-product comparison

### 1) Helmer Scientific GX Solutions i.Series pharmacy refrigerator

**Temperature performance.** The iPR125-GX technical sheet lists **25.2 cu ft / 714 L** capacity, **+2°C to +10°C** operating range, **+5°C** set point, and **±1.0°C** uniformity, with performance data at **24.8°C ambient and 4.0°C** [1]. The GX brochure states that GX Solutions maintain temperatures within **±1°C** throughout the unit and that forced-air circulation supports recovery after prolonged door openings and frequent access [6]. The brochure’s recovery claim is qualitative—“recover to target temp in minutes”—rather than a precise post-restoration recovery time after a long outage [6].

**Outage behavior.** Helmer documents **20 hours** of battery backup for the i.C3 touchscreen display and alarms using a rechargeable 12V lead-acid battery [1]. It does not document compressor operation on that battery, so the correct design for 6–12 hour outages is a clinic generator or UPS/inverter sized to the refrigerator load, with CDC’s 72-hour fuel/service discipline [11].

**Compliance posture.** Helmer’s GX pharmacy refrigerators are described as **NSF/ANSI 456 vaccine storage standard certified** and as supporting CDC vaccine-storage guidelines; the iPR125-GX listing identifies a version with “NSF/ANSI 456 Vaccine Storage Certified Labeling and Certificate of Calibration” [6], [7]. Helmer also provides product-simulation-bottle probes, min/max display/reset, high/low temperature alarms, door-open alarms, power-failure alarms, automatic alarm testing, and logs downloadable by USB [1].

**Remote monitoring.** Helmer has the strongest native integration base for local infrastructure: **USB, remote alarm contacts, RJ45 Ethernet, and an API for Ethernet connectivity development** [1]. For rural clinics where wired broadband is unreliable, the procurement specification should require a third-party cellular router or cellular environmental-monitoring gateway connected to Helmer’s dry-contact alarm/Ethernet path. Helmer’s own remote alarm accessory extends alarms up to **3,000 ft / 914 m** from the cabinet and can connect to a central alarm system, but that is not itself a cellular network solution [6].

**10-year cost components.** The published unit price for the iPR125-GX configuration is **$12,800/EA**, or **$102,400** for eight units [7]. Energy is low enough that it should not drive the decision: **2.5 kWh/day** equals **73,000 kWh** over 10 years for 8 units. Using EIA 2024 average commercial electricity prices—**11.87¢/kWh in Montana** and **9.27¢/kWh in Wyoming**—that is **$8,665** in Montana or **$6,767** in Wyoming over 10 years, giving a documented equipment-plus-electricity floor of **$111,065 MT / $109,167 WY** before service-contract pricing [1], [8]. Helmer’s warranty terms are **7 years compressor, 2 years parts, 1 year labor** in the U.S. and Canada [1]. For remote Montana/Wyoming service, the bid should price extended warranty or preventive-maintenance coverage after the first labor year, plus travel time [1].

### 2) Thermo Fisher Scientific TSX Series pharmacy refrigerator

**Temperature performance.** The TSX pharmacy refrigerator family is designed for **2°C to 8°C** storage, with factory set point at **5°C** [3]. Thermo describes V-drive adaptive control that increases compressor speed after door openings to restore temperature quickly and lowers speed when conditions are stable, and the distributor listing describes forced-air circulation for temperature uniformity and fast recovery [2], [9]. The retrieved TSX materials do not state a precise recovery time after full power restoration from a 6–12 hour outage.

**Outage behavior.** TSX documents battery backup for alarm function—“continuous charge provides full alarm function in case of power failure”—plus a low-battery alarm [2]. Its optional alarm-delay module can delay a central-alarm/telephone-dialer signal by **0.5 to 32 minutes** to avoid nuisance alarms from lightning or brief outages [2]. Because the TSX battery statement concerns alarms rather than compressor operation, TSX should be treated as dependent on the same site generator/UPS architecture required by CDC outage guidance [11].

**Compliance posture.** Thermo/Fisher states that the TSX Series is **certified to NSF/ANSI 456 Vaccine Storage standard** and can help meet CDC, WHO, and other health-ministry vaccine-storage and handling guidelines [3]. It has microprocessor controls, setpoint security, alarms, optional chart recorders, standard door locks, and 2°–8°C pharmacy storage alignment [3].

**Remote monitoring.** TSX has the clearest vendor-cloud monitoring path in the retrieved materials: it is compatible with wireless monitoring systems and remote alarm contacts, and Thermo Scientific DeviceLink HUB works with Thermo Fisher Cloud to monitor conditions inside TSX refrigerators/freezers and send equipment notifications [3]. For spotty rural connectivity, the practical requirement is local data buffering plus cellular failover; TSX’s product materials support cloud/wireless monitoring, but the cellular-network performance and offline-buffer duration should be specified in the monitoring-system line item rather than assumed from the refrigerator [3].

**10-year cost components.** The retrieved TSX sources provide the relevant utility-rate inputs but not a kWh/day figure for TSX3005PA/PD/PV, so a defensible 10-year energy total cannot be computed from the retrieved TSX materials. The EIA rate basis for Montana/Wyoming remains **11.87¢/kWh** and **9.27¢/kWh** for 2024 commercial customers [8]. In a final solicitation, require Thermo to quote annual kWh/day under ENERGY STAR or NSF/ANSI 456 test conditions and include on-site preventive maintenance with travel for the eight rural clinics.

### 3) Panasonic/PHCbi MDF-DU702VH-PA

**Temperature performance.** The MDF-DU702VH-PA is a **729 L / 25.74 cu ft ultra-low freezer** with **-40°C to -86°C** temperature range, not a 2°–8°C vaccine refrigerator [4]. Its technical sheet gives **±5°C** interior uniformity at -70°C and -80°C, average steady-state temperatures of **-69.38°C** and **-80.53°C**, and **8-minute temperature recovery to ±5°C of set point** after ENERGY STAR-style inner-door openings [4]. A PHC product sheet gives a tighter uniformity dataset—**±1.86°C at -70°C** and **±2.01°C at -80°C**—and steady-state energy of **5.91 kWh/day at -70°C** and **8.20 kWh/day at -80°C** [12]. The directly comparable technical sheet for daily consumption with six door openings gives **6.72 kWh/day at -70°C** and **9.00 kWh/day at -80°C** [4].

**Outage behavior.** With optional backup cooling kit MDF-UB7 and a **30 kg liquid CO₂ cylinder**, the MDF-DU702VH is documented at **approximately 9 hours** of backup cooling at **30°C ambient**, **-70°C set temperature**, and **no load** [5]. This is the only option among the three with a documented chamber-cooling backup duration, but it still does not cover the high end of the requested 12-hour outage window and requires CO₂ cylinder logistics and ventilation assessment because CO₂ can deplete oxygen near the equipment [5].

**Compliance posture.** The MDF-DU702VH-PA is ENERGY STAR certified and appropriate for ultra-low biological storage [4]. The retrieved materials do not position it as an NSF/ANSI 456 2°–8°C vaccine refrigerator, and its -40°C to -86°C range makes it the wrong primary platform for routine refrigerated vaccines [4].

**Remote monitoring.** PHC documents a remote alarm terminal for an external alarm device and a power-failure alarm battery; the controller provides touchscreen display and data-management functions, with USB access for logs and settings [5], [10]. As with Helmer, cellular coverage in remote clinics would need to be provided through an external cellular monitoring device connected to the remote alarm/contact or through a separate calibrated cellular data logger.

**10-year cost components.** For eight units over 10 years, using six-door-opening daily consumption from PHC’s technical sheet, energy use is **196,224 kWh at -70°C** or **262,800 kWh at -80°C**. At EIA 2024 commercial rates, that equals **$23,272 in Montana / $18,169 in Wyoming** at -70°C and **$31,536 in Montana / $24,557 in Wyoming** at -80°C [4], [8]. These figures exclude CO₂ cylinder rental/refills, optional backup-kit maintenance, annual service, and remote technician travel; those costs matter more for this model because the outage plan depends on CO₂ backup hardware [5].

## Rural Montana/Wyoming implementation requirements

For all three systems, specify the following as mandatory bid requirements rather than optional accessories:

1. **Facility backup power sized for the full outage window.** CDC’s benchmark is generator fuel sufficient for **at least 72 hours**, with quarterly testing and annual service; this exceeds the requested 6–12 hour outage window and avoids dependence on marginal battery/runtime assumptions [11].
2. **Continuous calibrated temperature monitoring.** CDC calls for review of continuous digital data logger software or website information and for recording min/max temperatures during outages [11]. The refrigerator’s internal controller should not be the sole compliance record; use a calibrated digital data logger with buffered storage and cellular alerting.
3. **Cellular failover, not Wi-Fi-only alerting.** Helmer and PHC provide dry-contact/remote-alarm paths, while Thermo provides wireless/cloud compatibility; none of the retrieved manufacturer materials proves robust performance over spotty rural cellular links. The procurement spec should require carrier diversity where available, local logging during network loss, and delayed escalation workflows for after-hours staff.
4. **Service contract with travel explicitly priced.** Helmer’s baseline warranty includes only **1 year labor**, Thermo’s retrieved page says warranty varies by country, and PHC’s manual assigns battery and backup-kit battery replacement to qualified service personnel, including replacement about every **3 years** for the backup cooling kit battery [1], [3], [5]. For eight dispersed clinics, require quoted annual preventive maintenance, calibration, emergency response SLA, travel/mileage, and loaner or emergency transport support.

## Recommendation

Buy **Helmer GX i.Series pharmacy refrigerators** for the eight clinic locations, with NSF/ANSI 456 labeling/certification, third-party calibrated cellular data loggers, and clinic backup power sized to CDC’s **72-hour** generator-fuel benchmark. Thermo TSX should be retained as the competitive alternate if Thermo quotes a lower fully burdened 10-year service price and provides a kWh/day figure and outage monitoring design. Do not use Panasonic/PHCbi MDF-DU702VH-PA as the standard vaccine refrigerator; procure it only for a separately justified ultra-cold program where the **9-hour CO₂ backup** and -70°C/-80°C operating profile are clinically required [5], [4].
