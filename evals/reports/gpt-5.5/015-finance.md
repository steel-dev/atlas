# vTv Therapeutics equity compensation, warrants, and dilution risk

**Bottom line.** vTv’s 2024–2025 financing pattern is best read as an equity-dependent runway strategy: the company raised large PIPE/private-placement proceeds while increasing non-cash stock-option compensation, but that runway came with a much larger contingent share base. Annual ASC 718 share-based compensation rose from **$1.578 million in 2023 to $2.757 million in 2024**, an increase of **$1.179 million, or 74.7%** [1]. In Q3 alone, stock-based compensation rose from **$853,000 in Q3 2024** to **$1.007 million in Q3 2025**, an increase of **$154,000, or 18.05%** [2][3]. At September 30, 2025, the company had **3,938,018 Class A shares outstanding** but **13,714,356 equity-classified warrants** and **1,007,306 stock options** outstanding, making the warrant-and-option overhang many times larger than the current issued Class A share count before considering ownership caps and exercise contingencies [3].

## Key quantified changes

| Measure | 2023 / Q3 2024 | 2024 / Q3 2025 | Increase | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Annual ASC 718 share-based compensation | **$1.578m** in FY 2023 | **$2.757m** in FY 2024 | **+$1.179m / +74.7%** | Large move toward equity awards as part of compensation mix [1]. |
| Q3 stock-based compensation, R&D | **$228k** for three months ended Sept. 30, 2024 | **$311k** for three months ended Sept. 30, 2025 | **+$83k / +36.4%** | More equity cost flowing through clinical/R&D expense [2][3]. |
| Q3 stock-based compensation, G&A | **$625k** for three months ended Sept. 30, 2024 | **$696k** for three months ended Sept. 30, 2025 | **+$71k / +11.4%** | Continued equity-heavy executive/administrative compensation [2][3]. |
| Q3 total stock-based compensation | **$853k** | **$1.007m** | **+$154k / +18.05%** | Non-cash expense now running at about $1.0m per quarter [2][3]. |

The annual increase is not itself immediate dilution: ASC 718 expense recognizes the grant-date fair value of awards over service periods. Dilution occurs later if options are exercised or shares are otherwise issued. But the accounting trend matters because it shows vTv using stock awards more heavily while it is also issuing equity and warrants to finance operations.

## Stock-option grants and retention signal

vTv’s Q3 2025 Form 10-Q reports that, during the nine months ended September 30, 2025, the company granted **388,816 stock options** at a **weighted-average exercise price of $19.67 per share** and a **weighted-average grant-date fair value of $15.00 per option**; outstanding options rose to **1,007,306** at a **$25.07 weighted-average exercise price**, while **372,211** were exercisable at a **$37.11 weighted-average exercise price** [3]. The same footnote states that vTv’s non-qualified stock options are issued to management, key employees, consultants, and non-employee directors, generally vest ratably over a **four-year service period**, that options issued in connection with the February 27, 2024 private placement vest ratably over **three years**, and that option awards expire after **ten years** from grant [3].

The most specific grant terms retrieved from the 2024–2025 period are therefore: **388,816 options granted in the nine months ended September 30, 2025 at a $19.67 weighted-average exercise price**, under the company’s equity-compensation framework; the Q3 2025 Form 10-Q does not break that aggregate into each individual grant date in the option roll-forward [3]. The company’s later disclosed Nasdaq Listing Rule **5635(c)(4)** inducement awards are outside the 2024–2025 period requested, so they are not treated as part of the Q3 2025 option roll-forward unless separately identified in vTv’s 2025 filings.

Analytically, the **$19.67** exercise price functions like an upside incentive rather than a cash-equivalent bonus: employees receive value only if the stock price exceeds the strike price and they remain through vesting. For a clinical-stage company with operating cash burn and periodic going-concern pressure, that supports a retention/recruitment strategy built around conserving cash, retaining clinical-development and corporate leadership through milestones, and aligning employees with shareholder value creation. The shareholder trade-off is that successful retention and share-price appreciation can convert the non-cash expense into actual new share issuance.

## Equity financings and immediate dilution

vTv’s financing record shows repeated equity issuance rather than debt-based or internally funded operations.

| Date / transaction | Securities issued | Gross proceeds | Net proceeds / accounting impact | Pricing and warrant terms | Dilution channel |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|---|
| **Feb. 27, 2024 PIPE/private placement** | **464,377 Class A shares** and pre-funded warrants for **3,853,997 Class A shares** | **~$51.0m** | 2024 statement of equity shows **$50.335m** net of offering costs | Class A shares at **$11.81/share**; pre-funded warrants at **$11.80** with **$0.01** exercise price; immediately exercisable; no expiration | Immediate dilution from issued shares and near-immediate dilution from pre-funded warrants [1]. |
| **Mar. 5, 2024 exchange** | Investors exchanged **116,493 Class A shares** for **116,590 additional pre-funded warrants** | No new gross proceeds stated | Left **3,970,587** pre-funded warrants outstanding at Dec. 31, 2024 | Same pre-funded warrant economics | Reduced issued shares but preserved near-share-equivalent overhang [1]. |
| **Sept. 17, 2024 TD Cowen ATM sale** | **179,400 Class A shares** sold under ATM program | Not separately stated as gross in the retrieved footnote | **$2.5m net proceeds**; 3.0% commission under sales agreement | Market-sale ATM; no warrant attached in the retrieved disclosure | Direct issued-share dilution for cash [3]. |
| **Aug. 29, 2025 SPA / Sept. 2025 private placement** | **5,243,732 units**, consisting of **682,018 Class A shares**, pre-funded warrants for **4,561,714 shares**, and accompanying common warrants for **5,243,732 shares** | **~$80.0m** | Q3 2025 equity statement records **$77.476m** net proceeds/offering-cost effect | Shares at **$15.265**; pre-funded warrants at **$15.255** plus **$0.01** exercise price; common warrants at **$22.71** for shares or **$22.70** for replacement pre-funded warrants; expire earlier of five years or 90 days after positive CATT1 top-line data | Immediate dilution from 682,018 issued shares; near-share-equivalent dilution from pre-funded warrants; contingent upside-linked dilution from common warrants [3]. |

This structure is a classic dilution-management compromise. vTv limited some immediate voting/ownership effects by using pre-funded warrants with beneficial-ownership blockers, but economically those pre-funded warrants are close to common stock because they require only **$0.01** of additional exercise consideration and do not expire [3]. The 2025 common warrants shift part of dilution into a contingent future event and could bring exercise cash if the stock exceeds the warrant strike, but they also create a visible cap-table overhang.

## Cash need and going-concern context

For the nine months ended September 30, 2025, vTv reported **$98.504 million** of cash and cash equivalents at period end, a **$23.942 million net loss**, and about **$16.004 million of net cash used in operating activities**, while financing activities provided **$77.762 million**, driven by the 2025 private placement [3]. The company also disclosed that its June 30, 2025 Form 10-Q had identified substantial doubt about its ability to continue as a going concern within one year, but that, based on cash and cash equivalents available after the financing, there was **no longer substantial doubt** for at least twelve months from issuance of the September 30, 2025 Form 10-Q [3].

That context explains why the company relied on equity financing and equity compensation: equity capital extended runway, while stock-based compensation reduced the need to pay equivalent cash compensation during a period of clinical-development spending and operating losses. But it also means existing shareholders bear the cost through a larger fully diluted share base.

## In-the-money / out-of-the-money status

At issuance, the 2025 common warrants were struck above the unit purchase price: the **$22.71** share-exercise price was above the **$15.265** common-share purchase price in the private placement, making them out-of-the-money relative to the financing price at issuance [3]. The 2025 options’ **$19.67 weighted-average exercise price** appears designed as an at-market or near-market upside incentive rather than a discounted cash substitute, while the company disclosed that the aggregate intrinsic value of in-the-money awards outstanding at September 30, 2025 was **de minimis**, indicating the option book was largely out-of-the-money or only minimally in-the-money at that date [3]. Pre-funded warrants with **$0.01** exercise prices are economically in-the-money/near-share equivalents whenever the Class A common stock trades above a penny [3].

As of the latest market-price check requested, the specific June 23, 2026 Class A common stock price was not in the pinned materials; without that price, the June 23, 2026 moneyness conclusion for the **$22.71** common warrants and **$25.07 / $19.67** option strikes cannot be finalized in this draft. The mechanical rule is straightforward: above the exercise price, the instrument is in-the-money and likely to dilute if exercised; below the exercise price, it is out-of-the-money but remains an overhang that can influence valuation and financing terms.

## Authorized share capacity and dilution management

vTv had substantial authorized share capacity at September 30, 2025: **200,000,000 authorized Class A shares** and **100,000,000 authorized Class B shares**, compared with **3,938,018 Class A shares** and **241 Class B shares** outstanding [3]. That capacity gives the company room to issue more equity, settle warrant exercises, and maintain equity plans without an immediate charter constraint, although it increases the importance of investor monitoring of fully diluted share count rather than only issued shares.

## Implications for existing shareholders

The evidence points to an equity-first financing and compensation strategy, not an effort to avoid dilution altogether. vTv appears to be managing dilution by staging it: issued shares and ATM shares create immediate dilution; pre-funded warrants function as near-share equivalents with ownership blockers; common warrants defer dilution until a higher stock price or catalyst; and employee options defer dilution until vesting and exercise. This approach extends runway and reduces cash compensation pressure, but it shifts a meaningful portion of financing and retention cost to shareholders through contingent equity.

For existing shareholders, the outcome branches are clear:

- **If warrants and options are exercised:** ownership percentages and per-share upside are diluted, but vTv receives exercise cash from common warrants and options; pre-funded warrants add little cash because their exercise price is only **$0.01** [3].
- **If warrants and options remain underwater:** actual share issuance may be delayed, but the overhang can still pressure valuation, complicate future financing negotiations, and leave discounted equity issuance as the fallback if cash needs re-emerge before clinical or partnership milestones generate non-dilutive funding.

Overall, vTv’s 2024–2025 pattern shows a company using equity to buy time: the financing solved near-term runway pressure, and options helped conserve cash and retain personnel, but the cost is a large contingent claim on future equity value.
