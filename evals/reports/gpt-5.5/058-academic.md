# Methodological divide over the Iliad and the Epic Cycle

**Bottom line.** The divide is no longer a simple opposition between “Homer borrowed from the Cycle” and “all parallels are generic tradition.” Neoanalysts such as Burgess, Currie, and Tsagalis treat the *Iliad*’s Cyprian material as meaningful engagement with earlier or collateral Trojan-war traditions; oralists such as Nagy, Foley, and Bakker are more likely to read the same material as multiform traditional resonance rather than text-to-text dependence. The most productive recent position is a synthesis—often called oral neoanalysis—that accepts oral multiformity while allowing specific, audience-recognizable allusion. P.Oxy. 3829 and digital Venetus A work have sharpened the evidence base since 2000, especially for *Iliad* 1 and the Zeus-plan tradition, but they have not settled whether the *Iliad* is alluding to a particular *Cypria* or to a wider epic system.

## 1. The methodological fault-line: source, motif, or tradition?

Finkelberg’s useful formulation is that both sides now accept the basic phenomenon: the *Iliad* and *Odyssey* repeatedly evoke episodes whose “proper place” lies in the Epic Cycle. The disagreement is what that evocation proves. Neoanalysts infer intertextual relations and often a special status for Homeric composition, sometimes linked to writing; oralists put Homer and the Cycle on the same plane as independent variants of a shared oral tradition [2]. Kullmann already softened the older neoanalytic model: Cyclic poems may have been written down after Homer, but much of their narrative content could have circulated orally before Homer [2].

Tsagalis’ review of Currie clarifies the modern spectrum. At one pole, oralists treat Homeric poetry as traditional poetry shaped by generations, so external intertextual allusion is excluded or minimized. At the other, classical Neoanalysis posits a single creative poet reworking earlier pre-Homeric epic sources, often in a written medium. Between them are “oral neoanalysts,” who reject writing as an indispensable condition but argue that developed or post-traditional orality can support specific allusion [4].

Burgess is central because he moves Neoanalysis away from crude source-hunting. He grants that Cyclic verbal and phraseological similarities often reflect formulaic typology rather than borrowing, but argues that “motif transference” can still be specific: what moves is not necessarily wording but a narrative-action motif normally attached to a particular heroic myth, redeployed in Homeric poetry [1]. Currie presses the same synthesis more assertively. *Homer’s Allusive Art* argues that Homer uses both Foley-style “traditional art” and Pasquali-style allusive art; Currie explicitly challenges the Lord/Foley claim that oral epic cannot make specific cross-references, citing Danek and Čolaković on Serbian and Bosnian epic traditions where singers do refer to individual stories or story-parts outside the immediate song [3], [4].

Foley supplies the strongest oralist counterweight. His “traditional referentiality” treats repeated diction, epithets, scenes, and motifs as signs that evoke the whole tradition or an “immanent” meaning, not necessarily a specific antecedent text [3]. Bakker is not simply anti-textual: he is cited in this debate as accepting Homeric epic’s oral-derived or transitional character and criticizing a purely dictation-based model, but still insists that oral poetics and traditional performance conditions shape what counts as evidence [3].

## 2. The *Cypria* evidence: what the fragments and summaries actually say

The relevant *Cypria* dossier is small but consequential. Proclus’ summary says that Zeus planned with Themis to bring about the Trojan War; the scholion-quoted opening fragment says Zeus saw Earth overburdened by humanity, resolved to lighten her through the Trojan War, and ends with the formula “the plan of Zeus was fulfilled,” the same formulaic field as *Iliad* 1.5 [10], [11]. Another fragment, from the Herculaneum material as printed by West, says Thetis avoided union with Zeus to please Hera; Zeus became angry and swore she would marry a mortal [11]. West also treats the Peleus–Thetis wedding on Pelion as central to the Cyprian opening sequence and notes the Chiron setting and spear tradition [11].

These data matter because the *Iliad* opens and closes at points where this Cyprian complex can be felt. The opening quarrel, Thetis’ supplication, Zeus’ nod, and the proemial “plan of Zeus” can be read as self-contained Iliadic plot. But the *Cypria* evidence makes it possible to hear a second register: Zeus’ larger plan to thin the heroic age, Thetis’ mortal marriage, and the chain that leads from the wedding to Paris’ judgement and the war.

## 3. *Iliad* 1: the “plan of Zeus,” Thetis, and Cyprian overpopulation

The strongest neoanalytic claim about *Iliad* 1 is not that Homer copied the *Cypria*, but that the proem and Zeus–Thetis scene are composed against a known Cyprian story-pattern. Burgess stresses that the *Cypria*’s withdrawal setup differs from the *Iliad*’s: in the *Cypria*, the detachment of Achilles is part of Zeus’ plan to relieve the Trojans, whereas in the *Iliad*, Zeus helps the Trojans as a means of honoring Thetis’ dishonored son after Achilles’ quarrel with Agamemnon [5]. Yet Burgess also argues that the *Cypria*’s Briseis/Chryseis material implies knowledge of the quarrel tradition and that *Iliad* 1.5 can suggest both the local Iliadic plan and the wider Cyclic manifestation of Zeus’ will [5].

The Venetus A scholion to *Iliad* 1.5 is the crucial ancient witness. It reports a story in which Earth is burdened by humanity and impiety; Zeus first causes the Theban War, then, advised by Momus, avoids total destruction by thunderbolt or flood and arranges two instruments of war: Thetis’ marriage to a mortal and the birth of beautiful Helen. It then quotes the *Cypria*’s Zeus-plan lines and finally notes that Aristarchus and Aristophanes instead interpreted the *Iliad*’s Διὸς βουλή as Thetis’ plan or request at *Iliad* 1.508 [10], [12]. The scholion therefore preserves the debate in miniature: cosmic Cyprian plan versus local Iliadic plot.

Oralists do not need to deny the connection. Nagy’s reading of the “Will/Plan of Zeus” treats the *Iliad* and *Cypria* as converging parts of one overarching epic theology: Zeus wills the destruction of many heroes, and the *Iliad*’s local narrative participates in that master-plan even when it does not spell out Earth’s overburdening [6]. The difference is methodological: Nagy does not have to posit a recoverable source relation; the shared formula belongs to a larger oral epic system.

## 4. *Iliad* 24: Judgement of Paris, Peleus–Thetis, and the ransom of Hector

The final book of the *Iliad* also reopens the Cyprian storyworld. Mackie notes that the poem’s only explicit reference to the Judgement of Paris occurs at *Iliad* 24.25–30, in the divine quarrel over Hector’s body; Hera, Poseidon, and Athena remain hostile to Troy because of Paris’ judgement, and the passage is juxtaposed with the Peleus–Thetis wedding at 24.59–63 [8]. That combination—wedding, divine beauty contest, hostile goddesses, and Trojan doom—is precisely the mythic complex that Proclus places at the beginning of the *Cypria* [10], [8].

Neoanalysts treat this as an especially pointed return. It is not a random mythological aside but a closing gesture toward the prehistory that made the war possible. The poem ends by resolving Hector’s body while recalling the earlier divine offence that guaranteed Troy’s destruction. Oralists can read the same passage as traditional resonance: the audience knows why Hera and Athena hate Troy, and the mention of Paris activates that knowledge without requiring a fixed *Cypria* text.

Burgess’ work on the *lusis* or ransom tradition adds a second *Iliad* 24 dossier. He argues that there was an alternative tradition in which Priam weighed out Hector’s body with gold; the *Iliad* still preserves gold and gifts, since Zeus orders gifts at 24.143–148, Priam packs robes, vessels, and gold at 24.228–237, and Priam gives them at 24.502 [5]. Burgess also points to early visual variants in which Hermes appears despite the *Iliad*’s handling of the scene, Trojans or Andromache accompany Priam, Priam and Achilles may both stand, and chin-touching suggests a non-Iliadic form of supplication [5]. This supports his broader model: the *Iliad* is not simply the origin of later Cyclic and artistic versions; it is one powerful crystallization within a multiform tradition.

## 5. Achilles’ awareness of his death

Achilles’ death is the strongest test case for the divide because the *Iliad* never narrates the death, yet repeatedly makes it present. Horn’s 2021 analysis lists a progressively sharper network: Achilles is marked for early death at 1.352, 1.416–417, 1.505, and 18.95; he faces the two-fates choice at 9.411–413; he will not sack Troy at 16.707–709 and 17.406–408; he will not grow old in his armor at 17.194–197; he will die soon after Hector at 18.95–96; his horses say he will die by god and man at 19.416–417; Paris and Apollo at the Scaean Gates are indicated at 21.277–278 and 22.358–360; and his bones will be joined with Patroclus’ at 23.80–83, 23.91–92, and 23.125–126. Achilles’ acceptance formula recurs at 18.115–116 and 22.365–366 [9].

Neoanalysts explain this density through motif transfer. Horn summarizes the older Pestalozzi/Burgess/West debate: the motif that Achilles’ death follows soon after Hector’s likely derives from another epic context, and Aethiopic evidence has Thetis prophesying Achilles’ fate before the Memnon episode, with Proclus saying that Thetis foretells “things concerning Memnon” to Achilles [9]. On this view, the *Iliad* absorbs the emotional and structural force of the post-Iliadic death of Achilles into its own narrative.

The oralist counterpoint is not that Achilles’ death is absent, but that such prolepsis can be traditional-referential rather than source-specific. Foley’s model lets formulas and motifs activate the audience’s knowledge of Achilles’ heroic destiny without proving dependence on a particular lost poem [3]. Horn also notes the main difficulty for a strong neoanalytic reading: the *Iliad* never hints at Memnon. West argued that the *Iliad* knew Achilles’ death details but not necessarily the Memnon story, while Kullmann objected; Horn explains the omission narratologically, since a Memnon allusion would delay the aura of imminent death after Hector [9]. The current balance is therefore asymmetrical: the *Iliad* strongly presupposes Achilles’ death, but it does not securely identify the exact Cyclic narrative that later contained it.

## 6. The Zeus–Thetis scene as the hinge of the debate

The Zeus–Thetis scene concentrates the entire methodological problem. Locally, Thetis asks Zeus to honor Achilles after Agamemnon’s insult; Zeus grants the request, and the Trojan success that follows serves Achilles’ τιμή. Cosmically, Thetis’ marriage to Peleus and the birth of Achilles are part of the same sequence that the *Cypria* and the Venetus A scholion connect with Zeus’ plan to reduce the heroic population [10], [11].

For Burgess, the scene’s force lies in the coexistence of these levels. The *Cypria*’s Διὸς βουλή and the *Iliad*’s proemial formula need not be derivative in either direction. Near Eastern and Indo-European population-destruction myths make the Cyprian Zeus-plan look traditional rather than a late expansion of *Iliad* 1.5; at the same time, the *Iliad* reshapes the cosmic plan into the immediate drama of Achilles’ dishonor and Thetis’ supplication [5], [10].

For Nagy, the same material is less a problem of priority than of poetics. The various plans of Zeus in different epics are manifestations of one master-plan, the Will of Zeus, whose telos is the destruction of heroes [6]. Foley-style oralism would describe the scene’s power as traditional referentiality: it invokes an epic system in which Thetis, Achilles, Zeus, and the end of the heroes are already semantically charged [3]. Currie and Tsagalis accept the oral-system premise but add that the *Iliad* can allude pointedly within it [3], [4].

## 7. Evidence: Bernabé fragments, Venetus A scholia, and comparative poetics

The schools differ less over which evidence counts than over how it is weighted.

## 8. P.Oxy. 3829 and digital evidence since 2000

P.Oxy. 3829 matters most for *Iliad* 1. The papyrus hypothesis to Book 1 of the *Iliad*, which West dates to the later second century CE, says Zeus judged the heroic race impious and consulted Themis to destroy them utterly [11]. Its most concrete effect was to confirm Heyne’s emendation of Proclus’ “Thetis” to “Themis” for the *Cypria*’s opening consultation [11]. It also gives a fuller prose account of the Peleus–Thetis wedding: the feast on Pelion with Chiron, Zeus’ invitation to the gods, Hermes blocking Eris at Zeus’ command, and Eris’ golden apple producing the Hera–Athena–Aphrodite beauty contest [11].

The papyrus did not resolve the debate. West and Barker both stress that the impiety motif is absent from Proclus and likely foreign to the *Cypria*; Barker explicitly warns that the papyrus account and the *Cypria* should not be treated as exactly equivalent [11], [10]. Its effect, therefore, was methodological rather than decisive: it improved the source criticism of the Cyprian opening, confirmed Themis as the better reading, and exposed how later hypothesis traditions could combine Cyprian, scholastic, and broader mythological material.

Digital work since 2000 has had a similar effect on the scholia. The Homer Multitext project provides the first complete web-based digital edition of the Venetus A, the tenth-century and oldest complete witness to the *Iliad*, with its marginal scholia [13]. The project notes that Erbse/TLG omitted, truncated, or emended some scholia, whereas HMT transcribes all 654 pages from high-resolution 2007 images, encodes the material in TEI-XML, and links each Iliad line and scholion to a unique identifier and image location [13]. Its editors explicitly frame Iliadic transmission as oral recomposition-in-performance and argue that the fluidity and multiformity of the textual tradition follow naturally from oral recomposition [13].

This digital turn has strengthened the oralist side’s empirical language—multiformity, recomposition, and manuscript plurality—while also helping neoanalysts, because searchable and spatially encoded scholia make it easier to test ancient source attributions and compare scholia systematically rather than relying on the selectivity of print editions [13]. In short, digital papyrology and HMT have made the debate more precise, not less divided.

## 9. Current synthesis in recent monographs

The current synthesis is real but incomplete. Currie’s *Homer’s Allusive Art* is the most explicit monograph-length attempt to combine Foley-style traditional referentiality with specific allusion: it insists that the two are not mutually exclusive and that Homeric poetry should not be flattened either into purely oral traditional resonance or into purely literary source-dependence [3]. Tsagalis’ “oral neoanalysis” occupies the same middle ground, treating Homeric poetry as post-traditional or developed orality in which external allusion can function without requiring written sources [4].

The 2015 *Greek Epic Cycle and its Ancient Reception* companion, with Currie’s chapter on the *Cypria*, represents this plural ecology at field level: source-research, oral tradition, reception history, linguistics, and meter are all brought to bear, and Cyclic epic is treated as a central component of Greek epic culture rather than a mere appendix to Homer [7]. Davies’ 2019 *Cypria* commentary continues the revival of fragment-based Cyclic philology; it is described as a detailed monograph on the poem’s authorship, title, date, sources, and relationship to Homer, and as part of a wider rediscovery of lost Cyclic poetry [14], [15].

The synthesis has not produced a single consensus model. Recent overviews still frame the field through open tensions: “sober synthesis” versus “extreme caution,” multiformity between oral and written traditions, and renewed oral-textual models involving multitext, scholia, and Cyclic epic [7]. The best current description is therefore not reconciliation but controlled pluralism: neoanalysis supplies the question “which motif has been transferred and why?”; oral theory supplies the warning “do not mistake traditional resonance for textual dependence”; and recent oral neoanalysis tries to decide case by case how specific the resonance is.
