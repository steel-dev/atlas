# Data-breach insurance comparison for a 15-employee Austin business with PCI-DSS and HIPAA exposure

## Executive takeaways

For a 15-employee Austin business processing customer payment cards and maintaining employee health records, **Chubb and Coalition can be compared at policy-wording level from the fetched materials; Cowbell can only be compared at product-overview level because the fetched Cowbell sources did not include equivalent specimen wording for regulatory fines, PCI/HIPAA sublimits, or exclusions.** That matters: regulatory fines, PCI assessments, breach-response costs, and e-commerce exclusions are wording-sensitive and can change by endorsement, state, admitted/non-admitted form, and declarations page.

**Best-supported conclusions from the sources:**

1. **Coalition is the clearest in the fetched wording for PCI and regulatory coverage structure.** Its policy has separate insuring agreements for **Regulatory Defense and Penalties**, **PCI Fines and Assessments**, and **Breach Response**, with the declarations showing how each limit/sub-limit and retention is scheduled; the policy states that the aggregate policy limit is the maximum for all damages, loss, PCI fines, regulatory penalties, claim expenses, and other amounts unless an endorsement changes that structure [1]. Coalition also has documented “Breach Response Services” outside limits with $0 retention, including a 24/7 hotline, two hours of panel legal advice, and initial forensic/intelligence support [2].
2. **Chubb is also strong on dual PCI/HIPAA wording, but the buyer must inspect the declarations for sublimits.** Chubb’s Cyber ERM wording expressly includes medical/healthcare data and protected health information in “Protected Information,” and it treats PCI/merchant assessments as **Payment Card Loss** separate from government **Regulatory Fines** [3]. Chubb’s sublimits, where scheduled, are part of and reduce the applicable limit rather than adding to it [3]. Chubb provides 24/7 incident reporting through a hotline and Cyber Alert app, with a panel of coaches, forensic firms, notification providers, credit-monitoring providers, and PR firms [4].
3. **Cowbell appears service-oriented and small-business-focused, but the fetched sources support only product-overview—not specimen-policy—detail.** Cowbell’s Prime 100 overview says the program is for businesses with up to $100 million in revenue and lists medical offices, retailers, hospitality, healthcare, and other classes in appetite; it also says Security Breach Expense can include investigation/forensics, customer notification, call-center services, and post-event monitoring such as credit monitoring, while Security Breach Liability can include defense expenses, compensatory damages, settlements, and fines or penalties assessed by a regulatory agency/government entity or for PCI-DSS noncompliance [5]. However, the fetched Cowbell materials available here do not provide the same specimen definitions, sublimit mechanics, credit-monitoring duration, defense-cost erosion rules, or e-commerce exclusions that were available for Chubb and Coalition. A Cowbell quote should therefore be evaluated by requesting the full specimen policy, endorsements, and declarations.

## Regulatory fine coverage and how sublimits work

### Chubb

Chubb separates **government regulatory exposure** from **payment-card/merchant exposure**. Government fines are handled through the policy’s regulatory framework: “Regulatory Fines” are civil monetary fines or penalties imposed by a governmental regulator because of a regulatory proceeding, but the definition excludes amounts that are uninsurable under law, criminal fines, disgorgement/restitutionary amounts, and multiplied-damage portions [3]. For the Austin business’s employee health records, the important wording point is that Chubb’s “Protected Information” includes medical or healthcare data and protected health information [3]. That makes HIPAA-type exposure textually relevant, but only if the proceeding arises from a covered cyber incident and the fine is insurable.

PCI-DSS exposure is treated separately as **Payment Card Loss**. Chubb’s wording covers monetary assessments, fines, penalties, chargebacks, reimbursements, and fraud recoveries, including card reissuance costs, arising from a network security failure or failure to protect payment-card data under payment-card/merchant agreements due to actual or alleged noncompliance with PCI-DSS, EMV, or mobile-payment security requirements [3]. That is favorable for merchants because PCI assessments are often contractual/association-driven rather than government fines.

The trade-off is that Chubb’s limit structure must be read from the declarations. The fetched wording states that covered costs, damages, and claims expenses apply against limits, and sublimits shown in declarations or endorsements are part of and not in addition to the applicable limit [3]. In practical terms, a $1 million regulatory sublimit inside a $2 million cyber limit would not create $3 million of total protection; payment would erode the policy aggregate.

### Coalition

Coalition is explicit in the fetched policy. The declarations in the fetched policy show separate third-party coverage lines for **Network and Information Security Liability**, **Regulatory Defense and Penalties**, **Multimedia Content Liability**, and **PCI Fines and Assessments**, and first-party coverage lines including **Breach Response** [1]. In that example, the declarations schedule each coverage with a limit/sub-limit and retention/sub-retention, while the aggregate policy limit remains the most the insurers will pay regardless of the number of insuring agreements purchased [1].

Coalition’s **Regulatory Defense and Penalties** insuring agreement pays claim expenses and regulatory penalties that the insured becomes legally obligated to pay resulting from a regulatory proceeding for a security failure or data breach [1]. “Regulatory penalties” are monetary fines and penalties imposed in a regulatory proceeding to the extent insurable by law; they do not include costs to comply with injunctive relief, establish or improve privacy/security practices, or audit/reporting/compliance costs [1]. For HIPAA exposure, the key question is whether the employee health-record event is a covered data breach/security failure and whether the HIPAA penalty is insurable in the relevant jurisdiction.

Coalition’s **PCI Fines and Assessments** coverage is also separate. It pays PCI fines/assessments resulting from a claim for security failure or data breach compromising payment-card data [1]. The definition includes direct monetary fines/assessments for fraud recovery, operational expenses such as card reissuance and cardholder notification, and case-management fees owed under a merchant-services agreement, but it expressly excludes chargebacks, interchange fees, discount fees, and other service-related fees/rates/charges [1].

Coalition’s sublimit mechanics are buyer-friendly in their clarity but still aggregate-limited. The aggregate limit is the maximum for all damages, loss, PCI fines/assessments, regulatory penalties, claim expenses, and other amounts; each insuring-agreement limit/sub-limit is the maximum for that agreement [1]. A separate help article explains that Breach Response Costs may in many cases be provided as an additional limit equal to the overall policy limit, but the issued policy and endorsements control [2].

### Cowbell

The fetched Cowbell overview does support high-level regulatory and PCI coverage concepts: Security Breach Liability can include defense expenses, compensatory damages, settlement amounts, and fines or penalties assessed by a regulatory agency/government entity or for non-compliance with PCI-DSS [5]. But it does **not** provide the policy definitions needed to confirm HIPAA treatment, whether PCI assessments are covered the same way as card-brand assessments under Chubb/Coalition, whether coverage is limited to insurable penalties, or how sublimits erode the aggregate [5]. For this reason, Cowbell should not be rejected, but it should be treated as **not yet comparable at wording level** until the broker provides the specimen form, Texas endorsements, and quote declarations.

For a dual PCI/HIPAA small business, the Cowbell quote review should ask for written answers to five points:

- Are HIPAA civil monetary penalties covered, and only “to the extent insurable”?
- Are PCI fines and card-brand assessments covered separately from government regulatory fines?
- Are PCI chargebacks, interchange fees, and payment-processor service fees excluded?
- Do defense costs and breach-response costs erode the aggregate?
- Are regulatory, PCI, notification, credit-monitoring, and legal-defense costs subject to sublimits lower than the requested policy limit?

## Incident response services and SLAs

**Ranking on documented response detail in fetched sources:**

1. **Coalition** — most explicit. It documents a 24/7 hotline, two hours of panel legal consultation, initial forensics/intelligence support, outside-limits/$0-retention Breach Response Services, and a stated 5-minute average claims response time [2], [1]. Coalition’s panel list includes breach-response law firms, notification providers such as Epiq and Experian, forensic/IR providers including Coalition Incident Response, Kivu, Crypsis, Mandiant, and Charles River Associates, and PR/crisis providers [1].
2. **Chubb** — strong panel and 24/7 access, but no hard SLA found. Chubb states policyholders can call a 24/7 hotline or use the Cyber Alert app, and its partner list includes coach firms, forensic firms, notification/credit-monitoring vendors, and PR providers [4]. The policy also permits Cyber Incident Response Team providers while stating that those providers are independent contractors and the insured is not required to use them [3].
3. **Cowbell** — 24/7 claim-expert availability in overview, but limited detail. Cowbell says its cyber-claim experts are on call 24/7 to help immediately with post-incident recovery services [5]. The fetched sources do not provide a response-time metric or panel/vendor rules.

For a 15-employee business without in-house security counsel, the practical difference is that **Coalition’s initial services are the easiest to understand from the fetched materials**, while **Chubb’s panel depth appears broad but less SLA-specific**. Cowbell requires quote-level verification.

## Credit monitoring and legal defense

Credit monitoring and legal defense matter because the Austin business has two affected populations: customers whose payment-card data may be compromised and employees whose health information may trigger breach-notification and privacy obligations.

- **Chubb**: Cyber Incident Response Expenses include legal consultation, forensic services including PCI forensic work, public-relations services, privacy-law compliance work, regulator/individual notification, call-center and mailing expenses, credit monitoring/freezing/thawing, and identity/fraud-related services with consent where applicable [3]. Chubb also covers claims expenses for covered cyber/privacy/network-security claims [3].
- **Coalition**: Breach Response Costs include legal and forensic services, regulator and individual notification, and credit/identity monitoring. The policy wording states up to one year of credit or identity monitoring, or longer if required by law, and the fetched policy includes endorsement language that can expand monitoring/identity restoration in some contexts [1]. Coalition’s legal defense wording includes payment of claim expenses for covered liability and regulatory proceedings, and an endorsement gives Coalition the right and duty to defend covered claims/regulatory proceedings subject to limits, retentions, exclusions, and policy terms [1].
- **Cowbell**: The fetched overview says Security Breach Expense can include investigation/forensics, customer notification, call-center services, and post-event monitoring such as credit monitoring; it also says Security Breach Liability can include defense expenses, compensatory damages, and settlements [5]. It does not state credit-monitoring duration, identity-restoration terms, defense-cost erosion, or panel counsel rules [5].

## Policy exclusions for e-commerce businesses

For an e-commerce business, the highest-risk exclusions are not usually the headline cyber exclusions; they are the exclusions that remove **merchant fees, chargebacks, contract liabilities, IP/media problems, platform outages, and known noncompliance**.

### Chubb exclusions to review

Chubb’s fetched wording contains several e-commerce-relevant limitations. Intentional, fraudulent, or knowing unlawful acts; prior-known incidents or proceedings; and certain infrastructure or service-provider outages can limit recovery [3]. Chubb also excludes contractual liability subject to carvebacks, and it treats merchant-service fees/chargebacks carefully: ordinary fees and chargebacks are excluded except where they fall within the policy’s Payment Card Loss coverage [3]. Media-related exclusions also matter for online advertising, product descriptions, and website content because false/deceptive advertising and goods/services quality/performance representations may fall outside media coverage [3]. Chubb’s Payment Card Loss wording also does not pay for later PCI/EMV/mobile-payment noncompliance fines unrelated to a specific covered claim or for upgrade/compliance-improvement costs beyond pre-incident controls [3].

### Coalition exclusions to review

Coalition’s e-commerce exclusions are explicit in the fetched policy. The contractual-liability exclusion has carvebacks for confidentiality/security obligations and PCI Fines and Assessments [1]. The merchant-liability exclusion bars chargebacks, interchange fees, discount fees, service-related fees/rates/charges, and liability or fees from a merchant service provider, processor, card company, or bank reversing/freezing payment transactions, except for PCI Fines and Assessments [1]. The IP exclusion is also important for online sales because it excludes various intellectual-property violations connected with goods, products, or services, subject to a multimedia-content carveback that does not restore all IP coverage [1].

### Cowbell exclusions to review

The fetched Cowbell materials do not show the relevant exclusion wording [5]. Before binding, ask the broker to identify exclusions or conditions for:

- payment-card processor or shopping-cart platform outages;
- PCI-DSS noncompliance or failure to maintain minimum controls;
- chargebacks, assessments, interchange fees, and merchant-service fees;
- online tracking/website privacy claims;
- product/service advertising or IP disputes;
- prior-known vulnerabilities or incidents;
- outsourced IT, cloud, payment processor, or e-commerce platform failures.

## Premium drivers for $2–5 million revenue businesses with dual PCI/HIPAA exposure

The fetched materials do not provide an Austin-specific quote table for a 15-employee, $2–5 million revenue company with both PCI and HIPAA exposure. Public small-business benchmarks are only context: Insureon reports an average small-business cyber premium of **$129/month ($1,552/year)** and annual premiums ranging from about **$400 to over $8,000**, while noting that premiums depend on policy limits, sensitive data handled, controls, location, and revenue [6]. A separate small-business broker benchmark says standalone cyber coverage with a **$1 million** limit often starts around **$1,500 annually**, but emphasizes there is no universal rate card and that healthcare/retail/payment-data exposure prices differently from lower-data businesses [7].

For this risk profile, the premium is likely to be driven by:

| Driver | Why it matters for this business | Source support |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue and limit requested | $2–5 million revenue affects business-interruption exposure and the size of requested limits/retentions. A $1M vs $2M or $5M limit changes expected severity. | Insureon identifies policy limits/deductible, types of coverage, industry risks, employee count, sensitive data, cyber risk management, and claims history as rating factors, and notes cyber limits typically range from $1M to $5M [6]. |
| Payment-card volume and PCI posture | More card transactions and weak PCI controls increase card-brand assessment and forensic exposure. | Insureon states businesses that handle cardholder information need PCI compliance, including retailers taking credit cards on e-commerce sites and healthcare providers taking credit cards in office or online patient portals; it also says PCI-related cybersecurity measures affect pricing [8]. Chubb and Coalition both have specific PCI/payment-card coverage categories tied to PCI-DSS or merchant-service agreements [3], [1]. |
| Health-record/PHI exposure | Employee health records can trigger privacy-law notification and regulatory defense costs even for a small workforce. | Chubb expressly includes protected health information in Protected Information; Coalition covers legally protected PII and regulatory proceedings for data breach/security failure [3], [1]. |
| Security controls | MFA, endpoint protection, backups, patching, email security, remote-access controls, and incident-response planning affect ransomware, breach-response, and funds-transfer risk. | Coalition materials emphasize active risk management and claims/security engineering; Cowbell describes risk-engineering guidance to remediate exposure and optimize premium [1], [5]. |
| Claims history and known incidents | Prior-known circumstances can be excluded and will affect pricing/eligibility. | Chubb and Coalition both contain prior-knowledge/continuity concepts that can limit coverage for known incidents [3], [1]. |
| Vendor and platform dependence | E-commerce businesses rely on payment processors, hosting, shopping carts, cloud services, and MSPs; outages or third-party failures may be excluded or need endorsements. | Chubb and Coalition both contain third-party infrastructure/merchant-service limitations that should be checked for online businesses [3], [1]. |

Sublimits may make a nominal policy limit misleading. | Chubb states sublimits are part of and reduce applicable limits; Coalition schedules limits/sub-limits and retentions by coverage [3], [1]. |
| Use of insurer services/panel | Use of approved breach coaches, forensic firms, and panel counsel may affect consent, retention, and payment timing. | Chubb and Coalition both use panels and consent mechanics; Coalition’s Breach Response Services are described as outside limits/$0 retention [3], [2]. |

## Practical recommendation for the Austin buyer

If choosing based only on the fetched evidence, **Coalition is the most transparent for this specific PCI/HIPAA comparison**, because the sources include policy wording for regulatory defense, PCI fines, breach response, exclusions, limits/retentions, and a documented response-time metric [1], [2]. **Chubb is also a strong candidate**, especially where broad incident-response vendor choice and explicit protected-health-information wording are priorities, but the broker must show the declarations page to confirm whether Payment Card Loss, Regulatory Fines, breach response, and notification/credit monitoring are sublimited below the headline limit [3], [4]. **Cowbell remains a viable quote to obtain, but not one to bind without specimen wording**, because the fetched sources support 24/7 claims help and risk engineering but not the regulatory, PCI, credit-monitoring, legal-defense, or exclusion details needed for this exposure [5].

For a 15-person business, the final comparison should be done on a quote grid that lists, at minimum: aggregate limit, retention, regulatory-fine sublimit, PCI-fines/assessments sublimit, breach-response outside-limits status, credit-monitoring duration, panel/non-panel counsel rules, defense-cost erosion, business-interruption waiting period, dependent-system coverage, funds-transfer/social-engineering sublimits, and all e-commerce/merchant exclusions.
