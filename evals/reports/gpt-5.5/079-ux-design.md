**Bottom line.** Design should not treat “collectivism” as a single switch: the current Hofstede/The Culture Factor country comparison displayed South Korea as **PDI 60, IDV 58**, Finland as **PDI 33, IDV 75**, and Mexico as **PDI 81, IDV 34** as of the 2026-06-23 fetch, correcting the expected IDV values of 18, 63, and 30 respectively [1]. The strongest practical recommendation is therefore: **South Korea** should default to face-saving drafts, anonymous or semi-anonymous peer review, and teacher-mediated escalation; **Finland** should permit more open student-to-student critique and public help requests by default; **Mexico** should keep teacher authority visible while using warm group spaces, private correction, and teacher-endorsed peer roles. The measurable platform-specific evidence is uneven: Wilma has quantified Finnish lower-secondary communication/marking data, Mexico has quantified deployment scale but a qualitative secondary Google Classroom study, and Classting has product/adoption details plus a Korean online peer-feedback study rather than Classting-specific engagement metrics.

## What the three platform cases actually support

### South Korea: Classting as a closed, teacher-created class community, not an open peer-review tool

The current South Korean platform case is **“Classting - Class management”** by CT Corp. Its core interaction model is a teacher-created classroom entered through an invitation code or URL; only class members access posts. The functions documented in the product listing are noticeboard announcements, class-album photos, assignment submission and grading status, photo/video sharing, real-time opinions and debate, and “Ting” connections with other classes for collaborative projects. The listing identifies the target users as classroom participants and shows **5M+ downloads**, **62.4K reviews**, and a “Users Interact” rating [3]. The current Classting AI secondary offering, “클래스팅 AI 중고등,” targets middle and high schools [12], and the Classting AI site reports **over 26,000 educational institutions** using Classting AI; its current functions are less peer-review-centered and more teacher/assessment-centered: personalized AI diagnosis, CAT assessments, real-time progress dashboards, tailored supplementary learning, essay/constructed-response grading through the teacher-only AI assistant Jello, and mock-exam/test-prep functions [4].

That implementation profile matters. Classting’s documented collaboration affordances are bounded class feeds, comments/media, debate, assignments, and cross-class projects, but the available platform sources do not provide a Classting-specific causal comparison such as “anonymous comments increased peer replies by X%.” The closest Korean empirical evidence found is a named online peer-feedback study, **“Implementation and Perception of Online Peer Feedback by Korean EFL Students,”** in which **15 Korean students** in a **two-month TOEFL beginner class** commented on peers’ writing on an online blog after class. The study reports that most comments and revisions were local rather than global, with alternation most frequent in local feedback and suggestion most frequent in global feedback [13]. This supports designing structured prompts that make higher-order critique easier; it does not support a causal claim about Classting engagement rates.

### Finland: Wilma is dominant infrastructure, with quantified lower-secondary marking frequency

The Finnish platform case is **Wilma**, the official Visma Aquila Oy / Visma app and the successor to the StarSoft product name used in older literature. Google Play reports **1M+ downloads** and identifies the users as teachers, students, and guardians. The app lets users read and write messages, view schedules, homework, exams, lesson notes, and announcements; it sends notifications for new messages and lesson notes; guardians verify or pre-report absences; and users can see course information, homework, exam dates, and absence data [6]. Omnia’s current Finnish institutional description places Wilma as a student-information system: students choose courses, view schedules, track performance, read bulletins, and communicate with teachers/guardians/workplace supervisors; teachers enter evaluations and absences; guardians monitor absences, communicate with teachers, and read bulletins [7].

Wilma’s governance model is role-based and teacher/guardian-visible. A 2019 Finnish high-school study in the *International Journal of Communication* describes Wilma as one-to-one and one-to-many communication among teachers, students, and guardians; parents cannot message students; principals have broad rights; teachers access the students/groups they teach; students access their own data, grades, absences, and school communications. The same study notes that some students do not regularly check Wilma, causing teachers to use other channels such as Facebook [8].

The strongest platform-specific measured evidence is Sirkko, Kotilainen & Mononen’s 2025 *Nordic Studies in Education* study of parents of pupils in Finnish grades **1–9**, **N=558**, including **N=340** primary and **N=218** lower-secondary parents; **32.6%** were parents of pupils receiving special educational support, with data collected through two online questionnaires from **December 2023 to June 2024** [14]. Wilma was the dominant **parent-channel** for home–school contact: **551/558 parents, 98.7%**, used Wilma; in lower secondary, **217/218, 99.5%**, used it; phone calls were **165/558, 29.6%**, WhatsApp/SMS **128/558, 22.9%**, and e-mail **18/558, 3.2%** [14]. In lower secondary grades 7–9, the measured outcome was **teacher marking frequency**, not student peer collaboration: markings differed significantly by special-support status, **χ²(2)=15.556, p<.001, Cramer’s V=.27**. No-support pupils received markings daily **25/155, 16.1%**, weekly **105/155, 67.7%**, and less than weekly **25/155, 16.1%**; pupils receiving special support received markings daily **20/62, 32.3%**, weekly **24/62, 38.7%**, and less than weekly **18/62, 29.0%** [14]. This is evidence about parent-channel use and teacher feedback/marking frequency, not direct peer-interaction or assignment-completion frequency.

### Mexico / Latin America: Google Classroom at scale, with teacher-controlled assignment and feedback workflow

The Mexico/Latin America case is **Google Classroom within Google for Education / Google Workspace for Education deployments**. During COVID-19, Google reported that in Mexico the Secretaría de Educación Pública delivered **more than 1 million** G Suite for Education accounts for teachers and **more than 18 million** for students, with Classroom activities aligned to curriculum and **18 webinars** that generated **more than 1 million registrations** and **more than 5 million views** [10]. The later Mexico case study identifies Baja California as the first Mexican state to adopt Google for Education more than **10 years** earlier and reports that in **March 2025** it became the first Mexican state to invest officially in Google Workspace for Education Plus; by then **more than 162,000 students**, hundreds of teachers, **480 public secondary schools**, and **more than 175,000 Education Plus licenses** were using the technology every day [11]. In Jalisco, the education authority supplied **more than 45,000 Chromebooks** to teaching teams, covering **more than 90%** of basic and upper-secondary teachers, plus **32,000 Chromebooks** in mobile “Aulas Google” carts; the named deployment documents specify simultaneous work, shared projects, more agile/immediate feedback, teacher organization of activities, and online review of assignments/exams for **1.3 million students** using Workspace, Classroom, and Gemini [11]. They do **not** specify whether those programs used Classroom Stream posts, public comments, private comments, rubrics, originality reports, or Google Meet as implemented interaction conditions, so those affordances should not be treated as documented behavior in Baja California or Jalisco [11].

Google’s official Classroom support materials define the broader available interaction features: teachers create and manage classes, create/review/grade assignments, start a video meeting, post materials/questions/announcements in the class Stream, provide direct real-time feedback, and invite guardians for e-mail summaries; students track classwork, submit assignments, check originality/feedback/grades, share resources, and interact in Stream or e-mail [15]. Classroom also supports public and private comment notifications, rubrics, and originality reports: teachers can enable originality reports per assignment; students can run up to **3** originality reports before turning in; and the teacher’s originality report is visible only to the teacher after submission [16] [17].

The closest Mexican secondary Google Classroom implementation study found is Vega Reyes & Berumen Enríquez’s 2024 **“Implementación de la plataforma Google Classroom, en la Unidad Académica Secundaria de la UAZ en el ciclo escolar 2021–2022.”** It is qualitative, cross-sectional, and descriptive; its subjects were **32 teachers** and **6 administrative staff**, representing **100%** of the operational staff using Google Classroom; summer 2020 training lasted **30 hours** in **10** three-hour sessions. The study reports positive continuity/adaptation during pandemic remote learning but does not report exact student participation, comment, private-help, or assignment-completion percentages [18].

## What the measurable evidence says about anonymity, moderation, and public/private correction

The clearest experimental evidence on anonymity comes from the peer-review literature rather than from Classting, Wilma, or Google Classroom deployments. In **“Effects of anonymity on online peer review in second-language writing,”** **126** students were invited and **114 Dutch second-year university students** aged **18–24** participated in a quasi-experiment comparing anonymous and non-anonymous online peer review of English essays. Researchers coded **1,103** revision-oriented feedback items: **620** in the anonymous condition and **483** in the non-anonymous condition. Total feedback volume did not differ significantly: anonymous **M=6.08, SD=3.74** versus non-anonymous **M=5.55, SD=3.43**, **t(187)=-1.00, p=.318**. However, anonymous students gave significantly more directive higher-order concerns, processed more directive higher-order feedback, and earned significantly higher final writing-module grades [19]. Although the sample is Dutch university rather than Korean/Finnish/Mexican secondary, the mechanism is directly relevant to adolescent group work: anonymity did not merely increase quantity; it changed the type and uptake of critique.

For Korea-specific peer feedback, the **15-student**, **two-month** online blog study of Korean EFL students shows that unstructured online peer review tends to concentrate on local corrections rather than global critique [13]. That points to a design pattern: anonymity or semi-anonymity should be paired with rubrics and prompts for higher-order issues, otherwise students may avoid challenging a peer’s argument or project design even when a digital comment box exists.

Teacher moderation evidence is more about **visibility and authority structure** than randomized approval workflows. Classting’s teacher-created, invitation-only class model, Wilma’s role-based teacher/guardian visibility, and Google Classroom’s teacher-controlled Stream/Classwork/assignment workflow all put teachers in the supervisory layer [3] [8] [15]. The Finnish Wilma parent study shows a measurable moderation/visibility effect in practice: lower-secondary pupils receiving special support were more likely to receive daily teacher markings (**32.3%**) than no-support pupils (**16.1%**), with a significant association **χ²(2)=15.556, p<.001, V=.27** [14]. Qualitatively, the same study reports that negative markings alone decreased motivation, while positive markings enhanced it, and that negative aspects were shared in Wilma more easily and often than positive things [14]. This does not prove that teacher approval increases peer replies or assignment completion, but it does show that teacher-visible correction records shape perceived safety and motivation.

Public-versus-private correction evidence is also strongest as triangulation. Google Classroom explicitly separates public Stream interaction from private comments/assignment feedback and teacher-visible originality reports [15] [16]. Wilma, by contrast, can make lesson markings visible to both pupils and parents, and parents reported that negative-only markings could reduce motivation and feel like a permanent disciplinary record [14]. Therefore, for mistakes in drafts, plagiarism/originality, low scores, or behavior, the safer design pattern is **private correction first**, then optional student-controlled public sharing of the corrected insight. Public displays should be reserved for praise, exemplars, voluntarily shared questions, and group process updates.

## How power distance shows up in digital help-seeking

The evidence supports a directional but not platform-causal interpretation. Mexico’s **PDI 81** is the highest of the three, South Korea’s **PDI 60** is moderate-high, and Finland’s **PDI 33** is low [1]. PISA 2022 adds that Mexican students report strong teacher support (**77%** interest, **79%** extra help) but also higher loneliness/exclusion (**25%** lonely, **26%** outsider/left out) than Korea or Finland [9]. Korea combines moderate-high power distance with high belonging and orderly classrooms: only **9%** reported they could not work well and only **7%** said students do not listen [2]. Finland’s lower power distance aligns with a design tolerance for more direct peer channels, but PISA shows more classroom noise/digital distraction: **35%** say students do not listen and **41%** are distracted by their own digital devices [5].

Named empirical help-seeking evidence by context is incomplete. For Korea, the online peer-feedback study shows Korean learners participated in peer comments but concentrated on local corrections, consistent with reluctance to challenge global meaning or organization without stronger scaffolding [13]. For Finland, the Wilma studies show help/correction channels are heavily institutionalized through teacher-student-guardian communication: **98.7%** of parents used Wilma, and lower-secondary marking frequency differed significantly by special-support status [14]. For Mexico, the UAZ Google Classroom study documents teacher-centered remote management of learning through **32 teachers**, **6** administrative staff, and **30 hours** of training, but not public help-request rates or private-comment rates [18]. Accordingly, the recommendations below should be read as design rules grounded in culture scores, OECD/PISA climate indicators, platform affordances, and adjacent peer-feedback evidence—not as claims that Classting, Wilma, or Google Classroom independently caused measured engagement differences across countries.

## Country-specific interaction-pattern recommendations

### South Korea: face-saving collaboration with teacher-mediated escalation

For Korean secondary group workspaces, use **named membership but private-first critique**. Classting already uses closed, invitation-based class membership and class-member-only posts [3], which fits a bounded classroom community. Within that boundary, critiques should move through stages: private draft notes, anonymous or semi-anonymous peer review, then teacher-mediated escalation for unresolved disagreements. The rationale is not “collectivism” alone: Korea’s Hofstede profile is **PDI 60, IDV 58** [1], PISA shows high belonging (**79%**) and low disorder (**7%** do not listen; **9%** cannot work well) [2], and the Korean peer-feedback study shows students can comment online but tend to focus on local revisions [13].

Recommended interaction patterns:
- **Anonymous/semi-anonymous first review:** reviewers visible to the teacher but not initially to peers; for secondary students, anonymity must be peer-facing only, with teacher/admin traceability, reporting, teacher override, and audit logs so harmful comments can be investigated and removed.
- **Rubric-forced higher-order prompts:** require at least one comment on claim, evidence, design, or teamwork before grammar/style comments, because Korean online peer feedback otherwise clustered around local issues [13].
- **Private mistake display:** show corrections to the author and teacher first; publish only student-approved “what we improved” summaries.
- **Teacher-mediated escalation:** let students flag “needs teacher help” privately rather than forcing a public challenge to a peer’s work.
- **Group feed for praise and milestones:** keep Classting-like class feed posts for progress updates, resources, and celebratory examples, not first exposure of errors.

### Finland: open peer critique with lightweight teacher guardrails

For Finland, default to **open peer help channels** but give teachers tools to manage attention and feedback tone. Finland’s Hofstede profile is low power distance/high individualism (**PDI 33, IDV 75**) [1], so students are more likely to accept horizontal peer critique than in Mexico or Korea. But PISA 2022 shows that Finnish classrooms report more discipline/digital-attention strain than Korea: **28%** cannot work well, **35%** say students do not listen, and **41%** are distracted by their own digital devices [5]. Wilma’s evidence also warns against overly public or guardian-visible negative correction: Wilma is nearly universal in the parent sample (**98.7%** overall; **99.5%** lower secondary), and negative-only markings were reported to decrease motivation [14].

Recommended interaction patterns:
- **Public group questions by default:** allow students to ask project questions in a group channel without teacher pre-approval, with teacher alerts only for unanswered or flagged threads.
- **Named peer critique:** use named comments for routine project feedback, because the cultural and platform context is less dependent on face-saving anonymity; reserve anonymity for sensitive peer assessment.
- **Separate learning feedback from disciplinary records:** do not make every correction guardian-visible; use private teacher-student messages for mistakes and public threads for shared problem-solving.
- **Notification throttling and focus modes:** because PISA reports high device distraction (**41%** own devices), batch non-urgent peer comments and highlight only teacher or deadline alerts [5].
- **Positive-visible, negative-private:** Wilma evidence supports visible positive markings but private handling of negative correction to avoid motivation loss [14].

### Mexico: high teacher authority plus warm group visibility and private correction

For Mexico, the best default is **teacher-endorsed peer collaboration**: make the teacher’s sponsorship visible, assign peer roles explicitly, and route sensitive correction through private comments. Mexico has the highest power distance in this comparison (**PDI 81**) and lower individualism (**IDV 34**) [1]. PISA shows high perceived teacher support (**77%** interest, **79%** extra help) but more loneliness/exclusion than Korea or Finland (**25%** lonely, **26%** outsider/left out) [9]. Google’s Mexico deployments are also explicitly teacher- and system-led: SEP accounts at national scale during COVID, Baja California’s **480 public secondary schools** and **>162,000 students**, and Jalisco’s **>45,000 teacher Chromebooks** and **32,000** student Chromebooks [10] [11].

Recommended interaction patterns:
- **Teacher-endorsed peer roles:** label roles such as “evidence checker,” “designer,” “summarizer,” or “question ambassador” as teacher-assigned responsibilities, not peer challenges.
- **Public warmth, private correction:** use Stream posts for encouragement, deadlines, exemplars, and group milestones; use private comments for corrections, missing work, originality issues, and help requests [15] [16].
- **Group-visible help without embarrassment:** permit “I’m stuck” templates that can be posted by a role-holder or anonymously to the group, with teacher visibility.
- **Rubrics before critique:** Classroom rubrics should define what peers may comment on, reducing the social risk of challenging classmates [17].
- **Teacher escalation thresholds:** notify the teacher when a private help request is unanswered, when assignment work remains unsubmitted near deadline, or when a peer-review thread has no substantive feedback.

## Separating culture from confounders

The recommendations should not be interpreted as proof that national culture caused a platform outcome. The confounders are substantial. The three platforms are not equivalent: Classting is a class-community and assignment space, Wilma is a student-information/home–school communication system, and Google Classroom is a teacher-controlled assignment and Stream/Classwork platform [3] [6] [15]. The measured Finnish Wilma data are parent-reported communication and markings, not peer collaboration; the Mexican UAZ Google Classroom study is qualitative and pandemic-era; the Korean peer-feedback study is small and not Classting-specific [14] [18] [13]. Device/connectivity and training differ sharply: San Luis Potosí background data in the Mexico Classroom practice reported **68.4%** household internet access, **53.9%** computer availability, and **92.8%** cellphone availability, while Baja California and Jalisco deployments supplied licenses and Chromebooks at state scale [20] [11]. Assessment stakes also differ: Classting’s current secondary AI product emphasizes diagnosis, CAT assessments, dashboards, essay/constructed-response grading, and mock exams, which may increase teacher authority and reduce spontaneous peer critique [4].

A robust product rule follows: **when platform-specific measured differences are weak or non-comparable, do not claim that Classting, Wilma, or Google Classroom caused engagement changes. Instead, set culturally safe defaults from Hofstede scores and OECD/PISA climate indicators, then A/B test within each country using the same metrics: public help posts per student-week, private help requests per student-week, peer comments per assignment, proportion of higher-order comments, assignment submission/completion rate, teacher intervention rate, and student-reported safety.**
