**Bottom line.** Classical gift theory is not a simple “nonmarket generosity” model. Malinowski’s 1922 kula, Mauss’s 1925 theory of obligation, and Sahlins’s 1972 reciprocity typology all show exchange as socially embedded, but they differ in scale: Malinowski explains a specific prestige-partnership system, Mauss theorizes a comparative moral-juridical obligation to give/receive/return, and Sahlins maps reciprocity onto social distance. Feminist anthropology revises the canon rather than merely adding missing women: Weiner, Strathern, and Rubin show that gendered production, reproduction, kinship, and personhood alter what counts as value, wealth, obligation, and exchange. Contemporary open-source software, social-media attention, and cryptocurrency governance are therefore best read as **hybrid exchange infrastructures**: they reproduce gift-like reputation, obligation, and status while embedding these relations in licenses, platform advertising markets, tokens, firms, and tradable governance rights.

## 1. The classical model: gift and commodity as contrasting exchange forms

The review’s baseline distinction is the anthropological contrast, formulated clearly by Gregory, between **commodity exchange** and **gift exchange**. Commodity exchange establishes objective relations of equality between **alienable objects** and independent transactors; it is oriented to quantitative equivalence, price, and market-mediated transfer. Gift exchange, by contrast, establishes personal relations of debt, rank, dependence, and obligation between related transactors through objects that may be inalienable or retain identity with persons and groups [24]. In compact terms: commodity exchange tends toward “x = y” equivalence between alienable things; gift exchange creates a directional social relation in which return may be delayed, non-equivalent, ranked, and status-making [24] [25].

That contrast is analytically useful but unstable. The classical authors themselves show that gifts are not “free”: they are binding, political, and often competitive. Feminist anthropology then shows that the gift/commodity opposition can obscure the labor and reproductive relations that make valued exchange possible. Contemporary digital systems intensify the instability: code, attention, and tokens circulate as reputation, obligation, data, property, and governance rights at the same time.

## 2. Malinowski, Mauss, and Sahlins: three explanatory scales

### Malinowski: ethnographic-functional explanation of partnership, valuables, and prestige

In **Bronisław Malinowski’s *Argonauts of the Western Pacific* (1922)**, the central ethnographic case is the Trobriand **kula**: a closed inter-island exchange circuit in the archipelagoes of Melanesian New Guinea in which only two classes of *vaygu’a* valuables circulate in opposite directions. Red-shell **soulava necklaces circulate clockwise**, while white-shell **mwali armbands/armshells circulate counter-clockwise** [1]. The valuables do not settle permanently: a participant receives one class, holds it for a time, and passes it onward to a named partner in exchange for the opposite class; Malinowski’s formula is effectively “once in the Kula, always in the Kula” [1].

The relevant unit for Malinowski is not a price-making market but a **named partner relation**. Kula participation is restricted to recognized partners, entered formally, maintained over life, and embedded in magic, ceremony, hospitality, rank, and fame [1]. The kula is therefore functional in a broad ethnographic sense: it binds inter-island travel, alliance, political rank, ceremonial labor, and prestige into a patterned system. It is not barter for utility; material gain is secondary to renown, partnership, and the capacity to move famous valuables through one’s network [1].

### Mauss: comparative moral-juridical theory of obligation

In **Marcel Mauss’s *Essai sur le don* / *The Gift* (1925)**, gifts are **“total social facts”**—or total social phenomena—in which religious, juridical, moral, political, familial, economic, aesthetic, and morphological institutions are expressed together [2]. Mauss shifts the explanatory scale from Malinowski’s detailed functional ethnography to a comparative theory of obligation: archaic exchange systems are organized by the three obligations **to give, to receive, and to reciprocate** [2].

Mauss’s famous Māori example is **hau**, the spiritual force or spirit of the gift associated with *taonga*. A received thing is not inert; it retains something of the giver, so the *hau* of the thing impels return, often of equivalent or superior value. This is why Mauss can say that to give something is, in a sense, to give something of oneself [2]. The gift is therefore neither altruism nor contract in the narrow modern sense: it is a moral-juridical mechanism through which persons, groups, gods, law, honor, and wealth are bound together [2].

### Sahlins: structural typology of reciprocity and social distance

In **Marshall Sahlins’s *Stone Age Economics* (1972)**, especially “On the Sociology of Primitive Exchange,” the explanatory scale changes again. Sahlins systematizes exchange as a continuum of **generalized reciprocity, balanced reciprocity, and negative reciprocity** [3].

| Sahlins category | Return expected? | Typical social distance | Classical examples/logic |
|---|---|---|---|
| Generalized reciprocity | Indefinite, unstipulated, not immediately balanced | Close kin and intimate social relations | Sharing, help, “pure gift” pole [3] |
| Balanced reciprocity | Direct, commensurate return within a finite period | Intermediate social relations | Gift-countergift, equivalence without impersonal market pricing [3] |
| Negative reciprocity | Actor tries to get more than they give, or something for nothing | Strangers, outsiders, enemies | Haggling, guile, chicanery, seizure [3] |

Sahlins’s key move is to arrange reciprocity by **social distance**. The closer the relation—especially close kin—the more generalized the reciprocity; the more distant or hostile the relation, the more exchange tends toward equivalence, guile, or seizure [3]. Compared with Malinowski’s kula and Mauss’s total social fact, Sahlins offers the most portable typology, but also the most abstract one.

## 3. Feminist anthropology as revision, not supplementation

Feminist anthropology does not simply add women to an otherwise intact model. It changes the analytical object: if gendered labor and reproduction are constitutive of exchange, then classical theories underestimated what counts as wealth, value, obligation, and personhood.

### Weiner: women’s wealth and keeping-while-giving

**Annette B. Weiner’s *Women of Value, Men of Renown* (1976)** and later ** *Inalienable Possessions* (1992)** revise Malinowski’s Trobriand account by showing that women’s production and exchange of **banana-leaf bundles and skirts** were central forms of wealth, especially in mortuary distributions and matrilineal reproduction [4]. These objects were not peripheral “domestic” goods; they carried reproductive, genealogical, and cosmological value that male-centered kula accounts obscured [4].

Weiner’s broader concept of **“keeping-while-giving”** is a direct challenge to reciprocity theories that focus only on circulation. Some possessions matter politically because they are never fully alienated; their circulation preserves rank, ancestry, and collective identity even while something is given [4]. In this light, the kula’s visible male prestige system rests beside—and partly on—women’s labor, cloth wealth, matrilineal continuity, and mortuary obligation.

### Strathern: dividual personhood and the critique of imported oppositions

**Marilyn Strathern’s *The Gender of the Gift* (1988)** further destabilizes the classical frame. In her account of Melanesian exchange, persons are **“as dividually as they are individually conceived”**: a person is a plural, composite site of the relations that produced them, not a Western self-contained individual standing over against society [5]. Gender is not simply a property of male or female individuals; it is relationally produced in exchange, domesticity, reproduction, and ceremonial presentation [5].

Strathern also criticizes the importation of Western oppositions such as **gift/commodity** and **individual/society** into Melanesian analysis. The opposition may be useful as an analytic fiction, but it is rooted in Western metaphysics; in Hagen, what analysts might call gifts are not opposed to commodities in that way but are apprehended as “things” that materialize relations [5]. Her intervention therefore does not merely say that women were omitted. It questions the conceptual architecture that allowed analysts to imagine exchange as relations among bounded individuals and alienable objects in the first place [5].

### Rubin: marriage exchange, kinship, domestic labor, and reproduction

**Gayle Rubin’s “The Traffic in Women” (1975)** provides an additional feminist intervention by recasting kinship and marriage exchange as a **sex/gender system**: social arrangements that transform biological sexuality into products of human activity [6]. Reading Lévi-Strauss critically, Rubin argues that marriages are treated as a basic form of gift exchange in which women become the “most precious gifts”; the exchange of women creates not only reciprocity but affinal kinship and descent relations [6].

Rubin’s point is not that “exchange of women” exhausts culture, but that it is a condensed way to see how social relations of sex and gender are organized. She also argues that economic reproduction alone does not explain why domestic work is usually assigned to women [6]. This complicates Maussian and Sahlinsian models because the exchange relation is not just between groups through objects; it may be built through gendered control over marriage, sexuality, domestic labor, and reproduction.

## 4. Open-source software: gift, commodity, or hybrid commons?

Open-source and free software are a strong contemporary comparison because their institutions are explicit. The **GNU Project** was organized around free software; the **Free Software Foundation (FSF)** was created in **1985** to support free software development [26]. The **GNU General Public License version 1** was released as **Version 1, February 1989**, by the Free Software Foundation [7]. Its copyleft logic requires that recipients receive the same freedoms to copy, redistribute, and modify, and that distributed object code be accompanied by corresponding machine-readable source code or a valid written offer for that source [7]. GNU’s copyleft explanation states the principle directly: anyone who redistributes the software, with or without changes, must pass along the freedom to copy and change it [8]. The **Linux kernel** was developed by **Linus Torvalds in 1991** as a Unix-compatible kernel called Linux and was made free software in **1992**, after which it could be combined with the GNU system [26].

Anthropologically, open-source contribution looks gift-like because patches, bug reports, documentation, review, and maintainership generate reputation, trust, and standing rather than immediate price-equivalent payment. It also resembles Sahlins’s balanced reciprocity in many project communities: contributors give now, receive review, status, help, or future contributions later. It resembles Mauss even more when contributions bind persons into durable obligations—maintainers are expected to review, users to report bugs responsibly, firms to comply with license obligations, and communities to preserve shared infrastructure.

But open source is not a pure gift economy. The GPL is a legal technology, not merely a moral norm; it makes sharing obligations enforceable through copyright licensing [7] [8]. Firms also sponsor and commodify the commons. Red Hat presents open source as a development model and business foundation [9]; Google, Microsoft, and Amazon Web Services all maintain official open-source programs or portals [10] [11] [12]. This does not negate the gift-like quality of contribution; it makes open source a **hybrid infrastructure** in which unpaid and paid labor, reputation, corporate strategy, licensing, cloud services, and community governance coexist.

**Yochai Benkler’s “commons-based peer production”** names open-source-style production as a nonmarket, decentralized mode of production: contributors work from a shared commons rather than a proprietary input pool; tasks can be modular and self-selected; motivation includes reputation and social-psychological rewards as well as payment; and coordination occurs outside the normal firm hierarchy or price system. The report’s stored Benkler fetches did not preserve a usable scholarly text—the available Benkler entries resolve to a host error rather than the article—so this definition is retained only as a standard literature-review frame, while the institutional grounding remains the GPL, GNU, Linux, and corporate open-source sources above [7] [8] [26] [9] [10] [11] [12]. **Christopher Kelty’s “recursive publics”** still requires separate grounding below.

## 5. Social-media attention economies: recognition gifts inside advertising markets

Social media makes attention quantifiable. The required Facebook item—**the Facebook Like button introduced in 2009**—was searched, but the fetched Meta Newsroom pages in the corpus were page-not-found results rather than usable authority; the report therefore does not treat that date as independently grounded from the stored sources [13] [14]. The broader platform mechanisms are grounded: Instagram’s creator materials organize monetization around creator tools and earning from audience activity [15]; TikTok explains the **For You** feed as a recommendation system that ranks and recommends videos for users [16]; TikTok for Business presents the platform as an advertising and marketing venue [17].

Analytically, likes, follows, shares, comments, reposts, and views are gift-like because they are small acts of recognition. They create obligations to acknowledge, reciprocate, follow back, comment, maintain presence, or perform gratitude. In Sahlins’s terms, they can range from generalized reciprocity among close friends to balanced reciprocity among peers and creators to negative reciprocity in spam, engagement bait, or clout extraction.

At the same time, attention is commodity-like because platforms convert recognition into advertising inventory, behavioral data, ranking signals, algorithmic visibility, and influencer income. TikTok’s For You system turns viewing and interaction into recommendation inputs [16], while TikTok for Business explicitly sells marketing access to audiences [17]. Instagram creator monetization similarly turns followers and engagement into monetizable creator-audience relations [15]. Social media therefore reinforces Mauss’s insight that gifts are status-making and obligation-producing, but challenges classical models because the platform—not just the giver and receiver—captures and prices the relational trace.

## 6. Cryptocurrency governance: gift, commodity, and sovereignty in one system

Cryptocurrency governance most directly challenges a binary between gift and commodity because protocol labor, public standards, tradable assets, and political authority are fused.

**Bitcoin** governance is organized around open-source coordination rather than a single sovereign legislature. The **Bitcoin Improvement Proposal (BIP)** repository defines BIPs as improvement proposals for Bitcoin; BIPs propose protocol features, coordinate client standards, and document design decisions, and may be submitted by anyone if the content is high quality [27]. The BIP repository also shows BIPs classified across consensus, peer services, API/RPC, applications, informational, and process categories [18]. Bitcoin’s proof-of-work system coordinates miners and nodes through block construction and propagation: mining documentation describes miners receiving block templates, constructing block headers, and responding when a peer-to-peer network publishes a new block [19].

**Ethereum** uses a parallel but distinct formal process. **EIP-1** defines Ethereum Improvement Proposals as the primary mechanism for proposing new features, collecting community input, and documenting design decisions [20]. The **DAO hard fork in 2016** is the key sovereignty case: Ethereum’s EIP-779 is titled **“Hardfork Meta: DAO Fork”** and records the DAO fork as a hard-fork meta proposal [21]; the Ethereum Foundation’s July 2016 “Hard Fork Completed” post confirms the fork’s completion [22]. The fork shows that protocol governance is not merely technical: it can adjudicate contested histories, property claims, and community legitimacy.

**Token-voting DAO governance** makes the hybrid even clearer. Uniswap governance documentation describes governance around the UNI token and proposal/voting processes [23] [28]. Maker governance is organized through an official governance portal showing polls and executive governance activity [29]. In these systems, governance rights are often linked to tradable tokens, so political voice can be bought, delegated, accumulated, or financially rewarded.

Anthropologically, crypto governance has gift-like features: open-source protocol labor, standards writing, unpaid review, public-goods funding, airdrops, and reputational authority all resemble contributions to a shared commons. It also has commodity features: tokens, staking rewards, mining rewards, governance tokens, and exchange-listed assets are alienable and price-bearing. Forks, airdrops, and staking rewards create obligations and status hierarchies, but they also allocate tradable value. The result is neither Malinowskian kula nor impersonal commodity market; it is a hybrid political economy where sovereignty, code, reputation, and liquid assets are co-produced.

## 7. Comparative synthesis

The classical canon remains indispensable because it shows that exchange is never merely material transfer. Malinowski grounds this in named kula partnerships and prestige; Mauss generalizes it into obligations that bind moral, legal, religious, political, and economic life; Sahlins formalizes it as a continuum from generalized sharing to negative extraction as social distance increases [1] [2] [3].

Feminist anthropology changes the stakes. Weiner shows that the value of Trobriand exchange cannot be understood if women’s banana-leaf bundles, skirts, mortuary distributions, and matrilineal reproductive value are treated as secondary to male kula valuables [4]. Strathern shows that Melanesian exchange cannot be adequately analyzed through Western individual/society or gift/commodity oppositions because persons and things materialize relations [5]. Rubin shows that kinship, marriage exchange, domestic labor, and reproduction make gender subordination part of exchange structures themselves [6]. Together, these interventions revise the canon’s theory of value: gendered labor is not a missing detail but a challenge to the categories through which exchange was made visible.

The contemporary cases then extend rather than simply overturn classical theory. Open source reinforces Mauss and Malinowski because reputation, obligation, and named relations matter, but it challenges them because licenses and firms turn contribution into infrastructure and business strategy [7] [8] [9] [10] [11] [12]. Social media reinforces gift theory because attention creates recognition and obligation, but it challenges it because platforms price and algorithmically govern the traces of recognition [15] [16] [17]. Cryptocurrency governance reinforces open-source gift labor and public-good contribution, but challenges the gift/commodity binary because tradable tokens can be governance rights, incentives, status markers, and speculative commodities at once [18] [19] [20] [21] [22] [23].

The most defensible conclusion is therefore not that digital economies are “new gift economies,” but that they expose what feminist and Melanesianist anthropology had already shown: gift and commodity are not natural opposites. They are analytical poles within mixed regimes of value, labor, obligation, rank, personhood, and power.
