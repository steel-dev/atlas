**Bottom line:** under the stated 2024, no-state-tax assumptions, the U.S. remains materially higher take-home for this taxpayer. Scenario A nets about **$162,762 in the U.S.** versus **$124,787 after German tax/social insurance**; Scenario B nets about **$173,468 in the U.S.** versus **$135,736 in Germany**. The main driver is not the capital-gains layer but German wage taxation plus capped employee social-insurance/health contributions; Germany’s capital-income tax rate assumed here, **25% plus 5.5% solidarity surcharge on that tax = 26.375%**, is higher than the U.S. 15% long-term capital-gains rate plus NIIT in the stated income band, but the gap is already large on salary alone.

## 5) Recommendation

**If the choice is purely after-tax annual cash take-home under these assumptions, remain under the U.S. no-state-tax scenario rather than relocating into full German tax residency/employment payroll in 2024.** The estimated annual cash advantage is **about $38,000** in both base cases: **$37,977** for the $220,000 salary-only case and **$37,731** for the $200,000 salary + $30,000 gains case.

That recommendation is conditional on four high-impact assumptions: **(1)** the U.S. state-tax rate is truly 0%; **(2)** U.S. employee health premiums are 0 or low enough not to close a roughly $38,000 gap; **(3)** the German role is a standard German payroll role with statutory social insurance and statutory health coverage; and **(4)** the $30,000 gains are long-term U.S. capital gains and ordinary taxable German capital income. The most important data-quality flag is the unspecified U.S. health-insurance premium: a U.S. post-tax premium of roughly **$38,000/year** would be needed to erase the modeled base-case U.S. advantage, while a normal employer-plan employee premium materially below that would not change the conclusion.
