**Bottom line:** Finch’s June 30, 2022 cash figure was **$104.7 million ($104.673 million on the balance sheet), not $98.8 million**. If “into the first quarter of 2024” is treated as funding through March 31, 2024, the June 30, 2022-to-March 31, 2024 runway is **21 months**, implying **$5.0 million/month** on the confirmed cash balance; the originally proposed **$98.8 million ÷ 21 months = $4.7 million/month** is arithmetically correct but uses the wrong cash balance. The strategic conclusion is still the same: the runway depended on a steep reduction from H1 2022’s actual operating cash use of **$6.91 million/month**, and Takeda’s review of TAK-524 removed the most important near-term non-dilutive upside from Finch’s planning, increasing financing urgency while weakening its bargaining position with financiers, licensees, and IP counterparties. [1]

## 1. Cash runway and implied monthly burn

Finch’s Q2 2022 primary filing reported **cash and cash equivalents of $104.673 million as of June 30, 2022**, compared with **$133.481 million as of Dec. 31, 2021**; the rounded management figure was **$104.7 million**. That corrects the approximately **$98.8 million** figure in the question. [1]

Management’s runway statement was that Finch believed its **existing cash and cash equivalents of $104.7 million as of June 30, 2022**, **together with anticipated income under an executed sublease for a portion of one office and lab facility**, would fund operations **“into the first quarter of 2024.”** The same disclosure warned that the estimate rested on assumptions, including operational spend, timely clinical-trial execution, and IP-litigation expenses. [1]

If that runway is interpreted as extending from **June 30, 2022 through March 31, 2024**, the interval is **21 months**. The implied average burn is therefore:

| Calculation basis | Formula | Implied monthly burn |
|---|---:|---:|
| Confirmed cash balance | $104.673M ÷ 21 months | **$4.98M/month** |
| Prompt’s unconfirmed cash figure | $98.8M ÷ 21 months | **$4.70M/month** |

The corrected value matters. Using the confirmed balance raises the implied burn by about **$0.28 million/month** versus the prompt’s calculation, but the bigger analytical point is that even **$4.98 million/month** was materially below Finch’s reported H1 2022 operating cash use of **$41.485 million**, or **$6.91 million/month**, and below H1 2022 net loss of **$47.267 million**, or **$7.88 million/month**. [1]

## 2. Why the top-down runway differed from H1 2022 cash use

Finch’s runway math was not simply H1 2022 extrapolated forward. It assumed the business would absorb the effect of the Takeda transition, sublease income, expense reductions, and program prioritization.

Because total revenue equaled collaboration revenue in the relevant Q2 and H1 2022/2021 periods, Finch had no offsetting product revenue or diversified revenue stream when Takeda-related collaboration/reimbursement revenue declined. [1]

The composition of the expense change explains the gap between historical cash use and the forward runway assumption. In R&D, Q2 2022 platform costs increased by **$2.0 million**, including **$1.3 million** of manufacturing-related expense and **$0.6 million** of personnel expense, while H1 2022 platform costs increased by **$6.7 million**, including about **$3.2 million** each of personnel and manufacturing-related costs, as Finch built internal manufacturing and platform capabilities. Those increases reflected Finch building internal manufacturing and platform capabilities following the OpenBiome transition, so a fixed-cost platform/manufacturing build-out remained in the cost base even as IBD spending fell—**$1.7 million** lower in Q2 and **$4.0 million** lower in H1—because primary TAK-524 responsibility moved to Takeda, plus savings from the suspended HBV program. That cost mix helps explain why H1 2022 operating cash use stayed above the roughly **$5.0 million/month** implied runway average despite lower IBD/Takeda spending. [1]

In G&A, Q2 2022 expense rose **$2.3 million**, driven by **$1.4 million** more professional fees, **$0.4 million** more facilities and supplies, **$0.2 million** more personnel expense, and **$0.2 million** more other expense; the professional-fee increase included **$2.1 million** more legal expense, partly offset by **$0.4 million** lower consulting expense. H1 2022 G&A rose **$7.1 million**, driven by **$2.9 million** more professional fees, including **$4.4 million** more legal expense partly offset elsewhere; **$2.2 million** more other expense, including **$1.1 million** business insurance and **$0.6 million** state excise taxes; **$1.5 million** more personnel expense, including **$1.3 million** more stock-based compensation; and **$0.5 million** more facilities expense. [1]

The April 2022 restructuring was therefore necessary but not, by itself, sufficient to make the H1 2022 run-rate match the Q1 2024 runway. Finch’s board approved expense-reduction measures on **April 14, 2022**, and Finch announced them on **April 19, 2022**: **37 full-time employees**, or about **20%** of the company, were cut to focus financial resources on recurrent CDI and ASD programs, along with Takeda-partnered IBD, after pausing chronic hepatitis B and amid the CP101 clinical hold and related CDI/ASD delays. Finch estimated about **$1.1 million** of one-time severance, healthcare coverage, outplacement, and related costs, mostly in Q2 2022, with substantial completion by the end of Q2; the April release did not quantify expected cost savings, saying runway-extension guidance would come with Q1 results. [3] [4]

## 3. Takeda collaboration: asset control, economics, and the review risk

At June 30, 2022, the Takeda collaboration was strategically central but no longer under Finch’s operational control. Takeda held a **worldwide, exclusive, sublicensable license** under certain Finch patents, patent applications, and know-how for **TAK-524**, a microbiome therapeutic candidate designed/optimized for **ulcerative colitis** within inflammatory bowel disease. The collaboration also included **FIN-525**, a program to develop a live biotherapeutic product for **Crohn’s disease**. Under the Aug. 2021 amendment, Finch and Takeda transitioned **primary development and manufacturing responsibility** for TAK-524 from Finch to Takeda, and Takeda assumed **sole regulatory responsibility** for TAK-524. [1]

The economics were large relative to Finch’s cash runway, but contingent. Finch had received a **$10.0 million upfront payment** and **$4.0 million** of TAK-524 milestone payments. The 10-Q described **$180.0 million** of maximum available milestone consideration under the agreement, of which **$4.0 million** had been earned and received; MD&A described up to **$176.0 million** of additional TAK-524 therapeutic milestones, up to **$177.7 million** of FIN-525 milestones, up to **$10.0 million** for the first diagnostic product for each of TAK-524 and FIN-525, and **mid-to-high single-digit royalties** on annual net sales of licensed products. [1]

Finch’s H1 2022 revenue concentration heightened the risk: total H1 2022 collaboration revenue was only **$0.715 million**, and Note 9 rounded Takeda revenue to **$0.7 million** while OpenBiome LMIC revenue was **$0**, meaning essentially all H1 2022 revenue came from Takeda-related collaboration/reimbursement revenue rather than diversified product sales or multiple partners. [1]

During the Q2 2022 transition period, Takeda had not yet terminated the collaboration, but it had informed Finch that, as TAK-524 neared its next development decision point, Takeda was reviewing the **plans, timeline, and budget** for TAK-524 as part of its portfolio review process. Finch responded by removing the associated near-term milestones from its cash runway analysis until Takeda completed the review. Finch also warned that if assumptions proved wrong, or if Takeda deprioritized the collaboration, Finch could exhaust capital sooner than expected. [1]

That risk crystallized soon after. On **Aug. 25, 2022**, Finch announced that Takeda, after a pipeline review, had decided to terminate the IBD collaboration effective **Nov. 17, 2022**. Finch would regain worldwide rights to develop and commercialize **FIN-524**, previously known as **TAK-524**, **FIN-525**, and other microbiome product candidates for IBD, and would receive a **royalty-free license** to all data and intellectual property generated during the collaboration. Finch also said it had received more than **$44 million** from Takeda during the collaboration: **$10 million** upfront, **$4 million** in milestones, and more than **$30 million** in R&D expense reimbursements. [2]

## 4. Does declining or uncertain Takeda revenue shorten the practical runway?

The formal Q2 2022 runway already excluded near-term TAK-524 milestones, so the mere absence of those milestones should not mechanically shorten the stated Q1 2024 runway. But the practical runway was still weaker than the headline date for three reasons.

First, the revenue base had already collapsed. Collaboration revenue fell from **$6.383 million in H1 2021** to **$0.715 million in H1 2022**, and then, after the transition continued, Q3 2022 collaboration revenue was only **$0.138 million** versus **$11.343 million** in Q3 2021; nine-month 2022 collaboration revenue was **$0.853 million** versus **$17.726 million** in nine-month 2021. [1] [5]

Second, the forward runway depended on expense reductions and sublease cash inflows occurring as planned, not on a stable recurring revenue line. Management’s Q2 2022 statement expressly combined cash with anticipated sublease income, and the 10-Q tied the runway to assumptions about operating spend, clinical trial timing, and IP-litigation costs. [1]

Third, the Takeda decision removed strategic optionality even where it did not remove cash already counted in the runway. The review made potential future milestones, reimbursements, and third-party validation uncertain before the runway endpoint. After termination, Finch regained rights and data/IP, but it also lost a global pharmaceutical partner that had previously provided more than **$44 million** in upfront, milestones, and reimbursements. [2]

The post-review Q3 2022 data show how management tried to offset this pressure. As of **Sept. 30, 2022**, Finch had **$85.3 million** in cash and equivalents and said cash plus executed sublease inflows would fund operations **into Q2 2024**; however, that updated runway came after an additional workforce reduction and portfolio review, not because collaboration revenue recovered. [5] [6]

## 5. Financing urgency and negotiating-position tension

Takeda’s TAK-524 review increased Finch’s financing urgency because the timing mismatch was severe: the runway extended only into Q1 2024, while Takeda’s review concerned near-term development decisions and milestones that could have been among Finch’s few non-dilutive sources of cash. Finch explicitly removed those near-term milestones from its runway analysis, which was prudent, but it also meant the remaining plan depended more heavily on conserving cash, raising capital, sublease income, and internal prioritization. [1]

That created a negotiating-position tension:

1. **Cash constraints weakened Finch’s leverage.** A company with an implied forward burn near **$5.0 million/month**, H1 2022 operating cash use of **$6.91 million/month**, no product revenue, and a Q1 2024 runway would have less ability to wait for optimal financing, licensing, or IP-monetization terms. Finch itself warned that failure to raise capital when needed or on acceptable terms could force it to delay, reduce, or eliminate R&D programs. [1]

2. **Takeda uncertainty weakened asset valuation.** Before Aug. 25, 2022, a third-party counterparty could see TAK-524 as subject to Takeda’s portfolio review rather than as a clean Finch-controlled asset; after the termination notice, Finch had full rights but the market also knew Takeda had declined to continue the program after its review. That likely reduced the value of the same assets in negotiations, even though Finch gained royalty-free access to collaboration-generated data and IP. [2]

3. **Expense reductions preserved cash but reduced flexibility.** The April 2022 **20%** workforce reduction focused Finch on CDI, ASD, and Takeda-partnered IBD; after Takeda discontinued the IBD collaboration, Finch announced on **Sept. 1, 2022** an additional **37%** workforce reduction as part of a strategic review, framed the action as occurring amid unfavorable capital-market conditions, and suspended efforts to initiate the Phase 1 trial of **FIN-211** in ASD while exploring use of third-party data. Those market conditions increased the urgency of conserving cash and finding financing or partners, while also weakening Finch’s bargaining power because counterparties knew external capital was harder to secure. These steps lengthened runway but narrowed operating capacity and made execution more dependent on partners and external data. [3] [6]

## 6. IP monetization strategy and the risk timeline

Finch’s later strategic pivot confirms the direction implied by the 2022 constraints. After discontinuing the PRISM4 Phase 3 trial of **CP101** in recurrent CDI in January 2023, Finch described itself as a microbiome technology company seeking to realize value from its IP estate by **licensing its technology to collaboration partners**, **enforcing patent rights against infringing parties through IP litigation**, and, in selected cases, generating additional data through **academic collaborations**. [7]

The named asset base included **CP101**, an investigational orally administered microbiome candidate with Phase 2 recurrent CDI data, and preclinical assets targeting **ulcerative colitis, Crohn’s disease, and autism spectrum disorder**. Finch’s January 2023 release described a robust IP estate with more than **70 issued U.S. and foreign patents**; its 2023 Form 10-K later described more than **113 issued U.S. and foreign patents** relevant to both donor-derived and donor-independent microbiome therapeutics across potential indications. [8] [7]

Finch also took concrete steps consistent with monetization through partnerships. On **April 18, 2023**, it announced a clinical collaboration with **Brigham and Women’s Hospital** to evaluate CP101 in ulcerative colitis and updated its University of Minnesota license so Finch could satisfy certain performance milestones through sublicensing agreements, aligning the license with its collaboration-and-partnership strategy. The UMN license covered **13 issued patents** and **7 patent applications** on formulations comprising human fecal microbes, methods of increasing microbiota diversity, and methods of decreasing the relative abundance of certain bacteria. [9]

The strategic risk timeline is therefore clear:

| Period | What Finch needed to accomplish | Risk created by cash/Takeda intersection |
|---|---|---|
| **June 30-Aug. 25, 2022** | Preserve cash while waiting for Takeda’s TAK-524 review and continuing prioritized programs | Near-term TAK-524 milestones were excluded from runway, leaving no reliable Takeda upside in the Q1 2024 plan. [1] |
| **Aug.-Nov. 2022** | Absorb Takeda’s termination, regain rights/data/IP, and reset the portfolio | Rights returned, but Takeda’s exit reduced validation and eliminated the collaboration as a funding source. [2] |
| **Sept. 2022 onward** | Cut burn fast enough to preserve runway and bargaining time | Additional **37%** workforce reduction and FIN-211 suspension conserved cash but reduced internal development flexibility. [6] |
| **2023-before Q1 2024 pressure point** | Convert IP and assets into licensing, enforcement, collaborations, or financing | Monetization depended on counterparties valuing assets after Takeda’s exit and on Finch retaining enough cash and staff to negotiate without distress. [7] |

**Assessment:** Finch could preserve operational flexibility only by aggressively reducing spend and shifting from internally funded clinical development toward partnering and IP monetization. The June 30, 2022 runway was not immediately invalidated by Takeda’s review because Finch had already excluded near-term TAK-524 milestones from the runway analysis, but the review materially increased financing urgency and weakened negotiating leverage. Once Takeda terminated the collaboration, Finch gained clean rights and a royalty-free license to collaboration-generated data/IP, yet lost future Takeda economics and third-party validation. That made the Q1 2024 runway a deadline for strategic execution: Finch needed to complete the Takeda transition, secure new financing or partners, and monetize IP before cash pressure forced further reductions that would impair its ability to negotiate and operate flexibly.
