**Bottom line.** Texas law is the likely governing substantive law because the job performance location and the May 15, 2024 lease are in Austin, Texas; the summary-judgment standard is Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 166a(c) in Texas state court, or Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 56(a) in federal court. On this record, Hayes is unlikely to obtain summary judgment enforcing full-remote work: the May 1, 2024 signed offer letter says only “hybrid schedule per company policy,” omits any remote-work frequency, and contains a bolded “Integration and Merger” clause stating, “This agreement supersedes all prior verbal representations.” The company also is not assured summary judgment: the manager’s June 2024 confirmation email, Hayes’s fully remote work through June without objection, and two comparator engineers’ written remote approvals create triable issues on promise, company knowledge/ratification, and reasonableness. The most plausible fallback, if any promissory-estoppel remedy survives, is reliance damages tied to proven relocation loss—especially the $40,000 Seattle home-sale loss and lease consequences—not prospective specific performance of an ongoing remote-work employment arrangement.

## Governing law and summary-judgment standard

Texas law is the likely substantive law. Texas follows the Restatement (Second) of Conflict of Laws § 188 approach for contracts without an effective choice-of-law clause, asking which state has the most significant relationship to the transaction and parties by considering the place of contracting, negotiation, performance, the location of the subject matter, and the parties’ domiciles/residences/places of business. [1] Here, even though the negotiations included a Seattle-to-Austin relocation, the work location and the May 15, 2024 lease are in Austin, Texas, so Texas has the strongest performance and relocation contacts.

The procedural summary-judgment rule depends on forum. In Texas state court, Texas Rule of Civil Procedure 166a(c) requires the movant to show no genuine issue of material fact and entitlement to judgment as a matter of law on the grounds expressly set out. [2] In federal court, Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 56(a) requires summary judgment if there is no genuine dispute as to any material fact and the movant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. [3]

## Texas promissory-estoppel framework

The controlling Texas promissory-estoppel framework comes from Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 90(1), Wheeler, and Moore Burger. Section 90(1) makes a promise binding if the promisor should reasonably expect it to induce action or forbearance, it does induce that action or forbearance, and injustice can be avoided only by enforcement; the Restatement (Second) adds that the remedy “may be limited as justice requires.” [4] Wheeler v. White, 398 S.W.2d 93, 96–97 (Tex. 1965), adopts § 90 in Texas and emphasizes that the promise must be designedly made to influence the promisee’s conduct, with no-contract promissory-estoppel relief generally limited to foreseeable, definite, substantial reliance damages rather than expected profits. [4] Moore Burger, Inc. v. Phillips Petroleum Co., 492 S.W.2d 934 (Tex. 1972), quotes § 90, treats promissory estoppel as a confession-and-avoidance doctrine that can prevent a promisor from denying enforceability, and requires the nonmovant on summary judgment to raise a fact issue on promissory estoppel when another defense has been established. [7]

Applied here, Hayes must prove: (1) a clear promise of full remote work; (2) foreseeable and reasonable reliance; (3) substantial detrimental reliance; and (4) that enforcement, or a limited remedy, is required to avoid injustice. [4] [7]

## The record facts that matter

The signed writing is unfavorable to Hayes. The May 1, 2024 offer letter stated “hybrid schedule per company policy,” did not define remote-work frequency, and contained a bolded “Integration and Merger” clause saying, “This agreement supersedes all prior verbal representations.” The alleged extra-contractual promise is that, during April 2024 negotiations, the hiring manager verbally promised Jordan Hayes full remote work to induce relocation from Seattle to Austin. The only post-offer documentation is the manager’s June 2024 email confirming that promise; the manager retired in June 2024.

The authority record is also mixed. HR records say the hiring manager lacked authority to modify standard offer terms or grant written remote-work exceptions, while two other software engineers hired at the same time received actual written remote approvals. Company policy changed on July 1, 2024 to require three office days per week; Hayes had worked fully remote through June without objection and refused on July 8, 2024 to comply with the office requirement, invoking detrimental reliance. Hayes’s reliance expenditures occurred after the May 1 integrated offer letter: Hayes sold the Seattle home at a $40,000 loss and signed a Texas lease on May 15, 2024.

## Clear-promise factor

The clear-promise analysis should separate four events.

1. **April oral promise.** A verbal promise of “full remote work” is, in isolation, more definite than vague assurances about flexibility: it identifies the work arrangement—fully remote—as the promised term. Because it was allegedly made to induce a Seattle-to-Austin relocation, it also fits Wheeler’s requirement that the promise be designedly made to influence conduct. [4]

2. **May 1 written offer.** The signed May 1 offer does not promise full remote work. It says “hybrid schedule per company policy” and does not define remote frequency. That phrase points to policy-based office attendance rather than an individualized full-remote right. Under Texas contract principles reflected in the merger-clause cases, a written integrated agreement can make prior oral statements unenforceable when they cover the same subject and are superseded by the writing. Italian Cowboy explains that a merger clause has the ordinary function of invalidating or superseding prior agreements covering the same subject, even though it is not necessarily an anti-reliance clause. [8]

3. **Bold supersession clause.** The clause here is stronger than a bare statement that the writing is integrated because it expressly says, “This agreement supersedes all prior verbal representations.” Texas law distinguishes general merger clauses from clear anti-reliance provisions. Schlumberger holds that a clear and specific disclaimer of reliance can negate reliance as a matter of law in an appropriate negotiated context, while Italian Cowboy holds that a standard merger clause, even one stating that no outside representations were made, does not automatically disclaim reliance. [9] [8] Barrow-Shaver adds that reliance on oral statements is unjustifiable as a matter of law when they directly contradict an unambiguous written provision, especially where red flags exist. [10] Hayes’s clause does not use the classic “I am not relying” formulation, but the explicit supersession of “all prior verbal representations” is a significant red flag for enforcing any April oral promise.

4. **June email confirmation.** The June email is the only post-offer documentation of the alleged full-remote promise and is stronger evidence than an undocumented oral statement. But its legal force depends on authority. Under Gaines v. Kelly, actual authority depends on the principal’s communication to the agent; apparent authority depends on the principal’s conduct or communication to the third party, not merely the agent’s statements. [11] Because HR records say the manager lacked authority to modify standard terms or grant written remote exceptions, the June email alone likely cannot modify the offer as a matter of actual authority. For apparent authority, Hayes needs company conduct—such as allowing the manager to communicate employment terms, permitting fully remote work through June, or issuing comparable written approvals to other engineers—not just the manager’s own email. [11]

**Clear-promise conclusion:** Hayes has enough evidence to argue a definite full-remote promise existed, but not enough to win summary judgment that it survived the May 1 integrated offer and bound the company. The company has strong written-contract and authority arguments, but the June confirmation email, June remote performance without objection, and comparator approvals may prevent company summary judgment on whether a company-recognized exception existed or whether Hayes reasonably perceived one.

## Reasonable reliance: pre-offer vs. post-offer

Reliance before May 1 would have been stronger because the April promise allegedly induced the move before Hayes signed any contradictory or superseding writing. But the key reliance acts in the record occurred after May 1: Hayes sold the Seattle home at a $40,000 loss and signed the Texas lease on May 15, 2024. Those dates matter because Hayes had already signed an offer letter that said only “hybrid schedule per company policy” and expressly superseded all prior verbal representations.

Under Italian Cowboy, a general merger clause does not automatically bar reliance because it is not necessarily a clear anti-reliance disclaimer. [8] Under Schlumberger, a clear disclaimer can negate reliance in proper circumstances. [9] Under Barrow-Shaver, reliance is unjustifiable when the oral statement directly contradicts an unambiguous written provision and red flags exist. [10] This case sits between those rules: “hybrid schedule per company policy” does not define a specific three-day office requirement as of May 1, but it contradicts a personal guarantee of full remote work by making schedule dependent on company policy, and the bold supersession clause is a red flag that prior verbal representations no longer control.

**Reasonable-reliance conclusion:** Selling a Seattle home and signing a May 15 Texas lease after the integrated May 1 offer is not reasonable as a matter of law for Hayes on summary judgment; at minimum, reasonableness is disputed. Conversely, the company may not obtain summary judgment if a factfinder credits the June email and the company’s June acquiescence as post-offer conduct reviving or confirming an exception.

## Substantial detriment and causation

The detriment is substantial in amount and timing: after the May 1 offer, Hayes sold a Seattle home at a $40,000 loss and signed a Texas lease on May 15, 2024. Texas promissory estoppel requires detrimental reliance that is foreseeable, reasonable, and substantial, not merely a disappointed preference. [4] [7]

The harder question is causation. The $40,000 loss and lease were plainly caused by relocation from Seattle to Austin, but the issue is whether they were caused by reliance on **full remote work specifically**. If the job itself was Austin-based, the company can argue Hayes would have moved or leased in Texas to accept the job regardless of remote frequency. Hayes’s response is that the manager used the full-remote promise to convince Hayes to relocate and that, without that promise, Hayes would not have sold the Seattle home or taken the Texas lease on those terms. That is a fact issue unless the written May 1 terms make the reliance legally unreasonable.

**Detriment conclusion:** The $40,000 home-sale loss and May 15 lease are enough to satisfy substantial detriment if a factfinder finds they were induced by full-remote work. But causation cannot be resolved for Hayes on summary judgment because those losses may reflect acceptance of an Austin job and relocation generally rather than reliance on a full-remote term.

## Express-contract bar and at-will unilateral change

Texas law generally does not allow promissory estoppel to override a valid contract covering the same disputed subject. The Texas promissory-estoppel authorities summarized in Practical Law state that the promise must not be covered by a valid contract; a valid and enforceable contract covering the dispute generally precludes promissory-estoppel recovery, though exceptions exist when the contract does not govern, is invalid/unenforceable, or where the promisor promised to sign an existing written agreement in the statute-of-frauds context. [5] Moore Burger is consistent with that limited exception: it allowed a promissory-estoppel theory to avoid a statute-of-frauds defense where the relevant promise was to make or sign the necessary memorandum. [7]

Here, the written offer covers the schedule subject by saying “hybrid schedule per company policy.” That creates a substantial contract-bar problem for using promissory estoppel to impose a conflicting individualized full-remote term. Hayes’s best answer is that the writing is incomplete because it does not define remote frequency, and that the June email and June performance show a separate remote-work exception rather than an attempt to vary the May 1 writing. The company’s best answer is that full remote and hybrid-policy scheduling are the same subject matter, so promissory estoppel cannot supplement the signed offer.

Texas at-will doctrine reinforces the company’s position on the July 1 change. Montgomery County Hospital District v. Brown holds that Texas employment is at will absent a specific agreement to the contrary and that oral assurances do not modify at-will status absent a definite stated intention. [12] Hathaway v. General Mills holds that in at-will employment either party may impose modifications as a condition of continued employment; an employee with notice must accept the change or quit, and continuing work with knowledge accepts the change as a matter of law, although notice must be unequivocal. [13] If Hayes had no enforceable contrary full-remote agreement, the July 1, 2024 policy requiring three office days weekly was a prospective employment-term change, and Hayes’s July 8 refusal did not preserve the old term.

## Remedy

Even if Hayes proves promissory estoppel, the remedy is likely limited. Restatement (Second) § 90(1) permits a court to limit the remedy as justice requires. [4] Wheeler states that in no-contract promissory-estoppel cases recovery generally should be limited to reliance damages measured by the detriment sustained, not expected profits. [4] The Texas promissory-estoppel damages sources likewise describe the measure as reliance damages restoring the plaintiff to the former position, not expectation damages or lost profits. [5]

Specific performance of a full-remote employment arrangement is especially unlikely. T.E. Moor & Co. v. Hardcastle states the Texas rule that equity ordinarily will not enforce purely personal-service contracts and that mandatory injunctions specifically enforcing personal services are disfavored because of enforcement difficulty and the undesirability of compelling a continuing personal association after disputes arise. [6] A full-remote order would require ongoing supervision of an employment relationship and company scheduling policy, which fits the category courts hesitate to specifically enforce.

**Remedy conclusion:** If Hayes loses on enforcement of a permanent remote-work term but proves reasonable reliance, the proper fallback is reliance damages limited to proven relocation-related loss caused by the remote-work promise—potentially the $40,000 home-sale loss and lease-related losses—rather than an injunction requiring indefinite full remote work.

## Summary-judgment bottom line

**Hayes’s motion should be denied.** Hayes cannot establish full-remote enforcement as a matter of law because the May 1 offer letter says “hybrid schedule per company policy,” does not define remote frequency, and expressly supersedes all prior verbal representations; HR records say the manager lacked authority to modify standard terms or grant remote exceptions; and the reasonableness and causation of Hayes’s May 15 reliance are disputed under Texas merger, authority, and contract-bar principles. [11] [8] [10]

**The company’s motion also may be denied.** The company has strong arguments, but a factfinder could view the June 2024 email, the company’s tolerance of Hayes’s full-remote work through June, and the two simultaneous hires’ written remote approvals as evidence that full-remote exceptions existed and that Hayes may reasonably have understood the manager’s confirmation as reflecting a real company-approved arrangement. Under Rule 166a(c) or Rule 56(a), those disputes can be material to promise, authority/apparent authority, reliance, and injustice. [2] [3] [11]

**Practical ranking of outcomes:** (1) Hayes is unlikely to win summary judgment for prospective full-remote specific performance; (2) the company has a substantial but not certain path to summary judgment because of the integration/supersession clause, the hybrid-policy term, the manager’s lack of authority, and the valid-contract bar; and (3) the most defensible surviving claim is a limited promissory-estoppel reliance-damages claim, capped by proof that the $40,000 home-sale loss and any lease losses were caused by reliance on the full-remote promise rather than by the Austin job relocation generally.
