# DiamondRock financing strategy: swaps, debt mix, financing cost, and FCF

**Bottom line.** DiamondRock Hospitality Company (NYSE: DRH; SEC CIK 0001298946) has shifted from hotel-specific mortgage debt toward unsecured term-loan funding while using SOFR interest-rate swaps to partially fix cash interest costs. The strategy has **improved collateral and refinancing flexibility**—mortgage debt declined from **$373.285 million at June 30, 2024 to $0 at September 30, 2025**, and the portfolio became fully unencumbered—but it has **not fully neutralized floating-rate exposure**, because swaps covered **$400.000 million, or 36.36%, of $1.100 billion** of unsecured term-loan principal at September 30, 2025, leaving roughly **$700 million** floating-rate exposure [1] [2]. The cash hedge benefit is real: swap settlements reduced interest expense by **$2.351 million in Q2 2024** and **$0.847 million in Q3 2025**, while mark-to-market fair value moved separately through OCI, including a **$394,000 deterioration** in derivative asset value from December 31, 2023 to June 30, 2024 [1] [2].

| Metric | Earlier point | Later point | Change / interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Interest-rate swap fair value | **$4.321m asset** at Dec. 31, 2023 | **$3.927m asset** at Jun. 30, 2024 | **-$0.394m**, a deterioration in derivative value [3] [1] |
| Total interest expense, Q2 | **$15.567m** in Q2 2023 | **$16.202m** in Q2 2024 | **+$0.635m**, **+4.079%** [1] |
| Total interest expense, Q3 | **$16.986m** in Q3 2024 | **$17.111m** in Q3 2025 | **+$0.125m**, **+0.736%** [2] |
| Mortgage debt | **$373.285m** at Jun. 30, 2024 | **$0** at Sep. 30, 2025 | **-$373.285m**; all secured debt repaid [1] [2] |
| Unsecured term loans | **$800.000m** at Jun. 30, 2024 | **$1.100b** at Sep. 30, 2025 | **+$300.000m** under amended unsecured facilities [1] [2] |
| Weighted-average debt rate incl. swaps | **5.22%** at Jun. 30, 2024 | **5.31%** at Sep. 30, 2025 | **+9 bps**, despite unsecured mix shift [1] [2] |
| 9M 2025 free cash flow | Operating cash flow **$175.730m** less capex **$60.941m** | **$114.789m FCF** | Covered **38.8%** of **$295.808m** mortgage repayments [2] |

## Filings and issuer used

The analysis uses DiamondRock Hospitality Company’s filings that bracket the requested periods: the **FY 2023 Form 10-K** for December 31, 2023 swap fair value and year-end debt context [3], the **Q2 2024 Form 10-Q** for June 30, 2024 swaps, Q2 interest expense, and debt composition [1], the **Q3 2024 Form 10-Q** for Q3 2024 interest expense and the intermediate mortgage-repayment plan [4], and the **Q3 2025 Form 10-Q** for September 30, 2025 debt composition, swaps, interest expense, mortgage repayments, and cash-flow/capital-allocation data [2]. DiamondRock is identified as DiamondRock Hospitality Company, common stock symbol **DRH** on the NYSE, with SEC **CIK 0001298946** [2].

## Swap fair value: cash hedging worked, but mark-to-market moved against the asset value

DiamondRock’s FY 2023 Form 10-K showed interest-rate swaps as a **$4.321 million aggregate fair-value asset** at December 31, 2023, made up of $1.660 million, $1.658 million, $0.554 million, and $0.449 million fair-value assets on active SOFR swaps, with expired October 2023 swaps at zero [3]. The Q2 2024 Form 10-Q showed the comparable June 30, 2024 balance-sheet fair value as a **$3.927 million aggregate fair-value asset** [1].

**Calculated fair-value change:**

| Date | Presented balance-sheet position | Fair value |
|---|---|---:|
| Dec. 31, 2023 | Interest-rate swap fair-value asset | **$4.321m** [3] |
| Jun. 30, 2024 | Interest-rate swap fair-value asset | **$3.927m** [1] |
| Change: Jun. 30, 2024 minus Dec. 31, 2023 | Deterioration in derivative value | **-$0.394m** [3] [1] |

That **$394,000 deterioration** is not the same as the cash economics of the hedge. The June 30, 2024 derivatives were designated as cash-flow hedges and involved DiamondRock receiving variable SOFR amounts and paying fixed rates without exchanging notional principal [1]. In Q2 2024, those cash-flow hedge settlements were recorded as **$2.351 million of interest income within interest expense**, reducing reported interest expense, while the same quarter also recorded a **$1.355 million OCI loss** on interest-rate derivatives [1]. The distinction is central: the swap mark-to-market moved against reported derivative asset value, but the cash settlements still lowered cash interest cost in the period.

## Interest expense trends across the financing transition

### Q2 2023 to Q2 2024

DiamondRock’s total interest expense increased from **$15.567 million in Q2 2023** to **$16.202 million in Q2 2024**, a **$0.635 million** increase, or **4.079%** [1]. The increase was concentrated in unsecured term-loan interest, while mortgage interest was essentially flat-to-lower:

| Interest expense component | Q2 2023 | Q2 2024 | Change |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Mortgage debt interest | $4.111m | $4.009m | -$0.102m |
| Unsecured term-loan interest | $10.608m | $11.368m | +$0.760m |
| Credit facility interest and unused fees | $0.317m | $0.312m | -$0.005m |
| Amortization of debt issuance costs and debt premium | $0.512m | $0.513m | +$0.001m |
| Interest-rate swap mark-to-market | $0.019m | $0.000m | -$0.019m |
| **Total interest expense** | **$15.567m** | **$16.202m** | **+$0.635m / +4.079%** |

The Q2 2024 increase occurred even though cash-flow hedge settlements reduced interest expense by **$2.351 million** in the quarter [1]. Without that cash hedge benefit, the rise in reported interest expense would have been materially larger.

### Q3 2024 to Q3 2025

After the 2025 mortgage repayments and the expansion of unsecured term loans, total interest expense increased only modestly, from **$16.986 million in Q3 2024** to **$17.111 million in Q3 2025**, a **$0.125 million** increase, or **0.736%** [4] [2]. The component bridge shows the trade-off: unsecured term-loan interest rose, mortgage interest fell, and a new finance-lease line added cost.

| Interest expense component | Q3 2024 | Q3 2025 | Change |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Unsecured term-loan interest | $12.665m | $14.223m | +$1.558m |
| Mortgage debt interest | $3.527m | $1.414m | -$2.113m |
| Credit facility interest and unused fees | $0.315m | $0.504m | +$0.189m |
| Amortization of debt issuance costs | $0.479m | $0.494m | +$0.015m |
| Finance lease expense | $0.000m | $0.476m | +$0.476m |
| **Total interest expense** | **$16.986m** | **$17.111m** | **+$0.125m / +0.736%** [4] [2] |

Excluding the new **$0.476 million** finance-lease expense, Q3 2025 interest cost would have been **$0.351 million lower** year over year. The derivatives again mattered on a cash basis, and reported interest expense already includes these settlement benefits: cash-flow hedge settlements reduced interest expense by **$0.847 million in Q3 2025** versus **$1.155 million in Q3 2024**, so the quarterly hedge benefit declined by **$0.308 million** [2]. For the nine months ended September 30, 2025, total interest expense declined from **$49.434 million in 9M 2024** to **$47.137 million in 9M 2025**, a **$2.297 million** decrease, or **4.647%**, as mortgage interest fell by **$4.367 million** and swap settlements reduced interest expense by **$2.485 million in 9M 2025** versus **$5.863 million in 9M 2024**, a **$3.378 million** decline in nine-month hedge benefit [2]. Those cash settlement effects are separate from noncash OCI losses on derivatives of **$0.664 million in Q3 2025**, **$4.237 million in Q3 2024**, **$4.505 million in 9M 2025**, and **$4.631 million in 9M 2024** [2].

At September 30, 2025, the term-loan spread was **SOFR + 1.35%** at leverage below 30%, with a margin grid up to **SOFR + 2.20%**; the facility also permitted an increase to **$1.8 billion**, and the revolver, Term 1, and Term 3 had two optional six-month extensions [2].

## Mortgage-debt repayment and collateral-release effect

DiamondRock repaid **$295.808 million** of mortgage debt principal during the nine months ended September 30, 2025 [2]. The disclosed repayment path shows a deliberate move away from asset-level secured debt:

| Mortgage repayment | Date | Funding source / result |
|---|---:|---|
| Worthington Renaissance Fort Worth Hotel mortgage | May 6, 2025 | Repaid using cash on hand [2] |
| Hotel Clio mortgage | July 3, 2025 | Repaid at maturity using a **$60.0m** revolver draw made July 2, 2025; the draw was repaid July 22, 2025 [2] |
| Westin Boston Seaport District mortgage | Sept. 5, 2025 | Repaid using proceeds from the Amended Credit Facility [2] |
| Aggregate 9M 2025 mortgage debt principal payments | 9M ended Sept. 30, 2025 | **$295.808m** [2] |

The result was strategically meaningful: after the Westin Boston repayment, DiamondRock stated that its portfolio was **fully unencumbered by secured debt** [2]. That improves capital-allocation flexibility because properties are no longer tied to mortgage-specific covenants, collateral restrictions, or property-level lender remedies, although it shifts more of the balance sheet toward unsecured floating-rate SOFR debt.

## Swap coverage and tenor versus the unsecured term-loan maturity profile

DiamondRock’s swaps reduced cash exposure but covered only part of the unsecured term-loan base. At December 31, 2023, DiamondRock had swaps related to **$325 million** of variable-rate indebtedness and disclosed that, if unhedged variable-rate debt market rates moved by 100 basis points, annual interest expense would change by **$4.8 million** [3]. At June 30, 2024, the same **$325 million** notional covered **40.625%** of the **$800 million** unsecured term-loan principal and **27.70%** of total debt principal [1]. The June 30, 2024 swap book consisted of two **$87.5 million** SOFR swaps at **1.63%** maturing **July 25, 2024**, with fair values of **$0.216 million** each; one **$75 million** SOFR swap at **3.36%** maturing **January 1, 2028**, with fair value of **$1.958 million**; and one **$75 million** SOFR swap at **3.50%** maturing **January 1, 2027**, with fair value of **$1.537 million** [1]. The **July 2024 maturity of $175 million** of low fixed-rate swaps helps explain why later hedge coverage and settlement benefits changed [1] [2].

At September 30, 2025, DiamondRock had **seven SOFR interest-rate swaps** on unsecured term loans, all designated as **cash-flow hedges of variable-rate debt**, with aggregate notional of **$400.000 million**, a weighted-average fixed swap rate of **3.30625%**, and a net fair-value asset of **$0.168 million** [2]. Those swaps covered **36.36%** of the **$1.100 billion** unsecured term-loan principal, leaving roughly **$700 million** floating-rate exposure; DiamondRock disclosed that a 100-basis-point move in unhedged variable-rate debt rates would change annual interest expense by **$7.0 million** at September 30, 2025 [2]. The hedge cash benefit remained positive but smaller year over year: settlements reduced interest expense by **$0.847 million in Q3 2025** versus **$1.155 million in Q3 2024**, and by **$2.485 million in 9M 2025** versus **$5.863 million in 9M 2024**; those amounts are cash interest-expense offsets, distinct from the noncash OCI losses of **$0.664 million**, **$4.237 million**, **$4.505 million**, and **$4.631 million** for Q3 2025, Q3 2024, 9M 2025, and 9M 2024, respectively [2].

DiamondRock did add a further **$50 million** swap on October 15, 2025, effective January 4, 2027, but that was subsequent to September 30, 2025 and does not change the September 30 coverage calculation [2].

## Net financing-cost change from mortgage debt to unsecured term loans

The debt transition reduced secured debt but did not produce a simple one-for-one interest saving, because DiamondRock replaced lower-coupon fixed mortgage debt with SOFR-based unsecured term loans while using swaps to dampen the cash-rate exposure.

**Balance and run-rate mechanics.** From June 30, 2024 to September 30, 2025, mortgage debt declined from **$373.285 million** to **$0**, while unsecured term loans rose from **$800.000 million** to **$1.100 billion**, so total debt principal declined by **$73.285 million** [1] [2]. Using the June 30, 2024 disclosed mortgage rates, the retired mortgages represented approximately **$15.779 million** of annual stated cash interest; the incremental **$300 million** term-loan tranche at the September 30, 2025 disclosed **5.47%** rate costs approximately **$16.410 million** annually, implying a **$0.631 million annual gross run-rate increase** before timing, fees, lease reclassification, and swap cash settlements [1] [2]. On the broader disclosed weighted-average-rate basis, however, the swap-inclusive indicative total-debt annual run-rate moved from **$1.173285 billion × 5.22% = approximately $61.245 million** at June 30, 2024 to **$1.100 billion × 5.31% = approximately $58.410 million** at September 30, 2025, an indicative **$2.835 million annual decrease** despite the 9-basis-point increase in the weighted-average rate including swaps [1] [2]. The apparent tension is explained by scope: the **+$0.631 million** figure isolates replacing the retired mortgage coupons with the incremental term-loan tranche, while the **-$2.835 million** figure applies the disclosed swap-inclusive weighted-average rates to the lower total debt principal; reported results landed between these mechanics, with Q3 interest expense up **$0.125 million** and 9M interest expense down **$2.297 million** [4] [2].

**Reported Q3 bridge.** The reported quarter-over-quarter bridge is more favorable than the gross run-rate comparison because the timing of repayments and swaps offset the unsecured-rate increase. From Q3 2024 to Q3 2025, total interest expense rose only **$0.125 million**, from **$16.986 million** to **$17.111 million** [4] [2]. Within that, unsecured term-loan interest increased **$1.558 million**, mortgage interest decreased **$2.113 million**, credit facility/unused fees increased **$0.189 million**, debt-issuance amortization increased **$0.015 million**, and finance lease expense increased **$0.476 million** [2]. Excluding the finance-lease line, financing cost fell **$0.351 million** year over year. The swap-settlement bridge cuts the other way year over year even though swaps still reduced reported interest expense: cash-flow hedge settlements reduced interest expense by **$0.847 million in Q3 2025** versus **$1.155 million in Q3 2024**, a **$0.308 million** smaller quarterly benefit, and by **$2.485 million in 9M 2025** versus **$5.863 million in 9M 2024**, a **$3.378 million** smaller nine-month benefit; these cash settlement benefits are already embedded in reported interest expense and are separate from noncash derivative OCI losses of **$0.664 million in Q3 2025**, **$4.237 million in Q3 2024**, **$4.505 million in 9M 2025**, and **$4.631 million in 9M 2024** [2].

**Nine-month evidence.** For the nine months ended September 30, total interest expense fell **$2.297 million**, from **$49.434 million in 2024** to **$47.137 million in 2025**, even though unsecured term-loan principal increased by **$300 million** during the transition [2]. This supports the conclusion that the hedging and repayment strategy has been effective at limiting reported cash financing-cost pressure so far, but not because the unsecured debt is inherently cheaper than the repaid mortgages. Rather, the result reflects repayment timing, lower total principal debt, mortgage interest elimination, and swap settlement benefits; the noncash derivative fair-value changes, such as the **$4.505 million** 9M 2025 OCI loss, are separate from the cash interest reduction [2].

## Overall assessment: effective but only partially hedged

DiamondRock’s evolving financing strategy **does balance interest-rate risk management with capital-allocation flexibility, but with a measured residual risk**. The strongest evidence for effectiveness is that total debt principal declined by **$73.285 million** from June 30, 2024 to September 30, 2025, the weighted-average rate including swaps rose only from **5.22% to 5.31%**, Q3 interest expense rose only **$0.125 million**, and 9M interest expense fell **$2.297 million** while all secured mortgage debt was eliminated [1] [2]. The release of hotel collateral and the move to a larger unsecured facility with a **$400 million revolver**, **$1.100 billion** of term loans, extension options, and possible expansion to **$1.8 billion** materially improves refinancing flexibility [2].

The trade-off is that the company exchanged relatively low-rate mortgage coupons of **3.66% to 4.40%** for a larger base of SOFR-linked unsecured debt, with only **$400 million** swapped against **$1.100 billion** of unsecured term loans at September 30, 2025 [1] [2]. The swaps reduced cash interest expense by **$0.847 million in Q3 2025** and **$2.485 million in 9M 2025**, but fair values remained volatile through OCI, including **$0.664 million** of OCI losses in Q3 2025 and **$4.505 million** in 9M 2025 [2]. If operating FCF is insufficient for future refinancing or shareholder/capex priorities, DiamondRock would need to rely on the undrawn revolver, cash balances, asset-sale proceeds, or reduced distributions, repurchases, or capital expenditures to complete refinancing without increasing rate or liquidity risk [2].
