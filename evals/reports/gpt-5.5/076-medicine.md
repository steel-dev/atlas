# DSM-5 vs ICD-11 autism diagnosis, with focus on sensory processing differences

## Executive findings

DSM-5 (2013) and ICD-11 (adopted 2019; effective 2022) both define autism spectrum disorder (ASD) through **two required domains**: (1) social-communication/social-interaction differences and (2) restricted, repetitive, or inflexible behaviour/interests/activities. The most important sensory-processing difference is not the wording alone but the **operational weight**:

- **DSM-5 299.00/F84.0** makes sensory features explicit in **Criterion B.4**: “Hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory input or unusual interest in sensory aspects of the environment.” It is **optional**—one of four restricted/repetitive-behaviour (RRB) options, of which **at least two** are required [1].
- **ICD-11 6A02** includes sensory features as one possible manifestation within the required “restricted, repetitive, and inflexible patterns” domain—“lifelong excessive and persistent hypersensitivity or hyposensitivity to sensory stimuli or unusual interest in a sensory stimulus”—but **does not require sensory symptoms** and does **not** set a DSM-like minimum count of RRB subcriteria [2].

The evidence base directly comparing **DSM-5 vs ICD-11** diagnostic accuracy is thin. The stronger quantitative evidence since 2013 evaluates DSM-5 against DSM-IV/DSM-IV-TR or best-estimate clinical diagnosis; ICD-11 evidence is mostly comparative conceptual/clinical-utility analysis rather than head-to-head diagnostic-accuracy studies. Overall, DSM-5’s stricter count rules can reduce diagnoses among milder, PDD-NOS/Asperger-like, female, older, or cognitively able presentations, whereas ICD-11’s broader wording may improve sensitivity but may reduce specificity and precision [3], [4], [5], [6], [7].

## A. Side-by-side criteria table

| Requested field | DSM-5 ASD, 299.00/F84.0 | ICD-11 ASD, 6A02 | Practical comparison for sensory processing |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Core social-communication criteria** | Requires **persistent deficits in social communication and social interaction across multiple contexts**, with **all 3** subcriteria present currently or by history: (1) social-emotional reciprocity; (2) nonverbal communicative behaviours used for social interaction; (3) developing, maintaining, and understanding relationships [1]. | Requires **persistent deficits in initiating and sustaining social communication and reciprocal social interactions** outside the expected range for age and intellectual development [2]. | DSM-5 is more operationalized: all three named social-communication subcriteria are mandatory. ICD-11 uses a broader essential-feature description without a DSM-like “3 of 3” rule. |
| **Restricted/repetitive behaviour domain** | Requires **restricted, repetitive patterns of behaviour, interests, or activities**, with **at least 2 of 4**: stereotyped/repetitive movements, objects, or speech; sameness/routines; highly restricted/fixated interests; sensory hyper-/hyporeactivity or unusual sensory interests [1]. | Requires **persistent restricted, repetitive, and inflexible patterns of behaviour, interests, or activities** that are clearly atypical or excessive for age and sociocultural context [2]. | Both require an RRB-type domain, but DSM-5 requires a minimum count; ICD-11 does not. |
| **Sensory processing mention/weighting** | Criterion **B.4**: “Hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory input or unusual interest in sensory aspects of the environment,” with examples such as pain/temperature indifference, adverse response to sounds/textures, excessive smelling/touching, and visual fascination with lights/movement [1]. **Weight:** optional; can count as one of the two required B criteria. | Sensory features are included as possible RRB manifestations: **“lifelong excessive and persistent hypersensitivity or hyposensitivity to sensory stimuli or unusual interest in a sensory stimulus,”** including sounds, light, textures such as clothing/food, odours/tastes, heat/cold, and pain [2]. **Weight:** optional manifestation within a required domain; no fixed count. | DSM-5 gives sensory processing a discrete checklist position. ICD-11 gives it explicit recognition but embeds it in a broader clinical description. A sensory-focused person still needs the social-communication domain and functional impairment under both systems. |
| **Severity specifiers / qualifier structure** | Requires current severity to be specified separately for **social communication** and **restricted/repetitive behaviours**: Level 3 “requires very substantial support,” Level 2 “requires substantial support,” Level 1 “requires support.” Also specify with/without intellectual impairment, with/without language impairment, associated medical/genetic/environmental factor, associated neurodevelopmental/mental/behavioural disorder, and catatonia [1]. | Uses 6A02 subcategories/qualifiers based mainly on **intellectual development** and **functional language**: 6A02.0 without disorder of intellectual development and mild/no functional-language impairment; 6A02.1 with disorder of intellectual development and mild/no language impairment; 6A02.2 without intellectual-development disorder and impaired functional language; 6A02.3 with both intellectual-development disorder and impaired functional language; 6A02.5 with intellectual-development disorder and complete/almost complete absence of functional language; plus 6A02.Y other specified and 6A02.Z unspecified. ICD-11 also allows coding loss of previously acquired skills as 6A02.x0 without loss or 6A02.x1 with loss [2]. | DSM-5 severity is support-need based and domain-specific; ICD-11 qualifier structure is more coding-oriented around intellectual development, language, and skill loss. Neither system has a dedicated sensory-severity qualifier. |
| **Age/onset requirements** | Symptoms must be present in the **early developmental period**, but may not become fully manifest until social demands exceed capacities or may be masked by learned strategies later in life [1]. | Onset occurs during the **developmental period**, typically early childhood, but may become fully manifest later when social demands exceed limited capacities [2]. | Both explicitly allow later clinical recognition when demands exceed capacity; this matters for adults, women/girls, and cognitively able people whose traits were masked. |
| **Functional impairment threshold** | Symptoms must cause **clinically significant impairment** in social, occupational, or other important areas of current functioning, and **Criterion E** requires that the disturbances are **not better explained by intellectual disability (intellectual developmental disorder) or global developmental delay**. DSM-5 also notes that intellectual disability and ASD frequently co-occur; when both are diagnosed, social communication should be below that expected for the person’s general developmental level [1]. | Symptoms must result in **significant impairment** in personal, family, social, educational, occupational, or other important functioning. ICD-11 notes that ASD can still be diagnosed when adequate functioning is maintained only through exceptional effort [2]. | ICD-11’s “exceptional effort” clause is more explicit about camouflaging/compensation. DSM-5 also recognizes masking in the onset criterion but does not phrase the impairment threshold this way. |

## B. Evidence synthesis: peer-reviewed sources, 2013–present

### Scope and limitations

The search identified **no robust head-to-head diagnostic-accuracy study** that prospectively applies both DSM-5 and ICD-11 6A02 criteria to the same clinical sample and reports sensitivity/specificity against an independent reference standard. The best available evidence therefore combines: (1) DSM-5 diagnostic-accuracy and prevalence-impact studies; (2) DSM-5 meta-analyses; and (3) peer-reviewed ICD-11 vs DSM-5 clinical-utility/commentary analyses. GRADE ratings below are applied transparently for the specific question “How accurately or usefully do DSM-5 and ICD-11 criteria classify ASD, especially sensory/RRB presentations?”

| Source | Design and comparator | Population / sample size | Key quantitative or clinical-utility findings | Sensory/RRB-specific findings | GRADE certainty for this question |
|---|---|---:|---|---|---|
| **Kulage, Smaldone & Cohn 2014, J Autism Dev Disord** [3] | Systematic review/meta-analysis of DSM-5 effect vs DSM-IV/DSM-IV-TR criteria. | 418 studies identified; **14 included**. | DSM-5 reduced ASD diagnoses by **31%** (95% CI 20–44; p=0.006); autistic disorder by **22%** (16–29; p<0.001); PDD-NOS by **70%** (55–82; p=0.01); Asperger’s by **70%** (26–94; p=0.38, not significant). Reported study-level decreases ranged **7.3%–68.4%**. | Does not isolate sensory-focused presentations, but the largest losses in PDD-NOS/Asperger-like groups imply risk where DSM-5 count/domain thresholds are not met. | **Low.** Starts as observational/diagnostic reclassification evidence; downgraded for indirectness to ICD-11, heterogeneity/inconsistency, and variable retrospective methods; upgraded slightly for consistent direction of reduced DSM-5 diagnosis. |
| **Bennett & Goodall 2016, Review Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders** [5] | Meta-analysis of DSM-IV/DSM-IV-TR ASD cases reclassified under DSM-5. | **24 studies**. | All 24 studies showed reductions. Reductions were **35%** for DSM-IV and **37%** for DSM-IV-TR. DSM-IV/DSM-IV-TR subgroups retaining DSM-5 ASD were lowest for PDD-NOS (**26%/47%**), then Asperger syndrome (**55%/19%**), and highest for autistic disorder (**80%/87%**). | Not a sensory-specific analysis; RRB-count requirements likely contributed to lower retention in less prototypical subgroups. | **Low.** Consistent direction, but indirect to ICD-11; likely heterogeneous source samples and reclassification methods; imprecision for subgroups. |
| **Sung et al. 2018, J Autism Dev Disord** [6] | Primary empirical clinical-utility/accuracy study; concurrent DSM-5 and DSM-IV-TR criteria vs best-estimate clinical diagnosis. | **N=110** Singapore specialty outpatient referrals, age **5.1–19.6 years**. | DSM-IV-TR sensitivity/specificity **0.946/0.889**; DSM-5 **0.837/0.833**. ASD-subcategory sensitivity **0.667–0.933** and specificity **0.900–0.975**. DSM-5 misclassified **40%** of best-estimate PDD-NOS cases. | No sensory-focused subgroup statistics reported; safety relevance is that DSM-5 may miss less classic/PDD-NOS presentations. | **Low.** Primary clinical sample and concurrent criteria strengthen applicability, but small sample, specialty setting, best-estimate reference not independent of clinical judgment, and no ICD-11 comparator. |
| **Wiggins et al. 2019, Soc Psychiatry Psychiatr Epidemiol / SEED2** [4] | Primary empirical diagnostic-accuracy study; DSM-5 and DSM-IV-TR coded after comprehensive developmental evaluation vs SEED2 final classification. | Community-based US preschool sample, age **2–5 years**. DSM-5 2×2 counts: **738 TP, 64 FP, 35 FN, 224 TN**. | DSM-5 sensitivity **0.95**, specificity **0.78**, PPV **0.92**, NPV **0.86**. Agreement with DSM-IV-TR autistic disorder κ=**0.78**; agreement with two PDD-NOS definitions κ=**0.57/0.59**. | Missed DSM-5 cases vs DSM-IV-TR were more likely to have mild symptoms; discordant PDD-NOS group more likely female and less likely intellectual disability. | **Moderate for DSM-5 preschool diagnostic accuracy; low for DSM-5 vs ICD-11.** Stronger sample and explicit 2×2 data; downgraded for indirectness to ICD-11 and preschool-only applicability. |
| **Kulage et al. 2019, J Autism Dev Disord** [8] | Systematic review/meta-analysis after DSM-5 publication; DSM-5 diagnosis and Social Communication Disorder impact. | **33 studies**. | DSM-5 suggested decreases in ASD diagnosis of **20.8%** (95% CI 16.0–26.7; p<0.001), autistic disorder **10.1%** (6.2–16.0; p<0.001), Asperger’s **23.3%** (12.9–38.5; p=0.001), and PDD-NOS **46.1%** (34.6–58.0; p=0.52, not significant). Less than one-third of DSM-IV-TR but not DSM-5 ASD cases qualified for SCD: **28.8%** (13.9–50.5; p=0.06). | No direct sensory subgroup; indicates that people losing DSM-5 ASD status often are not fully captured by SCD, creating service-access risk. | **Low.** Systematic review, but indirect to ICD-11, heterogeneous criteria implementation, and mostly reclassification/prevalence rather than independent diagnostic accuracy. |

### Additional ICD-11 clinical-utility evidence

Two peer-reviewed ICD-11-focused sources help interpret likely DSM-5 vs ICD-11 effects but are not diagnostic-accuracy studies. The ICD-11 psychological approach review states that ICD-11 and DSM-5 differ most in the RRB/inflexibility domain, not the social-communication domain; ICD-11 RRB language is broader and includes atypical sensory responses, a change from ICD-10 [9]. Kamp-Becker’s 2024 Molecular Psychiatry critique argues that ICD-11’s broader symptom combinations and lack of DSM-like symptom counts may increase sensitivity for older and cognitively able people, but could reduce specificity, increase false positives, raise prevalence, and make treatment planning less specific [7]. For GRADE, this ICD-11 material is **very low certainty** for diagnostic accuracy because it is indirect expert/clinical-utility evidence rather than empirical accuracy data.

## C. Clinical guideline and official positions

| Organization | Position on DSM-5 vs ICD-11 adoption/use | Position on sensory processing weighting |
|---|---|---|
| **WHO** | ICD-11 was adopted by the 72nd World Health Assembly in **May 2019** and came into effect globally for Member States on **1 January 2022**. WHO encourages implementation/reporting with ICD-11 for interoperability and consistency, but countries may continue ICD-10 as long as necessary and there is no penalty for missing a five-year implementation timeline [10], [11]. | WHO ICD-11 6A02 includes sensory hyper-/hyposensitivity or unusual sensory interests as possible manifestations within ASD’s restricted/repetitive/inflexible domain, but not as a required standalone feature [2]. |
| **APA / DSM-5** | APA’s DSM-5 provides standardized ASD diagnostic criteria; in US diagnostic/coding contexts ASD is listed as **299.00/F84.0** [1]. DSM-5 remains the dominant psychiatric diagnostic framework in the US. | DSM-5 explicitly recognizes sensory processing in **Criterion B.4**, but only as one optional RRB criterion among four, with at least two B criteria required [1]. |
| **American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP)** | The AAP 2020 clinical report uses the DSM-5 two-domain framework and states that diagnosis requires all three social-affective symptoms plus two of four RRB symptoms; diagnosis can occur later when school/social demands create functional impairment [12]. | AAP ties DSM-5’s inclusion of sensory symptoms to recognition of sensory processing differences, and lists hyper-/hyporeactivity and unusual sensory interests. It also warns that common sensory-based interventions such as brushing, weighted vests, kinesthetic stimulation, and specialized seating have limited peer-reviewed support and should be monitored with goals/outcomes [12]. |
| **NICE** | NICE under-19 guidance says autism diagnosis should be based on ICD-11 or DSM-5 criteria and that if a child has autism-spectrum features but does not meet definitive ICD-11/DSM-5 criteria, referral to appropriate services should still be considered based on profile [13]. NICE adult guidance similarly uses comprehensive clinical assessment rather than privileging one manual [14]. | NICE recommends profiling sensory sensitivities in children and asking about hyper-/hypo-sensory sensitivities in adults; adult services should adapt environments/interventions for sensory sensitivities. NICE frames sensory differences as assessment and accommodation targets, not as a standalone diagnostic route [14], [13]. |

## D. Advocacy organization positions and neurodiversity-affirming interpretation

### What the organizations explicitly say

| Organization | Explicit position found | Manual preference? |
|---|---|---|
| **Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN)** | ASAN’s joint DSM-5 statement with the Autism Society said the autism spectrum is broad and diverse; DSM-5 criteria should “do no harm” by preserving access for people who would have qualified under DSM-IV; ASAN rejected a simple overdiagnosis framing and highlighted diagnostic barriers for racial/ethnic minorities, women/girls, adults, and rural/low-income people [15]. ASAN’s identity-language page favors identity-first “autistic person,” says autism is not a disease, and frames autism as a lifelong neurological/developmental condition and meaningful identity component [16]. | **No explicit ICD-11-over-DSM-5 or DSM-5-over-ICD-11 preference found.** ASAN’s concern is broad, equitable diagnostic access and affirming language. |
| **National Autistic Society (NAS)** | NAS says diagnosis should be based on **DSM-5 or ICD-11** under NICE guidance; it notes that medical-manual language may not match NAS’s usual language, and that many people, including most autistic people, do not consider autism a disorder and autism is not a disease [17]. NAS uses “communication and social interaction differences” and notes RRB/interests are often called “stimming” by autistic people [17]. | **No explicit preference.** NAS accepts both DSM-5 and ICD-11 as professional classification systems while distancing itself from deficit-only/manual language. |
| **Autism Speaks** | Autism Speaks’ DSM-5 FAQ describes DSM-5 goals as more accurate diagnosis, identifying symptoms needing treatment/support, and severity assessment; it notes consolidation of subtypes, addition of sensory issues under RRB, and DSM-5 severity levels 1–3 [18]. Its sensory page says sensory issues are common, included in ASD criteria, and unique to each autistic person, including both hyper- and hyposensitivity [19]. | **No explicit preference.** Autism Speaks describes DSM-5 and sensory issues but does not state that DSM-5 or ICD-11 is more neurodiversity-affirming. |

### Analytical conclusion: which manual is more neurodiversity-affirming?

No advocacy organization located explicitly ranks DSM-5 against ICD-11 as “more neurodiversity-affirming.” On the criteria themselves, **ICD-11 is somewhat more compatible with neurodiversity-affirming assessment in three respects**, but with an important safety trade-off:

1. **Broader, less checklist-like RRB structure:** ICD-11 does not require a fixed “2 of 4” RRB count, which may better fit heterogeneous presentations and later-recognized/cognitively able people [2], [9].
2. **Explicit compensation/effort recognition:** ICD-11’s impairment text recognizes adequate functioning maintained only through exceptional effort, aligning with advocacy concerns about masking/camouflaging and delayed diagnosis [2], [15].
3. **Less support-level labelling:** ICD-11’s qualifiers focus on intellectual development and functional language rather than DSM-5 “severity levels” based on support needs, which some may view as less globalizing. However, DSM-5’s support-need severity can also be clinically useful for service planning [1].

DSM-5 is more operationalized and transparent: all three social-communication subcriteria and two of four RRB criteria are required, with sensory differences explicitly named as B.4 [1]. That structure can improve reliability but may be less affirming if it excludes people whose disability is real but whose traits are masked, mild, female-typical, adult-recognized, or sensory/RRB-atypical. ICD-11 may be more inclusive but, as Kamp-Becker argues, may sacrifice precision and specificity [7]. Thus, the fairest conclusion is: **ICD-11 appears more inclusive and potentially more neurodiversity-compatible in wording and flexibility; DSM-5 is more operationally precise. Advocacy organizations emphasize access, identity-respectful language, and support needs rather than endorsing one manual over the other.**

## E. Diagnostic safety risks, especially for sensory-focused presentations

### Under-diagnosis / missed diagnosis

The clearest safety signal is **missed diagnosis under DSM-5 relative to DSM-IV/DSM-IV-TR**, especially among PDD-NOS/Asperger-like, milder, female, cognitively able, or less intellectually disabled groups:

- Kulage 2014 found DSM-5 reduced pooled ASD diagnosis by **31%**, with the largest pooled decrease for PDD-NOS (**70%**) [3].
- Bennett & Goodall 2016 found all 24 studies showed DSM-5 reductions; only **26%/47%** of PDD-NOS and **55%/19%** of Asperger subgroups retained DSM-5 ASD, compared with **80%/87%** for autistic disorder [5].
- Sung 2018 found DSM-5 sensitivity **0.837** vs DSM-IV-TR **0.946**, and DSM-5 misclassified **40%** of best-estimate PDD-NOS cases [6].
- Wiggins 2019 found strong DSM-5 sensitivity in preschoolers (**0.95**) but DSM-5 misses were more likely mild; discordance with PDD-NOS was greater for females and those without intellectual disability [4].

For **sensory-focused presentations**, neither DSM-5 nor ICD-11 allows ASD diagnosis on sensory features alone: social-communication differences, RRB/inflexibility, developmental onset, and functional impairment are still required [2], [1]. A safety risk arises when sensory distress is prominent but social-communication history is subtle, masked, or under-documented. DSM-5’s B.4 can help by making sensory features count explicitly, but a person with sensory hyper-/hyposensitivity plus only one other RRB feature may still fail DSM-5’s “2 of 4” threshold [1]. ICD-11’s broader RRB language may reduce this risk, but empirical validation is limited [9], [7].

### Over-diagnosis / false positives

The main over-diagnosis concern is theoretical and tied to ICD-11’s breadth. Kamp-Becker argues ICD-11 permits more symptom combinations and lacks DSM-5’s symptom-count rules, which may improve sensitivity but may also reduce specificity, increase false-positive ASD diagnoses, increase prevalence, and produce less specific services/treatments [7]. This is **very low-certainty** evidence for actual false-positive rates because it is a peer-reviewed critique rather than a prospective validation study.

### Misdiagnosis and inappropriate intervention risk

Sensory symptoms are common and clinically important, but sensory processing disorder is not a discrete DSM-5 ASD diagnosis. AAP notes that sensory symptoms can be early developmental differences and treatment/accommodation targets, but commonly used sensory-based interventions—brushing, weighted vests, kinesthetic stimulation, specialized seating—lack strong peer-reviewed evidence and require monitored goals/outcomes [12]. Diagnostic safety therefore requires two safeguards:

1. Do not reduce ASD assessment to sensory symptoms alone; evaluate full developmental, social-communication, RRB, adaptive, and functional profile [2], [14], [13], [1].
2. If sensory supports are used, treat them as individualized accommodations or goals with outcome monitoring, not as proof of ASD or as a substitute for evidence-based developmental, communication, educational, or mental-health support [12].

### Sex/gender, intellectual/language-disability, and access disparities

ASAN explicitly warned that women/girls, adults, racial/ethnic minorities, rural and low-income communities face diagnostic-access barriers [15]. Empirically, Wiggins 2019 found PDD-NOS discordant cases were more likely female and less likely to have intellectual disability [4]. ICD-11’s broader RRB and “exceptional effort” wording may be safer for masked or later-recognized presentations, but DSM-5’s clearer thresholds may be safer against false positives. Clinically, this argues for structured assessment plus narrative formulation, not mechanical checklist use.

## F. Reimbursement and coding summary: US and Europe/EU

### United States

US billing remains based on **ICD-10-CM**, not ICD-11. DSM-5 ASD is coded as **299.00/F84.0**, and the current ICD-10-CM code commonly used for ASD presentations is **F84.0 “Autistic disorder,”** a billable/specific code that includes “Autism spectrum disorder,” “Infantile autism,” “Infantile psychosis,” and “Kanner’s syndrome” [1]. In practice, payers generally require:

- a valid/confirmed ASD diagnosis,
- an ICD-10-CM diagnosis code in the ASD range, commonly F84.0–F84.9 depending on policy,
- documentation of medical necessity,
- prior authorization for some services such as ABA, and
- individualized measurable goals and qualified providers.

Search results located payer examples requiring confirmed ASD plus ICD-10-CM codes F84.0–F84.9 and/or DSM-5-based diagnosis, rather than coverage turning solely on whether DSM-5 or ICD-11 diagnostic language was used. Thus, in the US, **adoption of ICD-11 criteria by WHO does not by itself change coverage or reimbursement** until US coding systems and payer policies change.

### Europe / ICD-11 jurisdictions

WHO’s ICD-11 came into effect for Member States on **1 January 2022**, and WHO encourages implementation, but countries can continue ICD-10 as long as necessary [10], [11]. European systems vary: some have begun implementation projects or limited mental-health use, while many reimbursement and morbidity systems still depend on national ICD-10 adaptations or other national coding/payment rules. Therefore, an ICD-11 6A02 diagnosis may improve semantic alignment where ICD-11 is implemented, but **coverage usually depends on national law, benefit design, medical-necessity rules, and local coding implementation**, not on the abstract superiority of DSM-5 vs ICD-11 criteria.

## Bottom line

- **Sensory differences are explicit but optional in both systems.** DSM-5 makes them a named optional criterion, B.4, counting toward the “2 of 4” RRB threshold; ICD-11 embeds them as an optional manifestation within the required RRB/inflexibility domain [2], [1].
- **DSM-5 is more precise; ICD-11 is more flexible.** DSM-5’s count rules support operational consistency but risk missed diagnosis in milder, masked, PDD-NOS/Asperger-like, female, or cognitively able presentations. ICD-11 may better accommodate heterogeneity and exceptional effort/camouflaging but needs stronger empirical validation for specificity and false-positive risk [7], [9], [4].
- **Clinical guidelines do not treat sensory processing as a standalone diagnostic route.** WHO, APA/DSM, AAP, and NICE all support assessing sensory differences, but diagnosis still rests on the full ASD profile and functional impact [2], [14], [13], [1], [12].
- **Advocacy organizations do not explicitly prefer DSM-5 or ICD-11.** Their shared emphasis is equitable diagnostic access, identity-respectful language, recognition that autism is not a disease, and supports/accommodations that fit lived sensory and social experience [16], [17], [19], [15].
- **Reimbursement currently follows coding and medical necessity.** In the US that means ICD-10-CM, especially F84.0 for ASD, plus payer rules; ICD-11 adoption internationally is uneven and does not automatically change coverage [1], [10].
