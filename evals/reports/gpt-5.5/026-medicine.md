# Clinical assessment update: WISC-V versus NEPSY-II for ADHD assessment in children aged 8–12

## Bottom line

For an 8–12-year-old child being evaluated for ADHD, neither the WISC-V nor the NEPSY-II should be treated as a stand-alone diagnostic test. Current ADHD diagnostic guidance remains DSM-based: symptoms and impairment must be documented in more than one setting, using information from parents, teachers, and school sources. The American Academy of Pediatrics explicitly states that neuropsychological testing “has not been found to improve diagnostic accuracy in most cases,” although it can clarify learning strengths and weaknesses [1]. Pediatric neuropsychology reviewers similarly argue that neuropsychological assessment may add value by detecting comorbidity and functional risk, but that controlled studies showing improved ADHD outcomes from such assessment are still needed [2].

On the narrower question—**which test is more sensitive to working-memory deficits**—the evidence favors the **WISC-V for direct detection and quantification of working-memory weakness**, because it contains a dedicated Working Memory Index and related working-memory measures. However, that conclusion must be qualified: WISC-V working-memory scores are not sufficiently sensitive or specific to diagnose ADHD, and the strongest ADHD working-memory deficits in the broader literature are often spatial and central-executive deficits, which are not fully captured by a simple WISC-V index score. The **NEPSY-II may be more useful for characterizing ADHD-relevant attention, inhibition, cognitive flexibility, and executive-control weaknesses**, and in one recent archival comparative study NEPSY-II/CPT variables outperformed WISC-V indexes for ADHD prediction [3]. But the NEPSY-II does not provide a cleaner or better-validated working-memory sensitivity signal than the WISC-V.

Therefore, the most defensible ranking is:

1. **For diagnosing ADHD:** neither WISC-V nor NEPSY-II; use DSM-5 multi-informant clinical assessment, with tests as adjuncts [1].
2. **For detecting working-memory weakness:** WISC-V is the better direct instrument, especially via WMI/AWMI-type indicators, but it is not ADHD-specific [4], [5], [6].
3. **For broader ADHD-related neuropsychological profiling:** NEPSY-II attention/executive subtests, especially when combined with continuous-performance measures, may be more informative than WISC-V indexes in prediction models, but this evidence is still limited and not equivalent to diagnostic validity [3], [7].
4. **For ecological validity:** claims that NEPSY-II is more “ecological” than the WISC/WISC-V are not strongly established by outcome-prediction evidence. Recent ecological-validity scholarship warns that the term is often used inconsistently and should be replaced with explicit criterion or predictive-validity claims [8].

## Search frame and evidence base

This update used a structured narrative-review strategy rather than a registered PRISMA systematic review. Searches targeted PubMed/medical sources, academic indexes, and web-accessible full texts for combinations of WISC-V, NEPSY-II, ADHD, children, working memory, sensitivity, specificity, diagnostic validity, executive function, pediatric neuropsychology, and ecological validity. The included evidence fell into several overlapping literatures: clinical ADHD guidelines; meta-analyses of executive-function and working-memory deficits in ADHD; WISC-V clinical-validity and profile studies; NEPSY-II ADHD/executive-function studies; test reviews of NEPSY-II; and ecological-validity reviews in neuropsychology. The key limitation is that the literature contains very few direct WISC-V-versus-NEPSY-II diagnostic-accuracy studies in children aged exactly 8–12. The most directly relevant head-to-head evidence located was an archival dissertation including children and adolescents aged 8–16, which compared WISC-V indexes, NEPSY-II subtests, and CPT-3/CATA measures for ADHD subtype prediction [3]. Most other evidence is indirect: WISC-V profile studies compare ADHD or ADHD-comorbid groups with controls or learning-disorder groups [5], [6], [4], while NEPSY-II studies often focus on inhibition, response set, attention, or broader neuropsychological profiles rather than pure working-memory diagnostic sensitivity [7], [9], [10].

## Theoretical framework: ADHD is associated with working-memory and executive-function weaknesses, but these are not universal

The seminal executive-function meta-analysis by Willcutt and colleagues synthesized 83 studies including 3,734 participants with ADHD and 2,969 comparison participants. ADHD groups showed significant impairment across executive-function tasks, with the strongest and most consistent effects on response inhibition, vigilance, working memory, and planning. However, the effects were moderate and not universal; the authors concluded that executive-function weaknesses are neither necessary nor sufficient to explain all ADHD cases [11]. This is critical clinically: a normal WISC-V WMI or NEPSY-II executive score does not rule out ADHD, and an impaired score does not prove ADHD.

The working-memory literature gives a more specific rationale for including working-memory assessment. Martinussen, Hayden, Hogg-Johnson, and Tannock’s meta-analysis found that children with ADHD had deficits in multiple working-memory components independent of language-learning comorbidity and general intellectual weakness. The component-level pattern is important for test selection: effect sizes were larger for spatial storage (ES = 0.85, 95% CI 0.62–1.08) and spatial central-executive working memory (ES = 1.06, 95% CI 0.72–1.39) than for verbal storage (ES = 0.47, 95% CI 0.36–0.59) or verbal central-executive working memory (ES = 0.43, 95% CI 0.24–0.62) [12]. A later meta-analysis by Kasper, Alderson, and Hudec described working memory as a prominent neurocognitive deficit or endophenotype in ADHD models and found large between-group effects across phonological and visuospatial working-memory domains [13].

Recent work complicates that picture. Fosco and colleagues emphasize that although working memory is consistently implicated in ADHD, not all children with ADHD have working-memory deficits; some studies estimate impairment in only about 30% of youth with ADHD, whereas meta-regression estimates suggest far broader below-mean performance relative to typically developing peers [14]. This heterogeneity helps explain why working-memory tests can show group differences yet still perform poorly as diagnostic classifiers for individual children.

## WISC-V evidence in ADHD

The WISC-V is best conceptualized as an intellectual and cognitive-profile measure, not an ADHD diagnostic test. Pearson’s WISC-V special-group material summarizes the conventional pattern: global IQ scores are generally not useful for discriminating ADHD from nonclinical populations, although children with ADHD often show lower auditory working memory and processing speed with relatively preserved verbal comprehension and perceptual reasoning [4]. That profile is clinically plausible but nonspecific.

Empirical WISC-V profile research supports the presence of working-memory weakness in ADHD-related samples but does not establish diagnostic sensitivity. Becker and colleagues studied WISC-V profiles in 62 children with ADHD+SLD (19 girls; mean age 10.44, SD 2.44), 35 children with SLD only, and 62 controls. The ADHD+SLD group scored lower than controls on Working Memory Index, Processing Speed Index, Full Scale IQ, Auditory Working Memory Index, and Cognitive Proficiency Index, with large effects reported especially for processing speed, auditory working memory, and cognitive proficiency [5]. This supports the use of WISC-V for identifying cognitive weaknesses relevant to intervention planning, but the sample was comorbid ADHD+SLD rather than a clean ADHD-only 8–12 sample, so it cannot establish ADHD-specific sensitivity.

A more recent WISC-V diagnostic-validity study comparing students with ADHD to a normative group reported that Processing Speed showed diagnostic validity, whereas Working Memory Index, Verbal Comprehension, Visual Spatial, and Fluid Reasoning did not adequately distinguish ADHD from non-ADHD [6]. Methodologically, this study is useful because it asks a diagnostic-validity question directly, but it is weaker than a modern diagnostic-accuracy study because it does not provide age-8–12 ROC-based sensitivity/specificity cut points and leaves important questions about diagnostic ascertainment.

In the most direct comparative evidence located, Newberry’s archival study analyzed 272 children aged 8–16 using WISC-V, NEPSY-II, CPT-3, and CATA variables. WISC-V Working Memory Index was described as one of the best WISC-V predictors, but WISC-V indexes were not retained when the goal was simply to distinguish ADHD from no ADHD. In the final binary logistic model, the retained predictors were gender, NEPSY-II Animal Sorting—Correct Sorts, NEPSY-II Response Set—Total Correct, CPT-3, CPT-3 squared, and CATA; model fit was χ²(6) = 104.93, p < .001, Cox & Snell R² = .320, and Nagelkerke R² = .621 [3]. Thus, WISC-V WMI appears to be the most relevant WISC-V score for ADHD-related cognitive weakness, but not the best overall predictor of ADHD classification.

## NEPSY-II evidence in ADHD

The NEPSY-II is a developmental neuropsychological battery designed to sample multiple functional domains. Test reviewers describe it as useful in comprehensive evaluations of children with atypical or low-incidence learning patterns because its subtest profile can identify specific skill deficits across aspects of performance [15]. Brooks, Sherman, and Strauss describe the NEPSY-II as a developmental neuropsychological assessment and review its clinical use, but the available review-level evidence does not show that it has superior ADHD diagnostic sensitivity or superior working-memory sensitivity relative to the WISC-V [16].

Recent ADHD-specific NEPSY-II evidence focuses more on inhibition and response control than working memory. A 2025 NEPSY-II study of 48 school-age children/adolescents aged 8–15 with ADHD, anxiety disorder, or comorbid ADHD+anxiety found no significant group differences in cognitive performance, attention, or working memory, but did find inhibition differences: the anxiety-disorder group showed more efficient inhibition than ADHD and ADHD+anxiety groups, and children with ADHD had longer response times [7]. Other NEPSY-II-related studies show that NEPSY-II auditory-response or attention tasks may relate to ADHD symptoms or later ADHD risk, but these measures combine sustained attention, dual attention, working memory, inhibitory control, and cognitive flexibility rather than isolating working memory [17], [9].

The NEPSY-II also has psychometric caveats. A recent exploratory factor analysis of the NEPSY-II conceptual template argues that the battery’s assumed domain structure should be tested against empirical evidence rather than accepted purely from the manual’s conceptual organization [18]. This matters for ADHD assessment because a clinician may be tempted to interpret a NEPSY-II “attention/executive” profile as a coherent latent construct; the factor-analytic literature cautions against overinterpreting domain labels without empirical support.

The strongest comparative argument for NEPSY-II is pragmatic rather than working-memory-specific: when ADHD prediction was modeled using WISC-V, NEPSY-II, CPT-3, and CATA variables, NEPSY-II measures such as Animal Sorting and Response Set survived in final models whereas WISC-V indexes did not [3]. That finding suggests NEPSY-II executive/attention tasks may be more sensitive to ADHD-related control processes than WISC-V index scores, but it does not prove that NEPSY-II is more sensitive to working-memory deficits.

## Direct comparison: WISC-V versus NEPSY-II

| Assessment purpose | Better-supported choice | Rationale | Strength of evidence |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Diagnose ADHD | Neither | ADHD diagnosis is DSM-5, impairment-based, and multi-informant; neuropsychological testing usually does not improve diagnostic accuracy [1]. | Strong guideline-level evidence. |
| Quantify working-memory weakness | WISC-V | WISC-V has a dedicated Working Memory Index and related working-memory indicators; ADHD samples often show WMI/AWMI weakness [5], [4]. | Moderate for group/profile use; weak for diagnostic classification. |
| Detect ADHD-related executive-control profile | NEPSY-II plus CPT-type measures | Comparative archival modeling retained NEPSY-II/CPT/CATA variables rather than WISC-V indexes [3]. | Limited; one direct archival study, broader age range. |
| Ecological/functional interpretation | Neither clearly superior | “Ecological validity” requires explicit prediction of real-world outcomes, and the field uses the term inconsistently [8]. | Strong conceptual critique; limited direct NEPSY-II-vs-WISC outcome evidence. |

The most clinically defensible synthesis is that **WISC-V is more sensitive to working-memory deficits as a construct**, whereas **NEPSY-II may be more sensitive to some ADHD-relevant executive-control manifestations**. If the research question is specifically “working memory,” the WISC-V should be ranked above NEPSY-II. If the question is “ADHD classification or subtype prediction,” neither test alone is sufficient, and the best available comparative evidence gives more support to NEPSY-II/CPT-type attention and executive measures than to WISC-V indexes [3].

## Ecological validity: NEPSY-II versus WISC/WISC-V

The ecological-validity question needs careful handling because “ecological validity” is often used imprecisely. Suchy and colleagues’ 2024 systematic review of ecological validity in executive-function assessment searched MEDLINE and PsycINFO using PRISMA procedures and identified 90 articles. They found that about two-thirds of articles used one of the two classical conceptualizations of ecological validity—veridicality alone, or veridicality plus verisimilitude—whereas about one-third used verisimilitude alone, combined ecological validity with other notions such as association with other tests or diagnostic discrimination, or left the meaning unclear [8]. Their conclusion is directly relevant: when a NEPSY-II subtest looks more like an everyday task than a WISC-V working-memory task, that is not enough to establish ecological validity unless it predicts functional outcomes.

Pediatric neuropsychology reviews support a cautious, functional-outcomes framing. Pritchard, Nigro, Jacobson, and Mahone argue that neuropsychological assessment in ADHD may help identify comorbidities and risks for poor academic, social, vocational, and practical outcomes, but they also state that controlled studies directly examining whether neuropsychological assessment improves outcomes in children with ADHD are needed [2]. Thus, pediatric neuropsychologists do not appear to endorse a simple conclusion that NEPSY-II is ecologically valid and WISC is not. Rather, the more rigorous position is that both tests can contribute to a functional formulation, but neither has decisive ecological-validity superiority unless linked to real-world criteria.

The NEPSY-II may have stronger face validity for some everyday executive-control behaviors because it samples attention, inhibition, set shifting, social perception, memory, language, sensorimotor, and visuospatial domains [15], [16]. But face validity is not ecological validity. The WISC-V may seem less ecological because it samples decontextualized cognitive abilities, yet its working-memory and processing-speed scores can still be clinically meaningful for school accommodations and learning supports. The decisive missing evidence is direct prediction of classroom functioning, homework completion, peer functioning, adaptive functioning, or treatment response from NEPSY-II versus WISC-V scores in the same ADHD sample.

## Conflicting evidence and synthesis

The main conflict is between robust group-level neurocognitive findings and weak individual-level diagnostic utility. Meta-analyses show that ADHD groups differ from controls on working memory and executive-function tasks [13], [11], [12]. Yet guidelines and clinical-validity work indicate that such tests generally do not improve diagnostic accuracy and are not necessary or sufficient for ADHD diagnosis [1], [6]. This is not a contradiction; it reflects heterogeneity, comorbidity, and base-rate problems. ADHD is behaviorally defined, and working-memory impairment is common but not universal [14], [11].

A second conflict concerns WISC-V versus NEPSY-II. WISC-V is stronger for direct working-memory quantification, but NEPSY-II may be stronger for ADHD-relevant executive-control prediction. Newberry’s comparative modeling supports the latter for ADHD classification, because NEPSY-II/CPT variables were retained over WISC-V variables [3]. But because the retained NEPSY-II variables were not pure working-memory measures, that finding should not be converted into a claim that NEPSY-II is more sensitive to working-memory deficits.

A third conflict concerns ecological validity. Clinicians may perceive NEPSY-II as more ecologically valid because its subtests are embedded in broader developmental neuropsychological domains and may resemble everyday executive demands more than WISC-V tasks. The ecological-validity literature cautions that this is at most face validity unless real-world prediction is demonstrated [8]. Pediatric neuropsychology reviews favor functional assessment and comorbidity clarification but also acknowledge the absence of controlled outcome studies [2].

## Citation and influence signals

The most influential works in this evidence base are the ADHD executive-function and working-memory meta-analyses, because later WISC-V, NEPSY-II, and pediatric neuropsychology papers repeatedly cite them as the basis for expecting working-memory and executive-control weaknesses in ADHD. Willcutt et al. synthesized 83 studies and remains foundational for the claim that EF deficits are important but non-universal in ADHD [11]. Martinussen et al. is the seminal working-memory component meta-analysis and is repeatedly cited in later WISC-V and ADHD profile work [12], [5], [4]. Kasper et al. updated the working-memory evidence and is cited in recent discussions of ADHD working-memory heterogeneity and large-magnitude group differences [13], [14]. Pritchard et al.’s pediatric neuropsychology review is less highly cited than the major meta-analyses but is directly relevant to clinical utility and functional-outcome arguments; the source page reported 22 Scopus citations [2].

## Practical implications for an 8–12-year-old clinical/research protocol

For a publication-grade protocol, the assessment logic should be staged rather than test-centered:

1. **Diagnosis:** establish ADHD using DSM-5 criteria, impairment in multiple settings, and parent/teacher/school informants, not WISC-V or NEPSY-II scores [1].
2. **Cognitive characterization:** include WISC-V when the goal is to quantify intellectual profile, working memory, processing speed, and learning-related strengths/weaknesses [5], [4].
3. **Executive/attention profiling:** include NEPSY-II attention/executive subtests when the goal is to examine inhibition, set shifting, attention, and broader neuropsychological functions; interpret these as adjunctive functional hypotheses rather than diagnostic proof [7], [3].
4. **Working-memory sensitivity:** if only one of the two tests can be used for working-memory deficit detection, choose WISC-V. If the research aim is ADHD-relevant executive-control sensitivity rather than working memory specifically, NEPSY-II plus CPT-type measures may be more informative [3].
5. **Ecological validity:** avoid saying “NEPSY-II is more ecologically valid than WISC-V” unless the study includes real-world outcome criteria. Use more precise terms such as face validity, criterion validity, predictive validity, or incremental validity [8].

## Research gaps

The literature still lacks the specific evidence needed for a definitive WISC-V-versus-NEPSY-II conclusion in 8–12-year-old ADHD assessment:

- Direct ROC studies comparing WISC-V WMI/AWMI and NEPSY-II attention/executive/working-memory-relevant subtests in the same ADHD and non-ADHD children aged 8–12.
- Sensitivity, specificity, positive predictive value, negative predictive value, and likelihood ratios for clinically meaningful WISC-V and NEPSY-II cut scores.
- Incremental-validity studies testing whether NEPSY-II adds diagnostic or functional prediction beyond WISC-V, rating scales, school records, and CPT measures.
- Studies separating ADHD-only samples from ADHD+SLD, ADHD+anxiety, ASD+ADHD, and other comorbid profiles.
- Ecological-validity studies using real-world outcomes: classroom productivity, homework completion, adaptive functioning, peer functioning, academic accommodations, and treatment response.
- Longitudinal studies testing whether baseline WISC-V or NEPSY-II working-memory/executive scores predict later impairment or response to intervention.
- Factor-analytic and measurement-invariance work establishing that NEPSY-II and WISC-V constructs can be interpreted consistently across ADHD, control, sex, age, language, and comorbidity groups. This is not a trivial assumption: a 2024 WISC-V study found configural and metric invariance across ADHD and matched controls but not full scalar invariance, and concluded that WISC-V primary-index profile interpretations in ADHD should be made cautiously; a 2024 NEPSY-II factor analysis found partial support for a six-factor model at ages 7–12 but also cautioned against overinterpreting some conceptual-domain scores [19], [18].

## Conclusion

For children aged 8–12, **WISC-V is the better-supported choice for detecting and quantifying working-memory deficits**, but **NEPSY-II may be more informative for broader ADHD-related attention and executive-control profiling**. Neither test has adequate evidence to diagnose ADHD independently. The strongest current evidence supports a multi-method model: DSM-based diagnosis, rating scales and cross-setting impairment evidence, WISC-V for cognitive/working-memory profile, NEPSY-II for targeted executive/attention hypotheses, and real-world outcome measures if ecological validity is a research claim. Claims that NEPSY-II is more ecologically valid than WISC/WISC-V should be framed cautiously unless supported by explicit predictive or criterion-validity data [8], [2].
