# FutureMeds company profile and private-equity assessment (public information to 17 Nov 2025)

## Executive view

FutureMeds Ltd is a fast-growing, PE-backed pan-European clinical-trial site management organisation (SMO) / dedicated research site network. Public filings show the company scaled from £15.6m turnover in FY2023 to £27.0m in FY2024 (+73.1%), while maintaining a broadly stable operating margin of c.12.4% and positive net income [1]. The operating profile is attractive for growth private equity: a fragmented outsourced-pharma-services niche, a multi-country owned site footprint, relationships with major CROs and health systems, and evidence of repeat institutional sponsorship through HGL Private Capital and Phoenix Equity Partners [2][3][4][5].

The main limitations for an outside PE underwriting are disclosure gaps and post-transaction leverage opacity. Gross profit, EBITDA, operating cash flow/free cash flow, CAC/LTV, customer concentration by revenue, debt quantum, maturities and covenants are not publicly disclosed in the accessible filings and transaction releases. The 2025 Phoenix transaction also means FutureMeds is no longer an independent founder-only company: Companies House records show FutureMeds Ltd is controlled by FutureMeds Bidco Limited, itself controlled by FutureMeds Midco 2 Limited, after Phoenix’s July 2025 investment/acquisition [6][7][3][8].

**PE attractiveness: medium-high, but diligence-dependent.** Growth, profitability and strategic scarcity are strong; the key underwriting questions are quality of earnings, cash conversion, true debt burden after the Ares secured financing, site-level profitability, and sponsor/CRO concentration.

### Gross margin and EBITDA disclosure

**Gross profit / gross margin:** not publicly disclosed in the accessible Companies House-derived summary or transaction releases. The available extracted P&L begins at turnover and operating profit, and does not disclose cost of sales. Therefore, a true gross margin cannot be calculated from public data. A rough operating-margin proxy is available: **12.4% FY2024** and **12.5% FY2023** [1].

**EBITDA / adjusted EBITDA:** not publicly disclosed. FutureMeds’ public financial data include operating profit, profit before tax and net profit, but not depreciation/amortisation or management adjustments. Therefore EBITDA cannot be reliably reconstructed from the accessible filings. The best public profitability proxy is operating profit: **£3.35m in FY2024** and **£1.95m in FY2023** [1].

Public sources do not disclose operating cash flow, free cash flow, monthly burn rate, capex, acquisition cash flows or a liquidity runway. The balance sheet does disclose cash and net assets. This is a useful liquidity indicator but **not** a cash-conversion measure, because working capital, capex, acquisition and financing flows are not separately available in the accessible public extraction.

## Debt and leverage profile

The latest public debt picture is incomplete. As of the pre-17 Nov 2025 Companies House charge filings, FutureMeds Ltd had **5 registered charges**, of which **4 were outstanding** and **1 satisfied**. Four outstanding charges in favour of **Ares Management Limited** were created on **3–4 Nov 2025** and delivered on **4–11 Nov 2025**; one includes security over the FUTUREMEDS trade mark **UK00003546236**. An earlier **Barclays Bank PLC** fixed charge created on **16 Feb 2024** was satisfied on **31 Oct 2025** [18].

The public HTML charge summary does not disclose loan principal, maturity, pricing, amortisation, covenant package, sponsor equity cheque, or debt/EBITDA. Therefore, leverage cannot be calculated directly. From the FY2024 accounts, TheAccounts reports liabilities / total assets of **77.1%** and equity of **£5.869m**, which implies approximately **£19.76m total liabilities** and **£25.63m total assets**; this is an analyst-derived balance-sheet approximation and includes operating liabilities, not just financial debt [1].

The financing risk to underwrite is therefore not whether debt exists — secured Ares charges indicate post-transaction debt/security — but the quantum and covenant headroom relative to EBITDA and cash conversion, which are not publicly disclosed [18].

## Customer concentration and commercial relationships

No public source reviewed discloses revenue by customer, named sponsor concentration, top-10 customer percentage, contract backlog, cancellation exposure or pricing structure. Public language indicates the company serves **major global pharma sponsors and CROs** [2][3]. Named relationship evidence includes:

- **IQVIA Prime Site Network** designation, announced Nov 2024 [13].
- **Medpace Flagship Site Programme**, with FutureMeds described as the first private site network to join, announced May 2025 [19].
- **Fortrea Site Advisory Board**, joined Dec 2023 [20].
- Health-system / provider relationships including the **NHS** in the UK and **Viamed Salud** in Spain, referenced in FutureMeds’ 2025 operating update [10].
- A strategic UK public-private site partnership with **GP Pathfinder Clinics** at Willesden medical centre in London, announced May 2025 [21].

For PE diligence, customer concentration remains a material unknown. CRO partnerships can generate repeat study flow, but sponsor/CRO outsourcing can also be episodic, protocol-dependent and subject to trial delays or cancellations.

## Comparable-company and transaction context

Publicly available FutureMeds transaction data are more useful as **strategic validation** than as valuation benchmarks, because deal values and EBITDA multiples were not disclosed. The relevant peer set is clinical-trial site networks, SMOs, patient-recruitment platforms and outsourced-pharma-services platforms rather than general healthcare providers.

| Comparable / precedent category | Relevance to FutureMeds | Publicly observed information |
|---|---|---|
| HGL investment and exit in FutureMeds | Direct precedent for FutureMeds as a site-network platform | HGL invested from 2021, provided additional 2023 funding, and exited to Phoenix in 2025; sources reported **10x money multiple** on HGL’s initial investment, but no EV, revenue multiple or EBITDA multiple [12][5][11]. |
| Phoenix acquisition/investment in FutureMeds | Direct sponsor-to-sponsor / founder-partnership precedent | Phoenix and BDO describe the 2025 deal as an investment/acquisition in a pan-European SMO with 27–28 sites across seven countries; amount and valuation not disclosed [3][8]. |
| CRO / outsourced pharma services relationships | Demand-side validation | FutureMeds has formal relationships with IQVIA, Medpace and Fortrea, suggesting recognised quality and trial-flow relevance, but no revenue shares are disclosed [13][19][20]. |
| Site-network / patient-recruitment peers | Relevant valuation universe | Fortrea’s Site Advisory Board places FutureMeds alongside Circuit Clinical, Elligo Health Research, Javara Research, MD Anderson Center, ObjectiveHealth, Pratia S.A. and Velocity Clinical Research, representing 440 sites across more than 25 therapeutic areas and nine countries; the fetched public sources do not provide observed transaction multiples for these peers [20]. |

For valuation, the most defensible public anchor is therefore not a specific multiple but a range of scenarios based on FutureMeds’ FY2024 revenue and public operating profit. At £27.0m FY2024 revenue and £3.35m operating profit, even small changes in assumed EBITDA margin, normalised working capital and post-Phoenix debt produce large equity-value differences [1]. A PE buyer would likely value FutureMeds on maintainable EBITDA, with a growth premium for site scarcity and DCT capability, but would haircut for limited disclosed cash conversion, customer concentration uncertainty and post-transaction leverage opacity.

## Financial strength assessment

**Strengths**

1. **High revenue growth at scale:** FY2024 turnover grew 73.1% to £27.0m [1].
2. **Positive operating profitability:** operating profit rose 71.5% to £3.35m, with operating margin stable at c.12.4% [1].
3. **Institutional sponsor validation:** HGL backed the company from 2021 and exited to Phoenix in 2025; Phoenix cited conviction in the management team and outsourced-pharma-services niche [12][5][3].
4. **Multi-country owned footprint:** 27 sites across seven countries at deal announcement; latest website claims 29 dedicated sites and 70m+ prospective patient reach [2][14].
5. **Improved liquidity headline:** cash increased to £2.64m and current ratio improved to 1.17x at FY2024 [1].

**Weaknesses / unknowns**

1. **No gross margin or EBITDA disclosure:** limits comparability and quality-of-earnings assessment [1].
2. **No cash-flow statement available in accessible public extraction:** cash conversion, capex, working capital absorption and burn are not publicly assessable [1].
3. **High liability intensity:** liabilities/asset ratio 77.1% and interest cover only 2.11x in FY2024 before the November 2025 Ares charges [1][18].
4. **Customer concentration unknown:** public sources name important CRO/health-system relationships but not revenue exposure by customer [10][13][19][20].
5. **Post-Phoenix debt opacity:** Ares charges indicate secured financing, but loan amount, covenants and maturity are not publicly shown [18].

## Private-equity investment thesis

### Thesis

FutureMeds is an attractive **growth buy-and-build / platform investment** in outsourced clinical-trial infrastructure. It occupies a valuable position between CROs/pharma sponsors and patients: owning site operations, investigator relationships and patient access, while adding DCT/home-visit capability. The model benefits from sponsor pressure to accelerate recruitment, improve enrolment predictability and reduce underperforming site risk. Public metrics show rapid scale-up, positive profitability and formal recognition by major CROs [1][13][19][20].

For a new PE investor, the cleanest thesis is not a passive financial-engineering case; it is an operational growth case: expand sites in underpenetrated European geographies, deepen strategic CRO channels, raise site utilisation, standardise operating processes, and professionalise data/technology systems. Phoenix’s own stated value-creation agenda — expand geographic footprint, increase patient recruitment, deliver more trials and broaden therapeutic expertise — aligns with this [3].

### Value-creation levers

1. **Organic site rollout:** replicate the dedicated-site model in additional European cities, using the Romania, Lviv and London openings as templates [10][17][21].
2. **Buy-and-build:** continue acquiring local site networks and specialist capabilities, as with Amed and emovis [16][15].
3. **CRO channel expansion:** convert IQVIA, Medpace and Fortrea relationships into repeat preferred-site workflows, while avoiding overdependence on any one CRO [13][19][20].
4. **Therapeutic-area depth:** build higher-value specialist recruitment in oncology, haematology, rheumatology, neurology and dermatology [10][17][16].
5. **DCT / hybrid trials:** use emovis and FutureMeds @home to expand beyond physical-site catchments and improve patient convenience [15][10].
6. **Margin improvement:** centralise study start-up, recruitment analytics, quality systems, procurement and staffing utilisation; FY2024 wage growth outpaced revenue growth, so labour productivity is a key lever [1].
7. **Finance optimisation:** refinance or rationalise secured debt once post-Phoenix trading stabilises; interest cover already weakened in FY2024 [1][18].

### Likely exit routes and timeline

A plausible PE hold period is **4–6 years** from the July 2025 Phoenix transaction, implying a potential exit window around **2029–2031**, assuming continued site growth and proof of cash conversion. Likely exit routes are:

1. **Strategic CRO / pharma-services buyer:** CROs seeking more direct site access and recruitment control.
2. **Larger PE sponsor / sponsor-to-sponsor sale:** if FutureMeds reaches a larger EBITDA scale with diversified geography and customers.
3. **Healthcare / life-sciences services platform merger:** combination with patient-recruitment, eClinical, home-health trial or specialist site assets.
4. **IPO:** less likely unless revenue scale, governance, margin and cash-flow predictability improve materially.

### Valuation considerations

A buyer should avoid valuing FutureMeds solely on headline revenue growth. The core valuation bridge should start with FY2024 revenue of **£27.0m** and operating profit of **£3.35m**, then normalise for: EBITDA add-backs, site ramp costs, acquisitions, central overhead investment, working capital, capex, and post-transaction debt [1]. A premium multiple may be justified if diligence confirms durable CRO/sponsor relationships, high site utilisation, strong cash conversion and low customer concentration. Conversely, the multiple should be discounted if earnings depend on a small number of studies, if Ares leverage is high, or if new sites require long ramp periods [18].

### Top five risks

1. **Customer / contract concentration risk:** no public revenue concentration disclosure; trial flow may depend on a small number of CROs, sponsors or large protocols [2][3].
2. **Cash conversion and working-capital risk:** no public operating cash flow or FCF; rapid growth and multi-country expansion can absorb cash even when profitable [1].
3. **Leverage and covenant risk:** FY2024 interest cover fell to 2.11x, and November 2025 Ares secured charges suggest post-deal financing whose terms are not public [1][18].
4. **Execution risk in site rollout and integration:** FutureMeds has grown through acquisitions and new sites; quality, recruitment consistency, staff utilisation and regulatory compliance must remain controlled across jurisdictions [15][16][17].
5. **Regulatory / clinical-quality risk:** the business depends on GCP compliance, audits, patient safety and data quality; a major audit failure or adverse trial event could harm sponsor trust. FutureMeds cites successful FDA, Korean MoH, BVMA Germany and sponsor audits, but this also highlights the importance of ongoing compliance [10].

## Bottom line

FutureMeds has the hallmarks of a strong PE platform: rapid organic and inorganic growth, positive operating profit, a multi-country owned site base, DCT capability, and validation from HGL, Phoenix and major CRO relationships. Public data also show why diligence is essential: EBITDA, gross margin, cash conversion, customer concentration and post-deal leverage are not disclosed. On publicly available information alone, FutureMeds appears financially viable and strategically attractive, but its investment attractiveness depends on confirming that FY2024’s growth converts into sustainable EBITDA and cash flow under the 2025 Phoenix/Ares capital structure.
