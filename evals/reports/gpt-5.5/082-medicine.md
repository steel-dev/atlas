**Bottom line:** The most defensible outpatient bridge is **urgent 30-day mobile cardiac outpatient telemetry/mobile cardiac telemetry (MCOT/MCT) with automatic bradycardia/AV-block triggers and 24/7 attended monitoring**, ordered as medically necessary for **recurrent near-syncope/episodic dizziness with progressive PR prolongation and suspected intermittent high-grade AV block**. It is favored over a 24–48 h Holter because his episodes have recurred over **6 weeks**, not daily, and because MCT can auto-transmit significant arrhythmias to a staffed monitoring center, whereas patient-triggered loop/event monitors can miss sudden incapacitating events. If he develops documented Mobitz II, high-grade/advanced AV block, complete heart block, symptomatic bradycardia with instability, or syncope with serious conduction disease, the pathway should switch from outpatient monitoring to **same-day ED/telemetry and pacemaker/electrophysiology consultation**.

## Clinical interpretation of the presented data

This is not a benign “negative troponin” follow-up problem. The concerning features are **four ED visits in 6 weeks** for lightheadedness/fatigue, sinus bradycardia at **52 bpm**, PR prolongation from **200 ms to 240 ms in 3 weeks**, and “occasional dropped beats” that were seen clinically but not captured on a telemetry strip. ACC/AHA/HRS defines first-degree AV block/delay as **PR >200 ms with every P wave conducted**; high-grade/advanced AV block is **≥2 consecutive nonconducted P waves** with some AV conduction, and complete heart block is absence of AV conduction [1]. The current ECG documents first-degree AV delay, but the reported dropped beats raise concern for intermittent second-degree or high-grade AV block.

The metoprolol timing matters because it was started **8 weeks ago**, before the recurrent visits and PR prolongation. The 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS bradycardia guideline lists beta blockers among medications that can cause or contribute to AV block, while also noting that true durable reversal after stopping an offending drug is uncommon in case series [1]. Metoprolol prescribing information states that metoprolol slows the sinus rate and decreases AV nodal conduction; it also warns that bradycardia, sinus pause, heart block, and cardiac arrest have occurred, that patients with first-degree AV block or conduction disease are at increased risk, and that severe bradycardia should prompt dose reduction or discontinuation [6]. In practice, that supports **same-day clinician review of whether to hold or taper metoprolol and substitute a non–AV-nodal-blocking antihypertensive**, while still pursuing monitoring because drug association does not exclude intrinsic conduction disease.

## Recommended monitoring order

Order **30-day MCOT/MCT with auto-triggered bradycardia, pause, and AV-block detection; patient symptom button; daily/near-real-time review; and urgent notification parameters**. The order should explicitly request monitoring for:

- intermittent **Mobitz II second-degree AV block**;
- **2:1 AV block** or grouped dropped QRS complexes, because 2:1 block cannot be classified as Mobitz I versus II on surface ECG alone and the level of block may require further evaluation [1];
- **high-grade/advanced AV block**, defined by ACC/AHA/HRS as ≥2 consecutive nonconducted P waves with some AV conduction [1];
- **third-degree/complete AV block**;
- symptomatic bradycardia, pauses, and symptom–rhythm correlation during lightheadedness/fatigue.

This order aligns with the ACC/AHA/HRS Class I principle that rhythm monitoring is useful for documented or suspected bradycardia/conduction disorders to correlate symptoms with rhythm, with monitor type chosen by symptom frequency and patient preference [1]. It also aligns with syncope guidance: for selected ambulatory patients with suspected arrhythmic syncope, Holter, transtelephonic monitoring, external loop recorder, patch recorder, and mobile cardiac outpatient telemetry can be useful, but monitor choice depends on frequency/nature of events and likelihood of arrhythmia [4].

A trial cited in the syncope guideline and summarized in CMS coverage evidence supports the preference for MCT when the question is intermittent arrhythmia capture: in **266 patients**, diagnosis occurred in **88% with mobile outpatient telemetry vs 75% with external loop recorder** (p=0.008), and in the syncope/presyncope subgroup the diagnostic yield was **89% vs 69%** (p=0.008) [4]. CMS LCD L39492’s evidence summary also reports clinically significant arrhythmias in **41% with MCOT vs 15% with external loop monitor** (P<0.001), and among prior syncope/presyncope patients, **52% vs 16%** (P<0.001) [2].

## Prior authorization pathway and wording

The authorization request should be framed as a **medical-necessity request for attended 30-day mobile cardiac telemetry**, not as convenience monitoring. The core documentation should include:

1. **Diagnosis/indication language**: recurrent near-syncope/episodic dizziness and fatigue with suspected intermittent bradyarrhythmia/high-grade AV block; progressive PR prolongation from **200 ms to 240 ms over 3 weeks**; heart rate **52 bpm**; dropped beats observed but not captured.
2. **Why monitoring changes treatment**: detection of Mobitz II, high-grade AV block, complete heart block, or symptomatic bradycardia would trigger urgent electrophysiology/pacemaker evaluation; absence of correlation would redirect evaluation away from pacing.
3. **Why Holter is inappropriate**: symptoms are recurrent over **6 weeks** but not daily/near-daily; CMS LCD L39492 states 24–48 h monitoring is most appropriate for daily or near-daily symptoms, while longer monitoring should match signs/symptoms and device indications [2].
4. **Why patient activation is insufficient**: the target arrhythmia may cause sudden presyncope/syncope, and syncope guidance notes external loop recorders can be limited when patients are temporarily incapacitated [4]. CMS NCD 20.15 recognizes automatically/event-activated devices that do not require patient input and can be worn usually up to **30 days** [5].
5. **Why attended MCT is safer**: the 2018 bradycardia guideline describes mobile outpatient telemetry as recording/transmitting up to **30 days** with significant arrhythmias auto-transmitted to a **24 h/day technician-attended central station** [1]. CMS LCD L39492 defines MCT as a service with a **24 h/day, 7 d/week receiving station**, immediate physician access for out-of-parameter data, and ability to contact emergency facilities [2].
6. **Social-risk modifiers**: he lives alone, drives daily, and lacks nearby family support, so real-time alerting is clinically relevant while awaiting definitive cardiology/EP assessment.

If the payer denies MCT, the appeal should cite CMS LCD L39492’s coverage basis for unexplained near-syncope/episodic dizziness and suspected bradycardia [2], CMS NCD 20.15’s recognition of auto-trigger event monitors up to **30 days** [5], and the 2017 ACC/AHA/HRS syncope guideline’s evidence that MCT had higher diagnostic yield than external loop recording in suspected intermittent arrhythmias, including **89% vs 69%** in syncope/presyncope [4]. If the payer insists on coding categories rather than clinical descriptions, older CMS LCD L34636 grouped memory-loop recordings under **93268/93270/93271/93272**, up-to-48 h recordings under **93224/93225/93226/93227/93228/93229**, and >48 h to 21-day patch recordings under **0295T/0296T/0297T/0298T**, but current code selection should be verified against the payer’s current CPT/HCPCS rules [7].

## Safety plan until definitive evaluation

Because his symptoms are recurrent and conduction disease may be intermittent, the bridge should be more than “wait three months.” Practical safety steps supported by the guideline logic are:

- **Same-day primary care or cardiology triage call** to document the conduction concern, request expedited EP/cardiology review, adjust AV-nodal-blocking medication, and place the MCT order.
- **Medication review immediately**, especially metoprolol. ACC/AHA/HRS lists beta blockers among drugs that can cause AV block [1], and the metoprolol label warns that patients with first-degree AV block/conduction disorders are at increased risk of bradycardia/heart block and that severe bradycardia should prompt dose reduction or stopping [6].
- **No driving until the presyncope/lightheadedness risk is clarified and controlled**, especially because he drives daily and lives alone. The syncope guideline says clinicians should know local driving laws/restrictions and discuss them; untreated syncope may disqualify driving, and syncope without prodrome/warning carries higher public-harm risk than typical vasovagal syncope [4].
- **Clear escalation instructions**: call emergency services for syncope, near-syncope while driving, chest pain, dyspnea, confusion, falls/injury, sustained severe weakness, or monitor-company urgent alert.
- **Device escalation protocol**: the monitoring company and ordering clinician should have explicit parameters for urgent physician notification/ED referral if MCT detects high-grade AV block, complete heart block, long symptomatic pauses, or profound bradycardia. This fits CMS’s definition of attended monitoring with immediate physician access and emergency-facility contact capability [2].

For the current ECG alone—PR **240 ms** with sinus rhythm and stable blood pressure—permanent pacing is not automatically indicated. The concern is the **trajectory plus symptoms plus dropped beats**. The appropriate bridge is therefore aggressive rhythm capture with attended 30-day MCT, rapid medication review, driving restriction counseling, and predefined triggers for immediate ED/EP escalation.
