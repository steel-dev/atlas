**Bottom line:** With no forum supplied, the governing baseline is the common-law respondeat-superior framework in Restatement (Third) of Agency § 7.07 and Restatement (Second) of Agency §§ 220 and 228, applied under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 56(a). On this record, the company should **not receive summary judgment**: app-mandated routes, no pricing discretion, company-branded gear, automatic suspension after more than two consecutive declined orders, automated acceptance-rate/account-standing warnings, quota pressure, and 55 hours of weekly platform work create a genuine dispute that the courier was an employee/servant acting within the scope of delivery work for the company’s benefit. The pedestrian’s cross-motion should also fail because self-set scheduling, a personal vehicle, the independent-contractor agreement, required personal liability insurance, the lapsed policy, and occasional self-paid helper permit competing inferences. Respondeat superior can attach despite the insurance lapse and lost training records; those facts mainly affect contract compliance, coverage/indemnity, direct-negligence, or spoliation issues unless the factfinder treats them as part of control or risk allocation.

## Governing authority and summary-judgment lens

No jurisdiction is specified. As of June 23, 2026, the controlling baseline is therefore the common-law agency rule, not a forum-specific employment statute. Restatement (Third) of Agency § 7.07 states that an employer is vicariously liable for a tort committed by an employee acting within the scope of employment; an employee acts within scope when performing assigned work or a course of conduct subject to the employer’s control, and is outside scope when acting in an independent course of conduct not intended to serve an employer purpose. Section 7.07(3)(a) defines an employee as an agent whose principal controls or has the right to control the manner and means of the agent’s work [1]. Restatement (Second) of Agency § 220 likewise defines a servant as a person employed to perform services in another’s affairs whose physical conduct is controlled or subject to the other’s right of control [2].

The procedural rule is Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 56(a): summary judgment is proper only when the movant shows that there is no genuine dispute as to any material fact and that the movant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law [3]. Anderson v. Liberty Lobby frames “genuine” and “material” disputes in the familiar way: a dispute is genuine if a reasonable jury could return a verdict for the nonmovant, and material if it could affect the outcome under governing law, a formulation also applied in the platform-driver summary-judgment context in Narayanasamy v. Issa [4]. Thus the question is not whether the pedestrian has conclusively proved employee status and scope, but whether the company has eliminated all reasonable contrary inferences.

## Employee/servant status under the control test

Restatement (Second) § 220(2) requires a multi-factor control analysis, including: control over details of the work; whether the worker has a distinct occupation or business; whether the occupation is usually performed under the employer’s direction or by an unsupervised specialist; skill required; who supplies tools, instrumentalities, and place of work; length of engagement; method of payment; whether the work is part of the employer’s regular business; the parties’ belief about the relationship; and whether the principal is in business [2]. No single factor controls. Courier-specific common-law guidance similarly treats insurance/expenses, freedom to accept or reject assignments, freedom to negotiate fees, and self-established routes as independent-contractor indicators, while treating company-set pay, required acceptance, set availability, company-established route/sequence/priority, and authority to ensure customer requirements as strong employment indicators for on-demand couriers [5].

### Factors supporting employee/servant status

The strongest employee-status facts concern operational and algorithmic control, not the contract label. The courier wore company-branded gear, followed app-mandated routes, had no pricing discretion, and faced automatic suspension after declining more than two consecutive orders. The company also sent automated warnings that acceptance rates affected account standing. Those facts go directly to § 220(2)(a)’s control-over-details factor and § 220(2)(h)’s regular-business factor because the courier was performing food delivery for a food-delivery platform.

Current gig-platform cases make those facts material. In Razak v. Uber Technologies, Inc., the Third Circuit reversed summary judgment on UberBLACK driver classification because factual disputes existed over control; relevant evidence included Uber setting pay, directing drop-offs, deactivating drivers below a 4.7-star rating, limiting consecutive hours, prohibiting fares outside Uber, disputes over cancellation punishment, and drivers’ economic dependence [6]. In O’Connor v. Uber Technologies, Inc., the Northern District of California treated unilateral fare setting, including surge pricing, and unilateral termination/deactivation rights as evidence relevant to control; it stated that surge pricing was unilaterally set by Uber, not negotiated by drivers, and that drivers accepted Uber’s offered piece rate or did not [7]. Lawson v. Grubhub, Inc. cuts both ways for this fact pattern: the Ninth Circuit affirmed that Grubhub’s lack of control over the manner and means supported independent-contractor status under California Borello, but it also recognized that regular-business factors favored employee status for food-delivery work [8].

Here, the platform-control evidence is stronger than the independent-contractor facts in Lawson because this record includes app-mandated routes, no pricing discretion, branded gear, automatic suspension after more than two consecutive declined orders, account-standing warnings tied to acceptance rates, and quota pressure. The courier’s 55 hours per week also supports economic dependence and length/regularity of engagement under § 220(2)(f), unlike a sporadic driver who works only a few hours weekly. The fact that the courier was three deliveries behind quota when the accident occurred further supports an inference that the company structured the work to exert performance pressure.

### Factors supporting independent-contractor status

The company still has substantial contrary evidence. The courier set his own schedule, used a personal vehicle, signed an independent-contractor agreement excluding employee status, was required to maintain liability insurance, and occasionally hired his teenage son for large orders and paid him directly. The personal vehicle and required insurance bear on § 220(2)(e), because the worker supplied major instrumentalities and assumed some operating expense. Black v. Shaffer/DoorDash treated a delivery driver’s own car, fuel, and insurance as part of the tools/equipment factor favoring independent-contractor status [9]. The self-set schedule also matters: Razak recognized that drivers’ ability to drive as little or as much as they wanted favored independent-contractor status, even though other control evidence created disputes [6].

The independent-contractor agreement is relevant under § 220(2)(i), the parties’ belief, but it is not dispositive. O’Connor, applying the common-law/Borello approach, states that the label placed by the parties on their relationship is not dispositive and that subterfuges are not countenanced [7]. The specific agreement here both excludes employee status and requires courier liability insurance; those terms support the company’s classification argument but do not erase evidence that the platform controlled routing, pricing, acceptance consequences, and account standing.

## Scope of employment, frolic/detour, and employer benefit

Restatement (Third) § 7.07(2) treats conduct as within scope when the employee performs assigned work or engages in a course of conduct subject to employer control, and outside scope when the employee acts in an independent course of conduct not intended to serve an employer purpose [1]. Restatement (Second) § 228 asks whether the conduct is of the kind the servant is employed to perform, occurs substantially within authorized time and space limits, and is actuated at least in part by a purpose to serve the master; § 229 treats acts of the same general nature or incidental to authorized conduct as within scope and considers time, place, purpose, similarity, expectedness, instrumentalities, departures from normal method, and seriousness of the departure [2].

The June 8, 2024 tort predicate is concrete: the courier’s personal vehicle collided with a pedestrian, and the pedestrian suffered a fractured ankle requiring surgery. That is the negligent-conduct/injury predicate for respondeat superior; it is distinct from direct claims such as negligent hiring, training, supervision, or app-design negligence, because respondeat superior makes the employer answerable for the employee’s negligent conduct rather than for the employer’s own negligent hiring, training, or supervision [2].

On the stated facts, the accident occurred within scope if the courier is found to be an employee. The courier was actively completing a platform order, was rushing during a surge-pricing period, had no pricing discretion, was three deliveries behind quota, and had received automated warnings that acceptance rates affected account standing. Those facts show both assigned delivery work and at least partial motivation to serve the company by completing platform orders and maintaining platform service levels. The courier’s simultaneous personal motive to earn delivery compensation does not defeat scope because § 228 requires only that the conduct be actuated at least in part by a purpose to serve the employer [2].

The frolic/detour and commuting doctrines do not materially help the company on this record. A detour is a minor departure still within scope, while a frolic is a major departure for the employee’s own benefit; ordinary commuting generally suspends the employment relationship because the employee is not rendering service [10]. This collision did not occur during an ordinary commute to or from work and no personal errand is stated. Rushing to complete an active delivery order during surge pricing is negligent performance of the assigned delivery task, not a personal frolic, unless additional route-deviation facts show a major independent personal departure.

## Insurance lapse, lost training records, and helper facts

The insurance lapse does not bar respondeat superior as a matter of law. The company required couriers to maintain liability insurance, and this courier’s policy lapsed two weeks before June 8, 2024. Black v. Shaffer/DoorDash treated a driver’s own insurance as part of the tools/equipment factor favoring independent-contractor status and held that contract provisions requiring the contractor to maintain insurance and indemnify DoorDash did not, by themselves, show DoorDash assumed a specific tort duty to roadway victims [9]. Applied here, the lapse primarily affects the courier’s contractual compliance, indemnity obligations, and insurance coverage. It can also be considered as part of the company’s risk-allocation structure and the worker-supplied-tools factor, but it does not defeat vicarious liability if a jury finds employee status and scope.

The lost training records are likewise not a respondeat-superior bar. Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 37(e) governs lost electronically stored information that should have been preserved in anticipation or conduct of litigation, was lost because reasonable steps were not taken, and cannot be restored or replaced. If prejudice is found, the court may order measures no greater than necessary to cure the prejudice; adverse presumptions, adverse-inference jury instructions, dismissal, or default require a finding that the party acted with intent to deprive another party of the information’s use in litigation [11]. Training records lost during a system migration, while an onboarding checklist references completion of safety protocols, may support discovery into training, direct negligent training/supervision, or a limited curative measure if prejudice is shown. Without an intent-to-deprive finding, Rule 37(e) does not supply an automatic adverse inference that the missing records would prove unsafe training [11].

The teenage-son helper fact is peripheral. Restatement (Third) § 3.15 defines a subagent as a person appointed by an agent to perform functions the agent consented to perform for the principal, with the appointing agent responsible to the principal; an agent may appoint a subagent only with actual or apparent authority [1]. The courier’s occasional practice of hiring his teenage son directly for large orders may cut against personal-service control or show unauthorized delegation, depending on whether the platform permitted helpers. But the son was not involved in the stated June 8, 2024 collision, so the helper fact does not remove the courier’s active delivery trip from scope or break causation for the pedestrian’s injury.

## Summary-judgment disposition

The undisputed record facts include the June 8, 2024 collision with a pedestrian; the fractured ankle requiring surgery; the courier’s 55-hour workweek; self-set schedule; personal vehicle; branded gear; app-mandated routes; no pricing discretion; independent-contractor agreement; required liability insurance; policy lapse two weeks before June 8, 2024; automatic suspension after more than two consecutive declined orders; automated warnings about acceptance rates and account standing; three-delivery quota deficit; active order during surge pricing; lost training records during system migration with an onboarding checklist referencing safety-protocol completion; and occasional self-paid teenage-son assistance.

Those facts create a genuine dispute over employee status because reasonable jurors could weigh the same § 220 factors differently. A jury could find employee status from detailed app control over route, pricing, acceptance consequences, branding, quota pressure, and economic dependence. A jury could also find independent-contractor status from self-scheduling, personal vehicle and insurance obligations, contractual classification, and informal helper use. Platform-control authorities such as Razak and O’Connor show that algorithmic pay, routing, cancellation/deactivation, and account-standing controls are material enough to defeat summary judgment when competing inferences remain [6] [7]. Lawson and Black show why the company’s contrary evidence is serious, particularly where workers supply vehicles/insurance and retain schedule flexibility [8] [9].

The scope and benefit issues are less balanced but still depend on the employee-status finding. If the courier was an employee, the active delivery during surge pricing, quota deficit, no pricing discretion, and acceptance-rate/account-standing warnings place the trip within assigned delivery work and at least partly in service of the company. The company therefore should be **denied summary judgment** on respondeat superior. The pedestrian’s cross-motion should also be **denied** because the independent-contractor evidence permits a reasonable contrary inference on employee status. Direct negligence, spoliation sanctions, indemnity, and coverage should be addressed separately; none of the insurance lapse, missing training records, or helper facts eliminates vicarious liability at summary judgment under the common-law control and scope tests.
