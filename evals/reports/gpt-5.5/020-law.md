# Independent directors under Nasdaq listing standards

## Bottom line

Under Nasdaq Rule 5605(a)(2), an **Independent Director** is a director who is **not** an Executive Officer or employee of the listed company and who has no other relationship that, in the board’s judgment, would interfere with the director’s independent judgment in carrying out director responsibilities [1]. Nasdaq’s framework therefore has two layers: (1) a **board judgment / affirmative determination** that the director has no impairing relationship, and (2) a set of **objective disqualifying relationships** that automatically prevent a director from being treated as independent [1].

For Nasdaq-listed operating companies, the practical rule is that the board generally must be **majority independent**: “a majority of the board of directors must be comprised of Independent Directors” as defined in Rule 5605(a)(2) [1]. Nasdaq’s Rule 5600 corporate-governance requirements apply to Nasdaq “Companies,” and Rule 5005 defines a “Company” as an issuer of a security listed or applying to list on Nasdaq [2]. Several categories of issuers are exempt or may follow alternative regimes, so the majority-independent-board requirement is broad but not universal [1].

## Who qualifies as an independent director

A person qualifies as a Nasdaq independent director only if all of the following are true:

1. **The person is not an Executive Officer of the company.** Nasdaq defines “Executive Officer” by reference to Exchange Act Rule 16a-1(f) [1].
2. **The person is not an employee of the company.** Current employees are outside the definition, and former employees are subject to a three-year look-back disqualification [1].
3. **The board affirmatively determines independence.** Nasdaq’s interpretive material states that the board is responsible for making an affirmative determination that no relationship exists that would impair independence [1].
4. **None of Nasdaq’s objective disqualifications applies.** Rule 5605(a)(2) lists relationships that “shall not” be considered independent [1].

Nasdaq also clarifies important scope points. For Rule 5605(a)(2), “Company” includes controlled and consolidated parents and subsidiaries, but not entities reflected solely as investments in the company’s financial statements [1]. A “Family Member” includes a spouse, parents, children, siblings, specified in-law relationships, and anyone sharing the director’s home other than domestic employees [1]. Stock ownership alone is not one of Nasdaq’s objective disqualifying factors; Nasdaq states that ownership of company stock by itself does not preclude a board independence finding [1].

## Who cannot be treated as independent

The following relationships disqualify a director from being counted as independent under Nasdaq Rule 5605(a)(2):

| Disqualification category | Nasdaq rule substance |
|---|---|
| Current or recent employment | A director who is, or during the past three years was, employed by the company is not independent [1]. |
| Compensation over threshold | A director, or a Family Member of the director, who accepted more than **$120,000** from the company during any 12 consecutive months within the three years before the independence determination is not independent, except for specified items such as board or committee fees, compensation to a Family Member who is a non-executive employee, tax-qualified retirement-plan benefits, and nondiscretionary compensation [1]. |
| Family tie to executive officer | A director who is a Family Member of someone who is, or during the past three years was, employed by the company as an Executive Officer is not independent [1]. |
| Material business payments | A director who is, or whose Family Member is, a partner, controlling shareholder, or Executive Officer of an organization that made payments to or received payments from the company for property or services in the current or any of the past three fiscal years exceeding the greater of **5% of the recipient’s consolidated gross revenues** or **$200,000** is not independent, subject to exceptions for payments arising solely from investments in company securities and payments under nondiscretionary charitable contribution matching programs [1]. |
| Compensation-committee interlock | A director, or a Family Member of the director, who is employed as an Executive Officer of another entity is not independent if, at any time during the past three years, any Executive Officer of the company served on that other entity’s compensation committee [1]. |
| Auditor relationship | A director, or a Family Member of the director, who is a current partner of the company’s outside auditor, or who was a partner or employee of the outside auditor and worked on the company’s audit during any of the past three years, is not independent [1]. |
| Investment-company interested-person status | For an investment company, Nasdaq uses a different test: instead of the preceding categories, a director who is an “interested person” under Investment Company Act § 2(a)(19), other than solely by board or committee service, is not independent [1]. |

Nasdaq’s interpretive material adds several nuances. The three-year look-back periods in the employment, family-executive, compensation-interlock, and auditor provisions begin when the relevant relationship ceases [1]. Interim service as an Executive Officer for no longer than one year does not automatically disqualify the director after the interim service ends, although the director is not independent while serving as interim officer and the board must still assess whether the former service or compensation impairs judgment [1].

## Committee-specific independence requirements

The general Rule 5605(a)(2) definition is the baseline, but Nasdaq imposes stricter independence standards for key committees.

- **Audit committee.** Each company’s audit committee must have at least three members, each of whom must be an Independent Director under Rule 5605(a)(2), meet Exchange Act Rule 10A-3 independence criteria, not have participated in preparing the company’s or a current subsidiary’s financial statements during the past three years, and be able to read and understand fundamental financial statements [1]. Nasdaq’s interpretive material explains that Rule 10A-3 generally bars audit-committee members from accepting consulting, advisory, or other compensatory fees from the company other than board-service fees and from being affiliated persons of the company [1].
- **Compensation committee.** Each company must have a compensation committee of at least two members, and each member must be an Independent Director under Rule 5605(a)(2). In affirmatively determining independence for compensation-committee service, the board must consider factors relevant to independence from management, including compensation sources and affiliate relationships [1]. Smaller reporting companies still must have a compensation committee of at least two Independent Directors, but Nasdaq relieves them of some additional compensation-committee eligibility requirements [1].

These committee rules matter because a director may satisfy the general board-independence definition but still fail a stricter committee-specific test, particularly for audit-committee service [1].

## Companies required to have independent directors

### General rule: Nasdaq-listed companies must have a majority-independent board

Rule 5605(b)(1) requires a majority of the board to be Independent Directors [1]. Rule 5605(b)(2) also requires Independent Directors to hold regularly scheduled executive sessions at which only Independent Directors are present, and Nasdaq’s interpretive material contemplates such sessions at least twice a year [1].

Because Rule 5005 defines “Company” as an issuer of a security listed or applying to list on Nasdaq, and the Rule 5600 Series sets corporate-governance requirements for Nasdaq Companies, the requirement generally applies to Nasdaq-listed companies rather than to only one Nasdaq market tier [2]. The requirement is therefore best understood as applying broadly to Nasdaq-listed operating issuers unless an exemption, phase-in, or alternative regime applies [1].

### Companies subject to phase-in rather than permanent exemption

Some companies are not immediately required to satisfy the full majority-independent-board requirement on day one but must phase into compliance. Nasdaq Rule 5615 provides phase-in treatment for categories including companies listing in connection with an initial public offering, companies emerging from bankruptcy, companies transferring from other markets or registering under the Exchange Act in specified circumstances, companies listing in connection with carve-out or spin-off transactions, and companies ceasing to qualify as smaller reporting companies, foreign private issuers, or controlled companies [1]. For example, an IPO company has **12 months from the date its securities first trade on Nasdaq** to comply with the majority-independent-board requirement [1]. These are timing accommodations, not permanent exclusions from the independence framework [1].

### Main exemptions or alternative regimes

The principal categories not fully subject to the majority-independent-board rule are:

| Category | Treatment under Nasdaq rules |
|---|---|
| Asset-backed and other passive issuers | Certain asset-backed issuers and passive issuers such as trusts with no board and only passive asset-holding functions are exempt from specified corporate-governance requirements, including the majority-independent-board requirement [1]. |
| Cooperatives without publicly traded common stock | Cooperatives without publicly traded common stock receive exemptions from certain Rule 5600 requirements [1]. |
| Foreign private issuers | A foreign private issuer may generally follow home-country practice instead of many Nasdaq corporate-governance requirements, subject to disclosure and audit-committee requirements [1]. |
| Limited partnerships | Limited partnerships are generally not subject to Rule 5600, although a corporate general partner must have enough independent directors to satisfy the audit-committee requirement [1]. |
| Management investment companies | Registered management investment companies are exempt from the Rule 5605(b) majority-independent-board requirement; business development companies that are not registered under the Investment Company Act must comply with Rule 5600 [1]. |
| Issuers listing only specified non-common securities | Companies whose only listed securities are non-voting preferred stock, debt securities, or derivative securities are exempt from Rule 5605(b). If the company also lists common stock, voting preferred stock, or their equivalents, the Rule 5600 Series applies [1]. |
| Controlled companies | A controlled company—one of which more than 50% of voting power is held by an individual, group, or another company—is exempt from the majority-independent-board requirement, though it remains subject to the executive-session requirement for independent directors and other applicable rules such as audit-committee obligations [1]. |

## Practical interpretation

Nasdaq independence is not simply a status label; it is a governance compliance tool. The disqualifications determine whether a director may be counted toward Nasdaq’s majority-independent-board requirement and independent-committee requirements. A non-independent person is not necessarily barred from sitting on the board altogether, but the company cannot count that person as independent for Rule 5605 purposes [1].

In priority order, the independence analysis should be applied as follows: first, confirm the issuer is a Nasdaq “Company” subject to Rule 5600 and not within an exemption or alternative regime; second, determine whether the board must be majority independent immediately or under a phase-in schedule; third, test each director against Rule 5605(a)(2)’s baseline definition and objective disqualifications; and finally, apply stricter committee-specific rules for audit and compensation committee service [1], [2].
