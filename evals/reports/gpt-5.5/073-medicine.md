# Monitoring and disposition for recurrent near-syncope with suspected intermittent bradyarrhythmia

## Bottom line for this patient

A 68-year-old man with **recurrent near-syncope/presyncope over one month**, witnessed transient episodes of pallor and inability to speak, **sinus bradycardia around 52 bpm**, **variable PR interval up to 240 ms**, and **occasional dropped beats** should **not be discharged to routine cardiology follow-up in six weeks while an insurance appeal for Holter monitoring is pending**. The guideline-consistent safe plan is to arrange **rhythm monitoring before discharge**, either from the ED or an observation unit. Given that the events are intermittent rather than daily and the concern is clinically important bradyarrhythmia/AV block, the preferred outpatient device is **longer-duration external ambulatory ECG monitoring**—a **7–14-day adhesive patch monitor or external event/loop monitor**, and **mobile cardiac outpatient telemetry (MCOT/MCT)** if real-time detection and action are needed. If such monitoring cannot be placed immediately, the alternative is **observation/admission with continuous telemetry**, not simple discharge awaiting insurance approval.

This recommendation follows two connected principles in the ACC/AHA/HRS guidance: (1) suspected bradycardia or conduction disease requires **correlation of symptoms with ECG rhythm**, and ambulatory ECG monitoring is Class I when the ED ECG does not capture a definitive symptomatic event; and (2) syncope/near-syncope disposition depends on whether a serious cardiac or conduction condition is suspected or identified, and on whether rapid diagnostic evaluation can actually occur [1][2].

## Why monitoring must be arranged before discharge

The key unresolved question is whether his transient neurologic-appearing episodes and fatigue/near-syncope coincide with **sinus pauses, clinically significant bradycardia, or intermittent AV block**. The 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS bradycardia guideline states that, for documented or suspected bradycardia or conduction disorders, **cardiac rhythm monitoring is useful to establish correlation between heart rate/conduction abnormality and symptoms**, with device choice based on symptom frequency, symptom character, and patient factors [1]. The syncope guideline likewise states that cardiac monitor selection should be based on the **frequency and nature** of events and that external monitors—including Holter, external loop recorder, patch recorder, and mobile cardiac outpatient telemetry—are useful in selected ambulatory patients with suspected arrhythmic syncope [2].

Here, repeated ED visits with normal troponins and chest imaging do not answer that question. The syncope guideline notes that broad routine laboratory testing has low diagnostic yield, and troponin is mainly useful when myocardial infarction is suspected; normal CBC/electrolytes/TSH/creatinine/troponins/CXR reduce competing explanations but do **not** exclude intermittent arrhythmia when the history and ECG suggest it [2].

Insurance denial therefore changes the clinical plan. Once ambulatory rhythm monitoring is medically indicated, lack of outpatient authorization is not a reason to discharge without monitoring. It converts the disposition choice to one of the following:

1. **Best outpatient-safe option:** ED/observation-unit placement of an external ambulatory monitor before discharge, with expedited cardiology/electrophysiology follow-up in days rather than weeks.
2. **If real-time risk is concerning:** MCOT/MCT before discharge, because it continuously records/transmits up to 30 days, detects significant arrhythmias automatically, and uses 24-hour technician-attended central monitoring for high-risk patients or symptoms too brief/subtle for patient activation [1].
3. **If no monitor can be placed now, or if high-grade block cannot be excluded:** observation/admission with continuous telemetry. The syncope guideline makes continuous ECG monitoring Class I for hospitalized patients admitted for syncope evaluation when a cardiac cause is suspected [2].

## Which monitor fits the symptom pattern?

His events are recurrent over a month but not documented as daily. That makes a routine 24–48-hour Holter a lower-yield choice than longer external monitoring. A Holter could be acceptable only if episodes are occurring daily or nearly daily.

| Symptom pattern / clinical need | Preferred rhythm strategy | Supporting rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Daily or near-daily symptoms | **24–48-hour Holter** | ACC/AHA/HRS bradycardia guidance identifies Holter as appropriate for frequent/daily symptoms; ISHNE-HRS lists standard Holter duration as 24–48 h [1][3]. |
| Symptoms every several days to weeks; likely recurrence within 2–6 weeks | **External patch monitor, external loop/event recorder, or event monitor**, commonly **7–14 days** for patch or up to **30 days / 1–4 weeks** for loop/event or MCT | Longer external monitors have higher diagnostic yield than 24–48-hour Holter because of longer monitoring; ACC/AHA/HRS describes patch monitors often 2–14 days and external loop recorders over weeks to months for symptoms likely to recur within 2–6 weeks [1][2]. |
| Symptoms are brief, subtle, associated with possible incapacitation, or require rapid action | **Mobile cardiac outpatient telemetry (MCOT/MCT)** | MCOT/MCT provides real-time analysis/transmission and 24-hour attended monitoring; the syncope guideline cites higher diagnostic yield than external loop recorder in a syncope/presyncope subgroup, 89% vs 69% in one RCT [1][2]. |
| Rare recurrent unexplained syncope after nondiagnostic external monitoring, especially events >30 days apart | **Implantable cardiac monitor / implantable loop recorder (ICM/ILR)** | The bradycardia guideline considers ICM reasonable when symptoms are infrequent, more than 30 days apart, and initial noninvasive evaluation is nondiagnostic; syncope guidance supports ICM for selected suspected arrhythmic syncope [1][2]. |

The ISHNE-HRS consensus quantifies why duration matters: estimated syncope diagnostic yield rises from **1–5%** for 24–48-hour Holter to **5–10%** for 3–7-day external/MCT monitoring, **15–25%** for 1–4-week external/MCT monitoring, and **30–50%** for implantable loop monitoring [3]. Therefore, for this patient’s non-daily recurrent events, a **7–14-day patch or 30-day event/loop monitor** is a better first external strategy than waiting for a denied Holter; **MCOT** is favored if clinicians judge that immediate notification of high-grade AV block, pauses, or profound bradycardia is needed.

## ECG interpretation and why the tracing is not benign

The ED ECG does not by itself prove the mechanism of the home episodes, but it is abnormal enough to require prompt rhythm documentation and possibly monitored evaluation.

| ECG finding in case | Guideline-grounded interpretation | Disposition implication |
|---|---|---|
| Sinus rate ~52 bpm | Adult bradycardia is often defined as HR <60 bpm; the bradycardia guideline uses sinus rate <50 bpm and/or pauses >3 s as possible sinus node dysfunction components, but sinus bradycardia alone does not diagnose SND [1]. | Mild sinus bradycardia alone in a stable patient does not mandate admission or pacing, but becomes significant if temporally linked to presyncope/syncope. |
| PR 180–240 ms | First-degree AV delay is PR >200 ms with 1:1 conduction; this tracing crosses from normal PR into first-degree AV delay range [1]. | Variable PR suggests dynamic AV nodal/conduction behavior; not enough to discharge without monitoring when symptoms recur. |
| Occasional dropped beats | Second-degree AV block means atrial impulses are intermittently not conducted. Mobitz I has progressive/inconstant PR prolongation before a dropped QRS; Mobitz II has nonconducted P waves without progressive PR prolongation and constant PR intervals [1]. | If the dropped beats are Mobitz I and symptoms are not linked, outpatient monitoring may be reasonable; if Mobitz II/high-grade block is present or cannot be excluded, admission/urgent cardiology is warranted. |

In practical terms: the described “PR varies 180–240 ms with dropped beats” could be **Mobitz I/Wenckebach** if PR intervals progressively lengthen before a dropped QRS, but could represent more dangerous AV conduction disease if nonconducted P waves occur without progressive PR prolongation. Mobitz II is treated as infranodal/high-risk until proven otherwise because it can progress to complete heart block [1].

## Admission versus expedited outpatient evaluation

Disposition should be based on the **highest-risk plausible diagnosis**, the observed ECG, recurrence pattern, and whether immediate monitoring/follow-up is actually available.

### Admit or keep in observation/telemetry if any of the following are present

**Immediate emergency admission/treatment thresholds**—manage as unstable/symptomatic bradycardia if bradyarrhythmia is accompanied by cardiopulmonary compromise: **hypotension, acutely altered mental status, signs of shock, ischemic chest discomfort, or acute heart failure**. The 2025 AHA bradycardia-with-pulse algorithm also instructs clinicians to attach a monitor, obtain ECG, treat underlying causes, use atropine 1 mg IV every 3–5 minutes to 3 mg maximum, and if ineffective use transcutaneous pacing and/or dopamine or epinephrine infusion with expert consultation/transvenous pacing consideration [4]. Symptomatic bradycardia that **persists despite correcting or removing reversible causes**—for example, holding/reducing AV-nodal blockers such as metoprolol when safe and correcting metabolic/electrolyte triggers—also warrants admission or urgent monitored treatment: the AHA algorithm explicitly includes identifying/treating underlying causes, drugs/toxicologic causes such as β-blockers, and electrolyte abnormalities, while the 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS bradycardia guideline recommends evaluation/treatment of reversible causes in symptomatic SND and eliminating/mitigating the offending condition; for reversible AV block such as drug toxicity, it recommends medical/supportive care, including temporary transvenous pacing if necessary, before deciding on permanent pacing [4][1].

**High-risk conduction or arrhythmic findings**—admit for continuous telemetry and urgent cardiology/electrophysiology evaluation if the ECG or monitoring shows or strongly suggests:

- **Mobitz II second-degree AV block**;
- **High-grade AV block** (two or more consecutive nonconducted P waves);
- **Third-degree/complete heart block**;
- **Symptomatic sinus pauses/asystole** or bradycardia temporally linked to presyncope/syncope;
- **Alternating bundle branch block**;
- Syncope with bundle branch block plus EPS evidence of infranodal disease/HV interval ≥70 ms;
- New bifascicular block or bundle-branch conduction disease with syncope when intermittent high-grade AV block is a concern [1][2].

The syncope guideline gives hospital evaluation/treatment a Class I recommendation when a serious medical condition potentially relevant to syncope is identified; its examples include **symptomatic conduction system disease, Mobitz II or third-degree heart block, symptomatic bradycardia or sinus pauses not neurally mediated, sustained/symptomatic VT, cardiac ischemia, acute heart failure, PE/aortic dissection, severe anemia/GI bleeding, major injury, or persistent vital-sign abnormality** [2]. ACEP’s ED syncope policy similarly recommends admission for syncope with heart failure/structural heart disease or other high-risk factors, including **older age/comorbidities and abnormal ECG with dysrhythmia or significant conduction abnormality** [5].

**Clinical-risk and systems-risk thresholds**—admit or observe if any of these apply:

- recurrent unexplained syncope/near-syncope with abnormal ECG and no benign diagnosis;
- age >60, male sex, known structural/ischemic heart disease, prior arrhythmia, or reduced ventricular function;
- exertional or supine events, palpitations, brief/no prodrome, injury, family history of premature sudden death, persistent abnormal vitals, bleeding, positive troponin, or heart failure signs;
- recurrent events in the ED;
- inability to place a medically indicated ambulatory monitor before discharge;
- lack of safe rapid follow-up, unreliable support, or inability to return promptly if symptoms recur [2][5].

### Expedited outpatient evaluation is reasonable only under stricter conditions

Expedited outpatient evaluation is reasonable only if **all** of the following are true:

1. He remains hemodynamically stable and has no ongoing altered mental status, ischemic chest pain, acute heart failure, shock, or recurrent ED events [4].
2. Repeat ECG/telemetry review does **not** show Mobitz II, high-grade AV block, complete heart block, symptomatic pauses/asystole, alternating bundle branch block, or another serious arrhythmia [1][2].
3. A monitor is **placed before discharge**—preferably a longer-duration patch/event/loop monitor or MCOT for this non-daily symptom pattern [1][2].
4. Follow-up is expedited in **days**, not six weeks, with cardiology/electrophysiology or a syncope/arrhythmia pathway able to review transmitted results and act on alerts [2].
5. He has reliable family support, clear return precautions, and medication changes are safely coordinated.

Discharge with only routine six-week cardiology follow-up is therefore the lowest-safety option and is inadequate in this scenario because the key diagnostic test has already been denied twice and the ECG is not normal.

## Pacing implications relevant to disposition

The pacing threshold differs by rhythm diagnosis:

| Rhythm diagnosis | Pacing/disposition implication |
|---|---|
| Acquired **Mobitz II**, **high-grade AV block**, or **third-degree AV block** not due to reversible/physiologic causes | Permanent pacing is Class I regardless of symptom correlation; admission/urgent cardiology is appropriate [1]. |
| **Infranodal AV block** or alternating bundle branch block | High risk of progression to complete heart block; pacing is recommended for alternating bundle branch block and for syncope with bundle branch block plus HV ≥70 ms/infranodal block [1]. |
| **Sinus node dysfunction** | No minimum heart rate or pause duration alone mandates pacing; temporal correlation of symptoms with bradycardia is central [1]. |
| **Mobitz I / AV nodal block** without high-risk features | Usually requires symptom correlation before pacing; outpatient monitoring may be reasonable if stable and rapid follow-up is secured [1]. |

Thus, this patient’s current tracing is not enough to order a pacemaker solely from the ED description, but it is enough to justify monitored evaluation and to avoid routine unmonitored discharge.

## Reversible-cause management

Metoprolol is clinically important here. The bradycardia guideline lists beta-adrenergic blockers among medications that can induce or exacerbate bradycardia or conduction disorders; negative chronotropes such as beta blockers, non-dihydropyridine calcium-channel blockers, and digoxin can lower sinus rate, and reducing or stopping the offending drug may be appropriate when symptomatic bradycardia occurs, after weighing risks and benefits [1].

For this patient, the discharge/observation plan should include:

- hold or reduce **metoprolol** if clinically safe, with an alternative hypertension plan if needed;
- review for other AV nodal blockers or interacting drugs;
- confirm no acute reversible metabolic trigger—his potassium, magnesium, creatinine, hemoglobin, TSH, troponins, and chest X-ray are reassuring, but they do not rule out intermittent AV block or pauses [2];
- if AV block persists despite removing reversible factors, do not assume the beta blocker was the sole cause; the bradycardia guideline notes that therapeutic-dose beta blockers/calcium-channel blockers are often not the only cause of new AV block and many patients ultimately require pacing if block persists [1].

## Recommended disposition for the presented scenario

**Recommended plan before discharge:** place him in ED observation or admit briefly to telemetry while arranging a **hospital-issued longer-duration ambulatory ECG monitor**. If no high-grade block is seen during observation and he remains stable, discharge can be considered only after the monitor is physically applied and follow-up is accelerated. Given two denied Holter authorizations and six-week cardiology access delay, the practical device should be one the hospital can arrange directly: **7–14-day patch monitor or external event/loop monitor**, with **MCOT/MCT preferred** if clinicians want real-time alerts for pauses, high-grade AV block, or profound bradycardia.

**Admit rather than discharge** if the dropped beats are Mobitz II, 2:1 block with uncertain level and concerning features, high-grade AV block, complete heart block, symptomatic pauses/asystole, recurrent ED symptoms, hemodynamic compromise, or if no ambulatory monitor and prompt follow-up can be secured. **Expedited outpatient evaluation** is defensible only when high-grade/dangerous conduction disease has been excluded, the monitor is placed before leaving, follow-up is in days, and support/return precautions are reliable [1][2][4][5].
