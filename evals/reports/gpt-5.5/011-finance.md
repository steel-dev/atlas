# India NCD public issues open on 8 December 2025 — retiree fixed-income credit and post-tax yield screen

## Executive view

Based on the NCD calendar and issue pages fetched, three public NCD issues were open in India on **8 December 2025**: **KLM Axiva Finvest** (1–12 Dec 2025), **Muthoot Mercantile** (4–17 Dec 2025) and **Edelweiss Financial Services** (opened 5 Dec; close date is shown as 11 Dec in Chittorgarh’s calendar but as 18 Dec on RR Finance/MyInvestmentIdeas, so it was open on 8 Dec under either date) [1][2][3][4][5]. Prachay Capital is shown opening on 11 Dec, so it was not open on 8 Dec [1].

For a retiree seeking fixed income, the relative-value result is not a simple “highest coupon wins” decision. The market was offering a clear credit-yield trade-off:

1. **Best credit quality among the open issues: Edelweiss Financial Services** — CRISIL A+/Stable, secured, diversified financial-services group, effective yields up to ~10.10% [4][5]. It is still below AA/AAA and has NBFC/financial-sector risks, but it is the only fetched issue with a CRISIL rating and the strongest rating in this set [4][5].
2. **Higher-yield but lower-rated options: Muthoot Mercantile and KLM Axiva** — both are secured NCDs but only BBB category in the fetched sources. Muthoot Mercantile is rated IND BBB/Stable by India Ratings, with effective yields up to 11.73%; KLM Axiva is rated Acuité BBB/Stable, with effective yields up to 11.30% [3][6][7][8][9].
3. **Debt-to-equity red flags:** Muthoot Mercantile’s standalone debt/equity ratio rises from **4.48x pre-issue to 5.19x post-issue** on the September 30, 2025 base, and KLM Axiva disclosed a **5.40x debt/equity ratio** for the September 30, 2025 quarter; both are high-leverage NBFC/gold-loan-style credit exposures and should be capped in retiree portfolios [6][10]. Edelweiss’ fetched September 2025 presentation shows consolidated net debt of ₹11,330 crore and net worth of ₹5,636 crore, implying roughly 2.0x net debt/net worth on a simplified basis, while also disclosing subsidiary-level NBFC net D/E of 1.2x and housing-finance net D/E of 2.3x [11].

**Investment thesis:** for an income-dependent retiree, **Edelweiss is the only issue that can be considered a core/anchor candidate within this three-name set**, because its rating is materially stronger than the BBB alternatives. Muthoot Mercantile and KLM Axiva compensate investors with 100–160 bps higher headline yields, but the lower ratings and high leverage mean they should be treated as satellite/high-risk income exposures rather than retirement-income anchors. If the investment committee requires an investment-grade screen only, all three are formally investment grade in the broad Indian scale sense, but the **quality ranking is Edelweiss first, then Muthoot/KLM as higher-yield BBB risk**.

### Post-tax yield illustration

For the post-tax illustration, the interest cash flows are treated as taxable income and the table applies a simple after-tax formula: **post-tax yield = stated effective annual yield × (1 – marginal tax rate including cess)**. The tax-rate assumptions are illustrative, not client-specific: **generic investor = 30% slab + 4% cess = 31.2% effective tax**, and **senior citizen = 20% slab + 4% cess = 20.8% effective tax**. The fetched tax source explains that tax is computed by adding income heads including “other sources,” applying slab rates, and adding 4% cess; it also gives the senior-citizen old-regime slab of 20% for ₹5–10 lakh and 30% above ₹10 lakh [12]. Actual post-tax yield depends on the investor’s chosen tax regime, total income, surcharge, set-offs and whether any capital gains arise from secondary-market sale.

## Competitive positioning and relative value

The three issues sit in different parts of the risk spectrum:

- **Edelweiss:** diversified financial conglomerate, CRISIL A+/Stable, lower yield. The 120-month series raises duration and issuer-event risk, but the credit rating is materially stronger than the BBB alternatives [4][5].
- **Muthoot Mercantile:** high-yield secured NCD with strong reported growth, but BBB rating and 5.19x indicated post-issue D/E. The yield premium over Edelweiss is meaningful, but it is compensation for leverage and lower rating, not a free spread pickup [3][6].
- **KLM Axiva:** high-yield secured NCD with broad branch distribution and granular lending segments, but BBB rating, 5.40x September 2025 D/E, PAT decline in FY25 and concentrated funding profile [7][10][9].

**Spread/yield pickup versus Edelweiss:** using maximum effective yields, Muthoot Mercantile offers about **+163 bps** over Edelweiss (11.73% vs 10.10%), while KLM offers about **+120 bps** over Edelweiss (11.30% vs 10.10%) [3][4][7]. For a retiree, that pickup is only attractive if the portfolio can tolerate BBB credit migration, liquidity and refinancing risk.

## Historical precedents and cycle pattern recognition

The fetched sources support a pattern that is familiar in Indian NBFC credit cycles: public NCDs with higher coupons often come from lenders with weaker ratings, higher leverage or less diversified funding, and the apparent “income advantage” can disappear quickly if liquidity tightens. In this set, the pattern is visible in the BBB issuers: Muthoot Mercantile and KLM Axiva offer the highest yields, but each carries high debt/equity and BBB-level credit risk [3][6][7][10][9].

The post-2018 NBFC cycle lesson is therefore applicable as a risk framework even where the fetched sources do not provide Bloomberg/FactSet-style historical spread series: retirees should avoid concentration in lower-rated NBFC NCDs, prefer shorter tenors when credit quality is weaker, and size exposures assuming that secondary-market liquidity may be poor in stress. The KLM rating note itself identifies dependence on NCDs/sub-debt, regional concentration and the need to mobilize low-cost funds as monitorables [9]. Edelweiss’ own/fetched review risk section similarly highlights NBFC vulnerability to liquidity tightness, economic slowdown and regulatory change [5].

## Regulatory landscape and policy implications

All three issues are public issues of listed NCDs governed by the SEBI non-convertible securities framework. The Muthoot abridged prospectus states that the issue is made pursuant to the **SEBI (Issue and Listing of Non-Convertible Securities) Regulations, 2021**, the Companies Act, 2013 and the SEBI NCS Master Circular; it is not proposed to be underwritten [6]. The Muthoot document also states that the NCDs will be considered secured only if the charged asset is registered with the sub-registrar/RoC/CERSAI/depository as applicable or independently verifiable by the debenture trustee, and that failure to execute the debenture trust-cum-hypothecation deed within the regulatory timeline requires at least **2% p.a. additional interest** over the agreed coupon until execution [6].

For institutional compliance, the regulatory implications are:

- Do not treat “secured” as equivalent to sovereign safety; security creation, asset cover and trustee enforcement matter [6].
- Verify final prospectus, rating letter, trustee agreement, asset-cover certificate and listing approval before subscribing; SEBI pages show final prospectus filings for Muthoot and KLM [13][14].
- Because allotment is on first-come-first-served basis within categories for the fetched issues, subscription timing can affect execution, but this should not override credit discipline [3][4][7].

## Forward curve, rates and leading indicators

The fetched source set did not include a Bloomberg/FactSet forward curve or live government-bond curve as of 8 Dec 2025. Therefore, the forward-curve section cannot be populated with institutional market data from those platforms without new licensed data. The available evidence still gives practical leading indicators to monitor:

1. **RBI rate/liquidity cycle:** NBFC NCD spreads are sensitive to system liquidity and policy-rate expectations; weaker-rated issuers usually reprice more sharply when liquidity tightens.
2. **Issuer refinancing access:** KLM’s rating note specifically makes mobilisation of low-cost funds a key monitorable and notes concentration toward NCDs and sub-debt [9].
3. **Asset quality:** KLM disclosed GNPA 2.21% and NNPA 1.19% for September 2025; Edelweiss disclosed NBFC GNPA 3.35% and NNPA 1.94%, and housing-finance GNPA 2.47% and NNPA 2.00% [10][11].
4. **Leverage/asset cover:** Muthoot’s post-issue D/E of 5.19x and KLM’s 5.40x D/E are the most important red-flag indicators in this live set [6][10].
5. **Rating migration:** a one-notch downgrade in BBB NCDs would likely impair secondary liquidity and investor appetite more than a similar downgrade from A+.

## Recommendation framework for an institutional retiree mandate

### If the mandate is conservative retirement income

- **Primary candidate:** Edelweiss, preferably shorter/intermediate tenors rather than the 120-month tail, because it has the only CRISIL rating and the strongest credit profile in the open set [4][5].
- **Avoid or cap:** KLM Axiva and Muthoot Mercantile should not be core retiree holdings because both are BBB and high leverage. If used, they should be capped as small satellite positions and diversified across issuers and maturities [6][10][9].

### If the mandate allows tactical high-yield NCD exposure

- **Muthoot Mercantile** offers the highest tax-adjusted yield, but this is paired with the clearest leverage flag: 5.19x indicated post-issue D/E [3][6].
- **KLM Axiva** offers slightly lower peak yield than Muthoot but still materially above Edelweiss; its 5.40x D/E, FY25 PAT decline and funding concentration are the main offsets [7][10][9].
- **Edelweiss** is the better risk-adjusted anchor even though it offers the lowest post-tax yield [4][5].

### Final ranking

| Rank | For retiree suitability | For headline/post-tax yield | Rationale |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Edelweiss** | Muthoot Mercantile | Edelweiss has strongest rating; Muthoot has highest yield but high D/E. |
| 2 | **Muthoot Mercantile / KLM Axiva only as capped satellites** | KLM Axiva | Both BBB; KLM has high D/E and funding concentration, Muthoot has 5.19x post-issue D/E. |
| 3 | **Avoid concentration in BBB NCDs for retiree core income** | Edelweiss | Edelweiss yields less but provides better credit quality. |

**Bottom line:** For a retiree-focused fixed-income portfolio, the evidence supports a **quality-first allocation**: use Edelweiss as the only potential core NCD in the 8 Dec 2025 open set, and treat Muthoot Mercantile and KLM Axiva as higher-yield, lower-rated, high-leverage credits that require strict position limits, shorter-tenor preference and enhanced monitoring. The additional 120–163 bps of pre-tax yield over Edelweiss is not sufficient, by itself, to justify a large retiree allocation to BBB/high-D/E issuers [3][4][6][7][10][9].
