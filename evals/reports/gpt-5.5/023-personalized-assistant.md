# Relocation comparison: Austin vs. Raleigh vs. Nashville for a Denver senior mechanical engineer household

## Bottom line

**Raleigh/Cary is the best 10-year financial and practical fit among the three, with Austin second and Nashville third.** The decisive reasons are: (1) Raleigh’s strong K–5 options in west Cary have high ratings and materially lower housing costs than comparable high-rated Nashville zones; (2) its effective property-tax rate is far below Austin’s and only modestly above Nashville’s; (3) the west Cary school zones are close to Research Triangle Park and adjacent advanced-manufacturing/life-science/industrial employers, making a sub-30-minute commute plausible; and (4) Raleigh’s BLS mechanical-engineer wage differential is lower than Austin’s, but the housing and tax savings dominate over 10 years.

This conclusion is not a claim that Raleigh has the deepest mechanical-engineering labor market. **Austin appears to have the strongest advanced-manufacturing/aerospace upside** because of semiconductor, EV, defense, robotics, airport, and North Austin/Round Rock industrial corridors, while Nashville’s engineering demand is more automotive/logistics/industrial than aerospace. However, the publicly retrievable sources available in this run did **not** support a defensible exact trailing-12-month posting count by city after deduplication. I therefore treat job-market depth qualitatively and flag the count as an unresolved diligence item rather than inventing a number.

## Inputs and assumptions used in the 10-year model

Household: married couple, one 6-year-old child, current Denver salary **$95,000**, buying in an 8+/10 elementary-school zone within about 30 minutes of plausible mechanical-engineering industrial employment.

Model assumptions, kept consistent across cities:

- Purchase in the identified high-rated elementary zone.
- 20% down, 30-year fixed mortgage, 6.75% nominal interest rate.
- 10-year holding period.
- Home appreciation forward-looking base case uses the city’s annualized FHFA metro appreciation since 2020, but capped at **4.0%/yr** to avoid mechanically extrapolating the pandemic-era boom; sensitivity is discussed.
- Selling cost at exit: 6% of resale value.
- Home insurance/HOA are included as broad estimates because city-, house-, carrier-, roof-, flood-, wildfire-, hail-, and HOA-specific data were not pinned down in the fetched sources. The ranking is not very sensitive to reasonable HOA variation, but Texas insurance can be material.
- Federal income tax is excluded because it is largely invariant across the move options at the adjusted salaries; the comparison focuses on state/local income tax, property tax, and sales tax.
- Sales-taxable consumption is estimated at 35% of gross pay. This is a modeling assumption, not a source-reported value.

For consistent appreciation, I used FRED/FHFA All-Transactions House Price Index metro series: Austin-Round Rock-Georgetown, Raleigh-Cary, Nashville-Davidson–Murfreesboro–Franklin, and Denver-Aurora-Lakewood. The FRED pages identify the series as FHFA all-transactions HPI, quarterly, not seasonally adjusted, index 1995:Q1=100, with Q1 2026 as the latest observation for Austin, Raleigh, Nashville, and Denver in the fetched/search results [13] [14] [15] [16].

| Raleigh | ATNHPIUS39580Q | Q1 2026 **367.92** | Broadly strong and more stable appreciation than Austin’s boom/bust pattern. |

Because the stored FRED pages clearly established the latest observations but the exact 2020 Q1 observations were not reliably extracted for every metro before source limits were reached, the base-case model uses a conservative **4% forward appreciation cap** rather than direct extrapolation. This prevents over-crediting any city for 2020–2022 appreciation.

## Job-market depth: what could and could not be established

I searched public web results for exact title and seniority variants such as **“Senior Mechanical Engineer,” “Sr Mechanical Engineer,” “Senior Aero-Mechanical,” “Staff Mechanical Engineer,”** paired with **aerospace, manufacturing, automation, defense, automotive, semiconductor, industrial**, and city/metro terms. I deduplicated conceptually by company + role + location.

The public web evidence was enough to identify examples, but **not enough to establish a defensible trailing-12-month count**. A reliable answer would require LinkedIn/Indeed/Lightcast/Burning Glass or employer ATS export with posting date, repost suppression, and duplicate-company filtering. The examples found suggest the following relative ranking:

1. **Austin — strongest depth/upside.** More diverse advanced-manufacturing/aerospace-adjacent ecosystem: semiconductor, EV, defense robotics, airport/aviation, industrial automation.
2. **Raleigh — solid but more specialized.** RTP, life sciences, industrial automation, GE Aerospace/Durham, electronics and advanced manufacturing. Fewer obvious pure mechanical/aerospace postings than Austin, but good white-collar engineering environment.
3. **Nashville — thinner for senior mechanical/aerospace.** Strong automotive, battery, tire, logistics, and general manufacturing base; fewer pure aerospace/senior mechanical roles surfaced publicly.

This is the main tradeoff: **Austin likely has the better career option set, while Raleigh has the better household financial geometry.** Nashville’s lower taxes do not compensate for the combination of lower wages, high top-school housing prices, and thinner aerospace/mechanical posting depth.

### Sensitivity

- If Austin compensation lands closer to its 75th–90th percentile BLS range because of semiconductor/EV/aerospace specialization, Austin can overtake Raleigh.
- If Nashville buyers choose Percy Priest/Julia Green rather than Clovercroft, the home price is materially higher and Nashville falls further behind.
- If Raleigh’s school assignment/cap rules force purchase farther from RTP or into a more expensive zone, Raleigh’s advantage narrows.
- If mortgage rates fall materially, higher-priced Nashville benefits more; if rates stay high, Raleigh benefits because it has the best combined tax/commute/housing balance.

## Overall ranking

1. **Raleigh/Cary — recommended.** Best balance of high-performing K–5 schools, manageable tax burden, plausible sub-30-minute commute to RTP/Morrisville industrial zones, and 10-year modeled financial outcome.
2. **Austin — best career upside, second-best financial outcome.** The selected Round Rock/northwest Austin school zones are cheaper than Raleigh’s west Cary target, and Austin has the strongest engineering-market upside. The drawback is Texas property tax and commute risk if the role is southeast/Tesla rather than north/Round Rock.
3. **Nashville/Franklin — best tax structure but weakest combined outcome.** No wage income tax and low effective property tax help, but high-rated school-zone housing is expensive, BLS mechanical-engineer wages are slightly below Raleigh’s, and senior mechanical/aerospace job depth appears thinner.

## Closing note

If the relocation decision is primarily financial and school/commute constrained, **target west Cary/Raleigh first**, then Austin’s Round Rock/northwest corridor as the career-upside alternative. Before committing, obtain a paid job-posting export or recruiter market map for senior mechanical-engineering roles over the trailing 12 months, verify exact school assignment/cap status, and test live Google/Apple/Waze commute times from specific addresses to specific employers during the hours you would actually travel.
