**Bottom line: do not treat this as “adjusting.”** Wheezing/chest tightness that becomes worse **within minutes of using a new blue albuterol/salbutamol rescue inhaler**, together with home SpO₂ readings down to **91%** from a prior **95%**, matches multiple urgent-warning patterns in the sources: albuterol labeling describes **paradoxical bronchospasm** as potentially life-threatening and says to **discontinue that albuterol inhaler immediately and use alternative therapy** if it occurs [1]; MedlinePlus says that if albuterol causes wheezing or difficulty breathing immediately after inhalation, **call the doctor right away and do not use albuterol again unless the doctor tells you to** [2]. If you are gasping now, your SpO₂ is around **91%**, symptoms are worsening, or the reliever is not helping, the safest source-consistent action is to **call emergency services now rather than keep re-dosing or drive yourself** [3], [4], [5].

The key distinction is that **ordinary adjustment side effects are different from worsening breathing**. NHS and Mayo Clinic patient guidance allow that effects such as shakiness, fast heartbeat, or headache can settle as the body gets used to salbutamol/albuterol, but worsening breathlessness, chest pain, coughing, wheezing, or difficulty breathing after use is listed separately as a serious warning requiring urgent medical help [10], [6]. A rescue inhaler is expected to help breathing within **a few minutes**, not make wheezing and chest tightness worse [11].

The most likely medication-specific concern raised by the sources is **paradoxical bronchospasm/bronchoconstriction**: an unexpected tightening of the airways when bronchodilation is expected [9]. FDA/DailyMed labeling says inhaled albuterol can cause paradoxical bronchospasm that may be life-threatening, and if it occurs the albuterol inhaler should be stopped immediately and alternative therapy instituted [1]. The FDA-approved patient labeling similarly lists worsening trouble breathing, coughing, and wheezing as serious effects and directs patients to stop the inhaler and call the doctor or get emergency help right away [1]. This is not proof that your exact inhaler is the cause, because asthma itself can deteriorate over days or weeks, but the **minutes-after-use** pattern is exactly the timing that makes a paradoxical reaction unsafe to ignore.

A formulation change can matter. In one peer-reviewed case report, a 25-year-old with asthma had acute shortness of breath immediately after albuterol for **4 weeks**; pulmonary-function testing showed significant declines after albuterol HFA and levalbuterol HFA, while nebulized albuterol and ipratropium improved lung function, and the patient was transitioned to an ipratropium rescue inhaler [9]. The authors suspected inactive ingredients, including oleic acid in Xopenex HFA and ethanol in ProAir HFA, as possible irritant or hypersensitivity triggers in inflamed/allergic airways, while noting the exact mechanism remained uncertain [9]. That case does not mean every new blue inhaler causes this, but it supports the plausibility that a switch in product/formulation can be clinically relevant.

Your oxygen readings add a second independent reason not to wait. Pulse oximeters can be wrong — FDA notes that readings may be affected by poor circulation, skin pigmentation, skin thickness, skin temperature, tobacco use, and fingernail polish, and should be interpreted with symptoms [7], [12]. But FDA also says to contact a healthcare provider if symptoms are serious or worsening, and low-oxygen warning symptoms include shortness of breath, difficulty breathing, chest tightness, worsening cough, fast/racing pulse, restlessness, and blue lips/face/nails [7]. In acute-asthma guidance, SpO₂ **<92%** is not a “watch and see” value: it is tied to oxygen supplementation, assessment for severe/life-threatening asthma, and in BTS/SIGN guidance arterial blood gas measurement when SpO₂ is **<92%** or other life-threatening features are present [4], [5].

So the practical answer is:

- **If you are currently gasping, cannot speak in full sentences, have chest pain/tightness that is worsening, feel faint/confused/exhausted, have blue lips/face/nails, or your SpO₂ is around 91%, call emergency services now.** Asthma + Lung UK says an asthma attack with a reliever inhaler not helping is a medical emergency and an ambulance is safer than a lift/taxi because paramedics can treat on the way [3].
- **Do not keep using the same new albuterol/salbutamol inhaler if it repeatedly makes breathing worse immediately after use**, unless a clinician specifically tells you to; this follows MedlinePlus and FDA/DailyMed warnings for immediate post-inhalation wheezing/difficulty breathing/paradoxical bronchospasm [2], [1].
- **Do not stop separate daily preventer/controller asthma medicines** just because the rescue inhaler is suspect; Mayo Clinic cautions not to stop anti-inflammatory asthma medicine unless the doctor tells you to [6].
- **Get same-day clinician review even if symptoms settle**, because needing more reliever than usual or having a reliever that seems less effective can mean asthma is worsening and treatment needs reassessment [11], [6]. NHS guidance says routine reliever use above **4 times in 24 hours**, use on **3 or more days per week**, or night use at least **once per week** should trigger contact with a doctor/pharmacist/nurse [11].

In short: **do not stop all asthma treatment, but do stop treating this specific reaction as normal.** A rescue inhaler should relieve symptoms within minutes; worsening wheeze and chest tightness immediately after use plus SpO₂ around **91%** warrants urgent medical assessment and, if happening now, emergency care [11], [4], [2].
